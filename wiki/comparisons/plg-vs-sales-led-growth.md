## Overview

**Product-Led Growth (PLG)** and **Sales-Led Growth (SLG)** represent two ends of the GTM spectrum. PLG relies on the product itself to acquire, activate, and retain users through self-serve mechanics, while SLG depends on human sales teams driving deals through pipeline generation, demos, negotiations, and contracts. Most mature SaaS companies operate a hybrid of both. This page compares the two approaches across key dimensions, drawing on podcast guests who have built both motions at scale.

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | PLG | SLG |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition engine** | Viral loops, freemium, content, SEO | SDR outbound, field marketing, events |
| **User journey** | Self-serve sign-up → activation → paid | Lead generation → demo → proposal → close |
| **Time to first value** | Minutes to hours | Days to weeks |
| **Typical ACV range** | $1K–$50K | $50K–$10M+ |
| **Org ownership** | Growth team, product, engineering | Sales, marketing, revops |
| **Pricing model** | Usage-based, tiered, per-seat | Negotiated, contract-based, multi-year |
| **Scaling cost** | Low marginal cost (infrastructure) | High (headcount, comp, enablement) |
| **Data signal** | In-product behavior (activation, adoption) | CRM pipeline stage, activity metrics |
| **Retention driver** | Habit and time-to-value | Relationship and contract lock-in |
| **Company archetypes** | Slack, Calendly, Canva, Stripe, Vercel | Salesforce, Oracle, SAP, Workday |

## Synthesis

**Jeanne DeWitt Grosser (Vercel, Stripe)** on Lenny: "PLG makes sense for a lot of companies at the outset, unless you are very explicitly building a product for enterprise." She advocates a hybrid: start PLG to build product-market fit and a user base, then add a sales layer to capture enterprise expansion. At both Stripe and Vercel, the PLG motion generated the leads that the sales team closed.

**Elena Verna** on Lenny (2023, 2025): Articulates the two paths to hybrid: (1) start PLG, add sales later ("product-led sales"), or (2) start SLG, add PLG later. She argues the first path is easier because PLG creates a natural lead flow for sales. The second path requires a fundamental cultural shift — sales orgs resist giving up control. Her framework: "If you're starting from PLG, growth owns the top of funnel and sales owns expansion. If starting from SLG, you need a dedicated PLG pod insulated from sales pressure."

**Hila Qu (GitLab, Reforge)** on Lenny: Explains why competitors force the shift — "if you are in a sales motion dominated industry, your competitors will be adding PLG. As soon as they add it they will have a benefit of attracting more end users who become cases to the employer." GitLab transitioned successfully by creating a distinct PLG org with its own product, marketing, and sales leads.

**Annie Pearl (Calendly CPO)** on Lenny: Describes Calendly's evolution from 99% PLG ARR to adding an SLG motion that became 20% of ARR — and the fastest-growing segment. "We hired our first CRO and scaled the sales team in our SLG motion. It now represents about 20% of our ARR and it's actually the fastest growing segment." This validates the hybrid model for companies that max out the self-serve ceiling.

**Bret Taylor (Sierra, former Salesforce CEO)** on Lenny: Notes that PLG became very popular, but "a lot companies use that label to describe a bottoms-up strategy that still required a sales team." He cites Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Oracle as archetypal SLG companies, and observes that the best modern companies (e.g., Stripe) mix both approaches depending on customer segment.

**Ravi Mehta (Reforge EIR)** on Lenny: Connects GTM model to product design — PLG products must be designed for rapid time-to-value and self-serve onboarding, while SLG products can afford more complexity because a salesperson bridges the gap.
