# Talent Decision Framework — When to Keep, Coach, or Transition Someone

A synthesis of frameworks from Lenny's Podcast guests on managing underperforming team members, having difficult conversations, and making talent decisions.

## The Core Question

From [[Rachel Lockett]] (former HR leader at Stripe):

> **"Would you enthusiastically rehire this person for the same role?"**

This is the Stripe question. It's binary — people have a physical, honest reaction. If the answer is no, you must act. But action doesn't mean immediate firing.

## The Decision Tree

```
Would you enthusiastically rehire them?
├── YES → Invest. Coach. Grow them.
└── NO → Options (not just firing):
    ├── Have the difficult conversation (NVC: observe → feelings → needs → request)
    ├── Put them on a performance plan
    ├── Move them to a different role (stage may have changed)
    ├── Restructure the role
    └── Let them go (with compassion)
```

### Key nuance from Rachel Lockett:

The stage of the company matters. "A CFO that was really fine back then is now completely wrong — he should be the controller." It's natural in quickly scaling businesses that the leadership team's jobs change.

## How to Have the Conversation

### Rachel Lockett's approach:
1. **Reframe** — "This is hard because I have something to learn here, and because it matters"
2. **Take responsibility** — "What is my piece in making this dynamic happen?"
3. **NVC framework** — Observe what's happening, name your feelings, state your needs, make a request
4. **Enter with humility and curiosity** — No model can fix a person coming in rigid and full of blame

### Alisa Cohn's approach (executive coach):
- Specific scripts and phrases for performance reviews
- **Key reframe:** "Through that upset on the other side can be a whole new possibility, a lot of joy and freedom"
- The job of a leader isn't to make people happy — it's to be clear and direct with compassion

### Carilu Dietrich's approach (Atlassian executive):
- **Interview question:** "How many people have you fired? Tell me about each experience."
  - If you've never fired anyone, you haven't managed many people for very long
  - How they talk about it (compassion, humanity, circumstances) reveals their leadership under pressure
- Firing is the hardest part of a leader's career

## Common Mistakes

1. **Avoiding the conversation** — "The cave you fear contains the treasure you seek" (Lenny quoting Joseph Campbell)
2. **Telling when you should coach** — If they're capable but stuck, coach them. If they lack info/experience, tell them.
3. **Waiting too long** — Rachel: leaders "keep getting let down, and actually they kind of know deep down, this is not working out"
4. **Treating it as binary (keep vs. fire)** — There's a whole spectrum of options
5. **Letting stage mismatch go unaddressed** — The person may be excellent for a different role as the company scales
