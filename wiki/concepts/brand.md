# Brand

## Synthesis

1. **Brand is not your logo — it's the emotional residue left after every interaction.** Arielle Jackson (First Round Capital, Google, Square) makes a powerful distinction: a brand is the sum of all customer touchpoints, not just visual identity. She argues founders obsess over logos and colors when they should obsess over every email, support interaction, and product experience.

2. **Category creation is the highest form of brand building.** Barbra Gago (Pando, Miro, Greenhouse) describes how category-defining companies build brands that are synonymous with the category itself. Miro didn't just build a better whiteboard — it created the "visual collaboration" category. Category creators get outserved brand value because they define the language customers use to think about the problem.

3. **Simplicity and taste are brand moats.** Stewart Butterfield (Slack) emphasizes that brand is what people say about you when you're not in the room, and the best brands are built on taste — making deliberate choices about what NOT to do. Slack's brand succeeds because of what it refuses to be: noisy, cluttered, or enterprise-corporate.

4. **Brand matters most when product-market fit is proven, not before.** Multiple guests agree that early-stage companies should focus on product-market fit before investing heavily in brand. Barbra Gago recommends waiting until you have clear evidence of product-market fit and the resources to maintain brand consistency at scale.

5. **Design literacy is brand infrastructure.** Jessica Hische argues that understanding typography, color theory, and visual hierarchy gives founders a "designer's eye" that pays dividends across every customer touchpoint. The best brands look effortless because enormous care went into the details.

## Key Disagreements

- **When to invest in brand:** Some argue brand building should start on day one; others say it's a waste before product-market fit.
- **Personal vs. company brand:** Debate over whether founders should build personal brands that ladder into company brands, or keep them separate.
- **Quantifying brand value:** Some believe brand ROI can be measured through surveys and NPS; others argue brand is inherently unmeasurable and requires faith.
