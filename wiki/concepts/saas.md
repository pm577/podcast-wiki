# SaaS

## Synthesis

1. **SaaS is the dominant business model for software, but AI is changing it.** Jason Fried (37signals) notes that for the last decade, SaaS (renting software) has been the default model. However, AI agents that execute tasks rather than provide tools may shift the paradigm toward outcome-based pricing rather than per-seat subscriptions.

2. **ARR is the universal SaaS metric, but depth matters.** Kyle Norton's playbook for going from $2M to $40M ARR emphasizes that not all ARR is equal — multi-year contracts, high net revenue retention, and low churn are worth more than the same ARR with monthly billing.

3. **SaaS unit economics determine survivability.** Patrick Campbell (ProfitWell) argues that most SaaS companies don't understand their unit economics well enough. Key metrics: CAC payback period (< 12 months is great), LTV:CAC ratio (> 3:1 is healthy), and gross margin (> 70% for pure SaaS).

4. **PLG is transforming SaaS GTM.** Hila Qu (Reforge) and Elena Verna document how SaaS companies are adding product-led growth motions to traditional sales-led models. The most successful SaaS companies now operate hybrid models — self-serve for low-touch customers, sales for high-touch.

5. **AI is compressing SaaS go-to-market cycles.** Lovable's journey to $200M ARR in one year shows that AI products can achieve SaaS-like recurring revenue with dramatically faster adoption curves. This compresses the traditional SaaS lifecycle from years to months.

## Key Disagreements

- **Per-seat vs. usage-based pricing:** Whether SaaS should stick with per-seat pricing (simple, predictable) or move to usage-based pricing (aligns with value, but variable for customers).
- **SaaS vs. "agents as a service":** Whether AI agents represent a new category altogether or just an evolution of SaaS. The answer affects pricing, go-to-market, and product architecture.
