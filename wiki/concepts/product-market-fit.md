# Product Market Fit

## Synthesis

1. **PMF is the single most important thing a startup does in its first three years.** Todd Jackson (First Round Capital) provides a comprehensive framework: PMF is underexplained and undervalued. He argues that most startups fail because they plateau before reaching true PMF — they have users but not "must-have" demand.

2. **The Sean Ellis Test is the most practical PMF metric.** Sean Ellis's framework: if 40%+ of users say they'd be "very disappointed" without your product, you have PMF. This simple survey question correlates strongly with long-term retention and growth — far better than usage metrics alone.

3. **PMF has two sides: demand-side and supply-side.** Benjamin Lauzier (Lyft, Thumbtack) notes that marketplaces need PMF on both sides. You can have great buyer demand but insufficient supply quality — or abundant supply but weak demand. True marketplace PMF requires both sides to be satisfied.

4. **Activation metrics predict PMF better than acquisition metrics.** Sean Ellis shows that the strongest PMF signal is whether newly acquired users reach the "aha moment" quickly and reliably. If onboarding is the bottleneck to value, fixing it is often the path to PMF.

5. **AI products can achieve PMF faster but face unique risks.** Elena Verna (Lovable) demonstrates that AI products can hit $200M ARR in one year — unprecedented velocity. However, Brendan Foody (Mercor) notes that AI products also face "novelty PMF" where users are intrigued but not truly reliant, creating fragile retention.

## Key Disagreements

- **PMF as a binary state vs. a spectrum:** Some treat PMF as a clear threshold (passed/failed), while others see it as a continuous scale where PMF deepens over time.
- **Early PMF metrics:** Debate over whether the Sean Ellis survey is still relevant for AI products or whether behavioral metrics (DAU/MAU, time-to-value, expansion rate) are more reliable signals.
