# Management

## Synthesis

1. **One-on-ones should include coaching conversations, not just status updates.** Keith Yandell (DoorDash, Uber) emphasizes that managers should reserve time in every 1:1 for career coaching and development conversations. Status updates can happen async; the 1:1's highest value is building the person, not the project.

2. **"Coaches, not managers" is emerging as a model.** Cam Adams (Canva) describes Canva's approach where managers function as coaches — they don't assign tasks but help team members develop judgment and capabilities. This creates more autonomous, higher-performing teams over time.

3. **Feedback is a practice, not an event.** Rachel Lockett argues that building high-trust teams requires making feedback a continuous, two-way practice. The key skill is shifting from judgment ("here's what you did wrong") to curiosity ("help me understand your approach"), which creates safety for growth.

4. **The sidecar model of management is highly effective.** Nikhyl Singhal (Meta, Google) offers a powerful metaphor: the manager is not driving the motorcycle but riding in the sidecar — attached, giving counsel, but not controlling the steering. This creates autonomy while maintaining support.

5. **Delegation is the fundamental management skill.** Matt MacInnis (Rippling) argues that understaffing forces effective delegation — when there aren't enough people to do everything, managers must truly let go and trust their teams. The most common management failure is holding onto responsibility without giving corresponding authority.

## Key Disagreements

- **Player-coach vs. dedicated manager:** Whether first-line managers should also be individual contributors (player-coach model) or purely focus on management. Player-coach models work in small teams but break down at scale.
- **Directive vs. supportive management:** Some contexts call for directive management (clear instructions, tight oversight) while others benefit from supportive management (autonomy, coaching). The best managers adapt their style to the individual and situation.
