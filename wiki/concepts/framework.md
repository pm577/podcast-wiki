# Framework

## Synthesis

1. **Frameworks compress experience into actionable models.** Molly Graham's high-growth handbook provides frameworks for navigating chaos, change, and scale — the result of years of experience at Facebook, Google, and Chan Zuckerberg Initiative. The best frameworks distill complex situations into memorable, actionable patterns that help others avoid reinventing the wheel.

2. **The best frameworks are memorable, not comprehensive.** Jason Cohen argues that frameworks fail when they try to capture every edge case. The most useful frameworks (Cohen's five-question growth diagnostic, Graham's "layers of scale") are simple enough to recall under pressure. A framework you can't remember at 2 AM during a crisis provides no value.

3. **Frameworks should be tested against experience, not just logic.** Albert Cheng emphasizes that many frameworks sound good in theory but fail in practice. The most reliable frameworks are those developed through repeated real-world application, not armchair reasoning. The best podcast guests share frameworks they've broken — and how they fixed them.

4. **Mental models are frameworks for thinking about thinking.** Stewart Butterfield discusses how metacognitive frameworks — models for understanding your own cognitive biases and decision-making patterns — are the highest-leverage frameworks. Understanding confirmation bias, availability bias, and the sunk cost fallacy helps leaders make better decisions across every domain.

5. **Frameworks evolve as companies and markets change.** Bangaly Kaba's concept of "managing complex change" includes a framework for organizational change that evolved through his work at Instagram, YouTube, and Facebook. The best frameworks are living documents that get updated as new data emerges. A static framework applied to a changing world creates false confidence.

## Key Disagreements

- **Proprietary vs. open frameworks:** Whether companies should develop their own internal frameworks or adopt industry-standard ones.
- **Simplicity vs. accuracy:** The tension between making frameworks simple enough to use and accurate enough to be useful.
- **Framework proliferation:** Whether having many frameworks creates useful optionality or analysis paralysis.
