# Enterprise

## Synthesis

1. **"Sell the alpha, not the feature."** Jen Abel (Jellyfish, State Affairs) argues that enterprise sales succeeds when you sell the emerging advantage (alpha) of your product — the unique insight or capability that early adopters get before the market catches up. Selling features puts you in a commodity comparison; selling alpha creates urgency.

2. **Layering enterprise sales on PLG requires a deliberate transition.** Kim Graves's playbook shows that adding enterprise sales to a PLG motion requires different sales reps, different compensation, and different pipeline management. The skills for self-serve conversion don't transfer to six-figure enterprise deals.

3. **Crossing the chasm requires a beachhead, not a broad market.** Geoffrey Moore's framework is foundational: enterprise startups should pick a narrow segment (beachhead) where they can dominate, then expand. Attempting to sell to the entire enterprise market from day one spreads resources too thin.

4. **AI is transforming enterprise sales.** Jason Lemkin replaced 20 sales reps with AI agents and found that AI excels at structured enterprise sales motions — lead qualification, follow-up sequences, and pipeline management. However, complex multi-stakeholder enterprise deals still require human relationship-building.

5. **Enterprise sales is founder-led in the early days.** Pete Kazanjy (Atrium) argues that founders must own enterprise sales initially — they have the deepest product knowledge and credibility. The transition from founder-led to rep-led sales is one of the hardest GTM inflection points.

## Key Disagreements

- **PLG-first vs. enterprise-first:** Whether startups should build for enterprise from day one (higher ACV, slower cycles) or start with self-serve PLG (faster adoption, lower ACV) and add enterprise motion later.
- **Vertical vs. horizontal enterprise products:** Some argue enterprise success requires deep vertical specialization; others argue horizontal platforms that serve multiple industries create larger TAMs.
