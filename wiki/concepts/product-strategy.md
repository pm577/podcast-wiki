# Product Strategy

## Synthesis

1. **Strategy comes before goals.** Ravi Mehta (Tinder, Facebook) argues that most teams start with goals (OKRs) without establishing strategy first. His "product strategy stack" framework works top-down: mission → vision → strategy → goals → roadmap. Goals without strategy are just wishes.

2. **Mission, vision, and strategy are distinct concepts.** Ravi Mehta clarifies: mission is what you're trying to achieve in the world; vision is what the world looks like once you've achieved it; strategy is the set of choices you make about where to play and how to win. Confusing these produces unfocused execution.

3. **Prioritization is the PM's most important tool.** Ian McAllister (Uber, Amazon, Airbnb) argues that prioritization is the number one skill of a product manager. With limited resources and unlimited ideas, the ability to choose what not to do is more important than choosing what to do.

4. **Roadmaps communicate strategy, not just schedules.** Janna Bastow (ProdPad) emphasizes that roadmaps should be outcome-oriented, not feature-oriented. A good roadmap communicates the strategic direction and the problems you're solving, not just a timeline of deliverables.

5. **Debugging strategy starts from the bottom up.** Ravi Mehta's framework works both ways: if you're having trouble meeting goals, the problem might be in the roadmap (wrong things built), the strategy (wrong approach), or the vision (wrong destination). Bottom-up debugging reveals where the strategy breaks.

## Key Disagreements

- **Data-driven vs. vision-driven strategy:** Some advocate for strategy derived from data patterns and user research; others argue that truly innovative products (like iPhone, Superhuman) come from a strong vision that data can't predict.
- **Annual vs. quarterly strategy reviews:** Whether strategy should be set annually with quarterly adjustments or whether the pace of change (especially with AI) demands continuous strategy iteration.
