# Angel Investing

## Synthesis

1. **Angel investing is about pattern matching, not spreadsheets.** Clark Landry's 20VC episode on angel investing emphasizes that early-stage angel investing is fundamentally qualitative. Angels invest in people and patterns they've seen succeed before, not discounted cash flow models. The key question: "Would I want to work with this founder for the next decade?"

2. **Syndicate investing reduces risk and expands deal flow.** Multiple 20VC guests from 2015 describe how angel syndicates became the dominant model for early-stage investing. By pooling capital with other angels, individual investors access better deal flow, share due diligence costs, and diversify across more startups.

3. **The best angels provide "smart money" — introductions and operating expertise.** Lisa Wu describes how angels who were former operators add the most value. Writing a check is table stakes; the real contribution is opening your network, advising on product strategy, and helping with hiring. Founders should choose angels not for their capital but for their Rolodex and expertise.

4. **Angel investing requires a 10-year time horizon and a portfolio approach.** Stefan Glaenzer (Passion Capital) emphasizes that angel investing is a "lemons problem" — most startups fail regardless of how good they look initially. The only rational approach is to write many small checks and expect the portfolio's return to come from 1-2 outlier successes.

5. **The best time to angel invest is when you don't need the money back.** A recurring theme: angels who invest funds they can afford to lose make better decisions. Financial pressure leads to short-term thinking, pushing founders to make bad decisions. Patience is the angel investor's superpower.

## Key Disagreements

- **Check size strategy:** Some angels write many small checks ($5-25K); others write fewer, larger checks ($100K+).
- **Sector specialization vs. generalist:** Debate over whether angels should focus on one sector or spread across industries.
- **Follow-on investing:** Some always reserve capital for follow-on rounds; others treat each round as a new decision.
