# Social Media

## Synthesis

1. **Social media is a distribution channel, not a brand strategy.** Jeanne DeWitt Grosser differentiates between companies that use social media tactically (sharing links) versus strategically (building audience equity). Strategic social media creates value that people seek out; tactical social media interrupts feeds. The most successful B2B companies on social media treat each platform as a distinct content medium with its own format, voice, and goals.

2. **Founder-led social media is the new PR.** Jason Cohen observes that founder Twitter/LinkedIn accounts have become more influential than traditional press coverage for B2B startups. When founders share authentic, transparent content about their company journey, they build trust that no press release can match. The key is consistency and vulnerability — showing failures alongside successes.

3. **Platform specialization beats omnipresence.** Albert Cheng (Duolingo, Grammarly) emphasizes that trying to be on every social platform dilutes quality. Duolingo famously concentrates on TikTok because the platform's format matches their brand voice. The lesson: pick one or two platforms where your content format and audience overlap, and go deep before expanding.

4. **Community-driven social media creates network effects.** Mike Seibel (YC, Twitch) describes how Twitch's growth was powered by streamers building audiences on the platform — the social media itself was the product. For product companies, the most durable social media strategy involves creating content that users want to share because it makes them look good.

5. **Measurement is broken for most social media efforts.** Multiple guests note that vanity metrics (likes, followers, impressions) don't correlate with business outcomes. The better approach: track social-media-sourced signups, content-to-conversion rates, and share-of-voice in relevant conversations.

## Key Disagreements

- **Founder vs. company accounts:** Debate over whether personal founder brands or company accounts drive more business value.
- **Paid social vs. organic:** Some argue organic social is dying and paid is required; others point to companies with massive organic reach.
- **Platform concentration risk:** Some advocate building on owned channels (email, blog) to avoid algorithm risk; others embrace platform-native strategies.
