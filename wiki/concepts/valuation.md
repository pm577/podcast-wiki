# Valuation

## Synthesis

1. **Early-stage valuation is largely arbitrary.** Michael Skok (Underscore VC) argues that valuation at seed and Series A is a "stupid concept" — there are too few data points and too much variation to make meaningful comparisons. Founders who obsess over valuation often optimize the wrong variable.

2. **Trust and delivery matter more than valuation terms.** Keith Yandell (DoorDash, Uber) deliberately shared conservative projections with investors and consistently exceeded them. This built trust that led to better subsequent terms — and he argues most founders would benefit from trading short-term valuation for long-term credibility.

3. **ARR multiples vary dramatically by category and growth rate.** Jason Lemkin (SaaStr) provides benchmarks: high-growth SaaS companies trade at 20-50x ARR, while moderate-growth companies trade at 5-15x. The variance within categories is larger than between categories — growth rate is the primary valuation driver.

4. **Valuation is a lagging indicator of business quality.** Multiple guests agree that the best way to increase valuation is to improve the underlying business — retention, growth rate, unit economics, and competitive position. Optimizing for valuation directly (rather than through fundamentals) almost always backfires.

5. **Comparable company analysis has limited utility for disruptive businesses.** Mike Maples (Floodgate) notes that the best venture investments (Airbnb, Pinterest) had no good comparables at the time of investment. True category creators are inherently hard to value using traditional methodologies.

## Key Disagreements

- **Market comparables vs. intrinsic value:** Some argue valuation should be based on comparable companies and market comps; others argue that early-stage companies should be valued on their specific fundamentals (team, traction, market size, business model).
- **Price vs. terms:** Whether founders should optimize for higher valuation (less dilution) or better terms (governance, liquidation preferences, board seats).
