# Year in Review

## Synthesis

1. **Year-in-review content serves as both reflection and forward-looking signal.** Ben Horowitz (a16z) uses annual retrospectives to identify patterns that are invisible day-to-day. The year-in-review format forces periodic sensemaking — stepping back from the week-to-week grind to ask what actually happened and what it means for the future.

2. **The best year-in-review content includes specific numbers and honest failures.** Melanie Perkins (Canva) shares that Canva's annual reviews include metrics that didn't meet expectations alongside wins. This honesty makes the content credible and useful. A year-in-review that only highlights successes is marketing, not reflection.

3. **AI is accelerating the pace of change that year-in-review content captures.** Simon Willison describes how the AI field moved so fast in 2025-2026 that year-in-review content written at the start of the year was outdated by mid-year. The solution: more frequent review cycles (quarterly or monthly highlights) plus a genuine year-in-review that identifies the truly structural changes amid the noise.

4. **Thematic structure beats chronological structure.** Rather than listing events month-by-month, the most impactful year-in-review content organizes around themes: "The Year AI Agents Became Real," "The Year Distribution Became the Moat," "The Year of Efficiency." Thematic framing helps readers understand the shape of change rather than just the sequence of events.

5. **Year-in-review content creates a historical record for the organization.** Multiple guests note that the most valuable year-in-review is not public content but internal reflection. Companies that document their annual lessons, strategic shifts, and missed opportunities create an organizational memory that helps new hires understand context and avoids repeating mistakes.

## Key Disagreements

- **Public vs. private:** Whether year-in-review content should be published externally or kept as internal reflection.
- **Forward-looking vs. backward-looking:** Whether year-in-review should include predictions for the coming year or focus purely on retrospective.
- **Quantitative vs. narrative:** Whether the review should emphasize metrics or stories.
