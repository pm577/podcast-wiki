# Team Building

## Synthesis

1. **AI-native teams need new structures.** Tomer Cohen (LinkedIn CPO) argues that the traditional product triad (PM, designer, engineer) is being disrupted by AI. Teams of the future will have "builders" who own the entire product development process — from ideation through coding and testing — supported by AI tools rather than large cross-functional teams.

2. **Org design should follow problems, not functions.** Multiple guests advocate for organizing teams around customer problems or outcomes, not functions (PM, design, engineering silos). Cross-functional pods focused on specific user journeys create better alignment and faster shipping.

3. **The right team structure depends on company stage.** Annie Pearl (Calendly) explains that early-stage teams benefit from generalists who can do everything. As the company scales, functional specialists become necessary — but hiring functional leaders too early can create overhead without corresponding value.

4. **Scaling teams requires intentional design, not reactive hiring.** Katie Dill (Stripe, Airbnb) warns against the fear of bold ideas that emerges as teams grow. Scaling organizations often become risk-averse; maintaining a culture of bold bets requires explicit design in team structure, incentives, and leadership behavior.

5. **Small teams with clear ownership outperform large teams.** Tomer Cohen shows that AI enables smaller, more capable teams. The winning organizations will be those that keep teams small, give them end-to-end ownership, and equip them with AI tools that amplify their capabilities.

## Key Disagreements

- **Team size philosophy:** Amazon's "two-pizza team" approach vs. larger functional teams with shared services. Smaller teams are faster but may lack specialized depth.
- **PM as mini-CEO vs. PM as facilitator:** Some organizations give PMs full ownership and decision authority; others see PMs as orchestrators who align cross-functional stakeholders.
