# Retention

## Synthesis

1. **Tactical retention is often more impactful than product improvements post-PMF.** Patrick Campbell (ProfitWell) distinguishes between strategic retention (product improvements) and tactical retention (payment failures, cancellation flows, term optimization). Once past product-market fit, tactical retention improvements often deliver 2-3x the impact of new features.

2. **Retention is a lagging indicator — activation is the leading indicator.** Archie Abrams (Shopify) explains that Shopify optimizes for churn by focusing on who they onboard, not who they keep. Aigrading the quality of users at signup (through intent signals and qualification) produces better retention than any post-signup intervention.

3. **Improving retention rarely starts with sending more emails.** Dan Hockenmaier (Thumbtack) advises against jumping to retention tactics (emails, push notifications) without understanding the root cause. The highest-leverage retention work is understanding why users churn at the product experience level and fixing those core issues first.

4. **Retention benchmarks vary dramatically by segment.** Jason Lemkin (SaaStr) provides benchmarks: great SaaS net revenue retention is 120%+; good is 100-110%. For consumer products, retention curves look different — D7, D30, and D90 retention rates are more meaningful than NRR.

5. **AI products face unique retention challenges.** Aishwarya Naresh Reganti and Kiriti Badam note that AI products suffer from "novelty churn" — users try AI features out of curiosity but don't integrate them into workflows. Building retention for AI products requires solving for habit formation, not just initial delight.

## Key Disagreements

- **Churn as a metric to focus on:** Some companies (Shopify) almost ignore churn, focusing instead on onboarding quality; others obsessively track it as a primary health metric.
- **Retention through engagement vs. value delivery:** Debate over whether retention is best driven by increasing product usage frequency or by delivering measurable outcomes even if infrequently.
