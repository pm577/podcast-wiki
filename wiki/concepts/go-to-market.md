# Go To Market

## Synthesis

1. **GTM strategy must match the product's adoption pattern.** Hila Qu explains that not every product is right for PLG — enterprise software sold to 3 clients (e.g., defense contractors) should stay sales-led. The GTM motion should be determined by the buyer profile and decision complexity, not by trend.

2. **World-class GTM in 2026 is multi-channel.** Jeanne Grosser (Vercel, Stripe, Google) argues that the best GTM strategies combine self-serve, product-led sales, enterprise sales, and channel partnerships. Companies that rely on a single channel cap their addressable market and create concentration risk.

3. **GTM teams need shared metrics.** Meltem Kuran (Deel) shows that aligning sales, marketing, and product around common metrics — conversion rates, time-to-value, expansion revenue — prevents the finger-pointing that kills early-stage GTM. Shared accountability creates cross-functional optimization.

4. **Founder-led sales is the original GTM motion.** Pete Kazanjy (Atrium) argues that founders must own sales in the early days because they have the deepest product knowledge and customer empathy. The transition from founder-led to rep-led sales is one of the hardest and most critical GTM inflection points.

5. **AI is reshaping GTM organizations.** Brian Halligan (HubSpot) documents how AI agents are replacing SDR/BDR roles, automating outbound sequences, and personalizing outreach at scale. The GTM org of 2026 looks fundamentally different from 2023 — leaner, more automated, and more data-driven.

## Key Disagreements

- **PLG-first vs. sales-first:** Some guests advocate starting with PLG and adding enterprise sales later; others argue enterprise-first with PLG as a top-of-funnel addition.
- **Outbound vs. inbound emphasis:** Debate over whether outbound selling works in the age of AI-inundated buyers or if all GTM should focus on inbound/content-driven demand generation.
