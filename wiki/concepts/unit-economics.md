# Unit Economics

## Synthesis

1. **Unit economics are the language of business health that investors and founders must both speak.** Alan Chiu (XSeed Capital) explains that VCs evaluate investment opportunities primarily through unit economics: customer acquisition cost (CAC), lifetime value (LTV), payback period, and gross margin. A startup with great unit economics will always find funding; one with bad unit economics can't be saved by growth.

2. **LTV/CAC ratio of 3:1 is the minimum for a healthy SaaS business.** Albert Cheng (Duolingo, Grammarly) emphasizes that companies with LTV/CAC below 3:1 are essentially losing money on every customer. The best companies achieve 5:1 or higher by optimizing both sides — increasing LTV through expansion revenue and reducing CAC through product-led growth.

3. **Net Revenue Retention (NRR) is the most important SaaS metric.** Elena Verna's framework places NRR above all other metrics because it determines whether a company grows or decays without new customers. NRR above 120% means the existing customer base alone compounds revenue growth. Below 100% means the company must acquire increasingly more customers just to stay flat.

4. **Payback period reveals efficiency more clearly than total spend.** Jeanne DeWitt Grosser recommends tracking months to recover CAC rather than just dollar amounts. A 12-month payback period is excellent for enterprise SaaS; consumer products may need 3-6 months. The faster the payback, the more capital-efficient the growth engine.

5. **Unit economics look different for multi-sided marketplaces.** Multiple episodes discuss that marketplace unit economics must account for both sides — the cost of acquiring producers and consumers. The magic happens when acquisition of one side improves the other side's experience, creating a virtuous cycle that improves unit economics over time.

## Key Disagreements

- **Gross margin thresholds:** Some VCs insist on 80%+ gross margins for SaaS; others accept 60-70% for certain verticals.
- **Blended vs. channel-specific CAC:** Debate over whether to average CAC across all channels or optimize per channel.
- **Contribution margin inclusion:** Whether to include R&D and G&A in unit economic calculations or only direct costs.
