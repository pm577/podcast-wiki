# Archie Abrams

Archie Abrams is VP of Product and Head of Growth at Shopify, where he leads an org of over 600 people across product, design, engineering, data ops, and growth marketing. Shopify represents ~10% of all US ecommerce, processing $235B in GMV (roughly the size of Finland's economy).

## Key Views

**Optimize for Cohort GMV, Not Churn** — Shopify's growth team optimizes for total GMV per cohort, not retention. Most businesses fail, but the few that succeed (Allbirds, FIGS, etc.) make the entire economics work. This is like angel investing: most bets fail, but the winners cover the portfolio. Shopify lowers barriers to entry, profits from payments tied to merchant success, and lets power-law dynamics drive returns. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/archie-abrams.md]

**30-40% of Short-Term Experiment Lifts Disappear** — When Shopify re-examines experiments after a year, 30-40% show no long-term GMV lift despite short-term gains. Up-funnel metrics often pull forward demand without creating lasting value. The most interesting cases are when experiments uncover valuable merchant pockets that short-term metrics missed entirely. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/archie-abrams.md]

**Long-Term Holdout Experiments** — Shopify runs permanent holdout groups and automatically re-evaluates experiments 1, 2, and 3 years later. This enables learning that short-term metrics can't capture. The practice trained the growth team to be humble about claimed impact and to refine which input metrics actually correlate with long-term value. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/archie-abrams.md]

**Three Time Horizons, One Company** — Shopify structures its product org into three groups on different time horizons: Core Product (100-year vision, no KPIs, taste-driven), Merchant Services (medium-term, building payments/shipping), and Growth (end-to-end customer journey). Core teams are banned from using metrics to guide the roadmap — decisions come from intuition and long-term vision. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/archie-abrams.md]

**Monetary Friction Can Unlock Hidden Winners** — Reducing early monetary friction (trial discounts, waived fees) doesn't necessarily attract low-quality merchants. It can causally make borderline entrepreneurs successful by giving them more time to find product-market fit. The very best merchants often come from these monetary-friction-reducing experiments. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/archie-abrams.md]

# Archie Abrams

**Role:** VP of Product and Head of Growth at Shopify, leading an org of 600+ people across product, design, engineering, data ops, and growth marketing  
**Known for:** Shopify's unconventional growth philosophy — optimizing for churn, banning KPIs in core product teams, running multi-year experiment holdouts, and Toby's 100-year vision driving product decisions.

## Key Views

### 1. Optimize for churn, not retention — the power-law business model

Archie flips conventional SaaS growth wisdom: Shopify optimizes around the fact that most small businesses fail, so the goal is to *lower barriers to entry* and get as many people as possible trying entrepreneurship. The winners (Allbirds, FIGS, etc.) generate so much lifetime GMV that they make the entire cohort profitable. Shopify's monetization via payments (tied to merchant success) means net dollar retention on winners is absurdly high, compensating for high churn — analogous to angel investing where a few outliers carry the portfolio.

> "The way we look at it is can we lower the barriers to getting started and get as many people in the door trying their hand at entrepreneurship? … The folks who do go on to be successful will make that entire cohort of merchants who started something that makes Shopify extremely successful." — Archie Abrams

### 2. 30-40% of short-term growth wins vanish over 12 months

Shopify runs long-term holdout experiments — keeping a control group for 1-3 years and re-measuring impact. Archie's finding: roughly 30-40% of experiments showing short-term lift have **zero** incremental GMV impact after a year. They're often pull-forward effects (borrowing future revenue) rather than true creation of new value. A classic example: payment failure "Dunning" notifications showed big short-term recovery but zero long-term GMV lift — the merchants who let payments fail weren't committed entrepreneurs anyway.

> "Look at some of the experiments that you thought were your biggest winners. Look at the downstream metrics for a year, two years on that experiment. And I'll bet you'd be surprised how many times the metric is different than what you thought it would be after a year." — Archie Abrams

### 3. KPI-free core product teams — taste over metrics

At Shopify, core product teams don't have OKRs or KPIs — they're "essentially banned." Instead, decisions are driven by taste, intuition, and alignment with Tobi's 100-year vision. Every six weeks, all R&D group leads review every project with Tobi, focusing heavily on **technical architecture** (the "how") rather than just the "what." Tobi believes that in a technology company, technical architecture determines strategy. Every project requires an "okay-to" approval from a group lead before shipping — a centralized quality bar enforced by a small number of leaders.

> "If you build the right technical how and set yourself up to have a platform that can be adaptable, flexible, that is incredibly valuable over the long term." — Archie Abrams

### 4. Ship neutral experiments — trust intuition when data is inconclusive

Shopify's culture is "aim heavy" — if an experiment is neutral (no statistically significant difference), they default to shipping the new version if the team has good intuition about it. The reasoning: if both options are equally valid, pick the one the team believes in. This trust-based approach encourages risk-taking and avoids the conservative bias of always sticking with the control. Archie saw this pay off with pre-configured store sections — neutral on signups but massively positive on long-term GMV because better stores led to more sales.

> "In our culture of kind of aim heavy, if the intuition is right that this probably is helping merchants, why do we start with that the original control is better if it's neutral? Let's start with, what would we have shipped if we were a blank slate?" — Archie Abrams

### 5. The 100-year vision as a decision-making filter

Everything at Shopify is oriented around building a 100-year company. This means decisions that look good short-term (e.g., chasing big enterprise deals) are deprioritized if they don't serve the long-term mission of increasing entrepreneurship. The "how" of technical architecture is debated at the highest levels — even CSV importers get 30-minute discussions with Tobi because the architectural choice determines future flexibility. The core product team ships major releases twice a year, packaging continuous work into big bang launches, while the growth team operates faster but under the same long-term quality constraints.

> "We are here to build a 100-year company … never reverse principles two and three [make the best product in the world, make money to do more of one]." — Archie Abrams
