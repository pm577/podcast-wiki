# Megan Cook

Megan Cook is Head of Product for Jira at Atlassian. Jira is used by 75% of Fortune 500 companies with over 125,000 customers globally. She has been at Atlassian for over 11 years, and before that was an analyst, developer, and agile coach.

## Key Views

**Fight Club — Structured Conflict for Product Leaders** — Megan instituted a weekly 30-minute meeting called "Fight Club" for herself, her engineering lead, and design lead. The purpose: go in knowing you're going to have conflict. By having a dedicated time for difficult conversations, issues get surfaced early instead of festering. "The relationship we all have is so much better because we get on top of these things early." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/megan-cook.md]

**Play Creates Flow and Innovation** — Inspired by Ben Crowe (mindset coach for Olympic athletes), Megan learned that being in a flow state requires a sense of play. When teams are having fun, minds are open to new ideas, people feel present, and they're not stressing. Creating psychological safety and space for play is essential for innovation — especially in high-pressure environments. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/megan-cook.md]

**Psychological Safety Enables Early Feedback** — When feedback becomes "painfully polished" before reaching leadership, it's a bad sign. It means people aren't comfortable giving raw, early input. Real psychological safety means the most junior person and the most senior person can have open discussions. Safe teams give feedback early, when it's most useful. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/megan-cook.md]

**Atlassian's Multi-Product Success** — Atlassian successfully operates 15+ product lines by maintaining clear product boundaries, shared platform infrastructure, and autonomous empowered teams. The key is knowing when a product needs its own identity vs. sharing a platform, and hiring leaders who can operate independently within a broader vision. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/megan-cook.md]

**Getting Buy-In for Ideas** — A tactical approach to getting buy-in: connect your idea explicitly to a business outcome, socialize it with key stakeholders before the formal meeting, and come with data that reduces the perceived risk of saying yes. The best advocates build coalitions, not arguments. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/megan-cook.md]
