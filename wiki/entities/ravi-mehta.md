# Ravi Mehta

**Role:** Co-founder and CEO of Outpace; former CPO at Tinder, Product Director at Facebook, VP of Product at Tripadvisor  
**Known for:** Creating the Product Strategy Stack framework, developing the Reforge product leadership and strategy programs, and pioneering the frontier-of-understanding approach to goal-setting.

## Key Views

### 1. The Product Strategy Stack: Mission → Strategy → Product Strategy → Roadmap → Goals

Ravi's framework separates commonly conflated terms into a clear hierarchy. **Company Mission** (aspirational, qualitative) sits at the top, followed by **Company Strategy** (rigorously logical plan to execute the mission), then **Product Strategy** (connective tissue between company strategy and what the product team does), **Roadmap**, and finally **Goals** at the bottom. The key insight: goals should come *last*, not first. You debug strategy bottom-up (if you're missing goals, check the roadmap; if the roadmap is off, check the product strategy) and define it top-down. Goals without a strategy context are like deciding to drive 250 miles without agreeing on a destination.

> "If you're having trouble meeting your goals, it might be because the roadmap isn't set up to move those goals forward. If the roadmap isn't right, it might be because the product strategy hasn't been clearly articulated." — Ravi Mehta

### 2. Wireframes *in* the strategy doc — not just words

Ravi's contrarian practice: **include wireframes directly in strategy documents.** He argues that describing strategy in words alone leads to different interpretations — everyone imagines a different nav bar. By including low-fidelity wireframes (even just Balsamiq sketches), you create the same alignment that an architect's blueprint provides. For trip planning at Tripadvisor, his team defined the strategy partially through wireframes of the future product, forcing concrete decisions about what would and wouldn't be in scope. He says PMs should learn basic wireframing skills to avoid depending entirely on designers for strategic visualization.

> "The strategy doc wouldn't be complete without wireframes. Being able to describe a house in words alone is not enough. Everyone will come away with a different interpretation of what is needed." — Ravi Mehta

### 3. The Frontier of Understanding — set understanding goals, not outcome goals

Ravi's diagnostic for why OKRs fail: teams commit to moving a metric they don't understand. He defines four types of goals along the **frontier of understanding**: (1) **Understanding risk** — you don't know the levers yet, so the goal should be to learn; (2) **Dependency risk** — you know the levers but lack tools; (3) **Execution risk** — you have everything needed, just execute; (4) **Strategic risk** — execute and validate the hypothesis. The worst place to be is hitting your goals without knowing why — it's good for now but eventually catches up. Managers should be sold on the approach by framing it: "We missed our goal, but here's what we learned and here's the path to certainty next quarter."

> "Ultimately the goal is to drive outcomes, but often there's things that come before that need to be addressed ahead of time so that you can really understand what the plan for meeting those outcomes is going to look like." — Ravi Mehta

### 4. Startup speed = latency (tight turning radius), not velocity

Ravi's insight from moving from big company CPO to founder: big companies have higher velocity (more resources, more output), but startups win on **latency** — the time from idea to testing a hypothesis. The turning radius is tighter at a startup, so you can change direction faster. This means founders should resist the urge to use experimental decision-making (requires large sample sizes) and instead adopt **conviction-oriented decision-making**: get enough data for informed conviction, execute, then validate quickly. Paralysis from analysis is the bigger risk at an early stage than making the wrong call.

> "Startups have a really tight turning radius and bigger companies have a really high rate of velocity. That was one of the things that took some adjustment — boiling down big ambitious plans into much smaller pieces where you can iterate towards things." — Ravi Mehta

### 5. Tinder vs. Hinge: How mission drives radically different product strategy

Ravi uses his experience at Tinder to illustrate the Product Strategy Stack. Tinder's mission ("make single life more fun") leads to a lightweight, serendipitous product with minimal filters — intentionally resisting becoming a "search engine for people." Hinge's mission ("designed to be deleted") leads to detailed profiles with prompts designed for deeper conversations. Both succeeded with similar core mechanics (swipe-based matching) but completely different strategy stacks. The lesson: even within the same category, clear mission and strategy alignment produces differentiated products that don't need to copy each other's features.

> "If you can use the strategy stack, you can get a really good feel for where strategy is informing particular decisions and when a decision should look like competitors and when it should be different." — Ravi Mehta
