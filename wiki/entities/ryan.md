# Ryan Singer

Ryan Singer was one of the first employees and the former Head of Strategy at 37signals (the makers of Basecamp), where he spent nearly two decades refining a unique approach to product development. He authored the book *Shape Up*, which has become a widely adopted methodology for teams seeking an alternative to Scrum, SAFe, and traditional Agile frameworks.

## Key Views

### Appetites Instead of Deadlines
The core idea of Shape Up: instead of asking "how long will this take?", ask "how much time are we willing to spend?" This shifts the constraint from estimation to design. "We're not going to start something unless we can see the end from the beginning. We're going to go the other way around — what is the maximum amount of time we're willing to go before we actually finish something?" The appetite defines the scope, not the other way around.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

### Shaping: Design Before Commit
Before any coding begins, a "shaping" session brings together product, design, and engineering to define the key elements of a project at the right level of abstraction. The output is a shared understanding — a diagram or sketch that everyone agrees represents the solution — but leaves room for designers and engineers to work out details during building. "If you're doing a home renovation, you can have the most beautiful rendering... but if you haven't checked if there's electricity in that wall, it's going to drastically change the cost and time."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

### Six-Week Cycles With a Cool-Down
Shape Up uses six-week cycles (long enough to build something meaningful, short enough to feel pressure) followed by a two-week cool-down period for bug fixes, exploration, and reflection. This rhythm creates natural boundaries that prevent scope creep. The fixed cycle creates urgency: teams must figure out what they can actually ship within the appetite.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

### The Betting Table Decides What Gets Built
Instead of backlogs and prioritization, Shape Up uses a "betting table" approach. Every six weeks, stakeholders decide which shaped concepts to bet on. Unselected ideas don't go into a backlog — they're discarded. If a good idea resurfaces later, it can be shaped again. This prevents the accumulation of dead-weight work and keeps the team focused on what matters now.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

### Designed for the Constraint of Limited Engineering Time
Shape Up was born from a specific constraint: David Heinemeier Hansson was only programming 10 hours a week when they built the first version of Basecamp. This forced an intense focus on using engineering time efficiently. "We don't want to ever give something that's the thing we want to build, and then it turns out not to be what we want and we have to throw it away." The methodology is fundamentally about reducing waste and maximizing the value of every engineering hour.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]
