# Varun Parmar

Varun Parmar appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss an inside look at how miro builds product — varun parmar (cpo of miro).

## Key Views

**Every Release Is a Chess Move** — Varun's core philosophy: every code push makes the product either better or worse for the customer. With every competitor release, you are gaining or losing ground. This framework 'drives an insane amount of clarity' about what to invest in and why, forcing teams to think about relative competitive position continuously. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/varun-parmar.md]

**AMPED: Embedding Product Marketing Into the Product Org** — Miro's product organization is called AMPED (Analytics, Marketing, Product, Engineering, Design). By deeply embedding product marketing inside every stream, Miro ensures positioning, competitive differentiation, and packaging are considered from the start — not retrofitted after shipping. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/varun-parmar.md]

**Be First to Hit the Brick Wall — Speed as the Innovation Moat** — In competitive markets, speed is the single biggest determinant of success. Varun's philosophy: you want to be the 'first one to hit the brick wall' — discovering what doesn't work faster than anyone else so you can course-correct sooner. Speed enables learning loops that keep you steps ahead of the competition. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/varun-parmar.md]

**Miro Connect: The Biweekly Demo Ritual That Saved Months** — Every other Friday, Miro runs 'Miro Connect' where teams demo work in progress. During one session, an engineer overheard a team describe a problem he'd solved before, coded the entire feature in 3-4 hours — saving what the core team estimated as 3 months of work. This illustrates how creating serendipitous cross-pollination unlocks massive velocity gains. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/varun-parmar.md]

**Quality Through Classification, Not Documentation** — Miro's design leadership team does a monthly triage ranking every shipped feature as 'high quality' or 'not high quality.' Rather than writing long definition documents, they use a classification system so the organization develops a shared, intuitive understanding of what great looks like through concrete examples. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/varun-parmar.md]
