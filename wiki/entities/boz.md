# Boz

Andrew "Boz" Bosworth is the Chief Technology Officer of Meta and head of Reality Labs. He joined Facebook in early 2006 as one of the first engineers and spent 18 years building some of the most impactful products in internet history: the Newsfeed (the first algorithmically ranked content feed), the original Facebook mobile ads platform, the messaging system, and timeline. He created Meta's AR/VR organization (Reality Labs) in 2017, overseeing Quest, Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses, and mixed reality.

## Key Views

**Ask Your Manager for Help — Your Job Is to Get It Done, Not Do It Yourself**
Boz's most frequently given advice: people dramatically under-leverage their leaders. He sees it constantly — employees silently toil, miss deadlines, build the wrong thing — all because they didn't ask for help. "Your job is not to do it yourself. Your job is to get it done." He recommends sending short weekly updates ("no response required") to keep managers informed, and when blocked, drafting the specific email or ask to make it cheap for the manager to help. Crucially, "Nobody wants you to be more awesome than your manager does, because when you're amazing, your manager's life gets easier." The HPMs (Highlights, People, Me) format at Facebook institutionalized this.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/boz.md]]*

**The Eye of Sauron — Leaders Go Deep on What Matters Most**
Boz describes Mark Zuckerberg's management style as the "eye of Sauron": when something is the most important priority, no detail is too small for the founder to notice — pixel-level feedback alongside strategic course-correction in the same review. Boz operates the same way, priding himself on going "hair-low on the things that matter a ton" while leaving space on things that have clear roadmaps or aren't success-critical. The counterintuitive insight: there's no perfect place to be. "You suffer in the intensity of the gaze of Sauron; you also suffer in the shadow of its absence." Leaders must develop the judgment to know which weeds matter and which don't.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/boz.md]]*

**Passion Is a Skill You Can Find — Give Yourself Six Months Before Judging**
Boz thought of himself as an "AI infrastructure product guy" and didn't want to work on ads or hardware. He was wrong both times. He learned that "I am somebody who is inclined to be passionate about things," and that giving himself six months in a new domain often reveals unexpected fascination. "Don't assume you won't be excited about something that may come up." If after six months the passion hasn't come, it's time to move on — but the self-awareness to distinguish between "I haven't found the passion yet" and "this isn't for me" is a critical career skill.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/boz.md]]*

**Information Transparency Is an Economic Imperative — But It Comes at a Cost**
Meta's radical transparency (all data shared, all Q&As open, anyone can post to leadership) isn't a moral position — it's economic. Talented people are the most valuable asset, and depriving them of information blocks their ability to contribute fully. "Anytime they have the wrong information, or they don't have the information, you've now blocked one of the economically most valuable things that your company possesses." But the cost is real: senior hires must learn to find signal amid the noise, and the risk of leaks is constant. The tradeoff is worth it because the best work comes "from people understanding not just what we're trying to accomplish top down, but also having way more information at their disposal to be able to act on it."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/boz.md]]*

**The Newsfeed Lesson — Conviction in the Face of Outrage, But Listen for the Details**
When Facebook launched Newsfeed, users were outraged — even as they immediately doubled their usage. The revealed preference (behavior) contradicted the stated preference (outrage). The lesson: have conviction in your vision, but distinguish between "the thing itself is wrong" vs. "the details are wrong." Newsfeed was right; they made some small mistakes (oversharing wall posts publicly). "When did you screw something small up, and when did you do something big up? That is an art." The key is having a clear vision of what you expected to happen, diagnosing the delta, and trusting revealed preferences over loud complaints.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/boz.md]]*
