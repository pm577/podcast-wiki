# Eli Schwartz

Eli Schwartz is a leading SEO consultant and author of *Product-Led SEO*. He has worked with industry giants including Zapier, Tinder, Coinbase, Quora, LinkedIn, SurveyMonkey, and WordPress to build and execute their SEO strategies. He is known for his product-centric approach to SEO and for his analysis of how AI Overviews are reshaping search.

## Key Views

**SEO Is a Product, Not a Marketing Tactic — Own the Buyer Journey**
Eli's core thesis is that SEO should be treated as a product — with product managers overseeing it, design and engineering resourcing it, and user research driving it. The classic "find keywords, write content, build links" model is broken. Instead, SEO teams must ask: what is the user searching for, and what product experience can we build for them in the mid-funnel? The Zapier model is the canonical example: instead of writing blog posts about automation, they built programmatic pages showing every possible app integration — creating a product that directly served the user's search intent. "The product managers are the people that should be thinking about this SEO question because it's a product question."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/eli-schwartz.md]]*

**AI Overviews Kill Top-of-Funnel SEO — Mid-Funnel Is Where the Opportunity Moves**
AI Overviews (Google's AI-generated answers in search results) are reshaping the search journey by absorbing top-of-funnel queries — "best beach vacation near me," "top CRM tools" — into instant summaries. This kills the click-through for traditional content SEO on those queries. But mid-funnel searches — specific comparisons, pricing, feature evaluations — remain untouched and may even grow in importance. The key insight: "The discovery step of search is going to be swallowed up by LLMs... and then once you have a sense of what you want, you go back to Google." Companies must build SEO assets that serve mid- to bottom-of-funnel intent — like Tinder building city-specific dating pages or TripAdvisor's programmatic hotel reviews.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/eli-schwartz.md]]*

**Most SaaS Companies Shouldn't Do SEO — The Buyer Journey Doesn't Fit**
This is Eli's most contrarian take: the majority of B2B SaaS products lack a natural SEO journey because people don't search for problems they don't know they have, and they rarely convert from a search result into a complex sales-led or committee-purchase product. Mixpanel couldn't convert SEO traffic despite ranking well because no one Googles for analytics and buys an enterprise tool on the spot. Even Google Cloud shouldn't invest heavily in SEO. The decision framework: if the buyer journey requires a sales conversation, committee approval, or integration across the company, the ROI on SEO is likely worse than other channels (influencer, paid, events). "If you can't answer the question about what it is that someone's going to do a search on, then don't do SEO."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/eli-schwartz.md]]*

**Programmatic SEO Over Editorial — Build Systems, Not Blog Posts**
Eli strongly advocates programmatic SEO — building pages from data sources at scale — over editorial content marketing. The canonical examples are TripAdvisor (a page for every hotel, assembled from structured data + UGC), Zapier (every possible app-to-app integration), and Canva (every template as a landing page). The key is that these pages serve the buyer journey directly: they help the user solve a problem, and the product is the next logical step. Blog posts that don't feed into the product journey are worthless traffic — "Most companies should just not write a lot of content unless they're a media company." AI can accelerate this, but only if used to create genuinely useful content (e.g., product descriptions for e-commerce), not just slop.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/eli-schwartz.md]]*

**Links Are Worthless Without Brand — Build Brand, Not Guest Posts**
The biggest myth in SEO is that link building is the secret sauce. Eli argues that buying guest posts on low-authority sites that nobody reads is obviously futile — "everyone complained about Google as this really, really smart, all-knowing LLM, and at the same time, they think they're dumb enough to fall for these guest posts." The right way to build links is to build a brand: create a product that people love and want to mention. Google can now read content and understand context — a mention is as good as a link. SEO, brand, and PR should be one unified effort. "Any SEO effort [is] promoting a brand, building up a brand, rather than building a product, and then separately building out an SEO effort."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/eli-schwartz.md]]*
