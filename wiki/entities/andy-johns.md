# Andy Johns

**Role:** Former VP Growth/Product at Facebook, Twitter, Quora, Wealthfront; now mental health advocate and coach for burned-out high achievers  
**Known for:** Pioneering growth at Facebook and Twitter, then leaving his CEO path at Wealthfront to become a mental health advocate.

## Key Views

### 1. The flashing red alarm — when core behaviors get disrupted

Andy's framework for recognizing burnout: just as animals show distress when diet, exercise, playfulness, socialization, and sleep are disrupted, humans have the same warning signs. If your sleep always sucks, relationships are strained, and physical health is failing — those are undeniable signals that something must change. Your body is telling you to stop.

> "When your fundamental functions — diet, exercise, playfulness, socialization, sleep — when those things get disrupted, it's a sign that there is something going on here that you need to take a look at." — Andy Johns

### 2. The CEO path isn't right for everyone — and that's OK

Andy was in line to become CEO of Wealthfront but realized through deep self-work that this path wasn't right for him. His story is a powerful example of distinguishing between what you *should* want (based on external validation) and what you *actually* want. The pressure to follow a trajectory of "more" — more responsibility, more money, more status — can lead to profound unhappiness.

### 3. Growth skills are transferable to mental health work

Andy found that the same skills that made him a great growth leader — experimentation, data analysis, pattern recognition, systematic thinking — apply directly to mental health. He now applies a growth mindset to wellbeing: what experiments can I run? What does the data say? What patterns do I see? This reframing makes mental health improvement feel achievable rather than overwhelming.

### 4. High achievers are especially vulnerable to burnout

Andy's work with burned-out high achievers reveals a pattern: the same traits that drive professional success (ambition, grit, perfectionism, inability to say no) make people uniquely vulnerable to burnout. The solution isn't to dampen ambition — it's to build sustainable systems, boundaries, and self-awareness that allow high performance without self-destruction.

### 5. "Enough is enough" — redefining success on your own terms

Andy's core message: there comes a point where external metrics of success (title, comp, company valuation) stop meaning anything if your health and relationships are in shambles. He helps people identify their personal "enough" — the point at which more isn't better. This requires unlearning the Silicon Valley programming that more is always better.
