# Jessica Lachs

**Role:** VP of Analytics and Data Science at DoorDash (built one of the largest and most respected data orgs in tech); former founder of GiftSimple; former investment banker at Lehman Brothers  
**Known for:** Building a world-class data organization from scratch; championing a centralized data team model; pioneering the "analytics as a business impact function" philosophy — not just answering "why" but "so what do we do now?"

## Key Views

### 1. Analytics Should Be a Business Impact Function, Not a Service Desk

Jessica's core philosophy: "For me, analytics is a business impact driving function and not purely a service function." Her team isn't just answering questions or building dashboards through Jira tickets. They find opportunities, have points of view on decisions, and answer "the so what" — what to do now that we know something. The analytics team earns its seat at the table by bringing opportunities, not by being a utility.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]

> "For me, analytics is a business impact driving function and not purely a service function, not just answering the 'why,' but answering the 'so what. What do we do now that we know this?'" — Jessica Lachs

### 2. Centralized Data Org > Embedded Models

Jessica strongly advocates for a centralized data team model. In a central model, data scientists form pods that map to business functions (marketing, product, ops) but report up through a central analytics org. Benefits: **(1)** Consistent, high talent bar across all hires. **(2)** Better growth opportunities — data scientists can rotate between marketing and merchant analytics without leaving the company. **(3)** Consistent methodologies and metrics — one definition of "sales" for the whole company. **(4)** Avoid recreating the wheel — build one churn prediction model with input from six teams instead of six separate models. **(5)** Stronger team culture and identity.

### 3. Carve Out Intentional Time for Exploratory Deep Dives

Jessica's lesson on proactive vs. reactive analytics: "You have to be very intentional to carve out time for exploratory work for deep dives because there are always more questions and more work to be done than hours in the day." She uses hackathons and self-directed time goals to ensure the team doesn't default to only answering incoming questions. At DoorDash, a hackathon deep-dive into the referral program uncovered systematic fraud — team members literally created fake accounts to test the system — which drove a major product change.

### 4. Hiring Data Talent: Look for "A-Team" Mindset

Jessica looks for data scientists who want to be business partners, not just analysts. Her early team ethos: "You are a data scientist, but your goal is to figure out what's happening. And if that means that you're going to pick up the phone and call customers, then that is what you're going to do." She calls her team the "A-Team" — a culture of ownership where data people proactively find problems.

### 5. Retention Is Hard to Goal On — Find Short-Term Proxies

Jessica's counterintuitive insight: "Retention is a terrible thing to goal on. It's almost impossible to drive in a meaningful way in a short term." Instead of gunning for retention directly, find the short-term metrics you can measure that drive long-term retention outcomes. This guides the data team's work toward actionable leading indicators rather than lagging outcomes.

# Jess Lachs

Jessica "Jess" Lachs is VP of Analytics and Data Science at DoorDash, where she's built one of the largest and most respected data organizations in tech. She joined DoorDash over 10 years ago, was its first GM (launching new markets), and previously founded GiftSimple and worked in investment banking. Her data team has become a benchmark for how analytics should drive business impact.

## Key Views

**Analytics Should Drive Business Impact, Not Just Answer "Why"**
Lachs insists analytics is a business-impact-driving function, not a service function. "For me, analytics is about finding opportunities, about having a point of view on the decisions that we should make — not just answering the why but answering the 'so what.'" Her team doesn't take Jira tickets to build dashboards; they sit at the table as thought partners alongside product, engineering, and operations, bringing data-driven opportunities for the business to pursue.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*

**Centralize Data Teams for Scale, Embed for Impact**
Lachs holds a contrarian view: a centralized data org is superior to embedding analysts in business units. She admits business leaders like embedded teams because they control the roadmap and have certainty about resources. But a central org preserves career growth for analysts, enables cross-functional learning, and prevents the fragmentation of data practices. The trick is to structure the central team into pods that map perfectly to business units while maintaining a unified reporting structure — giving both alignment and scale.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*

**Retention Is a Terrible Thing to Goal On — Use Leading Indicators Instead**
Lachs argues that retention, while ultimately what matters, is almost impossible to drive in a meaningful way in the short term. "You want to find a short-term metric you can measure that drives a long-term output." This insight shapes how DoorDash's data team thinks about metrics: instead of goal-setting on lagging indicators, they identify proximate metrics that correlate with retention and can be moved week-to-week. This makes the data team more actionable and less reactive.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*

**Data Scientists Should Pick Up the Phone — Extreme Ownership Extends to Research**
Lachs expects her data scientists to go beyond running queries. "Your goal is to figure out what's happening. And if that means you're going to pick up the phone and call customers, then that's what you're going to do." This philosophy of extreme ownership means data scientists don't just analyze data — they triangulate with qualitative research, operational context, and business strategy. It's what makes the team more than a reporting function and earns them their seat at the table.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*

**Earning the Seat at the Table Requires Bringing Opportunities, Not Just Dashboards**
Lachs's team earns its influence by proactively surfacing opportunities, not by waiting for requests. "We get a seat at the table and we need to earn it by bringing opportunities that we all can go and go after." This means her analysts and data scientists are expected to identify untapped growth areas, flag emerging risks, and propose specific actions — not just build reports. This proactive posture transforms data from a cost center into a value driver.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*
