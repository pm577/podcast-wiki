# John Mark Nickels (JM Nickels)

Senior Director of Product Management at Uber. Previously Head of Product for Commercialization at Waymo and led product teams at DoorDash. Built and launched the first version of Uber Pool. Also an engineering manager at Groupon and equity trader at Getco. A coach and advisor focused on conscious leadership.

## Key Views

### 1. Conscious Leadership Means Taking Responsibility for Your Influence

Leadership is having influence in the world — and everyone has it. The "conscious" part is waking up to your biases, inherited belief systems, and the power dynamics you create. As a senior leader, your words carry disproportionate weight. Being conscious means creating space for others, speaking last, and recognizing that "I don't need to win the argument in the meeting" because there will be a follow-up.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/john-mark-nickels.md]

### 2. Teams Need Emotional Safety, Not More Content Experts

At Uber, Nickels observed rooms full of PhDs arguing from a place of fear and threat — everyone in lizard brain. The bottleneck wasn't another technical expert; it was the inability to shift from a "righteous, defensive" state to a "co-creative, curious, trusting" space. This insight drove him to pursue coaching skills: the highest-leverage intervention in a room of brilliant people is often emotional regulation, not more information.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/john-mark-nickels.md]

### 3. Get Clear On Your Objective Function

Inspired by thinking about "future me," Nickels argues that most people avoid awareness of mortality and thus fail to prioritize. "Five years from now, I'm not going to care if I made the presentation slightly better, but I'm going to care a lot about what kind of relationship I have with my daughters." Awareness that life ends punctuates reality and forces honest priority-setting. Your objective function should guide day-to-day decisions.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/john-mark-nickels.md]

### 4. Evolve from "Loudest Rightest Voice" to Creating Space

Early in his career, Nickels believed influence meant having the right slides and being the loudest voice. As he became more senior, he realized that power dynamics make junior people reluctant to disagree. The evolved approach: sit back, hear from others first, and recognize that your position means your views can shut down dialogue. The soft skills of creating space are higher leverage than the hard skills of making arguments.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/john-mark-nickels.md]

### 5. Uber's Three Eras: Lessons in Organizational Evolution

Nickels identifies three eras: Uber 1.0 (Travis era — ran on fear, visionary but stressful), Uber 2.0 (post-Travis, board feuding, Dara as peacemaker), and Uber 3.0 (profitable, S&P 500, Cambrian explosion of transportation modalities). Each era required different leadership approaches. The insight: organizations that survive their chaotic founding can systematize without losing the spark, but it requires conscious effort to evolve the culture.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/john-mark-nickels.md]
