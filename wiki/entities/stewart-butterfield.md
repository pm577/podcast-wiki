# Stewart Butterfield

Stewart Butterfield appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss slack founder: mental models for building products people love.

## Key Views

**Utility Curves** — Product features follow an S-curve: initial investment yields little value, then a threshold where value explodes, then diminishing returns. Teams must evaluate whether a feature is on the shallow part (needs more investment) or the plateau. Features are binary (you have it or you don't) — the question is whether the team has invested enough to reach the value threshold. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

**Taste is a Competitive Advantage** — Most companies don't invest in taste and craft. The 'umbrella test' — only ~⅓ of people tilt their umbrella to avoid poking others — illustrates that small courtesies and attention to detail create emotional connections that drive organic growth. 'Your failure to exercise this courtesy is an advantage you can create.' ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

**Friction is a Comprehension Problem** — The goal isn't to minimize clicks/taps but to minimize cognitive load: 'How can I prevent people from having to think in order to use my software?' Friction often signals a comprehension gap, not a UX issue. If software stops you to ask a decision you don't understand, it makes you feel stupid. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

**Cold Rationality on Pivots** — 'You have to be coldly rational about it because it's fucking humiliating.' The key question: have you exhausted the possibilities? Creating distance from emotional attachment is essential to making an intellectual, rational decision about whether to pivot. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

**Divine Discontent & the Rising Bar** — The quality bar is always rising. Features that delighted users yesterday are table stakes today. Most apps never revisit core flows (signup, forgot password, timezone pickers). 'In the long run, the measure of our success will be the amount of value that we create for customers, and there's no substitute for actually having created it.' ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]
