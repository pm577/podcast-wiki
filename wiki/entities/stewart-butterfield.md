# Stewart Butterfield

Stewart Butterfield appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss slack founder: mental models for building products people love.

## Key Views

**Utility Curves** — Product features follow an S-curve: initial investment yields little value, then a threshold where value explodes, then diminishing returns. Teams must evaluate whether a feature is on the shallow part (needs more investment) or the plateau. Features are binary (you have it or you don't) — the question is whether the team has invested enough to reach the value threshold. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

**Taste is a Competitive Advantage** — Most companies don't invest in taste and craft. The 'umbrella test' — only ~⅓ of people tilt their umbrella to avoid poking others — illustrates that small courtesies and attention to detail create emotional connections that drive organic growth. 'Your failure to exercise this courtesy is an advantage you can create.' ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

**Friction is a Comprehension Problem** — The goal isn't to minimize clicks/taps but to minimize cognitive load: 'How can I prevent people from having to think in order to use my software?' Friction often signals a comprehension gap, not a UX issue. If software stops you to ask a decision you don't understand, it makes you feel stupid. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

**Cold Rationality on Pivots** — 'You have to be coldly rational about it because it's fucking humiliating.' The key question: have you exhausted the possibilities? Creating distance from emotional attachment is essential to making an intellectual, rational decision about whether to pivot. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

**Divine Discontent & the Rising Bar** — The quality bar is always rising. Features that delighted users yesterday are table stakes today. Most apps never revisit core flows (signup, forgot password, timezone pickers). 'In the long run, the measure of our success will be the amount of value that we create for customers, and there's no substitute for actually having created it.' ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-20-stewart-butterfield.md]

# Stewart Butterfield

Stewart Butterfield is the co-founder of Slack and Flickr, two of the most influential products in internet history. After selling Slack to Salesforce, he stepped away and now focuses on family, philanthropy, and creative projects. He rarely gives interviews, making his product and leadership wisdom especially valuable.

## Key Views

**Utility curves determine where to invest product resources.** Stewart popularized the concept of the utility S-curve: the first effort produces little value, then there's a magic threshold where enormous value is unlocked, then diminishing returns set in. Most features are treated as binary (have it or not), but teams should instead ask: are we on the shallow part of the curve, the steep part, or the plateau? This determines whether to invest more or move on.

**Friction isn't always bad — comprehension is the real goal.** Stewart challenges the dogma of always reducing friction. The real enemy is making users think. If your software stops someone and asks them to make a decision they don't understand, you make them feel stupid. The goal shouldn't be reducing clicks or taps — it should be making the experience simple and requiring no conscious thought from the user.

**"In the long run, the measure of our success will be the amount of value that we create for customers."** At Slack all-hands meetings, Stewart made everyone chant this as a mantra. He emphasizes there's no substitute for actually creating customer value — no amount of marketing or positioning can replace genuinely useful products. This principle guided Slack's relentless focus on user experience.

**Pivoting requires cold rationality, not emotion.** Having famously pivoted both Flickr (from a game to a photo-sharing service) and Slack (from a gaming company to a communication platform), Stewart has deep experience with pivots. His advice: the decision is about whether you've exhausted the possibilities, and you need to create emotional distance to make a rational decision. The reason to be coldly rational is because "it's fucking humiliating" otherwise.

**Great products should feel slightly embarrassing at launch.** In 2014, Stewart told MIT Technology Review that Slack was "a giant piece of shit" and the team should be humiliated to offer it to the public. His point: if you can't see almost limitless opportunities to improve, you shouldn't be designing the product. This "divine discontent" — borrowing Bezos' term — drives continuous improvement even in successful products.
