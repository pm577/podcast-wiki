# Grant Lee

Grant Lee is the CEO and co-founder of Gamma, the AI-powered presentation tool that grew from zero to $100M ARR in just over two years with fewer than 30 employees. He previously was COO at an Optimizely-acquired startup and brings an experimentation-driven mindset to building a durable AI-native business.

## Key Views

**The Word-of-Mouth Machine — Don't Fool Yourself with Vanity Metrics**
Grant Lee's core insight about product-market fit is that it shows up as organic word-of-mouth growth. After Gamma won Product Hunt Product of the Day, Week, and Month, signups spiked and then flattened. Lee realized they had "fooled themselves" — the product wasn't growing on its own. He describes the temptation: "Let's just spend more on ads, more on marketing, fuel the top of the funnel." Instead, he bet the company by pulling the team (12 people) off everything else to make "the first 30 seconds of the product feel magical." When they relaunched with an AI-powered onboarding that delivered instant value, signups went from hundreds to 20,000 per day — all organic. His advice: "Go back and ask yourself — why isn't word of mouth happening naturally? That's the massive tailwind you need."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-13-grant-lee.md]]*

**Influencer Marketing as an Amplifier — Micro-Influencers Over Mega-Creators**
Gamma grew a massive influencer marketing program, but not the way most people think. Lee advises against putting budget into five big creators with million-follower audiences who read a script. Instead, he recommends finding thousands of micro-influencers (people with audiences of 1,000–50,000) in niche "echo chambers" (educators, consultants, specific professions) where trust is high. His key tactic: personally onboard every early influencer — jump on a call, walk through the product, brainstorm hooks in their voice — so they feel like an extension of the team. 90% of reach comes from less than 10% of influencers, and you can't predict which 10%. So cast a wide net ($10K–$20K/month with 20–40 creators), test and iterate, then double down on what works. LinkedIn converts 4–5X better than other platforms for Gamma.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-13-grant-lee.md]]*

**The First 30 Seconds — Shorten Time to Value Relentlessly**
Lee's framework for onboarding is rooted in the idea that new users are "selfish, vain, and lazy." They have no desire to learn a new tool. Your job is to earn the next 30 seconds, and then the next. He uses the "one egg" metaphor from consumer advertising: "If you throw a consumer one egg, they can probably catch it. Throw them four or five eggs, they're going to drop all of them." Founders often try to pitch 4–5 features at once, confusing the user. Instead, give them one clear thing — "create a slide in seconds" — and if they catch that, they'll stick around for more. He tested this with rapid prototype cycles (idea in the morning, functional prototype by afternoon, 20 user tests via Voicepanel or UserTesting by evening) to iterate before shipping anything at scale.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-13-grant-lee.md]]*

**Hire Painfully Slowly — Generalists, Player-Coaches, and Betting Big on Exceptional People**
Gamma runs with ~30 people serving 50M+ users. Lee's mantra: "Hire painfully slowly." The first 10 employees are all still at the company five years later. He warns against setting headcount targets, because "if your goal is to add 100 people, the goal is no longer to add the best people." He hires generalists who can wear multiple hats, and all leaders are "player-coaches" who do individual contributor work while also managing. The most important structural advice: "When you find someone exceptional, bet big on them." A players want more playing time, more hard problems. You don't need many of them — but you need the right ones who share the same DNA. At Gamma, ~25% of the team are product designers, an intentionally heavy investment that reflects their aesthetic-first approach.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-11-13-grant-lee.md]]*

**Building a Durable AI Company — Own the Workflow, Not Just the Model**
Lee directly addresses the "GPT wrapper" critique: if your product literally is just one model call, you're vulnerable. But Gamma orchestrates "20+ models" across different parts of the creation workflow — different models for outlines, for photorealism, for artistic visuals, for editing suggestions. The moat isn't the model; it's the deep workflow integration and the end-to-end user experience. He emphasizes that you must start with a problem you deeply care about (for him, democratizing visual communication), not with "what can AI do?" He also emphasizes early monetization: Gamma hit $1M ARR and became profitable within months of launching their AI product, because they had to — runway was low. This forced discipline: pricing anchored to ChatGPT's $20/month, unit economics that worked from day one, and a feedback loop where paying customers validated real willingness to pay (not just willingness to try).
