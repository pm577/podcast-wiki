# Ken Norton

**Role:** Executive coach who works with top product leaders to help them get unstuck and develop their leadership skills; former Partner at Google Ventures (GV); former product leader at Google, Yahoo, and Excite  
**Known for:** Coaching some of the most influential product leaders in tech; frameworks for product leadership development; helping PMs transition from individual contributor to leader.

## Key Views

### 1. PMs Are Leaders From Day One

Ken's fundamental belief: product management is a leadership role from day one — not a management role, but a leadership role. PMs must influence without authority, inspire teams to follow their vision, and make tough calls without having formal power over engineers or designers. This is the hardest transition for new PMs: they expect to "manage" the product, but they actually need to "lead" the team. Leadership in PM means earning trust through competence, clarity, and empathy.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]

> "Part of what's exciting about product management is you are a leader from day one. You're a leader. There's leadership all over the place, but that's your job. You're a leader." — Ken Norton

### 2. The Transition from "Doer" to "Leader"

Ken's coaching framework for PMs moving into leadership: (1) Stop trying to be the smartest person in the room — your job is to make the team smarter. (2) Learn to give away your Legos (echoing Molly Graham) — let go of the work you're best at. (3) Develop pattern recognition — the most valuable thing you bring is seeing patterns across situations. (4) Invest in relationships before you need them. The hardest part is the identity shift — going from being valued for your individual contributions to being valued for enabling others.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]

> "The hardest transition in product management is going from 'I need to be the expert on this product' to 'I need to build a team of experts.'" — Ken Norton

### 3. Find Your "Zone of Genius"

Ken encourages PMs to distinguish between their Zone of Competence (things they're good at), Zone of Excellence (things they're great at), and Zone of Genius (things they're uniquely, naturally brilliant at). The goal is to spend as much time as possible in your Zone of Genius and delegate or eliminate everything else. Most PMs spend too much time in their Zone of Competence because it's comfortable. Unlocking your career trajectory requires identifying and leaning into your genius.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]

> "Most of us spend our time in our Zone of Competence because it feels safe. But your career takes off when you find and operate in your Zone of Genius." — Ken Norton

### 4. The Best Product Leaders Are the Best Listeners

Ken observes that the most effective product leaders share one trait: they're exceptional listeners. Not just waiting for their turn to talk — truly listening to understand. This includes listening to customers (beyond what they say), listening to the team (reading between the lines), and listening to themselves (recognizing their own biases and blind spots). The best PMs create space for others to contribute and make decisions based on synthesized input rather than their own opinions.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]

> "I've coached hundreds of product leaders and the very best ones are the very best listeners. Not tactical listening — deep, empathetic, pattern-recognizing listening." — Ken Norton

### 5. Career Advice: Diversify Your Experiences

Ken's career framework for PMs: don't optimize for title or company brand — optimize for diversity of experience. Work across different company stages (startup, growth, mature), different product types (consumer, enterprise, platform), and different functional exposures (eng, design, data, marketing). The PMs who become great leaders have broad context that lets them see patterns and make connections that pure specialists miss. The linear career path (same company, same type of product) creates expertise but limits perspective.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]

> "The best product leaders I know have zigzagged through their careers. They've worked at different stages, different types of products, and with different functions. That breadth is what creates wisdom." — Ken Norton

# Ken Norton

Ken Norton spent 14 years at Google leading product teams for Google Docs, Calendar, Maps, and Google Ventures. He's now a full-time executive coach specializing in product leaders. His writing — including the famous "Bringing the Donuts" post — has shaped how a generation of PMs think about their craft.

## Key Views

**PMs Are Leaders From Day One — Without Any Formal Authority**
Norton's core insight about product management: "You are a leader from day one in product management. There's leadership all over the place, but that's your job. You're a leader. You don't have any formal authority, but you're a leader." This frames the PM role as inherently about influence, persuasion, and bringing people together around a shared vision. The "art" of PM — dealing with humans, building alignment, navigating ambiguity — matters far more than the "science" of frameworks and processes.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**

**The Donut Principle: Servant Leadership in Action**
Norton's legendary "Bringing the Donuts" philosophy is about filling the white space — doing whatever needs to be done to help the team succeed, even if it's beneath your pay grade. "Bringing donuts" was a metaphor for servant leadership: showing up, being useful, and demonstrating that no task is too small. In a remote world, the digital equivalent might literally be sending actual donuts to your team via DoorDash. The principle transcends the medium.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**

**The Creative vs. Reactive Mindset Determines Everything**
Norton distinguishes between two modes PMs operate in: creative (proactive, possibility-focused) and reactive (responding to demands, putting out fires). Most PMs spend most of their time in reactive mode, which feels productive but is actually a trap. The best PMs carve out time for creative thinking — stepping back, questioning assumptions, and generating new possibilities. This is where the highest-leverage product decisions come from.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**

**Imposter Syndrome Is a Feature, Not a Bug**
Norton reframes imposter syndrome as a sign of growth rather than a weakness. "If you never feel imposter syndrome, you're probably not stretching enough." The discomfort of operating at the edge of your competence is the feeling of learning. His advice: acknowledge the feeling, recognize it as a signal that you're growing, and keep going. The goal isn't to eliminate imposter syndrome — it's to not let it stop you.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**

**Find a Coach Who's One Step Ahead, Not One Who Has All the Answers**
Norton's perspective on coaching: the best coaches don't tell you what to do — they help you discover your own answers through skilled questioning. He recommends looking for a coach who has walked a similar path but isn't so far ahead that they can't remember what your struggles feel like. The relationship should feel like a partnership where the coach is invested in your growth, not like a consultant delivering pre-packaged solutions.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**
