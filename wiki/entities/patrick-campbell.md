# Patrick Campbell

Patrick Campbell appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss 10 lessons on bootstrapping a $200m business.

## Key Views

**Do something on pricing once per quarter — it’s the most neglected growth lever** — Patrick’s core pricing advice is almost boringly simple: pick one pricing action per quarter. Most companies spend heavily on acquisition and retention while doing nothing on monetization. "You have three growth levers: acquiring customers, monetizing them, and retaining them. You’re spending a lot on acquisition, some on retention, and probably nothing on pricing." The single metric to watch: revenue per customer, going up every quarter. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/patrick-campbell.md]

**The value metric is the most powerful pricing decision — charge per unit of value, not per user** — Patrick argues the pricing metric (per user vs. per action vs. per outcome) is the highest-leverage pricing decision. A good value metric means Disney pays Disney prices and a startup pays startup prices automatically. It reduces churn by 20-25% (people downgrade instead of cancel) and doubles expansion revenue (usage grows implicitly). Pound for pound, it’s the most impactful thing to get right. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/patrick-campbell.md]

**Tactical retention is a 25-40% churn problem nobody fixes** — Most product teams obsess over strategic retention (features, time-to-value, roadmaps) and ignore tactical retention: payment failures, cancellation flows, offboarding. Patrick’s data from 2M cancellation flows: the cancel flow should ask two questions (“why leaving?” and “what did you like?”), with the second creating a “nostalgia effect” that slows the cancel train. Fixing tactical retention is two months of work and directly recovers 25-40% of churn. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/patrick-campbell.md]

**Tempo framework > org design — ship frequency is the real alignment mechanism** — Patrick believes that defining "what good looks like" in terms of shipping tempo is more important than any org structure. The method: establish mission metric + guiding principles at the top, then each team determines their own cadence targets (launches/month, episodes/week). Leadership’s job is to close the gap between current and target tempo. "Real professionals ship — at a high frequency." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/patrick-campbell.md]

**Bootstrapping is not always better — Patrick’s biggest regret: not raising money** — Patrick’s contrarian take: bootstrapping was a mistake for ProfitWell because it capped their ambition. "If we had taken money, we could have had a billion-dollar exit or kept going." His framework: bootstrapping is for lifestyle/cash-flow businesses; funding is for companies targeting $1B+ in revenue. The ideal path: bootstrap the first 18-24 months through product-market fit, then raise to go for the fences. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/patrick-campbell.md]
