# Ayo Omojola

Ayo Omojola co-created and scaled Square's Cash Card and the Cash App. He's currently Chief Product Officer at Carbon Health, one of the fastest-growing health tech companies. Previously he was a founder (Mailform), an angel investor in Mercury Bank, Fair, and Modern Treasury, and sits on the board of Pinwheel.

## Key Views

**Differentiate in a Way That Matters** — Being different is easy. Being better is easy. The hard part is being both different and better in a way that matters to the end user. Cash App's winning differentiator: "Send me a dollar I can use now." No other app could do same-day cash access. That concrete, user-visible difference drove adoption. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ayo-omojola.md]

**Build a Startup Within a Startup** — Ayo's approach to building Cash Card within Square was to treat it as an independent startup with complete ownership, while leveraging Square's brand and resources. This requires clear boundaries, a separate P&L mindset, and a leader who can navigate internal politics while moving fast. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ayo-omojola.md]

**First-Principles Team Building** — When hiring, start from: "What does success look like for this role in 6 months?" then work backwards to the skills, experience, and temperament needed. Avoid "clone hiring" (hiring people like yourself). Look for pattern breaks — candidates who solved similar problems in different contexts bring fresh thinking. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ayo-omojola.md]

**Deep Problems Beat Broad Ones** — Go deep on one problem rather than shallow on many. Cash App focused relentlessly on peer-to-peer payments before expanding into banking, investing, and Bitcoin. The depth-first approach builds a moat: once you own a core user behavior deeply, adjacent expansions become natural. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ayo-omojola.md]

**Fintech and Healthtech Share a Core Truth** — Both are regulated, trust-intensive industries where the product is a promise. In both, the winning companies are the ones that make complex compliance feel invisible while delivering a consumer-grade experience. The principles for building in fintech (speed, simplicity, transparency) apply directly to healthtech. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ayo-omojola.md]

# Ayo Omojola

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at Carbon Health; former PM leader at Cash App (Square/Block), where he co-created and scaled the Cash Card; founder of Mailform; board member at Pinwheel; angel investor in Mercury, Fair, Modern Treasury  
**Known for:** Building Cash App from <50K to 50M+ actives, the "different AND better" product differentiation framework, hiring founders, and first-principles thinking in regulated industries.

## Key Views

### 1. Different AND better — the dual requirement for product success

Ayo's central insight from scaling Cash App: a winning product must be **different AND better** in a way that matters to the user. Being different is easy (look at what exists and build something else). Being better is also easy (make improvements and charge more). The hard part is being *different in a way that matters* — Cash App's killer differentiator was "instant" (push-to-debit, instant cash out, instant card issuance, instant stock/Bitcoin selling). When someone said "why not Venmo?" Ayo's answer was: "Try and send me a dollar that I can use now." Only one app could do that.

> "Being different is not enough … being better is not enough … it has to be better than what exists today in a way that matters to the end user." — Ayo Omojola

### 2. The "startup within a startup" needs a small, senior, trusted team

Cash App succeeded as an internal startup at Square despite enormous organizational friction. Ayo's key takeaway: a **small, tightly-knit senior team** (11-12 people for the first year) that truly trusts each other. The team stayed super senior for a long time — no junior dilution. They also had Brian (Grassadonia) who knew the organization, knew the talent, and could navigate internal politics. Most "startups within a startup" fail because they lack the existential fear of going out of business, leading to resource bloat that doesn't match milestones.

> "When you are in a large organization, trying to do an actually new thing, I think small teams are better than big ones, period." — Ayo Omojola

### 3. Hire founders — but know the costs

Ayo actively recruits former founders for his teams (~15% of hires). Founders bring incredible output, cut through organizational bullshit immediately, and level up everyone around them. But there are two real costs: **(1)** Founders see every inefficiency and will call it out relentlessly — you must be ready to hear hard truths. **(2)** Founders have a shelf life of ~2-2.5 years before their ambition pulls them to the next thing. The team gets differentially higher output with differentially higher attrition. Worth it, but you need to plan for the churn.

> "If there's any waste or bullshit in your organization, they fucking see it right away and call it out." — Ayo Omojola

### 4. Cash App's 10 things done right — not one silver bullet

Ayo rejects the narrative that Cash App's success came from one thing. It was 10 things done right simultaneously: insane talent density, insane focus, world-class fraud prevention, firewalled from Square's priorities, the consumer as the "hero customer" (100% of tradeoffs favored the consumer), incredible design depth, instant money movement as the wedge, regulatory depth, and more. The compound effect of all these things created the moat — not any single feature. This is a caution against seeking a single "lottery ticket number" from success stories.

> "The reason Cash App is differentially successful in the market today is the compound effect of all those things." — Ayo Omojola

### 5. Regulated industries are a durable skill — go deep on the "wallpaper"

Ayo intentionally positioned himself as someone who can build in highly regulated spaces (fintech at Cash App, healthtech at Carbon Health). His approach: go incredibly deep on the regulatory "wallpaper" — sitting in rooms with product, engineering, legal, and compliance, projecting regulations on a screen, debating what each clause really means for product design. This depth becomes a durable competitive advantage because most builders avoid the complexity. At Carbon Health, this means building full-stack vertically integrated healthcare (clinics + software + operations) where every button the doctor presses is custom-built software.

> "I casted myself instead as, 'Hey, what if I could be good at regulated businesses, instead of just good at money?'" — Ayo Omojola
