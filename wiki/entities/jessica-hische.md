# Jessica Hische

**Role:** Lettering artist and New York Times bestselling author; founder of a custom typography studio  
**Known for:** Typographical work for logos (Mailchimp, Philz Coffee, Eventbrite), films (Wes Anderson), book covers, and the United States Postal Service; bestselling children's book author; refreshing Lenny's brand.

## Key Views

### 1. "Good Enough" Is Fine for Early-Stage — Invest Later

Jessica's contrarian view on startup branding: brand people will tell you "brand is everything" and you need a massive investment upfront, but for early-stage startups, "good enough" is fine. The product and team drive success, not the logo. If you spend heavily on brand and then pivot, you've wasted money. Her recommendation: use what you have internally until you've achieved product-market fit and are ready to scale. Then refresh when you have real customer equity to protect.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jessica-hische.md]

> "If you invest super heavily on the whole brand vision from the jump, sometimes it's like throwing away money if you have to pivot." — Jessica Hische

### 2. Signs It's Time to Refresh Your Brand

When should you invest in a refresh? **(1)** You're about to print a lot of swag or host a big event — physical investment makes the cost worthwhile. **(2)** Your logo doesn't scale well (looks bad at small or large sizes). **(3)** You're about to significantly expand or pivot your product. **(4)** Your logo uses an off-the-shelf font — easily copied by competitors trying to trick your customers. Custom typography prevents copycat brand confusion.

### 3. The Brand Refresh Process: Start Broad, Then Narrow

Jessica's process: **(1)** Figure out goals — is this a close-in refresh (fix utilitarian issues) or a pivot (attract new audience)? **(2)** First round: explore broadly to capture looks and feels. **(3)** Subsequent rounds: narrow — weight, letter height, details. **(4)** Key principle: everything should feel like it was "created at the same time" — typography, colors, illustrations should blend into a cohesive dish, not "hot ham water." She hands off files early for in-situ testing, which is unusual in the design world.

### 4. Most People Have Better Design Instincts Than They Think

Jessica's empowerment message to non-designers: "What we are as people are endless absorbers of patterns." You can feel when something is off even if you can't name why. Exercise: look at fonts and ask "what feeling does this give me?" without overanalyzing. Legibility is crucial for new/unknown brands — "at a super fast glance, people can read it right away." Once you're a household name, color alone can identify you, but that takes years of equity.

### 5. Custom Typography Protects Against Copycats

Jessica's practical advice: using a popular or free font means anyone can copy your look easily. When you find success, copycats will try to confuse your customers. Custom typography (or custom logo work) adds a layer of protection because it's harder to replicate exactly. This is a utilitarian investment — not vanity.
