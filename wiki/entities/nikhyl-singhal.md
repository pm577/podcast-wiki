# Nikhyl Singhal

Nikhyl Singhal appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss why half of product managers are in trouble.

## Key Views

**The PM Renaissance & Stress** — AI is reshaping product management: 'information movers' are becoming dinosaurs. PMs who don't love building are in trouble. The industry has never been more tired, but the strongest builders have more offers than ever. 'In the next 12 to 24 months, we're going to see massive shedding of staffs and then massive rehiring — the 8,000 people are going to all be AI first.' ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-04-19-nikhyl-singhal.md]

**Judgment is the Core Skill** — As AI automates the mechanical parts of product development, the critical skill becomes judgment: evaluating whether a change is good, whether to build X vs Y, and building sustainable, differentiated platforms. 'The system skill that's existed from the beginning of the internet — it's not about the feature, it's about the platform enabling capability.' ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-04-19-nikhyl-singhal.md]

**Mid-Career Squeeze** — 30-something knowledge workers face the cruelest tension: their highest-power career years coincide with settling down, kids, aging parents, and physical changes. AI-driven pace compounds this, creating a generation that is 'insanely stressed historically.' ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-04-19-nikhyl-singhal.md]

**No More Bad Software** — Within two years, AI will obsolete the mechanical parts of building product. The cost of testing and changing drops to near zero, enabling 10-100x more changes per product. 'I think that in two years, I think there won't be any more bad software' — AI will fix apps that were never iterated on (smart home apps, etc.). ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-04-19-nikhyl-singhal.md]

**Product Leaders as Judgment Drivers** — 'Product leaders will increasingly get paid and be asked to drive judgment, and then be the tip of the spear on trying to essentially obsolete everything else through software, through AI, through agents.' The mechanical parts of building product will be obsoleted within two years. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-04-19-nikhyl-singhal.md]

# Nikhyl Singhal

Nikhyl Singhal is VP of Product at Meta, overseeing product teams building messaging, groups, stories, and the main Facebook feed. Previously, he served as Chief Product Officer at Credit Karma and held various leadership roles at Google (Google Hangouts, Google Photos). He has co-founded three startups and shares his career wisdom through his newsletter and podcast called *The Skip*.

## Key Views

### Think About the Skip — Not the Next Job, But the One After It
Nikhyl's central career framework: "I always think about not the next job, but the one after it. Maybe think about not your boss's job but your boss's boss's job." He calls his newsletter *The Skip* for this reason. Short-term thinking (hating your boss, things moving slowly) leads to lateral moves. Working backwards from your end state — treating your career as a product with versions — leads to intentional, compounding progress.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nikhyl-singhal.md]

### The Dog Track Warning: Don't Chase the Rabbit
Nikhyl uses a powerful analogy: at dog tracks, fake rabbits motivate dogs to run in circles. If a dog ever catches the rabbit, it stops running forever. "I think this happens a ton — people focus on 'one day I will be X, I will be that VP, I will have more money' — but they don't think about what happens next." The antidote: always have something meaningful and motivating to work toward, even after you achieve your goal.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nikhyl-singhal.md]

### Beware of Ex-Growth Companies
Nikhyl identified a dangerous category of companies he calls "ex-growth companies" — companies that raised substantial capital during the zero-interest era, are well-capitalized but no longer growing, and still carry multi-billion-dollar valuations without product-market fit. His blunt advice: "This is not the company to join, this is the company to leave." Signs include reframing the core product to find PM fit, spending heavily without user pull, and equity that's unlikely to be valuable.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nikhyl-singhal.md]

### Why You're Not Getting Promoted
Through mentoring hundreds of PMs, Nikhyl identified four common blockers: (1) **No advocacy** — your manager doesn't see the magic in you, so find someone who does; (2) **The role doesn't exist** — you're ready but there's no open position at the next level; (3) **Impatience** — leadership levels take longer to absorb, and annual promotions aren't sustainable after mid-career; (4) **Gaps in craft** — you need to pick a lane (craft, market ambiguity, organizational ambiguity, domain expertise, or team management) and become world-class at it.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nikhyl-singhal.md]

### Pick a Lane — Develop a World-Class Superpower
Nikhyl advises PMs to identify which type of product ambiguity they excel at and become known for it: crafting (design/UX), market ambiguity (finding new opportunities), organizational ambiguity (complex coordination), domain expertise (ML, hardware, growth), or team management. "Being a product manager means you're confronted with maybe all five. I want you to pick one of these as early as possible." Diversify later, but build a superpower first. This is what makes you memorable and promotable.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nikhyl-singhal.md]
