# Asha Sharma

**Role:** Corporate Vice President of Product for Microsoft's AI Platform (AI infrastructure, foundation models, agent toolchains); former COO at Instacart, VP Product at Meta (Messenger, Instagram Direct, Messenger Kids); Board member of Home Depot and Coupang  
**Known for:** The concept of "product as organism" vs. "product as artifact," the rise of the full-stack builder, and the coming "agentic society" where the org chart becomes the work chart.

## Key Views

### 1. Product as organism — the shift from static artifacts to living systems

Asha's central thesis: AI-native products aren't static artifacts that you ship and dashboard. They are living organisms that get better with every interaction. A product's "metabolism" — its ability to ingest data, digest it through reward models, and produce outcomes — becomes the core IP of the company. This changes everything about how product teams operate: feedback is continuous, observability becomes culture, and functions blur. The model inside the product becomes the primary artifact, alongside traditional software components.

> "All of a sudden, these are these living organisms that just get better with the more interactions that happen and in many ways I think this is the new IP of every single company." — Asha Sharma

### 2. Post-training is the new pre-training — adaptation over raw compute

Asha argues that with models hitting ~30B parameters, the CapEx to train a foundation model from scratch doesn't make economic sense for most companies. The leverage is now in **post-training**: fine-tuning, reinforcement learning, synthetic data, and reward design using your own proprietary data. The companies that win will be those that treat model adaptation like an assembly line — multiple tracks running in parallel to optimize for price, performance, and quality. She believes in a "model system" approach (ensemble of diverse models for different tasks) rather than "one model to rule them all."

> "You have to come up with the rewards design, you have to actually roll it out, you have to A/B test it rigorously. You have to find the job to be done or the use case that it makes the most sense for." — Asha Sharma

### 3. The rise of the full-stack polymath builder

Asha observes that AI-native companies succeed when builders can span product, engineering, and design — the "loop, not the lane." In traditional orgs, launching a product requires 10+ steps across 5+ functions and 6+ layers (500+ touchpoints). That cadence is lethal when the technology changes every week. The next era demands full-stack builders who understand the efficiency/cost of the product, reward system design, and UI/UX. She's seeing this pattern in both AI-native startups and 50-year-old enterprises that are starting to operate this way.

> "I really believe in the concept of the full stack builder. … I think that gives you velocity and throughput and then gives you the whole loop to start to actually metabolize and go through that much faster." — Asha Sharma

### 4. Three-stage AI adoption blueprint for enterprises

Asha's framework for enterprise AI maturity: **(1)** Make everyone AI-fluent — everyone uses copilots/AI tools in daily workflows so fear is replaced by understanding. **(2)** Apply AI to an existing process — map the process, apply AI, measure impact (e.g., reducing fraud resolution from 15 days to 10). **(3)** Use AI to inflect growth — improve LTV/retention, co-create new categories, or go from embedded agents to embodied agents. Companies fail when they do "AI for AI's sake" — launching too many projects without a blueprint, measurement, or evals.

> "Where companies fail is that they're doing AI for AI's sake. They have a ton of projects that they're kicking off at the same time without a blueprint to understand how it actually worked." — Asha Sharma

### 5. From GUIs to code-native interfaces — composability over canvas

Asha predicts a shift from graphical user interfaces to composable, code-native interfaces — following the historical pattern of databases (desktop → SQL), cloud (consoles → Terraform), and now AI (GUIs → streams of text that connect better with LLMs). The future of products is about how something composes — how an agent can read it, how it collaborates — not just how the canvas looks. Chat will be important but not sufficient; existing artifacts (docs, slides, emails) will become composable pieces in an agentic world. The "org chart becomes the work chart" as agents take on tasks and layers of management dissolve.

> "I think that the whole organizational construct might start to look different in a few years. … The way that you scale to that is with agents." — Asha Sharma
