# Aparna Chennapragada

**Role:** Chief Product Officer of Experiences and Devices at Microsoft (AI product strategy, productivity tools, agents); former Google executive  
**Known for:** Leading AI product strategy at Microsoft, prototyping-first approach to AI, the NLX (natural language experience) framework.

## Key Views

### 1. If you're not prototyping with AI, you're doing it wrong

Aparna's core message: the fastest way to understand what AI can do for your product is to prototype. Don't wait for perfect specs or complete understanding — build something, see how it behaves, learn, and iterate. AI products are inherently non-deterministic and can't be fully designed upfront. Prototyping is how you discover what's possible and what works.

> "If you're not prototyping and building to see what you want to build, I think you're doing it wrong. It becomes even more important to have that territorial and taste-making at the heart of it." — Aparna Chennapragada

### 2. NLX (Natural Language Experience) is the new UI paradigm

Aparna introduces NLX as the next interaction model beyond GUI. Instead of clicking buttons, users express intent in natural language. This shifts the product designer's job from "what buttons do I provide?" to "how do I interpret intent and guide the user through the right action?" Products need to be redesigned from first principles for this paradigm.

### 3. AI requires taste-making — don't end up with a Frankenstein product

As AI makes it easy to add features, the risk is ending up with a product that does everything but coheres around nothing. Aparna emphasizes that taste and curation become *more* important, not less, in the AI era. Someone needs to have a strong point of view about what the product is *for* and what it refuses to do.

### 4. A personal AI practice — the Chrome extension that asks "how can AI help?"

Aparna practices what she preaches: she has a Chrome extension that prompts her on every new tab: "How can you use AI to do what you're going to do right now?" This constant prompting builds an intuition for where AI fits and doesn't. It's a practical way to develop AI literacy and discover unexpected use cases.

### 5. From MVP to MVE — Minimum Viable Experience

Aparna argues that in the AI era, the bar has shifted from "does it work?" to "does it *feel* right?" The user experience of AI products — speed, tone, reliability, error handling — matters as much as the underlying capability. She advocates for designing the full experience, not just the feature, from the start.
