# Caitlin Kalinowski

Caitlin Kalinowski is a hardware leader who has worked at the intersection of AI and physical product design at OpenAI, Meta (Oculus/AR glasses), and Apple. She has built some of the most ambitious consumer hardware products in the world, including the Oculus VR headsets and the Orion AR glasses. Her expertise spans robotics, manufacturing, and the convergence of AI with the physical world.

## Key Views

### AI Saturation at the Keyboard Drives the Hardware Boom
Caitlin sees a clear trend: AI capabilities behind a keyboard are saturating rapidly, and the next frontier is the physical world — robotics, manufacturing, and industrialization. "There's a dawning realization, especially in the lab, the acceleration is going so vertical that what you can do behind a keyboard with AI is going to saturate. When that happens, the next frontier is the physical world." This is why everyone is suddenly interested in hardware.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-05-17-caitlin-kalinowski.md]

### Hardware Compiles Only 4-5 Times
Caitlin draws a sharp contrast between software and hardware development. Software engineers can compile code every day, but hardware teams only get to "compile" (release designs for mass production) four or five times total. "Once it's released, you compile it the last time you release it for mass production. If it's a mass production device, that's it. You're done. You can't ship over their updates." This forces a fundamentally more conservative, reliability-obsessed approach.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-05-17-caitlin-kalinowski.md]

### VR Was a Stepping Stone, Not a Destination
Despite massive investment from Meta and Apple, VR didn't take off as a mainstream product. Caitlin views this as a necessary step in a long technological arc. VR helped the industry solve fundamental challenges — SLAM positioning, depth sensing, spatial perception — that are now being applied directly to robotics and autonomous systems. The social barrier (covering your face) was the fundamental issue VR couldn't solve.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-05-17-caitlin-kalinowski.md]

### AR Glasses Are the Future — Once the Input Problem Is Solved
Caitlin is bullish on AR glasses as the successor to smartphones, but the path is harder than most expect. The Orion glasses prototype demonstrates a 70-degree field of view that creates genuine immersion. The key unsolved challenges: waveguides and MicroLED yields aren't ready for mass production, and the input problem (how do you interact silently and subtly in public) remains unsolved. "Once we start to figure out some of those challenges, having a display that's mostly off that you can turn on when you want it to be on seems like part of the future."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-05-17-caitlin-kalinowski.md]

### Reindustrialization as a National Security Imperative
Caitlin argues that the US needs to re-learn how to make things at scale — not just for economic reasons but for military security. "I do feel that we need to reindustrialize the country significantly to be safe in a military sense. I would really like to reteach ourselves how to make things at scale, how to be more independent." She points to drones and autonomous systems as areas where manufacturing capability directly translates to defense capability.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-05-17-caitlin-kalinowski.md]
