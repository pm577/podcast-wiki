# Maya Prohovnik

Maya Prohovnik is Spotify's Head of Podcast Product, where she oversees product, design, and engineering teams building tools and experiences for podcasters and listeners. She was employee #1 at Anchor, which was acquired by Spotify in 2019 and now powers over 75% of all new podcasts created globally. She started her career in customer support and is a passionate advocate for "dogfooding" — using your own product obsessively.

## Key Views

### Dogfooding Is Non-Negotiable for Building Creator Tools
Maya's core philosophy: you cannot build great tools for creators without being a creator yourself. She has four podcasts of her own — a Stephen King deep-dive, a Big Brother recap show (fully mobile-produced), a book review podcast, and a parenting journal. "I don't know how you can build those tools if you don't understand that mindset." She constantly pushes her product team to start their own podcasts. "Once they finally start doing their podcast, they're like, 'I get it. Something clicked.'"^[raw/transcripts/lenny/maya-prohovnik.md]

### Fake It Till You Make It — Interns as Distribution Infrastructure
In Anchor's early days, they couldn't automate podcast distribution to Apple Podcasts. So they hired college interns who manually created Apple Podcast accounts and submitted hundreds of thousands of podcasts. "I still don't know how many people know this. We just had college students making Apple podcast accounts and submitting podcasts through these accounts." This "Wizard of Oz" approach — doing manually what feels magical and automated — was a key reason Anchor captured so much market share so quickly.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/maya-prohovnik.md]

### Gut and Data Should Work Together, Not Compete
Maya believes that being "data-driven" and "gut-driven" are not opposites — your gut is a type of data. "I think of your gut actually as a type of data and it's a totally valid one. You need to be clear that that's what you're working with, but it should be taken as seriously as any other data point." The key is articulating why your gut is telling you something — referencing past experience, user conversations, or your own product usage. Simply saying "I believe this" without justification doesn't work in larger organizations.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/maya-prohovnik.md]

### Sometimes You Need to Pivot Even When the Data Looks Good
At Anchor, they faced a classic dilemma: Anchor 1.0 had great retention, growing users, and passionate fans — people were meeting and falling in love on the platform. But Maya and the CEO felt "it just wasn't ever going to be big enough." They made the painful call to pivot to 2.0, which alienated their existing users but ultimately led to the product that Spotify acquired. "Users don't always know what to ask for. You really have to be able to hear what's behind it, or even if the data is looking okay, think about where it's heading."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/maya-prohovnik.md]

### Starting in Customer Support Shaped Her Product Philosophy
Maya started her tech career in customer support, which she credits for her deeply user-centered approach to product. "I think I just am a very user-focused product person. There's different schools of thought for product, and for me it's always been about making people happy, making their experience better, solving real problems in their lives." This foundation makes dogfooding feel natural — becoming one of the users is the most direct way to advocate for what matters to them.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/maya-prohovnik.md]
