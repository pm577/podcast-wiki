# Jess Lachs

Jessica "Jess" Lachs is VP of Analytics and Data Science at DoorDash, where she's built one of the largest and most respected data organizations in tech. She joined DoorDash over 10 years ago, was its first GM (launching new markets), and previously founded GiftSimple and worked in investment banking. Her data team has become a benchmark for how analytics should drive business impact.

## Key Views

**Analytics Should Drive Business Impact, Not Just Answer "Why"**
Lachs insists analytics is a business-impact-driving function, not a service function. "For me, analytics is about finding opportunities, about having a point of view on the decisions that we should make — not just answering the why but answering the 'so what.'" Her team doesn't take Jira tickets to build dashboards; they sit at the table as thought partners alongside product, engineering, and operations, bringing data-driven opportunities for the business to pursue.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*

**Centralize Data Teams for Scale, Embed for Impact**
Lachs holds a contrarian view: a centralized data org is superior to embedding analysts in business units. She admits business leaders like embedded teams because they control the roadmap and have certainty about resources. But a central org preserves career growth for analysts, enables cross-functional learning, and prevents the fragmentation of data practices. The trick is to structure the central team into pods that map perfectly to business units while maintaining a unified reporting structure — giving both alignment and scale.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*

**Retention Is a Terrible Thing to Goal On — Use Leading Indicators Instead**
Lachs argues that retention, while ultimately what matters, is almost impossible to drive in a meaningful way in the short term. "You want to find a short-term metric you can measure that drives a long-term output." This insight shapes how DoorDash's data team thinks about metrics: instead of goal-setting on lagging indicators, they identify proximate metrics that correlate with retention and can be moved week-to-week. This makes the data team more actionable and less reactive.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*

**Data Scientists Should Pick Up the Phone — Extreme Ownership Extends to Research**
Lachs expects her data scientists to go beyond running queries. "Your goal is to figure out what's happening. And if that means you're going to pick up the phone and call customers, then that's what you're going to do." This philosophy of extreme ownership means data scientists don't just analyze data — they triangulate with qualitative research, operational context, and business strategy. It's what makes the team more than a reporting function and earns them their seat at the table.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*

**Earning the Seat at the Table Requires Bringing Opportunities, Not Just Dashboards**
Lachs's team earns its influence by proactively surfacing opportunities, not by waiting for requests. "We get a seat at the table and we need to earn it by bringing opportunities that we all can go and go after." This means her analysts and data scientists are expected to identify untapped growth areas, flag emerging risks, and propose specific actions — not just build reports. This proactive posture transforms data from a cost center into a value driver.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jess-lachs.md]]*
