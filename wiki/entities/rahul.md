# Rahul Vohra

Rahul Vohra is the founder and CEO of Superhuman, one of the most respected email clients in the market. Prior to Superhuman, he founded Rapportive, the first Gmail extension to scale to millions of users, which was acquired by LinkedIn in 2012. He is known for his deeply thoughtful approach to product building, his emphasis on craft and remarkableness, and his unique management philosophy centered on the "switch log" time-tracking system.

## Key Views

### The Real Secret Behind Virality: Word of Mouth
Rahul learned from LinkedIn's head of growth that no app has ever sustained a viral factor greater than one for any real period of time. Even Facebook at its peak had a viral factor of about 0.7. Address book imports (the classic viral mechanic) have a factor of about 0.4. "What then is the true secret behind virality? It is word of mouth — the virality you can't measure, that isn't a mechanic, that isn't in a feature. It is when one user spontaneously tells another user about your product." This insight shaped everything at Superhuman — they optimized for creating something remarkable that people naturally want to tell others about.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]

### The Switch Log: Free Your Time From the Calendar
Rahul's radical approach to time management: he does whatever he wants, whenever he wants, and logs every task switch by DMing his EA ("TS: working on X"). No calendar tethers. "If what I feel right now is, 'Oh boy, I really need to prepare for Lenny's podcast,' I'll go ahead and do that. If I get bored eight minutes in because something else bubbles up, I go attend to that." This revealed that he was spending only 6-7% of his time on product, design, technology, and marketing — his zones of genius. By hiring a president and reducing his direct reports from 8 to 2, he increased that to 60-70%.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]

### Solution Deepening vs. Market Widening
Rahul's framework for understanding product velocity: every feature is either solution deepening (making the product better for existing users) or market widening (making it available to more users). Early-stage startups should focus on solution deepening — it's where product-market fit comes from. But eventually every company must invest in market widening (new platforms, new integrations), which feels slow because it doesn't improve the product for existing users. This perceived slowdown is often unavoidable but worth it for long-term growth.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]

### Don't Act on All Customer Feedback — Even When Finding PMF
Rahul's counterintuitive advice: "You have to deliberately not act on the feedback of many of your early users. This is at the same time as listening to people intensely and building what people want. The question becomes: how do you listen to them, and then even of what they say, what do you pay attention to and what don't you?" The key is finding the signal in the noise — listening for the deeper need, not the surface-level feature request. Most early feedback will lead you astray if you follow it literally.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]

### Remarkableness as a Growth Engine
One of Superhuman's core company values is "create something remarkable." Rahul believes this is the ultimate growth strategy. "If you think about what a company has to do, it has to grow. How do things grow? It's creating something that people share. Creating something that is so joyful, so striking, so compelling that people can't help but tell others about it." He bakes this into the company's values: Create Delight, Deliver Remarkable Quality, Build the Extraordinary. These are not just slogans — they're the raw ingredients for organic growth.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]
