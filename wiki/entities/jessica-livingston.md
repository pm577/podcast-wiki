# Jessica Livingston

**Role:** Co-founder of Y Combinator (the most successful startup accelerator, funding 5,000+ companies, 200+ unicorns); author of *Founders at Work*; host of *The Social Radars* podcast  
**Known for:** Being YC's "Social Radar" — the ability to read founders and evaluate people in 10-minute interviews; co-founding YC alongside Paul Graham, Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell; playing the decisive role in funding Airbnb when the partners were skeptical about the idea.

## Key Views

### 1. The Social Radar — Reading People in 10 Minutes

Jessica's superpower at YC: reading social cues that technical co-founders often missed. In 10-minute founder interviews, while her co-founders focused on technology, she observed: Do the co-founders get along? Are they committed? Are they ready to quit their jobs? Will they be cockroaches (survive on nothing)? Her nickname came from the ritual after every interview: "Jessica, what does their social radar say?" She looked for energy, commitment, co-founder dynamics, domain expertise, and open-mindedness.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jessica-livingston.md]

> "My three co-founders were deeply technical, but I would look at other things about founders. All these little social cues." — Jessica Livingston

### 2. Red Flags: Defensiveness, Co-Founder Imbalance, Hackers in Cages

Key red flags Jessica identified: **(1)** Founders who get defensive when questioned — "that was always a bad sign." Great founders engage in a tennis match of ideas. **(2)** Unequal equity splits (e.g., 99% to 1%) — signals a power imbalance. **(3)** "Hackers in a cage" — a business founder who recruited a technical co-founder but gives them no equity or voice. **(4)** Founders who won't let their co-founder answer questions. **(5)** Founders who say they'll quit their job but clearly won't.

### 3. The Airbnb Interview — Betting on Founders, Not Ideas

The iconic YC story: YC partners *hated* Airbnb's idea (staying on someone's airbed) and Paul Graham actively tried to get the founders to pivot. But Jessica saw something else: contagious energy, scrappiness, willingness to do whatever it takes. The moment Joe Gebbia pulled out the Obama O's and Cap'n McCain's cereal boxes (which they'd glue-gunned themselves to fund the company), she knew: "These guys are going to work hard to do whatever they can to make this company succeed." The lesson: **bet on founders, not ideas.**

### 4. The Best Founders Are Open-Minded, Scrappy, and Learn from Everyone

Jessica's founder archetype: open to feedback, learns from everyone, doesn't get defensive. The Collison brothers (Stripe) would ask questions and genuinely listen. The best founders see questioning as a debate, not an interrogation. They're "scrappy" — willing to do unscalable things (sell cereal boxes, cold-call users, hand-deliver products). They have "contagious energy" about their product and domain expertise.

### 5. Being Erased from YC's Narrative

Jessica is candid about being often left out of YC's origin story (Wikipedia edits, news articles). She attributes this partly to a narrative that YC is "run by white men, only invests in white men" — having a woman on the founding team "would undermine their narrative." Her response: "People within YC know what I've done, the Y Combinator alumni know what I've done... So that's fine." But she frames being overlooked as a reason to understand power dynamics and advocate for recognition.
