# Molly Graham

**Role:** Former early employee at Google and Facebook; worked closely with Mark Zuckerberg on the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative; helped Bret Taylor scale Quip (acquired by Salesforce); currently leads Glue Club, a community for leaders navigating rapid change and scale  
**Known for:** "Give away your Legos" framework; the J-curve vs. stairs career model; the Waterline Model for diagnosing team problems; six rules for goal setting; the "Bob" monster for managing change emotions.

## Key Views

### 1. Give Away Your Legos

Molly's most famous framework: in scaling companies, the ground constantly shifts under you. As soon as you master building "houses" (your current role), someone tells you it's now a "neighborhood" and you need to pass your work to someone else and go build something entirely new. The key insight is that giving away what you've gotten good at is actually the path to growth — you go from building houses to building entire worlds. The alternative is getting buried under a growing pile of Legos as the company outgrows you.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/molly-graham.md]

> "At Facebook, I got to a place where I was literally giving away my job every three weeks. I was constantly rehiring myself because you have to grow as fast as your company is growing." — Molly Graham

### 2. The J-Curve vs. Stairs Career Model

Most people approach careers as a set of stairs — steady promotions every two years. But the most fun and impactful careers are J-curves: jumping off cliffs, falling temporarily, then climbing out way beyond where the stairs could ever take you. Molly only takes jobs she's "highly unqualified for" and likes being on learning curves so steep she's scared she'll fall off. The J-curve involves short-term pain and uncertainty but creates exponential growth.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/molly-graham.md]

> "Chamath drew me a picture on a whiteboard. He said the way a lot of people do careers is a set of stairs... but the much more fun careers are like jumping off cliffs and you do fall, but then you climb out way beyond where the stairs could ever get you." — Molly Graham

### 3. The Waterline Model: Snorkel Before You Scuba

The Waterline Model frames a team as a boat on an ocean trying to reach goals. Below the waterline are four layers of issues: (1) structural (goals, roles, expectations), (2) dynamics (culture, decision-making, conflict resolution), (3) interpersonal (relationships between two people), (4) intrapersonal (within one person). The rule: snorkel before you scuba — 80% of team problems are structural or dynamics issues. Always start at the top. Most managers jump to "the people are the problem" when they should first check if roles and expectations are clear.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/molly-graham.md]

> "Your only goal as a manager, if you do nothing else, is clear roles and clear expectations. That's it." — Molly Graham

### 4. Six Rules for Creating Goals That Actually Work

Molly's goal-setting framework: (1) No company needs more than three goals — Facebook governed with growth (MAUs), engagement, and revenue for five years. (2) One goal must win in a fight — if you can't prioritize, goals aren't doing their job. (3) An intern should be able to understand your goals — they're a communication tool first. (4) Strategy should hurt — if you're not making painful trade-offs, you're not prioritizing. (5) One goal has one owner — shared ownership is no ownership. (6) Goals alone aren't enough — you must follow up with ongoing communication and reinforcement.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/molly-graham.md]

> "If your goal setting process is not painful, then you're not prioritizing heavily enough." — Molly Graham

### 5. Name Your Monster: Managing the Emotions of Change

Molly externalizes the emotional rollercoaster of scaling into a character called "Bob" — the monster that wants to send rage emails, cling to Legos, and make you the worst version of yourself. Bob never goes away but you don't have to act on his urges. The rule: give it two weeks. If you still feel the same way after two weeks, it's real data worth acting on. Before that, it's just Bob. This framework helps leaders separate normal change anxiety from genuine concerns.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/molly-graham.md]

> "Bob's job is to make you the worst version of yourself. So your job is to let Bob do his thing, but not act on the emotions. All these emotions are normal and they are not useful." — Molly Graham
