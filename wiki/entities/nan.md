# Nan Yu

Nan Yu is the Head of Product at Linear, one of the most beloved and fastest-growing B2B SaaS products. He is known for his philosophy that speed and quality are complementary, not opposing forces — a principle that has helped Linear build a product that engineers actively want to switch to from incumbents like Jira.

## Key Views

### Speed and Quality Are Not a Trade-Off

Nan's core philosophy: "People talk about this as if there were a trade-off because when they think about speed, the thing they over-index on is rushing or being sloppy. What they should be indexing on is being really competent." He uses the analogy of elite chess players (Magnus Carlsen) and master craftsmen — the best in any field work fast because expertise is second nature. Speed enables more iterations, which drives quality. "You can basically tell how good the output is going to be by how fast they're going."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]

### Have Something Working at 10% of the Time Budget

Nan's tactical rule: by the time 10% of a project's time budget has passed, you should have a working prototype. "After week one, you have something that works that tests some kind of key hypothesis internally so that you can feel like, is this thing actually panning out?" There's no expectation that the first version is great — it's just the starting point for learning. "You don't want to wait until you're 80% done to be able to make that kind of judgment because then it's just too late."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]

### My Goal Is to Feel Bad in the Same Way Customers Feel Bad

Nan's approach to customer research: "My goal is to feel bad in the same way that customers feel bad." He digs relentlessly on customer calls to understand not just the surface problem but the emotion behind it. He asks questions until he personally feels the pain, which leads to more empathetic and effective product solutions that go beyond feature parity.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]

### Say No to the Features That Create Bloat

"Linear's success is built on saying no to the exact kind of feature requests that lead to bloatedness that makes ICs hate their lives." Nan examines feature requests critically, debating not just whether they add value, but whether they would degrade the product over time. Saying no to bloat is harder than saying yes, but it's what keeps the product fast and loved.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]

### Competent Teams Move Fast — Hire for That

Speed comes from talent, not pressure. Nan emphasizes that the ability to move fast without cutting quality requires engineers who "don't get blocked by every single little design choice" and are "happy to just make something workable." Building a culture where the first version is expected to be imperfect allows the team to iterate rapidly toward excellence rather than polishing prematurely.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]
