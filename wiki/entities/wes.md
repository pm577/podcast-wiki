# Wes Kao

**Role:** Co-founder of Maven; co-creator of altMBA with Seth Godin; executive communication coach  
**Known for:** Raising the writing and communication quality of entire organizations, teaching persuasive communication and managing up.

## Key Views

### 1. The blast radius of a poorly written message is massive

Wes emphasizes that the cost of unclear communication extends far beyond the individual message. When you send a confusing Slack message to 15 people, the resulting back-and-forth wastes everyone's time. Taking an extra two minutes to clarify your writing can save hours of collective productivity. Her framework: write for the reader, not for yourself.

> "The blast radius of a poorly written memo is way bigger than most people think. If you are just shooting off a message in a Slack channel with 15 other people, and it's confusing, you didn't include information you should have included, there's going to be a bunch of back and forth."

### 2. MOO: Most Obvious Objection

A key framework for preparing for meetings and proposals. Before presenting, spend two minutes identifying the most obvious objections people will raise. You won't anticipate every question, but you can reliably predict the obvious ones. Addressing them proactively in your communication builds trust and reduces friction.

> "A lot of times we're surprised by the questions that we get especially in meetings, we feel blindsided. When really, if you thought for even two minutes about what are obvious objections, you often immediately come up with what some of those things are."

### 3. Take ownership of your communication outcomes

When you don't get the reaction you expected — confusion, skepticism, or apathy — Wes advises asking yourself: "How might I be contributing to this? How could I explain this more clearly? How can I be more compelling?" This flips the mindset from blaming the audience to improving your own communication.

> "I'm a big proponent of asking myself, if I'm not getting the reaction that I'm looking for, how might I be contributing?"

### 4. Reasoning and context are the true multipliers

Wes always includes her reasoning and context alongside every recommendation. This does two things: (1) it makes her proposals more persuasive because people understand the "why," and (2) it helps everyone around her learn faster. Over time, this teaching-by-osmosis raises the decision-making quality of the entire organization.

### 5. Precision in language is a competitive advantage

Colleagues describe Wes as "precise with her use of language" and "meticulous about examining her own ideas before bringing them in front of others." This reflects her belief that sloppy language leads to sloppy thinking, and that the clarity of your communication directly determines the quality of decisions made around you.
