# Varun Parmar

Varun Parmar is the Chief Product Officer of Miro, with over two decades of experience in the tech industry including senior leadership roles at Box. He shares deep insights into Miro's product culture, development processes, and competitive strategy.

## Key Views

**Product development as a chess game.** Every release either moves you forward or backward against competitors. Varun believes products never stay the same — with each release you're either gaining or losing ground. This framework drives clarity in prioritization and helps teams understand the real impact of every feature they ship.

**Cross-functional empathy as a core product value.** At Miro, the product organization is based in Europe while go-to-market teams are global. Varun champions practicing empathy not just for customers but internally across distributed teams. The key is asking probing questions during product reviews to understand what insights — from customer feedback, market trends, or internal interactions — led to each decision.

**Design Sprint framework for zero-to-one innovation.** Miro uses Jake Knapp's Design Sprint methodology to rapidly validate hypotheses. In a five-day window, small teams mock up concepts, create prototypes, and get external validation. Varun cites Miro Talktrack as a successful outcome — the team validated a unique approach (synchronized board movement with audio/video) through this sprint process rather than building what competitors were doing.

**Team-centric vs. persona-centric product strategy.** Unlike many tools that optimize for a single persona (e.g., designers), Miro focuses on enabling cross-functional teams. Varun argues that innovation happens at the intersection of different functions — PMs, designers, engineers, researchers — and Miro's product is designed to serve the team as a whole rather than individual roles.

**Practice empathy to gain insights.** Varun emphasizes that the most powerful way to ensure good product decisions is to double-click on the "why" behind every recommendation. By understanding what insight drove someone to make a particular choice — whether through inbound feedback, outbound research, or market signals — teams can surface and challenge their own assumptions.
