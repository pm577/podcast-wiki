# Ken Norton

Ken Norton spent 14 years at Google leading product teams for Google Docs, Calendar, Maps, and Google Ventures. He's now a full-time executive coach specializing in product leaders. His writing — including the famous "Bringing the Donuts" post — has shaped how a generation of PMs think about their craft.

## Key Views

**PMs Are Leaders From Day One — Without Any Formal Authority**
Norton's core insight about product management: "You are a leader from day one in product management. There's leadership all over the place, but that's your job. You're a leader. You don't have any formal authority, but you're a leader." This frames the PM role as inherently about influence, persuasion, and bringing people together around a shared vision. The "art" of PM — dealing with humans, building alignment, navigating ambiguity — matters far more than the "science" of frameworks and processes.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**

**The Donut Principle: Servant Leadership in Action**
Norton's legendary "Bringing the Donuts" philosophy is about filling the white space — doing whatever needs to be done to help the team succeed, even if it's beneath your pay grade. "Bringing donuts" was a metaphor for servant leadership: showing up, being useful, and demonstrating that no task is too small. In a remote world, the digital equivalent might literally be sending actual donuts to your team via DoorDash. The principle transcends the medium.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**

**The Creative vs. Reactive Mindset Determines Everything**
Norton distinguishes between two modes PMs operate in: creative (proactive, possibility-focused) and reactive (responding to demands, putting out fires). Most PMs spend most of their time in reactive mode, which feels productive but is actually a trap. The best PMs carve out time for creative thinking — stepping back, questioning assumptions, and generating new possibilities. This is where the highest-leverage product decisions come from.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**

**Imposter Syndrome Is a Feature, Not a Bug**
Norton reframes imposter syndrome as a sign of growth rather than a weakness. "If you never feel imposter syndrome, you're probably not stretching enough." The discomfort of operating at the edge of your competence is the feeling of learning. His advice: acknowledge the feeling, recognize it as a signal that you're growing, and keep going. The goal isn't to eliminate imposter syndrome — it's to not let it stop you.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**

**Find a Coach Who's One Step Ahead, Not One Who Has All the Answers**
Norton's perspective on coaching: the best coaches don't tell you what to do — they help you discover your own answers through skilled questioning. He recommends looking for a coach who has walked a similar path but isn't so far ahead that they can't remember what your struggles feel like. The relationship should feel like a partnership where the coach is invested in your growth, not like a consultant delivering pre-packaged solutions.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/ken-norton.md]]**
