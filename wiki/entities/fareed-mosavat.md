# Fareed Mosavat

Fareed Mosavat is Chief Development Officer at Reforge, where he leads production, content, and new-product experiences. Previously he led growth at Slack and Instacart, was VP of Product at Runkeeper, GM at Zynga, and spent six years at Pixar working on films including Finding Nemo, Cars, WALL-E, and Up.

## Key Views

### The Only Way to Get Better at Product Is Real Reps

Fareed believes you can't learn product management through exercises or fake scenarios. "You have to work on real products at real companies with real customers, with real data to get better at product management. Any kind of training, mentorship, reading — it's just a layer on top of that. The real acceleration happens from doing it and getting more reps." Courses and books are accelerants, not substitutes, for actual execution.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/fareed-mosavat.md]

### Growing Scope Is the Path to Leadership

The key to advancing as a PM is taking on more scope — not just managing more people. Fareed advises PMs to constantly look for gaps that need filling and volunteer to fill them. The PM who expands their scope naturally gets promoted, while the one who waits for a title change before taking responsibility stalls. His own career progression followed this pattern: from Pixar engineer to Zynga GM by repeatedly stepping into undefined spaces.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/fareed-mosavat.md]

### Product Management Requires Technical Literacy

Having started as an engineer at Pixar, Fareed believes technical literacy is a massive advantage for PMs. You don't need to be able to code, but you need to understand engineering constraints, trade-offs, and workflows. This allows you to earn engineering trust, participate in technical discussions, and make better prioritization decisions. His Pixar background taught him how creative and technical teams can collaborate effectively.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/fareed-mosavat.md]

### Trust Is Built Through Consistency Over Time

Fareed emphasizes that trust — with engineering, design, leadership, and stakeholders — is the PM's most important asset. It's built through consistent delivery, transparent communication, and admitting mistakes. You can't shortcut it with titles or clever arguments. "You can't do homework. You can't do exercises. You can't do fake stuff." Trust comes from repeatedly demonstrating that you make good product decisions.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/fareed-mosavat.md]

### Crossing the Canyon From IC to Manager

The transition from individual contributor to manager is one of the hardest in product. Fareed advises new managers to focus on: (1) letting go of doing the work yourself, (2) learning to communicate through others, and (3) finding joy in enabling your team's success rather than your own output. The biggest trap is continuing to operate like an IC with a "direct reports" label.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/fareed-mosavat.md]
