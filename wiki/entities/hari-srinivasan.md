# Hari Srinivasan

Hari Srinivasan is VP of Product at LinkedIn Talent Solutions, overseeing LinkedIn Recruiter, LinkedIn Jobs, and LinkedIn Learning — LinkedIn's biggest business. He has been at LinkedIn for over 8 years and is a frequent guest lecturer at Stanford on product management and building complex systems.

## Key Views

### Skills-First Hiring Is the Future

Hari led LinkedIn's push into "skills-first hiring" — translating people's experiences into skills rather than matching job titles. During COVID, they realized the job market wasn't rebalancing because people were locked into title-based searches. By shifting to skills-based matching, LinkedIn helped people find roles they'd never have considered. He believes this approach creates a more efficient, equitable labor market and is a permanent change.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hari-srinivasan.md]

### Building Complex Systems Requires Simplicity at the Core

LinkedIn serves multiple customer types (job seekers, recruiters, companies, advertisers) with complex, interconnected systems. Hari's philosophy: keep the core simple and let complexity live at the edges. When systems become too complex, they break in unpredictable ways. The most important engineering and product work is simplification — reducing the number of states, paths, and interactions.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hari-srinivasan.md]

### LinkedIn's Content Transformation Was Intentional

Hari shares how LinkedIn deliberately shifted from a "cringey" platform to a genuinely valuable content destination. This required changes to the feed algorithm, creator tools, and content moderation — all driven by a clear thesis: professional content can be as engaging as personal content if it's relevant and authentic. The result: LinkedIn has become a real source of valuable content that people actively seek out.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hari-srinivasan.md]

### Serendipity Amplifies Great Product Work

Hari's path to Lenny's podcast came through a tweet from a community member (The Curious PM) — illustrating his belief that great product work attracts attention organically. He believes in being responsive to inbound opportunities and building in public. When you build something people care about, serendipitous connections follow. This applies to careers, products, and personal brands alike.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hari-srinivasan.md]

### Product Management Is a Craft, Not a Formula

Having taught PM courses at Stanford, Hari emphasizes that product management is a craft learned through practice, not a formula learned through theory. The best PMs combine analytical rigor with human empathy, data with intuition. He advises aspiring PMs to seek diverse experiences — building products, working with engineers, talking to customers — rather than focusing on frameworks and certifications.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hari-srinivasan.md]
