# Jake Knapp and John Zeratsky

Jake Knapp and John Zeratsky are bestselling authors of *Sprint* and *Make Time*, co-creators of the Design Sprint methodology, and co-founders of Character VC. They've helped over 300 teams at Google, Microsoft, YouTube, Slack, Uber, and leading startups refine and test product ideas. Their latest book, *Click*, introduces the Foundation Sprint — a structured two-day process for clarifying strategy before building.

## Key Views

**The Foundation Sprint: 10 Hours That Replace Months of Misalignment**
Knapp and Zeratsky's Foundation Sprint is a two-day (10-hour) structured process where the core team — co-founders or product leadership — clears their calendars and works through three phases: basics (who's the customer, what's the problem), differentiation (what sets you apart), and approach (implementation path). The output is a single-sentence Founding Hypothesis: "If we solve this problem for this customer with this approach, they'll choose us over competitors because of differentiator X and Y." They argue the ROI on these 10 hours is extraordinary because most teams go months without nailing down their core strategy.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jake-knapp-john-zeratsky-20.md]]*

**Going Fast Can Actually Slow You Down — Slow Down to Think Deeply**
Zeratsky warns that in the AI era, the more AI-generated or AI-assisted work becomes, the more generic products tend to turn out. "Put yourself in a situation where you can slow down and do some hard thinking, some deep thinking about what's actually going to make your product unique. Going fast can actually slow you down in the long run." This flies in the face of the "move fast and break things" ethos and instead argues that deliberate, focused thinking time is a competitive advantage precisely because most teams skip it.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jake-knapp-john-zeratsky-20.md]]*

**The Basics Are Embarrassingly Simple — But Every Co-Founder Has a Different Answer**
Knapp observes that when teams start the Foundation Sprint, everyone has a different answer to basic questions like "who is your target customer?" and "what problem are you solving?" After working with hundreds of teams, they've found this is the single most common failure mode: teams don't agree on the basics. The Foundation Sprint forces alignment by having the decision-maker lock in one answer per question, creating clarity and confidence. This prevents the "three co-founders have three different answers" dynamic that kills alignment.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jake-knapp-john-zeratsky-20.md]]*

**Test Your Hypothesis Before Building — Don't Just Build and Hope**
After the Foundation Sprint, Knapp and Zeratsky recommend 2-3 weeks of Design Sprints to run experiments that test the Founding Hypothesis. "You have the chance to be wrong about your hypothesis — and that's the whole point." Each week-long Design Sprint produces a tested prototype that reveals whether you're on the right track. They frame this as "the missing manual for founders and product teams" because most teams skip directly to building without ever pressure-testing their core assumptions against real customer feedback.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jake-knapp-john-zeratsky-20.md]]*

**The Design Sprint Was Born From Survival Mode — and That's Why It Works**
Knapp shares the origin story: at Google, his side project was going nowhere for three years until his Stockholm office was at risk of being shut down. With survival pressure, the team cleared one week, built a prototype, and created what became Google Meet. "The notion that maybe you could repeat that and recreate that is what led me to create the Design Sprint." The lesson: extreme constraints and focus — clearing your calendar, turning off Slack and email — are what enable breakthrough clarity. The Design Sprint formalizes what happens naturally under existential threat.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jake-knapp-john-zeratsky-20.md]]*
