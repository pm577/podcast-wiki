# Jackie Bavaro

**Role:** Author of *Cracking the PM Interview* and *Cracking the PM Career*; former Head of Product Management at Asana (first PM hire, 8 years); former PM at Google (APM program) and Microsoft  
**Known for:** Writing two of the most influential books in product management; deep expertise in product strategy; pioneering PM career development content.

## Key Views

### 1. Strategy Has Three Components: Vision, Framework, and Roadmap

Jackie's hard-won definition of strategy (learned through multiple iterations of getting it wrong): **(1) Vision** — the inspiring picture of the future. **(2) Strategic Framework** — the market you're going after, what success looks like, and your "big bets" (pillars) that represent your unique view on how to win. **(3) Roadmap** — working backwards from the vision to pressure-test feasibility. A roadmap is "not a commitment — it's a way to double-check if your plan makes any sense." Most strategies fail because they lack the connecting dots between the high-level goal and the specific features.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jackie-bavaro.md]

> "A good strategy is all about connecting the dots. Connecting the dots from this high level business goal to 'this is the feature we're going to do.' And it might have many, many dots in between." — Jackie Bavaro

### 2. Say Yes to Everything for Two Weeks — The Counterintuitive PM Hack

Early in her Asana career, Jackie developed a defensive "say no to everyone" mindset. Her coach challenged her to **say yes to everything for two weeks**. The result: she discovered that saying "yes, that's a real problem" or "yes, let's test that" opened up genuine collaboration without compromising her priorities. She realized that interpreting others' ideas as "sharing a real problem" rather than "telling me how to do my job" transformed her effectiveness. This became a foundational lesson in stakeholder collaboration.

### 3. Management Isn't the Only Path — Senior PM Compensation Is Excellent

Jackie challenges the assumption that career growth means people management. Using data from levels.fyi, she notes that Senior PM compensation at top tech companies matches doctors and lawyers. "You can make a lot of money... much more than you can in a lot of startups, just by being on the IC path." The IC path works best for platform teams, partnerships, and problems small enough for one PM to own entirely. She advocates treating management as a "two-way door" — try it, and if it's not for you, go back to IC.

### 4. Being a Manager Is Lonely and Painful — But Worth Trying

Jackie is candid about the downsides of product leadership: "Being a manager is not as much fun as being an IC. It's a lonely job." The burden of confidential information (backfills, performance issues, compensation constraints) creates tension between transparency and the authenticity that made her effective as a PM. Her way through: build great teams that don't need you, then leave when you feel "a surge of energy" thinking about what's next.

### 5. Getting Into the Room — How to Create Your Own Growth Opportunities

Jackie's story of joining Asana's planning committee illustrates how to advocate for growth without overstepping. Instead of demanding a seat, she framed it as helping her boss: "Hey, you have your planning committee today. Here are the three questions I want you to go in and get answered. And if they say this, I want you to ask this and this." Then: "It might be easier if I just go, I might be able to save you some time." This earned her a front-row seat to company-building decisions and transformed her understanding from features to strategy to business.
