# Geoffrey Moore

Geoffrey Moore is the author of *Crossing the Chasm*, one of the most influential books on go-to-market strategy ever written. His frameworks on technology adoption lifecycles, beachhead markets, and ecosystem dynamics have shaped how generations of tech companies bring disruptive products to market.

## Key Views

### Start a Fire in One Place, Not Many

Moore's core metaphor: lighting a fire by running a match under a log doesn't work. You need kindling — a small, concentrated segment — and you hold the match in one place until the fire starts. This is why the beachhead strategy is essential. Companies fail when they try to serve multiple segments simultaneously, spreading their limited resources too thin to achieve market leadership anywhere.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/geoffrey-moore.md]

### Ecosystems Form Around Market Leaders

The reason to dominate a single segment is that partner ecosystems — the VARs, systems integrators, and complementary product companies that make a market — only form around category leaders. "If you're a category leader, if you're Oracle in databases, you're 40 years in. You're still the leader, because the ecosystem organized around you." For startups, the only way to attract ecosystem partners is to be a big fish in a small pond.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/geoffrey-moore.md]

### The Fish-to-Pond Ratio

Moore's practical heuristic: your initial target segment should be one where, if you hit your high-growth projections over the next two years, you could capture 30-50% market share. That concentration signals to partners: "If we're going to serve that segment, we got to work with these guys." Without that ratio, you remain invisible and undifferentiated.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/geoffrey-moore.md]

### Pragmatists Buy What Peers Buy

The key insight of the chasm: early adopters (visionaries) buy because "we believe what you believe" — they share your vision for the future. Mainstream customers (pragmatists) buy because "we need what you have" — they want proven, referenceable solutions. The chasm exists because these two groups have completely different buying criteria. Pragmatists won't buy without seeing three to five peer companies already using your product.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/geoffrey-moore.md]

### Frameworks Describe Patterns; They Don't Prescribe Actions

Moore is clear that his frameworks are conceptual models for recognizing patterns in emerging markets. "In disruptive innovation, there's no history. You're always projecting a possible history, and then seeing if you can make it come true." The frameworks give founders a lens for seeing what's happening and making better bets — they don't replace judgment. The most common mistake is applying the framework mechanically without adapting it to your specific context.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/geoffrey-moore.md]

## Notable Quotes

> "The tendency when you're in the chasm is, 'I just need more customers. I should take any customer I could find.' It's like taking a match and running it back and forth under a log."

> "If you're running in New Hampshire, votes in Vermont do not count."

> "After the chasm, customers say, 'I'm not sure about that, but we need what you have.'"
