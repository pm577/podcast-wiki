# Paul Adams

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at Intercom for over 10 years; former Global Head of Brand Design at Facebook, user researcher at Google, product designer at Dyson; started his career as an automotive interior designer  
**Known for:** Long-tenured CPO who has navigated Intercom through the AI transformation; frameworks for rethinking product strategy in the age of AI; uniquely diverse background spanning design, research, and product leadership.

## Key Views

### 1. AI Is a Meteor — Map Your Product Against What AI Can Do

Paul's framework for AI strategy: treat AI like a meteor coming toward your industry, not a gradual evolution. Start with your product's core premise — "Why do people use this? What problem does it solve?" — and then honestly assess: **Can AI do that?** For many products, the answer is "yes, it can" — meaning AI will replace your core function entirely. For others, AI will augment the experience. But you must be brutally honest with yourself. If your product's core value can be delivered by a language model, you need to either find a defensible position (proprietary data, workflow integration, vertical expertise) or accept that your product category is being transformed. Intercom went through this exercise and concluded that AI could handle most Tier 1 customer support — so they built it themselves rather than waiting to be disrupted.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/paul-adams.md]

> "This is a meteor coming towards you. This is going to radically transform society. Start with the thing your product does. Then ask, 'Can AI do that?' For a lot, the answer is going to be 'Yes.'" — Paul Adams

### 2. Build from a Place of User Need, Not Fear

Paul's most painful lesson from his time at Google working on Google Buzz, Google Wave, and Google Plus: these projects were motivated by **fear of Facebook** rather than genuine user insight. When companies build from fear — "We need to compete with X" — they create products that lack soul and fail to connect with users. In contrast, Google Maps and Android succeeded because they were solving real problems people had (getting directions, making smartphones accessible). The lesson: check your motivations. If your product strategy is driven by competitive anxiety rather than deep user empathy, you're likely building something doomed to fail. Paul applies this lesson at Intercom: every product decision starts with "What do our customers actually struggle with?" not "What is our competitor doing?"^[raw/transcripts/lenny/paul-adams.md]

> "A lot of the motivation for those projects came from a place of fear — not from 'Let's make a great product for people.' I learned how not to lead." — Paul Adams (on Google's failed social products)

### 3. The Failure That Freed Him — Freezing On Stage

Paul shares the story of freezing completely during a keynote at Cannes (the world's biggest advertising festival) in front of thousands of people. He had rehearsed word-for-word (which he never normally did), forgot everything, walked off stage mid-talk, and was still mic'd up when he cursed. The surprising outcome: he walked back on, disarmed and authentic, gave the best version of the talk, and became the talk of the festival that night. The lesson: **failure, even spectacular public failure, is survivable and can be transformative**. Since then, every time he walks on stage he feels that tiny seed of doubt — but it's never happened again, and that experience gave him permission to be more authentic and less polished in all his communications.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/paul-adams.md]

### 4. Formative Research: The Hidden Work That Creates Breakthrough Products

At Google, Paul carved out time for **formative research** within tactical usability studies. While the team wanted quick answers about whether people could use their products, Paul spent extra time mapping users' complete social networks — all their relationships, communication channels, what worked and what didn't. This 20-minute hidden exercise, repeated across dozens of users over 18 months, revealed the recurring pattern: people desperately needed better ways to communicate with **small groups of family and friends**. This insight was the seed for what became Google Circles in Google Plus — but the product was built from fear, not that insight, so it failed anyway. The lesson: deep user research is invaluable, but only if the organization is built to act on it with authentic motivation.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/paul-adams.md]

### 5. The Shape of an Effective Product Organization

Paul's philosophy on product organization: avoid the trap of process for process's sake. A healthy product org has three elements: (1) **Clear ownership** — every area has a single accountable person who can say no; (2) **Thin layers of management** — too many layers create distance from users and slow decision-making; (3) **Embedded specialists** — designers, researchers, and data scientists sit within product teams, not in centralized service orgs. The CPO's job is to create the conditions for great product decisions to happen at the lowest possible level, not to make all the decisions themselves. Paul believes strongly that product leaders should have design or research backgrounds because understanding how users experience your product is fundamental to making good strategic calls.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/paul-adams.md]
