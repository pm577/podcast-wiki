# Shreyas Doshi

Shreyas Doshi appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss product management frameworks, lno, and high-agency teams.

## Key Views

**Pre-mortems uncover hidden risks before they become disasters** — Instead of waiting for a post-mortem, gather your team early and ask them to imagine the project has already failed, then work backwards. The prompt gives people psychological safety to raise concerns they'd otherwise suppress. "If you do a pre-mortem right, you will not have to do an ugly post-mortem." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shreyas-doshi.md]

**The LNO Framework — not all tasks are created equal** — Categorize your work into Leverage (10x+ return), Neutral (~1x return), and Overhead (<1x return) tasks. Stop treating every item on your to-do list with the same perfectionism. "As a product manager or as anybody in a creative high impact high leverage role, all your tasks are not created equal, there are actually three type of tasks..." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shreyas-doshi.md]

**The three levels of product work — Impact, Execution, and Optics** — Conflicts on product teams often arise because people default to different levels. A PM fixated on execution clashes with a CEO thinking about impact, and neither realizes the mismatch. "This idea that there are three levels of product work, impact, execution, and optics... it explains a lot of what you see on product teams and organizations in general." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shreyas-doshi.md]

**Most execution problems are actually strategy or culture problems** — When teams are misaligned and execution suffers, the root cause is usually not execution at all — it's a missing strategy, a culture that over-optimizes for individual OKRs, or interpersonal friction between leaders. "Most execution problems that I encounter in a high performing environment where everybody has the right intentions are actually not execution problems, they are either strategy problems or interpersonal problems or cultural problems." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shreyas-doshi.md]

**Minimize opportunity cost, not maximize ROI** — In high-leverage roles, you can find hundreds of positive-ROI tasks — but that doesn't mean you should do them. The ROI formula tempts you toward quick wins. Instead, ask: "Is this the best use of my time?" "You should stop doing work that simply provides a positive return on investment, ROI. And you should start focusing on work that minimizes opportunity cost." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shreyas-doshi.md]

# Shreyas Doshi

Shreyas Doshi is one of the most respected product management thinkers in tech, having PM'd at Stripe, Twitter, Google, and Yahoo over a 20-year career. He's known for his deeply practical frameworks on product strategy, psychology, leadership, and the craft of product management.

## Key Views

**Ask yourself "why am I so busy?" — and be honest about the answer.** Shreyas spent 16-17 years of his PM career incredibly busy, stressed, and dissatisfied despite doing good work. He realized that chronic busyness often masks deeper issues: saying yes to things that don't matter, not trusting your team, or avoiding difficult prioritization conversations. The stress manifested physically in back pains and other health issues that were actually stress-related.

**Product leaders need to build taste, not just frameworks.** Shreyas emphasizes that developing good taste — the ability to instinctively know what makes a great product experience — is central to becoming a strong PM. This comes from deep exposure to great products, honest reflection on what works and why, and a willingness to develop your own point of view rather than just applying frameworks mechanically.

**You're probably not listening as well as you think you are.** One of Shreyas' core insights is that most PMs overestimate their listening ability. True listening means being open to being wrong, letting go of your own agenda in a conversation, and creating space for others to contribute. He considers this one of the highest-leverage skills a PM can develop.

**The job is frustrating by design — embrace it.** PM is inherently a role with more responsibility than authority. Shreyas believes that accepting this frustration rather than fighting it is key to longevity in the role. The product management "life full of suffering" he describes is actually a source of growth — if you can learn from it rather than be consumed by it.

**Use questions to discover your blind spots.** Rather than seeking answers, Shreyas advocates asking yourself the right questions early in your career. The questions he wishes he'd asked himself sooner include: Why am I so busy? What am I avoiding? What would I do if I weren't afraid? These questions surface assumptions and beliefs that keep PMs stuck in counterproductive patterns.
