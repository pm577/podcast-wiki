# Hilary Gridley

Head of Core Product at WHOOP, previously Senior Director of Product at Big Health and product marketing at Dropbox. Author of the widely-read newsletter "How to Become a Super Manager with AI." Known for integrating cognitive behavioral therapy principles into product management and leadership.

## Key Views

### 1. Teach Teams to "Take a Punch"

The core skill for modern product leaders is helping people handle adversity — taking a punch. When something goes wrong, people naturally want to litigate past events and correct others' impressions of them. Instead, Gridley teaches: focus on one small action that **counter-programs** the narrative you're afraid someone has of you. Don't defend, demonstrate. "What is one thing you can do that demonstrates the opposite of what you think this person thinks of you?" This shifts people from rumination to agency.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hilary-gridley.md]

### 2. Behavioral Activation: Act First, Feel Better Later

Borrowed from cognitive behavioral therapy, the principle is that waiting to feel better before acting is a trap. Depression is characterized by "I'll feel better, then I'll act" — the therapeutic insight is the reverse: "I will act, and then I will feel better." Managers should help team members identify small, concrete actions that reliably interrupt negative spirals. Gridley keeps a personal list of behavioral activations on her phone.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hilary-gridley.md]

### 3. Build Mental Models of How Leaders Think

Instead of just reading strategy documents, help your team understand **how** executives think — their mental models, risk tolerance, and what they optimize for. Gridley sends weekly Slack rundowns: "Here are the most interesting conversations I had this week. Here's what they said verbatim. Here's what I interpret that as." This artifact-based communication builds organizational alignment and reduces the need for approvals because teams can predict how decisions will land.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hilary-gridley.md]

### 4. The "Magic Questions" Technique

To understand someone's mental model, avoid open-ended questions. Instead, make a statement and end with "Do you agree?" This forces you to form a hypothesis, lets the other person calibrate you quickly, and trains your own judgment over time. "Rephrase 'What do you think?' as 'Tell me what you think you could have done differently' and then ask me if I agree." This builds self-sufficiency rather than dependency.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hilary-gridley.md]

### 5. Habit Formation Through Reward Loops, Not Education

Most managers approach AI upskilling as an education problem (teach → assess → hold accountable). Gridley thinks of it as a habit formation problem: consistency (start tiny, do daily), friction reduction (use non-work use cases first), and powerful reward loops (immediate, emotional, not just informational). Her "30 Days of GPT" program makes using LLMs feel rewarding, not daunting.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hilary-gridley.md]

### 6. You Are Not the Protagonist at Work

"People think the game is all about influencing the CEO... but in the story of work, you are probably not the protagonist." Gridley argues that trying to be the protagonist causes friction and burnout. Instead, understand the CEO's vision and figure out how to operationalize it. Fulfillment comes from knowing "if someone else were in this job, the product would be different because I was here" — finding the micro-places where you can zig while others zag.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hilary-gridley.md]
