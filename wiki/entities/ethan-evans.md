# Ethan Evans

Ethan Evans is a retired VP at Amazon, executive coach, and course creator focused on helping leaders grow into executives. During his 15 years at Amazon, he helped invent and run Prime Video, the Amazon Appstore, Prime Gaming, and Twitch Commerce (a billion-dollar business). He holds over 70 patents and helped draft one of Amazon's 14 leadership principles.

## Key Views

### The Magic Loop: A Five-Step Career Growth Framework

Ethan's most popular framework has five steps: (1) Do your current job well — you can't grow if your manager wishes you were different; (2) Ask your boss how you can help — most people never do this, and it immediately sets you apart; (3) Go do what they asked; (4) Tell them what you accomplished; (5) Repeat. The loop builds a relationship where you're seen as a partner in your manager's success, not just a task-executor. This was the 6th most popular post in Lenny's newsletter history.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ethan-evans.md]

### Invention Doesn't Require Constant Ideation

Ethan's counterintuitive insight from Amazon: "People think invention takes all this time, but you only need two hours once a month." The key is that once you have one good idea, it often takes years to express it. Amazon's Kindle, Prime, and AWS were single ideas that took decades to fully realize. Jeff Bezos and Amazon were seen as highly inventive despite having relatively few core ideas — they just executed them relentlessly over many years.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ethan-evans.md]

### The "Worse First" Principle

"Everything that you want is on the other side of worse first." Want a better body? The first gym sessions aren't fun. Want career growth? The first difficult conversation or new skill is uncomfortable. Ethan observes that most people plateau because they optimize for tomorrow's comfort rather than enduring short-term pain for long-term gain. The ability to tolerate "worse first" is a differentiating trait in careers.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ethan-evans.md]

### Know When to Quit — But Not Too Early

Ethan advises that the right time to quit something is when you can no longer learn from it or it's taking you in a direction misaligned with your values. But the timing must be calibrated against the "worse first" principle — initial discomfort isn't a reason to quit. The distinction: temporary pain that leads to growth vs. sustained misery that drains you. This discernment is a key executive skill.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ethan-evans.md]

### Amazon's Leadership Principles Are Decision-Making Tools

Having helped draft one of Amazon's 14 leadership principles, Ethan sees them not as cultural platitudes but as practical decision-making frameworks. Principles like "Bias for Action," "Dive Deep," and "Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit" were designed to resolve specific tensions in how Amazon operates. They replace the need for thousands of pages of policy with a set of decision heuristics that any employee can apply.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ethan-evans.md]
