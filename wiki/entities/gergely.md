# Gergely Orosz

**Role:** Creator of The Pragmatic Engineer — the #1 technology newsletter on Substack; former Senior Engineering Manager at Uber  
**Known for:** Leaving a $330K/year tech job at Uber to build a paid newsletter that now exceeds his previous compensation; writing deeply researched articles on software engineering practices, big tech culture, and industry trends.

## Key Views

### 1. Leaving Big Tech for Indie Writing — The "Fisherman's Dilemma"

Gergely's decision to leave Uber was triggered by COVID layoffs that shattered his sense of security. His original plan was to start a venture-backed startup, but while writing a book, he discovered that self-published technical books could make meaningful income (~$100K in year one). He then realized he was pursuing a startup exit to eventually do what he already was doing — writing and sharing knowledge. This "fisherman's story" (working hard to eventually buy the freedom to do what you already enjoy) led him to skip the startup path entirely and go all in on a paid newsletter.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/gergely.md]

> "I was thinking to myself, so hold on. I would go off and do this for like ten years, because that's how much you need to plan to do it. I need to stop doing what I'm doing right now, because I would owe it to my investors and my team to not spend all day on the internet writing about stuff." — Gergely Orosz

### 2. The Newsletter as a "One-Person Business" — Not Creator Economy

Gergely frames his newsletter not as "content creation" but as a serious one-person business. He produces 2 deep-dive posts per week (5,000-6,000 words each), each requiring days of research, drafting, and professional editing. His growth trajectory: 50K subscribers in the first 9 months, then accelerating to 189K (as of this episode) driven by Substack recommendations. A "single digit percentage" of subscribers pay — enough that his income now exceeds his Uber compensation, without a cap.

### 3. The Power of External Constraints and Deadlines

The #1 lesson from Gergely's transition: without external structure, productivity collapses. He went from a "star employee" who always delivered to struggling with unstructured time when working for himself. His solutions: **(1)** Publicly commit to a schedule (he promises two posts/week) — the fear of letting down paying subscribers creates accountability. **(2)** Use a 20-minute timer and block distracting sites via a custom script. **(3)** Model your workflow after journalistic deadlines — even Washington Post investigative journalists produce only ~1 deep article per month.

### 4. The Real Downsides of Full-Time Newslettering

Gergely is candid about the trade-offs: **(1)** Loneliness — he misses the camaraderie of a team and compensates with co-working spaces. **(2)** Constant pressure — thousands of paying subscribers expect quality every week, forever. **(3)** No exit — unlike a startup, a newsletter is personally tied to you; you can't sell it and walk away. **(4)** Lack of external validation — no performance reviews, no promotions, just subscriber counts that alternately motivate and stress.

### 5. The Substack Recommendation Engine as a Growth Accelerator

Gergely attributes his rapid growth acceleration (from 50K to 189K) primarily to Substack's recommendation system. Once you're a top publication, the platform's discovery features create a virtuous growth cycle. This parallels Lenny's observation that newsletter success often feels like "instead of one boss, I have thousands of micro bosses" — with the upside that a single great article can effectively "give yourself a raise" as new paid subscribers flow in.
