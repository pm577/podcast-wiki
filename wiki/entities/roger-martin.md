# Roger Martin

One of the world's leading strategy advisors, author of *Playing to Win* (alongside A.G. Lafley), and former dean of the Rotman School of Management. Named the world's #1 management thinker by Thinkers50 in 2017. Known for the Strategy Choice Cascade framework.

## Key Views

### 1. The Strategy Choice Cascade — Five Questions

Strategy is a set of five integrated choices: (1) **What is your winning aspiration?** (where will you play to win?), (2) **Where will you play?** (which customers, channels, geographies?), (3) **How will you win?** (what's your competitive advantage?), (4) **What capabilities must you have?** (what unique strengths do you need?), (5) **What management systems are needed?** (how do you enable it?). Each answer constrains the next, creating a coherent cascade.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/roger-martin.md]

### 2. There Are Only Two Ways to Win: Differentiate or Be Low Cost

"You have to be either differentiated or low cost — there's no way to protect yourself if you're not one of those two." This Porter-esque insight is central to Martin's framework. If you try to be both, you end up stuck in the middle, defenseless against competitors who focus on one approach. Every choice in the cascade must reinforce which of these two paths you're on.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/roger-martin.md]

### 3. Business Schools Teach Strategy Poorly

"What's taught now in business schools generally sucks. People aren't prepared educationally, and they sure don't get prepared for it in companies." Strategy is "intellectually challenging and emotionally intimidating." Most business education avoids the hard trade-offs that real strategy requires, teaching frameworks that produce goals and vision statements instead of actual strategic choices.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/roger-martin.md]

### 4. There Is No "Natural Strategist" — Great Strategists Practice

"I have never met this mythical beast called a great natural strategist. Great strategists have all one thing in common: they just practice." Strategy is a skill developed through repeated application of the choice cascade framework, learning from both successes and failures. It's not an innate gift but a discipline that anyone can develop with deliberate practice.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/roger-martin.md]

### 5. Strategy Is for Everyone, Not Just CEOs

Martin's framework works for any level — product managers, designers, engineers. The questions scale: "What's my winning aspiration for this project? Where will my team play? How can we win?" The same logic that guides a company can guide a product team, a feature, or even an individual's career. The cascade forces clarity at every level.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/roger-martin.md]
