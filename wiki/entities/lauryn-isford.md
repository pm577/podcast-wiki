# Lauryn Isford

**Role:** Growth leader; most recently Head of Growth at Airtable; previously growth roles at other top tech companies  
**Known for:** Deep expertise in onboarding and activation metrics; the "reverse trial" pricing model; frameworks for operationalizing activation; changing north star metrics dynamically as the business evolves.

## Key Views

### 1. Activation Metrics Should Be Specific, Not Broad

Lauryn's counterintuitive advice: a good activation metric should have a low percentage (5-15% of users) because that means it's highly predictive of long-term retention. If 40% of users hit your activation metric, it's too easy and won't correlate well with lasting value. The goal is to find the precise behavior that separates retained users from churned ones — even if only a small fraction currently reach it — then work to expand that fraction.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

> "I would much prefer to pick a more specific, more precise metric that maybe only 5% of users reach, but know that those 5% of users will be with us for the long haul." — Lauryn Isford

### 2. Use a Suite of Activation Metrics, Not a Single Number

Rather than one north star metric, Lauryn recommends a small suite of complementary metrics. At Airtable, she used week-four multi-user active (team-level activation), week-two user retention (individual retention), and "Build" (a sophistication score measuring intermediate-level product use). This freed the team to make progress on multiple dimensions of onboarding without being constrained by one metric that might not capture the full picture.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

> "This group of metrics actually set us free. It gave us constraints where we could measure success in a few different ways and think about activation as being more complicated than just a single metric." — Lauryn Isford

### 3. Bidirectional Activation Analysis: Decompose the Metric

Before rebuilding onboarding, Lauryn's team decomposed week-four multi-user active into all its components: how many teams sign up as teams vs. individuals, how many invite people, how many have active teammates in week four, what "active" means behaviorally. This granular decomposition revealed the specific levers to pull for outsized impact, rather than guessing at what might move the needle.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

> "Doing that really detailed work on the metric helped us understand what levers we could pull to have a really big impact on activation overall." — Lauryn Isford

### 4. North Star Metrics Should Evolve (Change Every 6-12 Months)

Lauryn challenges the notion of a permanent north star metric. At Airtable, the growth org shifted from revenue-focused to user-growth-focused, which delayed monetization but set them up for a decade of growth. Her advice: stability of 6+ months is table stakes for momentum, but past that, be dynamic. Let strategy drive the metric rather than the metric dictating strategy. Draw the funnel, find the biggest opportunity, then build a metric that reflects that strategy.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

> "If you're working on the same metric forever, there's probably some inefficiency in actually chasing the biggest impact for the business." — Lauryn Isford

### 5. The Reverse Trial: Offer Both Freemium and Free Trial

Lauryn's "reverse trial" model: offer both a free trial (full access to premium features for a limited time) and a freemium tier (indefinite free access with limited features). The free trial showcases everything the product can do, while freemium maximizes user growth and brand awareness. This dual approach lets companies prioritize long-term user growth while still converting premium customers — because you only get a few chances to ask for a credit card before losing trust.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

> "If you're in a long game where you're trying to grow a business for a decade or more, you want to get millions onto your product and you can always have that monetization conversation later." — Lauryn Isford
