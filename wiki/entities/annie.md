# Annie Duke

**Role:** Former professional poker player (WSOP bracelet winner, $4M+ in tournament winnings); bestselling author of *Thinking in Bets* and *Quit*; special partner at First Round Capital  
**Known for:** Decision-making frameworks derived from poker, combating resulting, pre-mortems with kill criteria, knowing when to quit.

## Key Views

### 1. Make the implicit explicit — improve decision quality

Annie's foundational insight: intuition isn't crap — it's sometimes right. But if you never make your reasoning explicit, you never get to find out when it's wrong. The core practice of good decision-making is forcing yourself to articulate *why* you believe something, so you can test, challenge, and refine those beliefs. This applies to product decisions, hiring, strategy — every domain.

> "It's so incredibly necessary in improving decision quality to take what's implicit and make it explicit. If you don't make it explicit, then you don't get to find out when it's wrong." — Annie Duke

### 2. Meetings are for discussion, not discovery or decision

Annie's counterintuitive meeting framework: the only thing that's supposed to happen in a meeting is the discussion part. Discovery (gathering information) should happen *before* the meeting. Decision-making should happen *after* the meeting, when people have time to reflect. Meetings where people try to do all three at once are inefficient and produce lower-quality decisions.

> "People generally think the purpose of a meeting is for three things: discover, discuss, decide. The only thing that's ever supposed to happen in a meeting is the discussion part." — Annie Duke

### 3. Pre-mortems need kill criteria to work

Annie's upgrade to the standard pre-mortem: imagining what could go wrong isn't enough. You must set explicit **kill criteria** — specific, observable signals that will trigger a change in course or a quit decision. Without pre-committed kill criteria, teams rationalize away warning signs and stay on failing paths too long.

> "A pre-mortem is great only if you set up kill criteria. Commit to actions that you're going to take if you see those signals." — Annie Duke

### 4. There are no long feedback loops — find the proxy signals

Annie rejects the excuse of "long feedback loops" in product or career decisions. The trick is identifying what's correlated with the desired long-term outcome, even if the outcome itself takes years to materialize. Shorten the loop by measuring proxy signals that predict the outcome you care about.

> "There is no such thing as a long feedback loop. You shorten it by asking: what are the things that are correlated with the outcome that I eventually desire?" — Annie Duke

### 5. If you're thinking about quitting, you've already waited too long

Annie's hard-won lesson from poker: by the time you consciously consider quitting something (a project, a job, a strategy), the evidence has been mounting for a while. Our bias toward sunk costs, social pressure, and optimism makes us persist past the rational quitting point. The solution: set kill criteria in advance and trust them when they trigger.
