# Ian McAllister

**Role:** Senior Director of Product for Vehicles at Uber; former product leader at Amazon (12 years, created Amazon Smile, led Alexa International) and Airbnb  
**Known for:** Author of the legendary post "What Separates a Top 1% PM from a Top 10% PM"; deep expertise in Amazon's Working Backwards process; building and scaling product teams.

## Key Views

### 1. The Core Skills for New PMs: Communicate, Prioritize, Execute

Ian breaks down the top 1% PM traits across career stages. For new PMs, three skills matter most: **(1) Communicate** — answer first, then explain. Use the "Book of Kim" principles: avoid weasel words, own your problems. Grade yourself after every communication. **(2) Prioritize** — this is "the number one key tool of a PM." A PM with great prioritization generates 5x the impact of one without it. Prioritization applies to themes, projects, features, and personal time management. **(3) Execute** — mold your work into a compact, high-impact package and drive the team forward relentlessly.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ian-mcallister.md]

### 2. For Senior PMs: Think Big, Earn Trust, Drive Impact

As PMs grow, the priority skills shift: **(1) Think Big** — "hunt for bigger elephants." Always ask: could this be bigger? Start with a bigger vision, even if you start small. **(2) Earn Trust** — trust is "the currency of a product leader." Built by repeatedly setting and meeting expectations. Call your shots, forecast accurately, tell the truth without fail, launch when you say you will. **(3) Driven by Impact** — forget politics, promotion, and org size. Wake up every day trying to have the biggest impact possible. "Take two people, one has a massive impact on the business and one who doesn't — the first person should be the one that grows."

> "If you forget about everything else, forget about politics, forget about promotion or having a bigger org or whatever, if you simply wake up every day trying to have the biggest impact you can, that's a really good guiding light." — Ian McAllister

### 3. The Working Backwards Process — Start with the Customer

Ian's core methodology from Amazon: start with a press release and FAQ *before* building anything. The press release must include a **problem paragraph** — Jeff Bezos taught Ian this lesson directly: "If you don't have a problem paragraph, there's not really a problem." The process forces you to work backwards from customer value rather than starting with a solution in mind. Ian learned this while pitching Amazon Smile concepts to Bezos, who would review press releases and provide coaching on the technique.

### 4. Jeff Wilke's Lessons: Teaching, Tough Love, and Operational Rigor

Ian credits Jeff Wilke (former Amazon Consumer CEO) as his most influential mentor. Three lessons: **(1) Teaching the why** — Wilke didn't just make decisions; he taught the mental model behind them, creating lasting impact. **(2) Tough love** — "a little bit of tough love" delivered professionally builds respect. **(3) Operational mindset** — product leadership isn't just about building new things; it's about running what you've built well. The Weekly Business Review (WBR) mechanism cascaded this rigor through the organization.

### 5. The Operator's Manual — Practical Communication Rules

Ian compiled "the Book of Kim" (from his Amazon SVP) and later extended it into "The Operator's Manual" — a set of communication and leadership best practices. Key rules: answer first then explain (don't ramble), use numbers to answer questions, avoid weasel words, own your problems. These were collected from years of observing what effective leaders at Amazon did differently. He emphasizes that communication is a skill you work on your entire career — "the stakes get higher as you work with more senior people."
