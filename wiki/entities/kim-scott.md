# Kim Scott

Kim Scott is the author of Radical Candor, the single most-referenced book on Lenny's Podcast (sold 1M+ copies, 23 languages). She was a member of the faculty at Apple University, led AdSense/YouTube/DoubleClick teams at Google, was a coach at Dropbox/Qualtrics/Twitter, managed a pediatric clinic in Kosovo, and started a diamond-cutting factory in Moscow.

## Key Views

**Radical Candor = Care Personally + Challenge Directly** — The framework is a 2x2: vertical axis is "care personally," horizontal is "challenge directly." Radical Candor is the top-right quadrant. The other three are: Obnoxious Aggression (challenge without care), Ruinous Empathy (care without challenge), and Manipulative Insincerity (neither). Most people land in Ruinous Empathy — they care but are too afraid to give honest feedback. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kim-scott.md]

**Don't Ask "Do You Have Feedback?" — Ask the Right Question** — "What could I do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?" is far more effective than "Do you have any feedback?" The latter invites "everything's fine." The former forces specific, actionable answers. But it must sound authentic to you — if you sound like Kim Scott and not yourself, people won't believe you want the answer. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kim-scott.md]

**Obnoxious Aggression Is Inefficient** — Being a jerk sends people into fight-or-flight mode where they literally cannot hear what you're saying. Beyond the harm it causes, obnoxious aggression wastes everyone's time. And when people realize they've been obnoxiously aggressive, their instinct is often to swing to manipulative insincerity — pretending to agree — which is even worse. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kim-scott.md]

**Ruinous Empathy Is the Most Common Problem** — Obnoxious aggression gets the attention (it makes for good TV), but ruinous empathy is far more prevalent. Most people try to be nice, so they remember to care personally. But they're so worried about hurting feelings that they fail to tell someone something they'd be better off knowing. This is the feedback style that silently kills teams. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kim-scott.md]

**Feedback Is a Gift, Not a Weapon** — The core mental model shift: giving feedback is an act of service, not criticism. If you genuinely care about someone's growth, the kindest thing you can do is tell them the hard truth. Withholding honest feedback because it's uncomfortable is actually selfish — you're protecting your own comfort at the expense of their growth. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kim-scott.md]
