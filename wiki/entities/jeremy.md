# Jeremy Henrickson

Jeremy Henrickson is SVP of Product at Rippling, where he leads product and design teams. Previously, he was Chief Product Officer at Coinbase, overseeing 10x growth during the 2017 crypto boom. He's known for his focus on maintaining velocity at scale, deep domain expertise, and leading teams through extreme uncertainty.

## Key Views

**Small Teams with Clear Missions Are the Universal Truth of Velocity**
Henrickson's first principle for maintaining speed as you scale: break large problems into sufficiently small bits that small groups can attack wholeheartedly with minimal horizontal communication. "If there's 300 people trying to work on one thing, the sheer communication challenges, Dunbar's number — it's really, really hard to act quickly." This is the foundational building block of organizational velocity, and every other optimization depends on getting this right first.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jeremy-henrickson.md]]*

**Dive Deep as a Leader — Don't Just Delegate Hills**
The most counterintuitive advice Henrickson gives: resist the temptation to stay at 30,000 feet as a leader. "It's very tempting to float up here as a leader and say 'Hey, you take that hill over there, you guys do this over here.' When in fact, where you really learn where the challenges are is by just being there with the people in the trenches." He personally picks the hardest or most complicated problem and goes deep on it. This not only surfaces hidden issues but signals to the team that no work is beneath leadership attention.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jeremy-henrickson.md]]*

**Debate Fiercely, Then Commit Completely — Speed Requires Clarity**
At Coinbase during crypto's explosion, the hardest challenge was navigating extreme uncertainty (e.g., "Is Ethereum going to be a thing?"). Henrickson's approach: "You have to be able to have those debates because lots is going on, but then you have to be able to come out of those conversations with a clear company point of view that you're all shooting toward." The key is that while there may still be differing points of view on the margins, you go full speed toward one answer until you deliberately decide to go toward a different one. Disagree-but-commit isn't just a value — it's a velocity mechanism.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jeremy-henrickson.md]]*

**The Crucible Accelerates Learning — Embrace the Hard Times**
Reflecting on Coinbase's 40x growth year, Henrickson acknowledges it was incredibly hard (new daughter, site-breaking Saturday mornings, constant pressure), but it was also the most accelerated period of personal growth in his career. "It's in the crucible of things being hard" that the most learning happens. His advice to those in the thick of it: take a step back and set it in context — "Three, four, five years from now when we look back on this, you realize we did something amazing and we learned a lot."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jeremy-henrickson.md]]*

**Platform Thinking Reduces Decision Complexity for Everyone**
The more you can bake clear, easy-to-use platforms with well-defined interfaces, the simpler the decision space becomes for product teams building on top. "You can't just write a platform and hope it's going to work for the products. It's very much an iterative thing." Investing in the right people who can do both systems thinking and product thinking simultaneously is critical — and it's what enables large organizations to maintain startup-like speed without descending into chaos.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jeremy-henrickson.md]]*
