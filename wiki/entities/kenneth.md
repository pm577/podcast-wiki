# Kenneth Berger

Kenneth Berger was the first product manager at Slack and spent over 10 years in tech before transitioning into executive coaching. He coaches startup leaders on preventing burnout, advocating for their desired lifestyle, and making meaningful impact. His core framework centers on one simple but transformative skill: asking for what you want.

## Key Views

**Ask for What You Want — The Single Most Underused Skill in Your Career**
Berger's entire coaching practice centers on one skill: "Ask for what you want." He's found that the fundamental blocker for most leaders is never articulating their actual desires — whether it's a promotion, a different role, more flexibility, or a different lifestyle. "Turns out when you actually ask for what you want out loud, you're much more likely to get it." The barrier isn't external — it's internal resistance, fear of rejection, and not even knowing what you want.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kenneth-berger.md]]**

**Complaints Contain the Seed of What You Actually Want**
Berger offers a practical technique for figuring out what you want: listen to your complaints. "Every complaint implies a dream." If you're complaining about something, it means you have a vision of how things could be better. Instead of staying in complaint mode, use it as raw material: envision the better future, then figure out an effective way to move toward it. Complaints are not whining — they're signals from your unmet desires.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kenneth-berger.md]]**

**The Two Camps: People-Pleasers and Control Freaks — Neither Asks Effectively**
Berger identifies two common patterns of ineffective asking. People-pleasers don't ask at all — they hope others will read their minds. Control freaks order people around instead of making genuine requests. Both patterns stem from the same root: discomfort with vulnerability. The skill of asking requires being clear about what you want while being open to the answer — including "no." It's a middle path between passive hoping and aggressive demanding.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kenneth-berger.md]]**

**The Most Important Part of Asking Is Hearing the Response**
Berger emphasizes that asking is only half the equation. The real growth comes from how you handle the response. If someone says yes, do you believe them? If someone says no, do you spiral into rejection or use it as data? If someone gives a conditional answer, do you negotiate or retreat? Learning to hear the response — with curiosity rather than defensiveness — is what turns asking into a superpower.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kenneth-berger.md]]**

**Burnout Prevention Requires Sustainable Impact — Not Just Grinding Harder**
Berger's coaching focuses on making high-impact work sustainable. "For me, the impact was about making this work sustainable so that we're not burning out or selling out, but actually able to pursue these hard goals that we have in startups." His framework helps leaders identify where they're over-functioning, where they're not asking for help, and where they're sacrificing their own wellbeing for short-term output — all patterns that lead to burnout regardless of talent or drive.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kenneth-berger.md]]**
