# Brian Tolkin

Brian Tolkin is Head of Product at Opendoor. Previously, he was one of Uber's earliest employees, instrumental in launching UberPool, UberHop, and UberExpress across 60+ cities. He brings a unique product + operations perspective from scaling two of the most operationally intensive tech companies.

## Key Views

### Product-Operations as a Twin-Turbine Engine
Brian describes Uber and Opendoor as businesses requiring both product and operations working together like a "twin-turbine jet plane." You can fly on one engine for a little while, but maximum efficiency requires both working in harmony. Product builds scalable systems; operations fills the gaps and provides real-world feedback. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]

### Operations Experience Makes Better Product Leaders
Having worked in operations before product gave Brian a deep understanding of how the business actually works on the ground. This foundation enables product leaders to build technology solutions that solve real operational problems — rather than building abstract features disconnected from on-the-ground reality. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]

### Scaling Marketplace Dynamics
At both Uber and Opendoor, Brian learned that marketplace businesses require constant balancing of supply and demand. The product+ops integration is essential because operational teams provide the real-time feedback loop that informs product decisions. Without operations, product teams build in a vacuum. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]

### The Importance of City-Level Thinking
At Uber, the city teams (local operations) had different priorities than the central product team. Brian learned that successful product leaders bridge this gap — understanding local dynamics while building globally scalable systems. The "Yes, and..." mindset is critical for integrating central and local perspectives. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]

### Building for the Next Stage Before You're There
Brian emphasizes that scaling companies need to anticipate the next stage of growth. At Uber, they were constantly building infrastructure for the company they would become, not the company they were. This means making architectural decisions that support 10x growth, even when current scale doesn't require it. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]
