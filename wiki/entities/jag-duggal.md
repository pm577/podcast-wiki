# Jag Duggal

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at Nubank ($30B+ neobank, larger than Coinbase + Robinhood + Affirm + SoFi combined); former Director of Product Management at Facebook (monetization of video, news, gaming); former Group PM and Head of Strategy for Display Ads at Google  
**Known for:** Building products that achieve fanatical customer love (80-90% of Nubank's growth is word-of-mouth); pioneering use of the Sean Ellis score as a gating metric; category-defining product strategy.

## Key Views

### 1. "Be Fundamentally Different, Not Incrementally Better"

Jag's core product philosophy: Nubank never aims to be incrementally better — they aim to be fundamentally different. This means redefining the category on dimensions of quality, complexity, and price simultaneously. The goal is "fanatical customer love" — not satisfaction. This is operationalized through Nubank's first cultural value: "We want our customers to love us fanatically." 80-90% of Nubank's growth comes from word of mouth, proving that exceptional products are their own best marketing channel.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jag-duggal.md]

> "We're not trying to be incrementally better, we are trying to be fundamentally different. We want our customers to love us fanatically." — Jag Duggal

### 2. The Sean Ellis Score as a Scaling Gate

Nubank is one of the most disciplined users of the Sean Ellis product-market fit survey. They ask users "How disappointed would you be if this product went away?" and require **50%+ saying "very disappointed"** (adjusted upward from 40% because Brazilians are culturally more polite). They **rarely scale a project** until this threshold is hit. This prevents the classic trap of "taking a small problem and scaling it into a big mess." The method: launch with a small cohort, find the bullseye segment that loves it, understand why, iterate to expand that segment.

### 3. Call 10 Customers — Pick Up the Phone Yourself

Jag's most tactical advice: PMs should call customers directly rather than delegating to researchers. "Call 10 of them. Pick up the phone yourself. By the time you've made your fifth call, you can predict what customers six through 10 will tell you." This eliminates the "degrees of distance" that dilute customer feedback. The goal is raw, unfiltered voice of the customer coming straight to the builders.

### 4. "Good Enough Isn't Good Enough — Is It Great Enough?"

A phrase coined by Nubank's lead designer that Jag adopted: the bar for product quality is not "good enough to launch" but "great enough to generate word-of-mouth." This is particularly important for tenpole products. Combine with: "We are not going to take a small problem and scale it." Nubank uses NPS scores (often in the 70s-90s, and alarm bells ring if they drop even 1-2 points) as a post-launch quality signal.

### 5. Hypothesis-Driven Customer Discovery

Jag's customer research framework: **(1)** Have a crisp hypothesis before talking to customers — otherwise you collect random data with no way to interpret it. **(2)** Don't fall in love with your hypothesis — be a judge, not a lawyer. **(3)** Observe more than ask questions. Ask indirect questions. Sell against your idea — make the customer convince you. Use the Swiffer analogy: P&G invented the Swiffer by observing that people hate mopping, even though customers never said "I need a Swiffer."

# Jag Duggal

Jag Duggal is Chief Product Officer at Nubank, a Brazilian neobank valued at over $30 billion that's bigger than Coinbase, Robinhood, Affirm, and SoFi combined. Previously he was Director of Product at Facebook (monetization of video and third-party content) and SVP of Product at Quantcast, and Group Product Manager at Google. Nubank grew 80-90% through word of mouth — a testament to its fanatical customer focus.

## Key Views

**Build Products People Love Fanatically, Not Just Like**
Duggal's most foundational principle is that Nubank doesn't aim to be incrementally better — they aim to be fundamentally different. The company's first value is "We want our customers to love us fanatically." This isn't just a wall slogan: when hard decisions arise, Nubank biases in favor of the customer's love and willingness to tell their friends. Duggal argues that most companies compromise on this at the edges for short-term revenue or convenience, but the companies that resist that temptation build unbeatable word-of-mouth flywheels. He recommends asking at every product review: "Is this great enough, not just good enough?"
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jag-duggal.md]]*

**Use the Sean Ellis Score as a Scaling Gate — Not a Vanity Metric**
Nubank rarely scales any product until the Sean Ellis score (percent of users who would be "very disappointed" if the product disappeared) hits a compelling threshold. While the standard bar is 40%, Nubank adjusted to 50% because Brazilians are culturally more optimistic. Duggal describes how they segment their Sean Ellis data to find "bullseye cohorts" — small groups where the score hits 70%+ — and then systematically expand those cohorts by building features those power users love. This method turned a struggling bill-pay product from hundreds of thousands of MAUs to over 10 million in 15 months.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jag-duggal.md]]*

**Tap Into Deep, Emotionally Held Pain Points**
Duggal emphasizes that Nubank's success started with identifying a profoundly hated problem: Brazilian banks were among the most profitable and most hated in the world. He recalls a colleague in 1997 pointing to his bank and saying "I hate them" — a sentiment that stuck with him for 20 years until David Vélez reached out about Nubank. The lesson: look for industries where incumbents are both extremely profitable and deeply resented by customers. That combination signals a massive opportunity for a fundamentally different entrant.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jag-duggal.md]]*

**Call Customers Directly — Pick Up the Phone, Don't Delegate Research**
Duggal insists product managers call customers directly instead of delegating to researchers who summarize findings with "many degrees of distance." He tells PMs to pick up the phone and call 10 customers themselves. "Nine times out of 10, by the time you've made your fifth call, you can predict what customers six through 10 are going to tell you." This radically reduces the distance between builders and the voice of the customer, enabling faster iteration cycles and deeper empathy.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jag-duggal.md]]*

**World-Class NPS and Churn Obsession Replace the Marketing Budget**
Nubank tracks NPS maniacally and has recorded scores in the 90s in Mexico — far above typical industry benchmarks. Duggal argues that a product good enough to generate world-beating NPS is itself the best marketing investment. "A lot of VC funding basically goes to Google and Facebook. It is a much better scenario if that's a small portion of what you're doing, and the main portion is investing in making the product great." He is personally maniacal about churn, having built leaky-bucket products earlier in his career, and ensures Nubank's viral loop doesn't have a hole in the bottom.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jag-duggal.md]]*
