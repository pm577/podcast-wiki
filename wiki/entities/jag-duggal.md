# Jag Duggal

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at Nubank ($30B+ neobank, larger than Coinbase + Robinhood + Affirm + SoFi combined); former Director of Product Management at Facebook (monetization of video, news, gaming); former Group PM and Head of Strategy for Display Ads at Google  
**Known for:** Building products that achieve fanatical customer love (80-90% of Nubank's growth is word-of-mouth); pioneering use of the Sean Ellis score as a gating metric; category-defining product strategy.

## Key Views

### 1. "Be Fundamentally Different, Not Incrementally Better"

Jag's core product philosophy: Nubank never aims to be incrementally better — they aim to be fundamentally different. This means redefining the category on dimensions of quality, complexity, and price simultaneously. The goal is "fanatical customer love" — not satisfaction. This is operationalized through Nubank's first cultural value: "We want our customers to love us fanatically." 80-90% of Nubank's growth comes from word of mouth, proving that exceptional products are their own best marketing channel.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jag-duggal.md]

> "We're not trying to be incrementally better, we are trying to be fundamentally different. We want our customers to love us fanatically." — Jag Duggal

### 2. The Sean Ellis Score as a Scaling Gate

Nubank is one of the most disciplined users of the Sean Ellis product-market fit survey. They ask users "How disappointed would you be if this product went away?" and require **50%+ saying "very disappointed"** (adjusted upward from 40% because Brazilians are culturally more polite). They **rarely scale a project** until this threshold is hit. This prevents the classic trap of "taking a small problem and scaling it into a big mess." The method: launch with a small cohort, find the bullseye segment that loves it, understand why, iterate to expand that segment.

### 3. Call 10 Customers — Pick Up the Phone Yourself

Jag's most tactical advice: PMs should call customers directly rather than delegating to researchers. "Call 10 of them. Pick up the phone yourself. By the time you've made your fifth call, you can predict what customers six through 10 will tell you." This eliminates the "degrees of distance" that dilute customer feedback. The goal is raw, unfiltered voice of the customer coming straight to the builders.

### 4. "Good Enough Isn't Good Enough — Is It Great Enough?"

A phrase coined by Nubank's lead designer that Jag adopted: the bar for product quality is not "good enough to launch" but "great enough to generate word-of-mouth." This is particularly important for tenpole products. Combine with: "We are not going to take a small problem and scale it." Nubank uses NPS scores (often in the 70s-90s, and alarm bells ring if they drop even 1-2 points) as a post-launch quality signal.

### 5. Hypothesis-Driven Customer Discovery

Jag's customer research framework: **(1)** Have a crisp hypothesis before talking to customers — otherwise you collect random data with no way to interpret it. **(2)** Don't fall in love with your hypothesis — be a judge, not a lawyer. **(3)** Observe more than ask questions. Ask indirect questions. Sell against your idea — make the customer convince you. Use the Swiffer analogy: P&G invented the Swiffer by observing that people hate mopping, even though customers never said "I need a Swiffer."
