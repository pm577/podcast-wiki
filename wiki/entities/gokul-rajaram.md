# Gokul Rajaram

Gokul Rajaram appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss gokul rajaram (doordash, caviar) on serendipity, product development process, hiring leaders, and angel investing.

## Key Views

**Embrace Serendipity and Pay It Forward** — Great careers aren't built linearly through promotions — they're built by being curious about what else is happening, building relationships, and helping people without expecting immediate returns. Gokul stumbled into Google AdSense by wandering into an office after hours, and later landed at Square through an informal advising relationship. The reservoir of goodwill from paying it forward comes back in unexpected ways. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/gokul-rajaram.md]

**Work at Market Leaders, Not for Titles** — Always choose to work at the leading company in a space even if it means a lower title — the brand halo, quality of talent, and network effects that accrue from being at a winner far outweigh the status of a senior title at a lagging company. Gokul argues you'd rather be an IC PM at Google than VP of Product at Yahoo. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/gokul-rajaram.md]

**Hire Your First PM from Within** — The first product manager should be an internal hire (engineer, analyst, or designer who already works there) because they already have the trust of both the founder and the engineering team. An external PM brought in with a completely different process often faces "organ rejection." Hire a PM when you have 8-10 engineers and the founder can no longer effectively care and feed that expensive resource. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/gokul-rajaram.md]

**Products Must Be Remarkable Enough for Organic Growth** — A healthy product has 40-50% of new customers coming from organic channels. If 90% come from paid acquisition, the music will eventually stop. The product must be truly remarkable — better along dimensions that matter, even if worse along others — so that people naturally talk about it and bring others in. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/gokul-rajaram.md]

**Hire Lieutenants, Not Heads, from Best-in-Class Companies** — When hiring senior leaders, don't go after the head of a function at a great company — go after the person reporting to them (the lieutenant), or even the lieutenant's lieutenant. Identify 3-4 companies that are best-in-class at the function you need (not your competitors), map their org chart, and recruit from the next tier down. Gokul saw this happen to Square post-IPO and weaponized it in reverse. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/gokul-rajaram.md]
