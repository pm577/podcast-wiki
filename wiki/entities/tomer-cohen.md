# Tomer Cohen

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at LinkedIn; previously product leadership roles at other top tech companies  
**Known for:** Pioneering LinkedIn's "Full Stack Builder" program — a radical new approach where PMs learn to code with AI; redefining the product manager role for the AI era; driving LinkedIn's product renaissance.

## Key Views

### 1. AI is Disrupting Traditional Product Management — Become a Full Stack Builder

Tomer's central thesis: by 2030, 70% of the skills required for product management will change. Traditional PMs who only write specs and manage backlogs will be replaced by "Full Stack Builders" — PMs who use AI to prototype, code, and ship features themselves. LinkedIn's Full Stack Builder program teaches PMs to build AI-powered features end-to-end. The PM of the future is someone who combines product thinking with the ability to execute using AI tools.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tomer-cohen.md]

> "When we look at the skills required to do your job, by 2030, it will change by 70%. Whether or not you're looking to change your job, your job is changing." — Tomer Cohen

### 2. The Product Manager as a "T-Shaped" Builder

Tomer reimagines the PM role as much more hands-on. Instead of being a pure strategist who hands off specs to engineers, the AI-era PM prototypes ideas, validates them with real users, and iterates rapidly — all with AI as their coding partner. This doesn't mean every PM becomes a senior engineer, but they need to be comfortable enough with AI coding tools to build working prototypes and ship simple features. This dramatically shortens the feedback loop between idea and learning.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tomer-cohen.md]

> "The PM who can prototype their own ideas using AI will have a massive advantage. They can validate or invalidate hypotheses in days instead of sprints." — Tomer Cohen

### 3. LinkedIn's Product Turnaround: Focus on Member Value First

Tomer describes LinkedIn's product strategy as a return to first principles: put member value before advertiser value. Features like collaborative articles, the new video strategy, and AI-powered job matching all started from the question "does this make members' lives better?" This member-first approach rebuilt trust and engagement, which ultimately created more value for advertisers too. The lesson: in platform businesses, long-term monetization comes from relentless focus on user value.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tomer-cohen.md]

> "The strategy was simple: build things that make members' lives better, and the business results will follow. We stopped optimizing for engagement metrics and started optimizing for member value." — Tomer Cohen

### 4. Product Culture Must Evolve With Technology Waves

Tomer argues that product management has gone through three eras: (1) the era of shipping (2000s) — get features out the door; (2) the era of data (2010s) — A/B test everything; and now (3) the era of AI (2020s) — where PMs must become builders again. Each wave requires different skills, different team structures, and different cultural values. Companies that fail to evolve their product culture with the technology wave get disrupted. LinkedIn deliberately rebuilt its product culture around AI-first thinking.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tomer-cohen.md]

> "Each technology wave redefines what a great PM looks like. The AI wave is the most dramatic shift yet because it collapses the distance between idea and implementation." — Tomer Cohen

### 5. Career Advice: Seek Out "Disequilibrium"

Tomer advises PMs to seek roles where they're slightly out of their depth ("disequilibrium") rather than optimizing for comfort. The best learning and growth happens when you're in a role where the skills that made you successful before don't fully apply, forcing you to develop new ones. He applies this to himself — moving from consumer products to enterprise to AI has been a deliberate pattern of seeking disequilibrium.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tomer-cohen.md]

> "If you feel like you've mastered your role, that's the signal to find the next challenge. The most interesting careers are sequences of being uncomfortable." — Tomer Cohen

### 6. **Curation Beats Volume — Feeding AI the Right Data Trumps Feeding It All Data** — Just giving an AI agent access to your entire knowledge base causes rampant hallucination. Tomer learned he had to hand-curate gold-standard examples and carefully define context boundaries, a process he compares to manually filtering what makes a good professional post on LinkedIn. You must think specifically about what context and knowledge base to give the AI at each moment.

### 7. **AI Amplifies the Talent Gap — Top Performers Benefit Most** — Contrary to the hope that AI democratizes capability, Tomer's data shows the opposite: the best builders use AI tools the most and get even further ahead. The key trait is a relentless drive to improve craft, which top talent already possesses. LinkedIn's highest-performing builders are the ones using AI-powered tools most intensively and giving the strongest feedback.

### 8. **Off-the-Shelf AI Agents Never Work at Scale — You Must Build Your Own** — Despite trying every major external tool, Tomer found that no agent works out of the box on LinkedIn's unique codebase, design systems, and trust vectors. Every tool required deep customization and often building entirely proprietary agents (trust agent, growth agent, research agent) from internal data. The general solution does not exist yet.

### 9. **Process Complexity, Not the Work Itself, Is the Real Enemy** — Tomer's core diagnosis: product development at scale isn't inherently hard, but organizations have layered on so many substeps (15 research sources, design reviews, privacy reviews, security reviews, micro-specialization across functions) that the process itself blocks velocity. Collapsing that complexity is what the full-stack builder model solves. The work is simple; the process is complex.

### 10. **If You're Waiting for a Reorg, You're Already Too Late** — Tomer actively discourages people from waiting for formal titles, restructures, or official tool rollouts. The critical shift is mindset before structure — individuals should just start building end-to-end with whatever tools are available and prove the model through action. Permission is something you take, not something you receive.



### 11. 'Might Be Wrong but Not Confused' — The Tomerism That Liberates Organizations — Tomer's signature phrase encodes a deep leadership philosophy: get attached to clarity and focus, not to being right or wrong. The first part (clarity of thought) means pushing past the polite masking of disagreement as 'I don't understand' — ask directly: are you disagreeing or misunderstanding? If disagreeing, stop hedging and decide. The second part (clarity of execution) means what you say is a priority must actually match your resourcing. If your top priority is X but most engineers work on Y, you haven't achieved clarity of execution. Teams find this liberating because it frees them from the anxiety of being wrong — they can just move fast together. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-12-04-tomer-cohen.md]

### 12. First-Principles Opinions Have Teeth — Tomer pushes teams to articulate what trade-offs they're actually making, not just what they're building. 'I want to build a simple product' is useless — everyone wants that. 'I'm willing to sacrifice engagement metrics for user trust' is an opinion with teeth. The best product strategies emerge from articulating what you're NOT willing to do. Tomer's framework: have a first-principles opinion, explain what you'd trade off to hold it, and let the organization align around those trade-offs, not around vague aspirations. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-12-04-tomer-cohen.md]
