# Sean Ellis

**Role:** Founder and CEO of GrowthHackers; coined the term "growth hacking"; invented the Sean Ellis Product-Market Fit survey and the ICE prioritization framework; author of *Hacking Growth*  
**Known for:** The "Sean Ellis Test" for product-market fit ("How disappointed would you be if this product disappeared?"); the ICE framework (Impact, Confidence, Ease); pioneering the growth discipline.

## Key Views

### 1. The Sean Ellis Product-Market Fit Test

Sean's most famous contribution: ask users "How disappointed would you be if this product went away?" If 40%+ say "very disappointed," you have product-market fit. This simple question predicts retention and word-of-mouth better than any complex metric. The key insight is that PMF is not about total users or revenue — it's about how core users feel about the product. The 40% threshold has been validated across hundreds of companies.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sean-ellis.md]

> "If you ask 'how disappointed would you be' and 40% say very disappointed, you have product-market fit. That's it. That simple question tells you more than any dashboard." — Sean Ellis

### 2. The ICE Prioritization Framework

Sean created ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease) as a simple scoring system for growth experiments. Each idea gets a 1-10 score on all three dimensions, and the average determines priority. Impact = how big the effect will be; Confidence = how sure you are it'll work; Ease = how fast/cheap to implement. The framework's power is that it forces teams to be honest about uncertainty (Confidence score) rather than just chasing high-impact, low-confidence moonshots.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sean-ellis.md]

> "ICE forces you to confront your uncertainty. It's easy to say an idea has high impact — much harder to admit you have low confidence it'll actually work." — Sean Ellis

### 3. Growth Hacking is About Finding the "Growth Lever"

Sean defines growth hacking not as a set of tactics but as a mindset: find the single highest-leverage experiment that will move your north star metric, run it, learn, repeat. It's not about doing 100 small things — it's about finding the one thing that creates exponential impact. The best growth hackers are relentless about identifying and prioritizing that lever, even when it's not obvious or requires unconventional thinking.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sean-ellis.md]

> "Growth hacking isn't about doing a thousand small experiments. It's about finding the one experiment that unlocks your next growth curve." — Sean Ellis

### 4. The Core Growth Team: Data, Product, Engineering, Marketing

Sean's growth team structure: a cross-functional team with dedicated data (analytics), product, engineering, and marketing resources. The team should have a clear north star metric and the autonomy to run experiments across the full funnel. Each experiment should have a clear hypothesis, a defined success metric, and a time box. The growth team doesn't own features — it owns growth, which means it can touch any part of the product.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sean-ellis.md]

> "A growth team needs four things: a data person, a product person, an engineer, and a marketer. And they need the freedom to change anything in the product." — Sean Ellis

### 5. Before Growth, Verify Product-Market Fit

Sean's strongest warning: don't invest in growth until you have PMF. Premature scaling (applying growth tactics before the product is something people truly want) is the #1 reason startups fail. His framework: (1) Validate the problem, (2) Build the MVP, (3) Measure PMF with the "disappointed" survey, (4) Only then invest in growth. Most famous startup failures weren't growth problems — they were PMF problems that growth couldn't fix.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sean-ellis.md]

> "The biggest mistake startups make is trying to grow before they've confirmed product-market fit. Growth doesn't fix a product people don't truly want." — Sean Ellis
