# Brian Tolkin

**Role:** Head of Product and Design at Opendoor; former early employee (#100) at Uber, where he launched uberPOOL globally, started the Product Operations function  
**Known for:** The "twin turbine" model of product + operations, bringing operational depth into product leadership, and scaling marketplace products that bridge physical and digital worlds.

## Key Views

### 1. Product and operations as twin turbines — not competing engines

Brian's core framework: companies with heavy operational components (Uber, Opendoor) need product and operations to work as "twin turbine jet engines." You can fly on one for a little while, but maximum efficiency comes when both work together. Ops teams can iterate faster, talk to customers more directly, and provide rich qualitative insights. Product builds scalable technology. The tension isn't competition — it's harmony. The birth of the "Product Operations" function at Uber came from this insight: ops insights feeding product priorities, and product capabilities amplifying ops execution.

> "Uber always has this mentality and Opendoor does too — the product and operations, twin turbine jet plane where you can fly the plane on one engine for a little bit if you need to, but it's operating most efficiently and effectively if both are working together." — Brian Tolkin

### 2. Operations is the best training ground for product leaders

Having started on the ops team at Uber, Brian argues that ops experience makes better product leaders. You gain a "really deep understanding of how the business actually works" — you're operating it day in and day out, talking to customers one-on-one, onboarding drivers, responding to support tickets. This ground-level understanding is the perfect foundation for deciding what to build in a scalable technology way. A PM sitting in San Francisco can't understand the nuances of 1,000 cities — but ops people who live in those cities can.

> "Starting on the operations side gave a really deep understanding of how the business actually works. … That foundation actually … is a pretty good foundation for them going on to say, okay, what do we actually want to build in a more scalable technology way?" — Brian Tolkin

### 3. Stay calm under pressure — stress is contagious

Brian's leadership lesson from launching uberPOOL globally: sleeping on the floor in China before launches, but never reflecting stress onto the team. When leaders get stressed, "everybody tenses out," which counterintuitively produces worse outcomes. Calmness under pressure is a leadership superpower — it allows the team to focus, think clearly, and execute even when everything is on the line. This is especially important in marketplace businesses where real-time operations can go wrong in a thousand ways.

> "I've slept on the floor in China before launching uberPOOL, and when you reflect the stress onto your teams, everybody tenses out. It counterintuitively doesn't produce better outcomes." — Brian Tolkin

### 4. The Jobs to Be Done framework in practice at Opendoor

Brian applies JTBD rigorously at Opendoor to understand what customers are really "hiring" the product to do. In real estate, the job isn't "sell my house" — it's things like "I need to move to a new city for a job" or "I need cash to start a business." By understanding the underlying job, Opendoor designs the product experience around that deeper need, not around the surface-level transaction. This framework prevents the team from building features that optimize for the wrong thing.

### 5. Product reviews as a leadership tool

Brian is known for running highly effective product reviews. His approach: create a structured forum where decisions are made transparently, the right people are in the room, and the focus is on outcomes, not outputs. A good product review surfaces disagreements about strategy and priorities early, aligns the leadership team, and gives product teams clear direction. It should feel like a collaborative problem-solving session, not a performance evaluation.
