# Brian Tolkin

Brian Tolkin appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss lessons from scaling uber and opendoor — brian tolkin (head of product at opendoor, ex-uber).

## Key Views

**Product and Operations as Twin Turbine Jet Engines** — Uber and Opendoor operate on the principle that product and ops are like two engines on a jet plane. You can fly on one for a bit, but maximum efficiency requires both working together. Operations teams iterate faster locally and have deep customer insights, while product teams build scalable technology. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]

**The Birth of Product Operations** — Brian helped formalize the product operations function at Uber to bridge the gap between centralized product teams in San Francisco and globally distributed operations teams. The role involved people who reported into operations but physically sat with product teams, creating a bidirectional feedback loop for launching features globally and gathering market input. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]

**Stay Calm Under Pressure — Stress Does Not Produce Better Outcomes** — Having launched uberPOOL in China on near-zero sleep, Brian learned that reflecting stress onto teams makes everyone tense up and produces worse results. His mantras include: 'You are never as good as you think you are, you are never as bad as you think you are.' Maintaining an even keel allows clearer thinking during crises. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]

**Honest Experimentation at Low Volume** — With small transaction volumes (unlike Uber's millions), Opendoor cannot always run canonical A/B tests. Brian advises: run power analysis first to know if you will get significant results; accept that some experiments need 6-month runtimes; use alternatives like difference-in-differences, geo experiments, or 80% confidence thresholds; and sometimes just trust your intuition and ship with a feedback loop. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]

**Product Is Finding the Kernel of Truth in a Sea of Ambiguity** — PMs get signals from everywhere: CS teams, customers, executives, field visits. The core job is understanding what really matters and where technology can be uniquely leveraged. At Uber early on, that meant focusing entirely on dispatching and pricing, not building sophisticated tooling or growth teams. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/brian-tolkin.md]
