# Katie Dill

Katie Dill is Head of Design at Stripe, overseeing product design, brand, marketing creative, user research, content strategy, and design ops. She was previously Head of Design at Lyft and Head of Experience Design at Airbnb. She's built and led design teams through 10x+ growth at three hypergrowth companies.

## Key Views

**Beauty Enhances Functionality — They Are Not Opposites**
Dill pushes back against the tech industry's tendency to treat beauty and functionality as trade-offs. "The use of the word beauty has decreased dramatically. It's aligned with this idea that 'functionality is king.' As if people think about functionality and beauty as opposite things. They're not." Beauty enhances functionality by making things easier to use, more approachable, and more compelling. It also increases trust: painstaking attention to visible details signals that you care about the invisible details too.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/katie-dill.md]]**

**Design Quality Is a System, Not an Event**
At scale, Dill argues you can't rely on heroics or individual brilliance to maintain quality. You need systems: design systems, review processes, quality thresholds, and clear standards. At Stripe, she operationalizes quality through structured design critiques, shared component libraries, and explicit craftsmanship expectations. Quality becomes a habit embedded in the process, not something you check at the end.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/katie-dill.md]]**

**Design Directly Drives Growth — Measure It**
Dill shares examples from Stripe where design changes directly lifted conversion rates. She advocates for treating design as a growth lever, not just a polish layer. Every design decision — from button placement to copy to visual hierarchy — can be measured for its impact on business outcomes. The best design leaders speak the language of metrics and can tie their team's work to revenue, activation, and retention.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/katie-dill.md]]**

**How People Feel About Your Product Matters More Than You Think**
Dill emphasizes that emotional response is a design output that's rarely measured but critically important. "Things that are more beautiful increase trust." The way a product makes someone feel — confident, confused, delighted, anxious — directly influences their willingness to use it, recommend it, and pay for it. Design teams should deliberately craft emotional outcomes and test for them, just as they test for usability and performance.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/katie-dill.md]]**

**The Intervention That Saved Her Leadership — Learning to Listen**
Dill shares a story from Airbnb where her design team staged an intervention because she was too directive. It taught her that great design leadership isn't about having all the answers — it's about creating the conditions for the team to do their best work. She shifted from "telling" to "asking," from "approving" to "coaching." This unlocked far better work from her team and made her a more effective leader.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/katie-dill.md]]**
