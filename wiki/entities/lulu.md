# Lulu Cheng Meservey

Lulu Cheng Meservey is Executive Vice President of Corporate Affairs and Chief Communications Officer at Activision Blizzard. She was previously Head of Communications at Substack, where she became known for taking bold risks and creating outsized attention for the platform. She writes Flack, considered the best newsletter on PR and communications strategy.

## Key Views

### Cultural Erogenous Zones: Find What Your Audience Already Cares About

Lulu's foundational framework: "People have things that they either care about or don't, and you're not going to change that. It's a huge lift to try to change someone's worldview. It's a light lift to take the thing you want to talk about and shape it to fit into their worldview." Her example: Kamala Harris connected national security to K-12 education by pointing out that army enlistees need a 10th-grade reading level — bridging a topic nobody cared about to one everyone cared about.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lulu-cheng-meservey.md]

### Concentric Circles: Spread Ideas from Your Desk Outward

Lulu's framework for building distribution as an underdog: start from your own desk (crisp one-to-three key messages), then expand outward — co-founders, employees, investors, power users, influencers. Substack used this masterfully, bringing power users like Lenny into early product updates, who then became organic evangelists. "If you want to spread something you go out in concentric circles starting from your own desk."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lulu-cheng-meservey.md]

### Mistakes of Commission Over Omission

"When you don't take risks you're letting the status quo win." Lulu argues that for startups, the enemy is the status quo. "If you make a mistake of commission you can observe it, learn from it, and move quickly. If you make mistakes of omission you're not observing, not learning, and not noticing opportunities slip by." Her analogy: sitting in cash feels safe but over time you get poorer in real terms, while investing creates volatility but long-term growth.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lulu-cheng-meservey.md]

### Make Ideas Sticky: Minimize Cognitive Burden

For an idea to spread, "make it something a second-grader could understand. Minimize the cognitive burden on the recipient." Techniques: turn it into a joke, use an analogy, create a vivid mental image, or tell a story instead of using adjectives. "Move fast and break things," "Software is eating the world," and "Put the pill in some cheese" all work because they're immediately understandable and repeatable.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lulu-cheng-meservey.md]

### Build Your Own Distribution as an Underdog

Startups can't rely on mainstream media or institutional backing. Lulu's advice: build your own distribution starting from day one, before you even have a company. Know you're underdog, don't use the GE playbook. Find the centers of gravity (influencers) who will spread your message. The sequence matters: get clear internally, then go to power users, then to the broader audience.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lulu-cheng-meservey.md]
