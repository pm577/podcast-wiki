# Brandon Chu

**Role:** VP of Product at Shopify (led product acceleration); former product leader at FreshBooks; prolific writer on product management  
**Known for:** Deep expertise in platform product management, Shopify's remote work practices ("bursts"), and influential writing on PM skills, decision-making, and career growth.

## Key Views

### 1. The PM's job: "Help teams ship the right thing at the right time in the right way"

Brandon rejects the "CEO of the product" framing. Instead, the PM is a servant leader who helps cross-functional teams make good decisions. At Shopify, everyone — engineers, support, sales — is responsible for product thinking. As PMs grow more senior, their role shifts from balanced collaboration to pure strategic leadership where they make high-conviction bets.

> "Your job is to help teams ship the right thing at the right time in the right way. It's servant leadership. You're not the CEO of the product — everyone's responsible for product thinking." — Brandon Chu

### 2. Writing to think — crystallize ideas through the writing process

Brandon's most influential Medium posts (which he hasn't written since 2018) came from a need to "disambiguate the chaos in his mind" about what his job was. He spent up to 40 hours per post, mostly on editing and gathering feedback. The writing process itself was the vehicle for forming the mental models, not the result of already having them figured out. He recommends sharing raw early drafts fearlessly to learn how narratives land.

> "I figured out everything in those posts at the exact moment I wrote them. The writing process itself allowed me to solidify those mental models. I wasn't ahead of the game — I just wanted to disambiguate the chaos in my mind." — Brandon Chu

### 3. Founder skills separate great PMs from good ones

The hard skills of PM — organization, analysis, communication — are table stakes. To reach the highest levels, PMs need "founder skills": storytelling, motivating people, fostering creative teams, making high-conviction decisions in ambiguity, and leading by example with accountability. Shopify intentionally hires 30–40% ex-founders into its PM team for this reason.

> "Founder skills — being a great storyteller, how to get the most out of people, foster creative teams, make really hard high-conviction decisions, and lead by example with accountability — those are the things that propel careers to the highest level of leadership." — Brandon Chu

### 4. "Bursts" — Shopify's model for remote team connection

Shopify went "digital by default" during COVID and never looked back. To compensate for the loss of in-person connection, they built an internal system for "bursts": teams get together for 3–5 days once a quarter for high-velocity creative work. The company handles all logistics (flights, hotels, food) through an in-house app. Employees can also work from anywhere for up to 90 days a year. The philosophy: optimize for what was amazing about in-person (deep creative work, bonding) and eliminate what wasn't (commute, distractions).

> "We tried to turn all the weaknesses of remote work on its head. If you were just working at your desk and never talking to each other, that wasn't great. So why don't we optimize for the stuff that was amazing about being together?" — Brandon Chu

### 5. Never fall into sunk-cost fallacy — pivot when the world changes

Shopify's product culture is built around the willingness to "throw it all away anytime if the world changes." During COVID, the company went from 40 planned initiatives to just 3 that mattered (gift cards, local pickup, etc.) and shipped them in two weeks. Teams set annual direction but remain fundamentally iterative — constantly asking "is this the most important thing we can do right now?" This cultural resilience requires humility and the absence of ego around plans.

> "We're pretty good at never falling into sunk-cost fallacy. We'll throw it all away anytime if the world changes. It's a really tricky place for a lot of folks because things change fast, but that's how the sausage gets made." — Brandon Chu
