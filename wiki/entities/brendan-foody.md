# Brendan Foody

**Role:** CEO and Co-founder of Mercor, the fastest-growing company in history to go from $1M to $500M in revenue (17 months); youngest American unicorn founder ($2B valuation); started his first business (Donut Dynasty) in 8th grade  
**Known for:** Building the AI evaluation and expert network market, the "era of evals" thesis, and an intense company culture of can-do attitude, high standards, and intensity.

## Key Views

### 1. The era of evals — the most important business opportunity in AI

Brendan's core thesis: the primary bottleneck for AI labs today is not compute or model architecture — it's **evaluation**. The labs need extraordinary professionals who can measure model capabilities and create high-quality training data. As models get more capable, the need for expert evaluators (people who can judge whether a model's output is truly excellent in a specific domain) grows exponentially. Mercor grew from $1M to $400M revenue run rate in 16 months serving this need. The labs are willing to spend "whatever it takes" to improve model capabilities, and the entire economy may become a reinforcement learning training environment.

> "We grew from 1 to 400 million in revenue run rate in 16 months, fastest ascent in history. … The lab's primary bottleneck to improve models is how they can effectively have some way of measuring what success looks like for the model." — Brendan Foody

### 2. Can-do attitude + high standards + intensity — the Mercor culture engine

Brendan's three cultural pillars: **(1) Can-do attitude** — set ridiculously ambitious goals and the trajectory forms around them (told Benchmark they'd go from $1.5M to $50M run rate in a year; hit it within two weeks of the target). **(2) High standards** — incredibly high hiring bar, especially for the first 10 people (half were former heads of functions at major companies). **(3) Intensity** — not mandated hours, but an output-oriented culture where people care deeply and move heaven and earth. These three create a flywheel: ambitious goals attract ambitious people, high standards ensure they're capable, and intensity ensures they execute.

> "If you look at the early cultures of the legendary companies, thinking of Meta or Google, they have these incredible, intense early-stage cultures of people just moving heaven and earth and doing whatever it takes to push the frontier." — Brendan Foody

### 3. Leading indicators in fast-moving markets — don't force product-market fit

Brendan's key insight on finding opportunity: look for **leading indicators** in markets that are changing rapidly. The "why now" isn't just about product capability — it's about new pockets of demand where the wealthiest customers in the world have an urgent, unsolved problem. At Mercor, the signal was AI labs needing expert evaluators — a market that barely existed 18 months ago but was growing exponentially. He advises founders to "sheer it from the market" — if it's difficult to sell to the marginal customer, you're not going to grow a huge business. Find the customer that's surprisingly easy to sell to.

> "If I had to distill it into advice for founders … sometimes you just need to sheer it from the market and know that it's there, the pull, to know the right places to focus." — Brendan Foody

### 4. First 10 hires slow, then accelerate — know when to step on the gas

Brendan's hiring philosophy: for the first ~10 people, be extremely patient and disciplined. The initial talent density shapes the entire organization. Mercor's first US employee was the former head of growth at Scale AI. But once you know the market is real and demand exceeds your capacity, step on the gas. The downside of moving too slowly on the 10→100 transition is leaving growth on the table. The inflection point is when you know "it's really working" — that's when speed becomes the priority over patience.

> "It's determined by the point where you know it's really working. … Once you know that there's so much more demand than you can handle, that's when you want to step on the gas and optimize for speed." — Brendan Foody

### 5. Zero sales and marketing — pure customer obsession

For the first 1.5 years, Mercor had zero people in sales or marketing. 100% of company resources went into building great products and experiences for customers. Growth came entirely from word of mouth — people who worked with Mercor at one lab wanted to keep working with them at the next. Brendan warns founders not to get caught up in marketing before they've figured out what drives "six-star customer experiences." The product and the customer love come first; everything else follows.

> "We've had no one in sales and marketing … because we focused 100% of company resources on how do we build great products and experiences for our customers." — Brendan Foody
