# David Frankel

Co-Founder and Managing Partner of Founder Collective, one of the most successful seed firms of the last decade. Portfolio includes Uber, Coupang, Airtable, Suno, SeatGeek, PillPack (sold to Amazon for ~$1B), and Whoop. Named to the Midas List six times (#11 in 2023, #15 in 2024). Previously co-founder and CEO of Internet Solutions, the largest ISP in Africa, acquired by NTT. Founding board member of Endeavor SA.

## Key Views

### 1. Pro Rata Is the Original Sin in Venture Capital

Frankel argues forcefully that pro rata rights are the "original sin" of venture capital. He believes the obligation to maintain ownership percentages distorts investment decisions and leads to poor fund economics. He explains why seed fund math doesn't work when every investor insists on maintaining their pro rata, and why LPs are increasingly frustrated with VCs over this dynamic.

^[raw/transcripts/20vc/20vc-2024-10-14-20vc-investing-lessons-from-f.md]

### 2. Assess Human Capital Risk, Not Just Product Risk

From his experience scaling PillPack from seed to Amazon acquisition, Frankel emphasizes that investors should spend more time assessing the human capital risk taken by founders. He argues that the founder's ability to take calculated career risks, their psychological resilience, and their capacity to build teams matter more than the initial product thesis.

^[raw/transcripts/20vc/20vc-2018-09-17-20vc-lessons-learned-scaling.md]

### 3. Managing Investor Psychology and Self-Doubt

Frankel opens up about the challenges of building Founder Collective, discussing how to manage investor psychology, self-doubt, and insecurity. He shares the secrets to truly successful venture partnerships and how alignment among general partners is more important than any individual deal.

^[raw/transcripts/20vc/20vc-2022-02-07-20vc-biggest-lessons-and-chal.md]

### 4. Liquidity Has Died and LPs Are Pissed

In his 2024 appearance, Frankel directly addresses the liquidity crisis in venture. He explains why liquidity has effectively died in 2024, why LPs are angry with VCs who can't return capital, and the hard truths about seed fund economics that most in the industry are unwilling to discuss publicly.
