# Sam Lessin

Sam Lessin is a partner at Slow Ventures, former VP of Product at Facebook, and a two-time founder. He has become an unlikely authority on etiquette in Silicon Valley, publishing a book on proper etiquette and teaching classes around the world. His core insight is that as technology becomes more central to business and trust becomes more critical, knowing how to show up in any room with poise and confidence is a superpower.

## Key Views

### Etiquette Is Showing Up With a Low Heart Rate
Sam's central framing: "Etiquette is a skill for how to show up in a room with a low heart rate." When you're at a high-stakes event (Kleiner Perkins holiday party, a key investor meeting), the natural instinct is scarcity mindset — "this is my one shot" — which makes you transactional and high intensity. The goal is to shift to abundance: "This is part of the story, not the entire story." Learning the rules of etiquette lets you stop thinking about the mechanics and focus on building genuine relationships.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-01-15-sam-lessin.md]

### The Deep Truth: Etiquette Builds Trust in a High-Stakes World
Sam argues that etiquette matters more now than ever because of what's at stake. "When technology is no longer some cute sideshow, but it's a major deal, people are worried about losing their jobs. Understanding how to meet people where they're at, build trust, mirror expected behaviors — these are all tools." In a world where software is becoming commoditized, the ability to build trust through social grace is a genuine competitive advantage.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-01-15-sam-lessin.md]

### Double Down on What's at the Intersection of Hilarious and Useful
Sam's philosophy for work and life: find things at the intersection of hilarious and useful. "You need both. You should have fun in life. Also, it's very hard to cut through the noise these days — humor is a great way to cut through it." But humor just for its own sake isn't enough — there needs to be a deeper truth. This is why his etiquette book works: it's genuinely funny to teach Silicon Valley people manners, but the underlying lessons about trust and relationships are profoundly useful.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-01-15-sam-lessin.md]

### Product Differentiation Is Rare — Etiquette Fills the Gap
Sam is realistic about startups: 99.9% don't have the kind of magical product breakthrough that lets you "get away with anything." For everyone else, how you show up matters enormously. "If understanding these rules can be the difference between doing really well and missing a business deal because you show up with too high a heart rate and burn a bunch of mild relationship opportunities — I don't know why you wouldn't want these skills." Etiquette is a force multiplier for any founder or operator.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-01-15-sam-lessin.md]

### Be Early, Don't Order the Most Expensive Thing, Don't Smell Bad
Sam's practical rules for founders are disarmingly simple but widely ignored: show up early, don't order the most expensive item on the menu at a business meal, wear appropriate attire, and have basic hygiene. "I just feel like no one's being honest in teaching founders this." These basics signal that you're someone people can work with and trust — a prerequisite for any business relationship, but one that many technically-oriented founders overlook.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2026-01-15-sam-lessin.md]
