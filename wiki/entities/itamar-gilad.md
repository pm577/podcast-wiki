# Itamar Gilad

**Role:** Product coach, speaker, and author of *Evidence-Guided: Creating High-Impact Products in the Face of Uncertainty*; former product manager at Google (Gmail, YouTube, identity) and Microsoft  
**Known for:** Creating the GIST framework (Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks); pioneering evidence-guided product development; his firsthand experience with the Google+ failure and Gmail tabbed inbox success.

## Key Views

### 1. The Google+ Failure vs. Gmail Tabbed Inbox — A Tale of Two Approaches

Itamar's foundational story: Google+ was a massive bet (~1000 people at its peak) driven by opinion-based decision-making. It failed because no one validated the core assumption that people wanted another social network. In contrast, the Gmail tabbed inbox started as a small idea no one believed in. The team researched users, established user-centric goals, tested rigorously on themselves and external testers, and used data to build the right solution. The result: a feature loved by 85% of Gmail's 1.8B users. The difference? **Evidence guidance** vs. opinion-based development.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/itamar-gilad.md]

> "Google was what I call an evidence guided company... They put a high premium on focusing on customers, coming up with a lot of ideas, looking at the data, looking at how these ideas actually worked out. They weren't shy about launching betas and learning from that." — Itamar Gilad

### 2. The GIST Framework — Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks

Itamar's meta-framework for evidence-guided product development:
- **Goals** — Define where you want to end up. Use a "value exchange loop": North Star metric (value delivered to users) + top KPI (value captured for business). Break these into metrics trees that show how inputs connect to outcomes.
- **Ideas** — Hypotheses for achieving goals. Evaluate using ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease). Confidence is the critical factor often ignored — it forces you to assess how sure you are about your estimates.
- **Steps** — Build-Measure-Learn loops that validate ideas incrementally. Use "fake door tests," smoke tests, and Wizard of Oz experiments before building.
- **Tasks** — The work items in your sprint/kanban board.

GIST is not a new invention but a **meta-framework** that integrates lean startup, design thinking, product discovery, and growth methodologies into one coherent system.

### 3. Evidence Guidance ≠ Data-Driven Decision Making

Itamar distinguishes "evidence-guided" from purely "data-driven." Evidence includes quantitative data, qualitative insights, user research, competitive analysis, and even smart intuition — but it must be *explicit and testable*. His Confidence Meter framework helps teams rate how reliable their evidence is on a scale. The goal isn't to eliminate opinion — it's to *supercharge* human judgment with evidence, and to know how much confidence to place in each decision.

### 4. Telltale Signs You're Not Evidence-Guided

Common warning signs: **(1)** Goals are unclear, vague, or output-focused. **(2)** Missing user-facing metrics — only revenue/business metrics exist. **(3)** Too much time spent on roadmap planning rather than discovery. **(4)** Little experimentation, and when experiments happen, there's no real learning. **(5)** Teams are disengaged — engineers are measured on output and don't care about users or business impact.

### 5. The ICE Framework for Idea Prioritization (and Why Confidence Matters)

Itamar uses the original Sean Ellis version of ICE: **Impact**, **Confidence**, **Ease**. Most teams misuse ICE by treating impact and ease as precise numbers — they're guesstimates. The critical insight: Confidence is the most important dimension because it forces you to bet on ideas where you actually have evidence, not just strongly held opinions. He prefers ICE over RICE because "reach" should be folded into impact. The scores are deliberately simple (high/medium/low) — the real value comes from the team debate, not the arithmetic.
