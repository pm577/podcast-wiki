# Naomi Gleit

**Role:** Head of Product at Meta, employee #29, longest-serving executive at Meta other than Mark Zuckerberg; has scaled with the company from 30 employees to a $1.5T business over nearly 20 years  
**Known for:** Early Facebook growth team member; expertise in simplifying complex product problems; long-tenured product leadership at one of the world's most influential technology companies.

## Key Views

### 1. The Canonical Doc — One Source of Truth

Naomi's foundational productivity principle: every project must have a single canonical doc that everyone knows about. When she ramps up on a new project, she asks five people and gets five different answers — that's unacceptable. The canonical doc is the one place to go for all essential information, with links to supporting material. It eliminates confusion, saves onboarding time, and ensures everyone operates from the same facts. This discipline scales from small teams to 100,000-person organizations.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/naomi-gleit.md]

> "I ask five different people, get five different answers. That is unacceptable. There needs to be one canonical doc everyone knows about — the one place I can go to get all the information I need about a project." — Naomi Gleit

### 2. The PM as Conductor

Naomi's mental model for the product manager role: the PM is the **conductor of an orchestra**. They don't play every instrument, but they ensure all the pieces come together at the right time to create a cohesive outcome. The PM holds the vision, coordinates across engineering, design, data science, research, and marketing, and removes blockers so the team can execute. This means the PM doesn't need to be the smartest person in the room — they need to be the person who ensures all the smart people are working in harmony toward the same goal.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/naomi-gleit.md]

### 3. Growth Lessons from Facebook's Legendary Early Team

As one of the earliest members of Facebook's growth team, Naomi witnessed and contributed to the playbook that grew Facebook from millions to billions. The core insight: **growth is a product discipline, not a marketing function**. Every product decision should be evaluated through the lens of how it affects acquisition, activation, retention, and referral. The early growth team ran hundreds of experiments simultaneously, with a culture of shipping fast, measuring everything, and killing losing bets quickly. The team's success came from deep integration with the core product team — growth wasn't a separate silo but embedded in how every feature was built.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/naomi-gleit.md]

### 4. Simplify the Gnarly Problems

Naomi's superpower is taking impossibly complex, messy projects and simplifying them into clear, executable plans. Her approach: resist the urge to add more process or more people. Instead, strip away everything that's not essential. Ask: "What is the smallest possible version of this that solves the core problem?" Then ruthlessly cut everything else. This applies to product features, organizational structures, and strategic plans. Complexity is the enemy of execution — simplicity creates speed and clarity.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/naomi-gleit.md]

### 5. Working with Mark Zuckerberg: The Value of Directness

Naomi's experience working with Zuck for nearly two decades reveals a leadership style defined by **radical directness and intellectual honesty**. Zuck doesn't want to be surrounded by yes-people — he wants to be challenged with well-reasoned counterarguments. The most effective way to influence him is to come prepared with data, a clear point of view, and a willingness to debate. Naomi has observed Zuck's evolution as a leader, including a recent "cool transformation" — but the constant has been his relentless focus on long-term vision and his willingness to make bold bets even when they're unpopular internally or externally. The lesson: great leaders combine vision with the humility to change their minds when presented with better evidence.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/naomi-gleit.md]
