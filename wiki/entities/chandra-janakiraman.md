# Chandra Janakiraman

**Role:** Chief Product Officer, EVP, and Board Member at VRChat; former product leader at Meta, Headspace (CPO), Zynga (GM), and Amazon (Senior PM)  
**Known for:** Developing an operator's playbook for product strategy — a repeatable 5-phase process that distills wisdom from Good Strategy Bad Strategy, Playing to Win, and Michael Porter into a practical, team-friendly methodology.

## Key Views

### 1. Strategy Is a Skill Anyone Can Learn — Not a Mystical Gene

Chandra's core mission: demystify strategy. He noticed a "strategy gene" perception where some people were seen as intrinsically good at strategy and others weren't. After his own crisis moment at Headspace (where the CEO revealed nobody understood *why* they were working on what they were working on), he developed a procedural, repeatable approach. His conclusion: "Anybody can build product strategy through a clear understanding of what it is and through a friendly and repeatable playbook."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/chandra-janakiraman.md]

### 2. The "Resonance" Definition of Strategy

Chandra defines strategy using a physics analogy: resonance. When you apply a frequency close to an object's natural frequency, you get a disproportionate increase in amplitude. Strategy is selecting that frequency — achieving resonance between product and market. It sits between mission/vision and the plan (roadmap), forcing choice to deploy scarce resources for maximum impact. A complete strategy has three components: **(1)** a handful of focus areas (strategic pillars), **(2)** areas explicitly *not* the focus, and **(3)** the *why* behind both.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/chandra-janakiraman.md]

### 3. The 5-Phase Strategy Development Process (8-12 Weeks)

Chandra's playbook has five phases with specific time allocations:
1. **Preparation (4 weeks)** — Form a strategy working group (engineering, product, design, data). Gather: behavioral insights meta-analysis, UXR insights, leadership interviews (use the "fruit story" — ask leaders what they want before building), competitive analysis, adjacent roadmaps, and user observations.
2. **Strategy Sprint (1 week)** — Day 1: share-out of all prep work. Day 2: enumerate problems, cluster into 10-15 groups, flip to opportunity framing, rank by impact/certainty/clarity of levers/differentiation. Select ~3 strategic pillars.
3. **Design Sprint (1 week)** — Generate "how might we" questions for each pillar.
4. **Document Writing (1-2 weeks)** — Write the strategy document.
5. **Rollout (2-3 weeks)** — Socialize and align.

> "It's amazing how few people actually engage with their leaders before building a strategy." — Chandra Janakiraman

### 4. Leadership Interviews Are the Most Underrated Step

Chandra's "fruit story": bringing a strategy to a leader without asking what they want is like offering a mango to someone who hates mangoes, then an apple to someone who stopped eating apples, then a banana. The fix: interview leaders upfront. Ask what success looks like, what failure looks like, and what their pet ideas are. Leaders *want* to be asked — it's a more fun conversation than their usual meetings, and it's a sign of strength, not weakness.

### 5. The Four Criteria for Prioritizing Strategic Pillars

When down-selecting from 10-15 opportunity clusters to ~3 strategic pillars, Chandra ranks them on four dimensions:
1. **Expected impact** — If you tackle this, what's the payoff?
2. **Certainty of impact** — How solid is the evidence this is a real problem?
3. **Clarity of levers** — Do you actually know how to solve it?
4. **Uniqueness/differentiation** — Is your team uniquely positioned to build this? If another team or company could do it better, it's probably not your strategic bet.

These are scored qualitatively (high/medium/low) as a working group — the debate itself creates alignment.

# Chandra Janakiraman

**Role:** Chief Product Officer and EVP at VRChat; former product leader at Meta (Facebook social experience, video, photos), CPO at Headspace, GM at Zynga, Senior PM at Amazon  
**Known for:** Developing an "operators guide to product strategy" — a repeatable 5-stage strategy process synthesized from Good Strategy Bad Strategy, Playing to Win, and Michael Porter, and his systematic demystification of what makes great product strategy.

## Key Views

### 1. The 5-stage product strategy process — strategy as a repeatable system

Chandra's core contribution: a structured, repeatable 5-stage process for developing product strategy that takes 8-12 weeks for a present-forward (2-year horizon) strategy. The stages: **(1)** Understand the current situation (data, user research, competitive landscape), **(2)** Define the strategic challenge (the key question you need to answer), **(3)** Generate strategic options (multiple paths forward), **(4)** Make strategic choices (where to play, how to win), **(5)** Develop the action plan (OKRs, roadmap, resourcing). The process builds alignment through inclusion — strategies that come from the team feel more familiar and are easier to execute.

> "In terms of what product strategy is? There is a smallest flavor of it which focuses on solving problems, called present forward, and it typically operates in a two-year horizon. We use a five-stage process to get there and it takes about eight to 12 weeks." — Chandra Janakiraman

### 2. Big "S" strategy vs. little "s" strategy

Chandra distinguishes between two types of strategy: **Big "S" Strategy** (aspirational, 5-10 year view — "what does the product look like in 10 years? Why is the world better?") and **little "s" strategy** (present-forward, 2-year horizon focused on solving specific problems). Both are necessary. Big S provides direction and inspiration; little S provides execution clarity. Inspired by Elon Musk's quote that "life's got to be about more than just solving problems," Chandra believes every company needs the aspirational component.

### 3. Strategy as demystification — there is no "strategy gene"

Chandra noticed that product strategy was treated as a mystical art — something some people were "born with." He rejected this and built a teachable system. His insight: strategy is a skill, not a gene. By codifying the process (borrowing from Richard Rumelt's *Good Strategy Bad Strategy*, Roger Martin's *Playing to Win*, and Porter), he made strategy accessible to any product leader willing to put in the work. The key is understanding that strategy is fundamentally about making explicit choices about where to play and how to win.

> "I started noticing that there was a certain mystique and aura about product strategy. There was this perception that some people were intrinsically really good at strategy and others were not. It was almost as if there was a strategy gene you needed to be born with to be good at it." — Chandra Janakiraman

### 4. Alignment is the hidden output of good strategy

Chandra's biggest lesson from his Headspace experience (where the CEO told him "people don't understand why we're working on what we're working on"): the most important output of a strategy process isn't the document — it's the **alignment** of the organization. A perfect strategy that nobody understands or buys into is worthless. The process must involve cross-functional stakeholders, create shared understanding, and result in everyone being able to explain "why we're working on this." The 8-12 week timeline is intentionally long enough to build deep alignment but short enough to maintain momentum.

### 5. Adapt frameworks from the best — don't reinvent strategy

Chandra doesn't claim to have invented new strategic theory. Instead, he synthesized the best existing frameworks into a practical system. From Rumelt: identify the core challenge. From Martin/Riel: make explicit choices about where to play and how to win. From Porter: understand competitive forces and your unique position. His contribution is making these frameworks operational — turning theory into a step-by-step process that a PM or CPO can follow with their team.
