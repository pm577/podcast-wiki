# Matt LeMay

Matt LeMay spent 13 years as a music critic at Pitchfork before becoming one of product management's most influential voices. He is author of *Product Management in Practice* and the recently published *Impact First Product Teams*. He has consulted with hundreds of product teams from startups to Fortune 500 companies.

## Key Views

**The One Question That Saves PM Careers** — "If you were the CEO of this company, would you fully fund your own team?" Most PMs don't know the answer immediately. This question exposes whether a team is driving business-critical impact or just doing "work around the work." The low-impact PM death spiral starts with adding little features and cosmetic improvements — and ends with layoffs. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-lemay.md]

**Impact-First Product Teams** — Three steps to becoming impact-first: (1) Set team goals no more than one step away from company goals — don't let them cascade into abstraction. (2) Align every piece of work with a measurable business outcome. (3) Hold yourself accountable for impact, not just output. Even if you follow all the best practices, if your company goes out of business, they won't keep paying you because your OKRs were 0.6. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-lemay.md]

**The Low-Impact PM Death Spiral** — A dynamic every medium-to-large company finds itself in: teams slowly drift from high-impact work to incremental feature additions. The work feels safe and productive but isn't moving the business forward. The PM becomes a project manager, focused on shipping rather than outcomes. When layoffs come, these are the teams and PMs most vulnerable. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-lemay.md]

**Exec Pushing a Bad Idea? Show the Data** — Don't just say "no" to an executive's feature request. Instead, connect the proposal to actual data and outcomes. Ask: "Help me understand how this connects to our top business priority. What would we deprioritize to do this?" Making the trade-off explicit and grounded in data shifts the conversation from opinion to evidence. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-lemay.md]

**Context Over Ceremony** — Many product management "best practices" (elaborate roadmaps, heavy OKR frameworks, detailed PRDs) create ceremony without context. The best teams focus on understanding the business context deeply and then making fast, informed decisions. Ceremony without context leads to feature factories. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-lemay.md]
