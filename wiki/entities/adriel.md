# Adriel Frederick

**Role:** VP of Product at Reddit (incubating new products); former Director of Product at Lyft (marketplace & pricing); early PM at Facebook (user acquisition growth team)  
**Known for:** Pioneering growth at Facebook, marketplace design at Lyft, incubating new products at Reddit, and championing diversity as a competitive advantage.

## Key Views

### 1. The PM's job is to decide what the algorithm should and shouldn't own

Adriel rejects "techno-utopianism" — the idea that algorithms given an objective will do the right thing. Algorithms don't understand long-term effects, human responses, or product intent. The PM's critical role in AI-heavy products is defining the framework for what algorithms handle vs. what humans own. This becomes more important as AI capabilities grow.

> "When you are working on algorithmic heavy products, your job is figuring out what the algorithm should be responsible for, what people are responsible for, and the framework for making decisions." — Adriel Frederick

### 2. Diversity isn't a PR obligation — it's a speed advantage

As the first Black PM at Facebook, Adriel experienced firsthand how diverse teams build better products faster. A team with varied ethnicities, religions, and geographies can resolve product debates in 15 minutes (arguing from real cultural knowledge) vs. weeks of user research. His Trinidadian background taught him that one-phone-number-equals-one-person is a Western assumption that breaks globally — insight that directly shaped Facebook's international growth.

> "When you recognize that you get business value from it, then it becomes something you look out for and take care of... You have to retain diverse talent and have an environment that values it, uses it, and rewards it." — Adriel Frederick

### 3. Three rules for R&D teams to avoid "organ rejection"

When incubating new products within a larger company, Adriel's hard-won principles: **(1)** The rest of the company must see the new initiative as core to the mission, not a side project. **(2)** Make success belong to everyone — don't let the R&D team be the only ones who win. **(3)** Don't let the rest of the org feel like all innovation happens on the R&D team. Otherwise, internal resentment starves the new initiative of resources and support.

### 4. As a PM, be a damper — not an amplifier — of chaos

Adriel's philosophy for leading through controversy (Facebook, Lyft, Reddit are all in the news constantly): when things are amazing, bring people back to earth. When the press says you're the worst thing ever, pull your team up. Controversy is often a sign you're changing patterns of behavior — which means some people are invested in the old pattern. The real skill is distinguishing valid criticism from noise, and the only way to do that is staying close to users.

> "Some controversy is necessary. You're not going to have any meaningful influence on the world without changing some pattern of behavior. And if you're changing a pattern of behavior, there's somebody who's invested in that pattern." — Adriel Frederick

### 5. Get in the field — drive the car, feel the pain

When Lyft faced driver-pay controversy, Adriel didn't just analyze data — he got in a car and drove passengers. His ride with "Rick" (an 80-year-old who drove 15 minutes for a 2-minute fare) revealed the real structural problem no dataset captured. His advice to PMs: "Plan the work, work the plan. Solve for customers in the middle of controversy. Don't get sucked into the press cycle."
