# Will Larson

**Role:** CTO at Carta; former CTO at Calm; engineering leader at Stripe, Uber, Digg  
**Known for:** Author of "An Elegant Puzzle," "Staff Engineer," and "The Engineering Executive's Primer"; prolific blogger at lethain.com; one of the most influential voices in engineering leadership.

## Key Views

### 1. Treat engineers like adults, not children

Will strongly believes the industry has historically coddled engineers — sheltering them from hard business realities and treating them as precious resources who must be handled delicately. The post-ZIRP era is correcting this: engineers are being given real responsibilities and expected to engage with the actual problems of the business. This is actually better for their growth.

> "I think that we often treat engineers a little bit like children instead of giving them the responsibilities and ability to actually thrive as adults."

### 2. The shift from hiring to right-sizing is the new normal for eng leaders

During the ZIRP era, engineering managers spent 50%+ of their time hiring. Now they're doing 0–2 interviews per month. The skills that made someone a great engineering director — primarily hiring ability — are no longer sufficient. Leaders must now develop competencies in team sizing, allocation, consolidation, and making hard decisions about what to stop doing.

### 3. Engineering strategy emerges from constraints, not top-down vision

Will's approach to strategy: it's less about grand vision and more about understanding your constraints — what you can't do — and making deliberate choices about what to prioritize. Good engineering strategy identifies the critical bottlenecks and allocates resources to address them.

### 4. The relationship between EM and PM is foundational

A key theme throughout Will's work: the engineering manager and product manager relationship is the most important partnership in a technology company. When it breaks, everything breaks. He advocates for explicit expectation-setting, shared ownership of outcomes, and regular structured communication between the two roles.

### 5. Writing is a force multiplier for engineering leaders

Will publishes prolifically despite intense full-time roles because he treats writing as an integral part of his job, not a side project. Writing forces clarity of thinking, creates leverage by sharing insights at scale, and builds a reputation that opens doors. His advice: start small, write about what you're learning, and treat it as a practice rather than a performance.

> "The engineers won't want to do that work? Well, that's actually not good for the engineers to be sheltered from what is important. This is how they're going to get the opportunity to grow."
