# Jason Fried

Jason Fried is the co-founder and CEO of 37signals, the maker of Basecamp and HEY. With fewer than 80 employees, they have over 100,000 customers, generate tens of millions in annual profit, and have no investors, no board, and no plans to ever raise money or sell. He's the creator of the Shape Up development methodology and a leading voice for bootstrapping and independence in tech.

## Key Views

**Independence is the Root — Bootstrap to Keep Full Control**
Fried's most foundational principle is independence: "Independence is the root of all the things we're able to do." 37signals has never taken venture capital and has been profitable for 24 consecutive years. Fried argues that raising money creates an invisible boss with a timeline: "The moment you go out and take money or have these big obligations, you end up working for someone else." This independence lets them make decisions based on what feels right rather than what maximizes growth. He encourages founders to consider that "there's a lot more room to make it and build a successful business if you throw out that outlier [VC unicorn] and look at all the other places you can land." His dad's advice sums it up: "No one ever went broke making a profit."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-fried.md]]*

**Shape Up: Appetites Over Estimates, Six-Week Cycles, Two-Person Teams**
Fried's Shape Up framework replaces traditional estimation with "appetites" — fixed time budgets that constrain scope. Every feature gets maximum six weeks with a team of exactly two (one programmer, one designer). "An estimate is like: how long do we think it's going to take? And however long it takes is however long it takes. Most human beings are terrible estimators." Instead, appetites force hard trade-offs: "We have to figure out the simplest, most effective version to get that done within six weeks." After each six-week cycle comes a two-week cool-down for bug fixes and internal projects. The most important rule: the work should die rather than overrun. "If we say we're going to give it six weeks and we give it seven or eight or nine, then we're not really giving it six weeks — then we don't really have a system." This prevents the long-running, demoralizing projects that drive people to quit.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-fried.md]]*

**Constrain to Create — Small Teams, One Price, No Salespeople**
Fried argues that constraints are the secret to 37signals' efficiency. While competitors like Asana (1,600 employees), Monday (1,500), and Slack (2,500) chase enterprise with tiered pricing and sales teams, 37signals operates with ~75 people, one product, two prices, and zero salespeople. "Nobody can pay us more than $299 — you get unlimited users." This eliminates the complexity of servicing whales, customizing for enterprise, and building multiple versions. His philosophy: "Small is not just a stepping stone, small is a great destination itself." He warns that having too much money leads to sloppiness — "you don't have to be efficient, you don't have to be profitable, you just get sloppy." Bootstrapping is like learning guitar: the more time you spend practicing making money, the better you get at the fundamental skill required to run a successful business.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-fried.md]]*

**Gut and Intuition Over Data-Driven Decision-Making**
Fried is a vocal advocate for intuition-based management in an industry obsessed with data. "I think everyone actually is gut-driven — it's just that some people admit it." He argues that every decision by a human is a judgment call: "They're bringing to bear thousands of things they know and don't know." At 37signals, they ask "how does this feel?" more than any other question. He believes companies hire executives for "judgment and experience" — not Excel skills — and that we should "give your intuition credit. Your gut has absorbed everything." He cautions that declaring yourself data-driven often obscures the real decision-making process. His upcoming book explores celebrating gut and intuition in business, inspired partly by Charlie Munger's advice to "trust your instinct on that — a lot of great things happen because you don't know how to do them."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-fried.md]]*

**Stay Ups Over Startups — Work Shouldn't Feel Like War**
Fried coined the term "stay up" to counter the startup obsession: "Starting a business is actually way easier than staying in business. Staying is harder than starting." He advocates building companies you actually want to work at forever — not companies designed for a quick exit. This connects to his philosophy that "work isn't war": business language is saturated with military metaphors (targeting customers, conquering markets, destroying competition, making a killing). Fried rejects this framing: "It's more about pride and excitement and a spirit of creation than it is destruction." At 37signals, success is measured simply: "Would I want to do this again?" There are no OKRs, no revenue targets, no growth goals — just profitability, great products, and enjoying the ride. His advice to founders: "Keep making great shit. Keep your costs in check. Charge appropriate prices. Share as much as you can. And let the chips fall where they may."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-fried.md]]*
