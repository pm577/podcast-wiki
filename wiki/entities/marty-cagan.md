# Marty Cagan

Marty Cagan is a luminary in product management. He founded the Silicon Valley Product Group (SVPG), authored *Inspired*, *Empowered*, and the newly released *Transformed*, and has worked with more product teams than any human alive. He spent formative years at Hewlett-Packard, Netscape, and AOL.

## Key Views

**Product Management Theater** — Many people carrying the "product manager" title are actually in feature teams doing project management, not product management. They manage backlogs, write tickets, and facilitate — but don't drive value and viability. This "theater" flourished during pandemic over-hiring and is now facing a reckoning as companies cut costs and GenAI reduces the need for mechanical product coordination roles. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/marty-cagan.md]

**Feature Teams vs. Empowered Product Teams** — Feature teams receive a roadmap of output (features to ship by a date). Empowered product teams receive problems to solve (one or two per quarter). The measure for feature teams is shipping; for empowered teams it's solving the problem. The difference is "time to money" vs. "time to market." Real product managers are needed only on empowered teams, where they handle value and viability (the engineer handles feasibility, the designer handles usability). ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/marty-cagan.md]

**The PM Is a Creator, Not a Facilitator** — On real product teams, the PM is a creator who works side-by-side with design and engineering. They must become experts on users/customers (Marty's rule: visit 30 customers before taking the role), experts on data (how products are used and purchased), and experts on business constraints (legal, sales, marketing, financial costs, monetization). Saying "why" takes 10 minutes — what do you do the rest of the week? ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/marty-cagan.md]

**Most Product Management Advice Online Is Wrong** — 90% of online product management content comes from people working in feature team / delivery team models, propagating bad practices. Certification programs often teach how to manage a Jira backlog, which is like equating knowing Google Docs with being a writer. The best advice comes from companies that have proven they can consistently innovate — look for common patterns across those. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/marty-cagan.md]

**Remote Work Has Hurt Innovation and Velocity** — While acknowledging that companies won't fully return to co-location, Marty observes that remote teams consistently struggle with innovation and velocity. And smaller teams often produce more and better results — many companies overdid hiring during the pandemic, creating bloated orgs with "ridiculous roles" (agile coaches, scrum masters, product ops, business analysts). The best companies do way more with far fewer people. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/marty-cagan.md]
