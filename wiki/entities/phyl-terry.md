# Phyl Terry

Author of *Never Search Alone*, the most widely recommended guide for job seekers. Founding team member of Amazon's first acquisition, CEO of Creative Good for 15+ years, author of *Customers Included*, HBR contributor, and 500+ keynote speaker for companies like Apple, Microsoft, and Facebook.

## Key Views

### 1. Job Search Needs a Spear, Not a Net

"When you're looking for a job, you need a spear and not a net." Most people spray and pray when laid off. Instead, apply the same product-market fit thinking to your job search: define a narrow, clear focus on what you want and what you don't want. "It's hard to figure out your candidate-market fit, but it's also a relief to know it's not about you."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/phyl-terry.md]

### 2. Form a Job Search Council (The "Village")

You need a group of peers who meet regularly to hold you accountable, review your approach, and support you through the emotional rollercoaster. "There's no I in team. Well, there is an I in village, and the I in village is that when you start to interview and negotiate, you've got to be in charge." Your council is your board of directors for your job search.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/phyl-terry.md]

### 3. Play to Win, Not Not to Lose

Most job seekers negotiate from a position of fear — they play not to lose. Shift to playing to win. Know your BATNA (best alternative to a negotiated agreement). Be willing to walk away. The most successful candidates approach interviews and negotiations with the confidence of someone who has options.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/phyl-terry.md]

### 4. The "Blow My Mind" Interview Tactic

Terry's standout tactic: send a personalized analysis of the company's product or strategy before the interview. "If someone did this, it would blow my mind. I would hire them on the spot." This demonstrates genuine interest, initiative, and strategic thinking in a way that no cover letter can. It also gives you a huge advantage going into the conversation.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/phyl-terry.md]

### 5. Know What You Want Before You Start Looking

Most people skip the most critical step: determining what they actually want (and don't want) in their next role, company, and culture. Without this clarity, you'll optimize for the wrong things — salary, title, prestige — and end up in a job that makes you unhappy. The job search council helps enforce this discipline.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/phyl-terry.md]
