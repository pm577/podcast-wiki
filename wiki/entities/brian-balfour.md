# Brian Balfour

**Role:** Founder and CEO of Reforge; former VP of Growth at HubSpot; student and teacher of product growth for over a decade  
**Known for:** Deep study of every major platform distribution shift (Facebook, Google, Apple, LinkedIn, TikTok), predicting ChatGPT as the next major growth channel, and the "Escape Velocity" distribution framework.

## Key Views

### 1. ChatGPT is the next great distribution platform — and the window is opening now

Brian's central prediction: ChatGPT (or a similar AI platform) is about to become a new distribution channel on the scale of Facebook's App Platform (2007), Google's SEO era, or the Apple App Store. The cycle follows a predictable pattern: **(1)** competitive environment where platforms fight for users, **(2)** they open distribution to developers/partners to build moats, **(3)** they eventually restrict and monetize that distribution. We're at stage 2 with AI platforms, and "the cycles seem to be getting shorter and shorter, so you actually have a smaller amount of time." His bet is on ChatGPT because of its "smile curve" retention — usage actually increases over time, a pattern only seen in the most successful platforms.

> "My prediction, the new distribution platform will be ChatGPT. There's a bunch of signals that they're about to launch that." — Brian Balfour

### 2. Distribution is the separator — great product is necessary but not sufficient

Brian's core philosophy (inspired by Alex Rampell's "Escape Velocity" post): building a great product is necessary but not sufficient for startup success. The real separation is between companies that build great **distribution** and those that don't. Startups win by getting distribution faster than incumbents can copy the product. This is harder than ever because incumbents can copy faster, and the window for "escape velocity" keeps shrinking.

> "Building a great product is one of those things that's necessary, but not sufficient. And actually the separation is between those that build really great distribution." — Brian Balfour

### 3. The platform distribution lifecycle — play the game or get played

Brian maps every major platform cycle (Facebook, Google, Apple, LinkedIn) to the same three stages: **(1)** Open distribution to attract developers/users and compete, **(2)** Build a moat around that distribution, **(3)** Restrict and monetize. His insight: **you cannot opt out of this game** — it's a prisoner's dilemma. If you don't build on the new platform, your competitors will, and customer expectations will change. The winning strategy is to be early, ride the open distribution wave, and anticipate the restriction phase before it hits by diversifying channels.

> "It ends up being a prisoner's dilemma … There is no opting out of the game. You have to play the game. So it's better to be early than to be super late." — Brian Balfour

### 4. For startups: place one big bet — for incumbents: hedge

Brian's strategic advice on platform shifts differs by company stage. **Startups** should choose one emerging platform and go all in — this is how Zynga, Uber, and Airbnb won on their respective platforms. **Late-stage companies** should place multiple bets because they can afford to. The key is recognizing that the cost of missing a new distribution platform is existential for startups but manageable for incumbents. Startups shouldn't try to hedge — they lack the resources.

> "If you're a late-stage company, you place multiple bets. For startups, it's a totally different ballgame. You have to choose one and go all in." — Brian Balfour

### 5. The moat is context and memory — not the model itself

Brian's analysis of the AI platform race: the model itself isn't the moat — models are converging in capability. The real differentiator is **context and memory** — which platform has the most user context to personalize outputs. Usage creates memory, which creates better personalization, which drives more usage — a virtuous flywheel. ChatGPT is furthest along on memory and data connectors (HubSpot, Google Drive, etc.), which is why Brian predicts they'll win the platform battle, not just the model battle.

> "The moat is about context and memory. These models by themselves, if you compare them side by side, they generate the same result, and so the actual difference-maker is which one has more of your context." — Brian Balfour
