# Tobi Lütke

**Role:** Founder and CEO of Shopify ($130B+ market cap, powering over 10% of all U.S. e-commerce)  
**Known for:** First principles thinking, long-term (100-year) vision, maximizing human potential, fostering a unique engineering-led culture.

## Key Views

### 1. First principles thinking: derive from atomic building blocks, not path dependence

Tobi's approach to product and strategy is to ignore how things are currently done and instead derive solutions from first principles — understanding the fundamental building blocks available today. He is deeply suspicious of pitches that aim to make a "good version" of what already exists, because everything is path-dependent and built on assumptions that may no longer be true. Instead, ask: "How would we solve this problem given every fundamental building block that we have available right now?"

> "If someone comes to me and says, 'Let's go do this thing. And we've looked around and here's how people solve this problem, let's make a good version of that,' I'm like, 'That was not the job.'"

### 2. Maximizing human potential: there is no speed limit for personal growth

Tobi's core leadership philosophy: no one on the planet is close to their maximum potential. His role as a leader is to constantly remind people of their own potential and hold them accountable to it. He believes in creating environments where people are pushed to be better — not through coddling, but through honest, direct feedback that compresses learning time.

> "I really, really, really think that there is not a single person on this planet who is even close to being at their maximum potential."

### 3. The "Tobi Tornado": compress time and avoid the sunk cost fallacy

When Tobi realizes a project or direction is wrong, he acts immediately — stopping the project and reorienting the team, even if it feels abrupt. He calls this the "Tobi Tornado" — compressing change management and conflict into a short timeframe. The alternative — letting people continue working on something that won't make it — is an abdication of founder responsibility. This approach inoculates the company against the sunk cost fallacy.

> "I'm either incorrect, at which point this is super important that I understand why, or I'm correct, at which point it's super unfair for letting people work on something that isn't going to make it."

### 4. Embrace the unquantifiable: fun, delight, and taste over pure metrics

Shopify's core product teams operate without traditional KPIs or OKRs. Tobi believes that Goodhart's Law is real — any metric that becomes a goal ceases to be a good metric. Instead, he prioritizes unquantifiable things: fun, delight, taste, and craft. Data is a critical input (Shopify has massive investment in data systems) but should inform decisions, not drive them. The most powerful unquantifiable things in business are fun and delight.

> "If all the metrics are pointing down but everyone says, 'My God, I'm having so much more fun,' I think that the very next thing that will happen with some time delay is all metrics will start going up."

### 5. Play the infinite game: long-term thinking and positive-sum partnerships

Tobi frames company building as an infinite game played on an ever-expanding board. Shopify's 100-year mission — making entrepreneurship more common — guides every decision. This long-term horizon enables better partnerships (like the Stripe partnership), avoids the temptation to extract short-term value from customers, and turns the company into a force for creating new businesses rather than extracting from existing ones.

> "On a long enough timeline, playing positive-sum games with your customers is the ultimate growth hack."

### 6. Courage is the rarest ingredient — lower the barrier to entry

Tobi believes courage is rarer than genius or high IQ in the business world. The highest-leverage thing a product can do is lower the amount of courage required for someone to try something new — whether that's starting a business, posting content, or using a feature. Shopify's focus on simplicity and great UX is a direct response to this: every time a complex thing is made simpler, more businesses will exist.

> "The rarest thing in the world is not even creativity or genius, it's courage. So let's lower the net amount of courage needed."
