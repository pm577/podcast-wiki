# Kevin Aluwi

**Role:** Co-founder and former CEO of Gojek, Indonesia's first decacorn and the largest startup in Southeast Asia; built a super app with 2.7M drivers and 3B+ annual orders  
**Known for:** Building the world's most successful super app outside China; pioneering brand-led growth in emerging markets; the philosophy that "doing hard things" is the only durable competitive advantage.

## Key Views

### 1. Super Apps Are Overrated — the Unifying Concept Problem

Kevin warns that the super app strategy sounds great on VC strategy decks but rarely delivers the promised benefits of lower CAC and higher retention. The critical missing piece is a **unifying concept**: customers need a single mental model that makes sense of all the services under one app. For Gojek, the unifying concept was the driver — customers intuitively understood ride hailing, food delivery, and package delivery all through the same driver. But when Gojek launched massage services, customers literally asked "Is the driver going to come into my house and give me a massage?" This showed that services without the unifying concept break customer trust and require expensive re-education to sell.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-aluwi.md]

> "A lot of those benefits don't pan out. There needs to be a unifying concept across all of your services within the app for your users to be able to think about your product in a sensible way." — Kevin Aluwi

### 2. Brand as the Competitive Moat for Capital-Constrained Startups

Gojek raised only $2M in its first six months while a regional competitor raised $250M — a 100x capital disadvantage. Kevin argues that **brand is the second most important thing in a consumer business after product**, and it's the most underutilized competitive weapon for capital-constrained startups. Great brands create associations that transcend transactionality and become part of the customer's identity. Gojek invested in consistency across all customer touchpoints, from copy and design to product features that became cultural artifacts (like allowing food to be sent as romantic gifts to distant locations). This brand loyalty kept drivers and customers loyal even when competitors offered bigger discounts.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-aluwi.md]

> "The two most important things in a consumer business are product and brand — in that order. Brand as an afterthought is a giant missed opportunity." — Kevin Aluwi

### 3. The Jackets and Helmets That Built a Billion-Dollar Brand

Kevin considers putting Gojek logos on driver jackets and helmets the single most important brand decision the company ever made. Unlike car ride-hailing (where drivers are hidden inside cars), Gojek's motorcycle drivers were visible everywhere on the streets of Jakarta. The jackets served a dual purpose: (1) constant visual recall of the brand name and colors, and (2) a **physical reminder of the value proposition** — stuck-in-traffic drivers saw Gojek drivers zipping past with passengers or delivering food, instantly connecting the brand to the service. This created the strongest possible brand association: seeing the brand reminded you of exactly how it could help you in that moment.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-aluwi.md]

### 4. Do the Hard Things — The Only Durable Moat

Kevin rejects the popular "moat" concept because, in his view, all moats can eventually be crossed with enough time and resources. The one competitive advantage that's genuinely durable is simply **being willing to do hard things that create customer value**. Gojek hired private security to protect drivers from motorcycle taxi mafia who threw bricks and brandished machetes. They built physical cash booths with vaults across Indonesia to pay drivers when digital payments didn't exist. They copied features from fraudulent third-party driver apps to win back users. Each of these was operationally intense, unscalable, and exactly what capital-rich competitors wouldn't do — but each built deep loyalty that money couldn't buy.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-aluwi.md]

> "The one so-called moat that doesn't get talked about enough is the fact that you can do hard things. Hard things are hard, and simply doing things that are hard, as long as they create value to your customers, makes it harder for competitors to win." — Kevin Aluwi

### 5. Founders Must Understand the Work Before They Can Delegate

Kevin held an extraordinary range of roles — performance marketer, copywriter, driver, CFO, CIO — not from a desire to be scrappy, but because he needed to understand what excellence looked like in each function before he could hire for it. In an emerging market where experienced talent was scarce, this was survival. His most vivid example: becoming one of Gojek's first car drivers to build empathy for the driver experience. A passenger asked him to carry her laundry, take a detour, and lug a heavy bag — all for little pay. This direct experience drove him to champion product features like waiting fees and multiple stops. The lesson: **leaders who haven't done the work cannot evaluate the people who will.**^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-aluwi.md]

> "I needed to understand what the job entailed — and seeing how bad I was at it allowed me to understand what good looked like, so I could then find somebody who could do it orders of magnitude better." — Kevin Aluwi
