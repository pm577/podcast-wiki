# Howie Liu

**Role:** Co-founder and CEO of Airtable  
**Known for:** Building Airtable into a ~$12B no-code platform, restructuring the company for the AI era with a "fast thinking / slow thinking" org model, and leading the IC CEO trend (CEOs getting back into code and product).

## Key Views

### 1. Fast thinking / slow thinking org structure

Howie restructured Airtable's entire EPD org around Daniel Kahneman's "Thinking, Fast and Slow" framework. The **fast thinking group** (AI platform) ships new AI capabilities on a near-weekly basis — each one should "drop your jaw" in value. The **slow thinking group** focuses on deliberate, infrastructure-heavy work (e.g., the HyperDB data store that handles multi-hundred-million-record datasets). The two groups complement each other: fast thinking creates top-of-funnel excitement and new use cases, slow thinking turns initial seeds of adoption into large enterprise deployments. This solves a classic AI-native startup challenge: wide top-of-funnel but difficulty converting into durable, retained growth.

> "The fast execution, the AI stuff, creates the top of funnel excitement that also inspires new use cases and new users to come to Airtable. The slow thinking allows those initial seeds of adoption to sprout and grow into much larger deployments." — Howie Liu

### 2. The IC CEO — get back into the details

Howie argues that every software product must be "refounded" for AI — and you can't do that from 10,000 feet. He cut his standing 1:1 roster, replaced meetings with urgent, insight-driven conversations, and became Airtable's #1 most expensive inference-cost user, aggressively using AI (including running LLMs against thousands of sales call transcripts) to extract product and market insights. His framing: if the CEO isn't personally using AI daily — not just reading about it but actually pushing models to their limits to understand new form factors — the company won't successfully navigate the AI transition.

> "To really understand the solution space of what's possible, you have to be in the details. You can't just look at screenshots or a pre-recorded video of a new product feature. AI is something you have to play with." — Howie Liu

### 3. PMs must become hybrid PM-prototyper-designers

Howie believes AI demands baseline competence across all three EPD functions (product, engineering, design) from every role. The best performing people in the AI era are those who can cross over into adjacent disciplines: a PM who can prototype in V0 or Lovable, a designer who's "just technical enough to be dangerous," an engineer who thinks about product and business outcomes. He encourages his team to take entire days or weeks off to "go play with every AI product" they can find relevant to Airtable. The emphasis is on experiential learning — trying products, not just reading about them.

> "As a PM, you need to start looking more like a hybrid PM prototyper who has some good design sensibilities. I think some of the best eng, PM, and design cultures over the past few decades have always been multidisciplinary in nature." — Howie Liu

### 4. Use AI greedily but smartly — the value-to-cost ratio is insane

Howie describes running LLM map-reduce operations against massive corpuses of data that cost hundreds of dollars per run in inference — but generate insights that would cost millions from a consulting firm. His principle: **be "intentionally wasteful"** with AI compute on high-value strategic problems. Understanding your product, market, and customers through AI-augmented analysis is one of the highest-ROI activities a CEO can spend company resources on. He specifically encourages prototyping "side projects" (e.g., creating an AI-generated video with HeyGen and ChatGPT as a weekend project) to build fluency with emerging form factors.

> "Hundreds of dollars spent on this exercise is trivial compared to the potential strategic value of having better insights. You could pay a consulting firm literally millions of dollars to get that quality of work." — Howie Liu

### 5. The "refound or sell" test for incumbents

Howie's litmus test for any pre-AI company: **if you were founding your company from scratch today with the same mission, how would you execute using a fully AI-native approach?** And can you leverage your existing building blocks, or are they dead weight? Airtable's answer: their no-code primitives (real-time collaborative data layer, rich UI components, automations) act as a "DSL" that the AI agent can manipulate reliably — unlike pure vibe-coding where you write everything from scratch. If you can't articulate the advantage of your existing assets, his advice is brutally honest: sell the company and start the next iteration.

> "If you can't, then you should find a buyer and then if you really care about this mission, go and start the next carnation of it." — Howie Liu
