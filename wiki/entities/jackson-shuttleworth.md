# Jackson Shuttleworth

**Role:** Group Product Manager at Duolingo, leading the retention team behind the Duolingo Streaks feature  
**Known for:** Deep expertise in the Duolingo Streaks feature — the single most impactful growth lever for a $14B company, with 9M+ users maintaining year-long streaks and over 600 experiments run in 4 years.

## Key Views

### 1. Streaks Are a Layer on Top of a Product People Actually Want

Jackson's foundational insight: "Streaks work best when they're sitting on top of an app that users care about." The streak is an engagement mechanic layered on a product that's already fun and delightful. For Duolingo, the streak works because language learning is hard to see day-to-day — you can't perceive 0.01% improvement. The streak provides a tangible, daily sense of progress. Any app can introduce a streak mechanic, but it must sit on genuine user value.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jackson-shuttleworth.md]

> "Streaks work best when they're sitting on top of an app that users care about. If you don't come back to Duolingo, then you're not learning." — Jackson Shuttleworth

### 2. Find the Right "Unit of Use" — Make It Simple

The most impactful experiment in streaks history was changing from an XP-based streak (hit 50 XP/day) to a **one-lesson streak** (do one lesson/day). This massively drove DAUs. But going further — making it "one exercise" instead of "one lesson" — had zero impact. The lesson: there's a right "unit of use" for your app. A "lesson" is meaningful; "one exercise" isn't. Users don't think in terms of one exercise. Simple doesn't mean trivial — it means a clear, meaningful minimum commitment.

### 3. The First 7 Days Are Everything — That's Where Loss aversion Kicks In

Duolingo's retention data shows that once a user hits a 7-day streak, loss aversion kicks in and retention flattens dramatically. The jump from 1 to 2 days is huge, 2 to 3 days slightly less, and it compounds until day 7. After that, the marginal retention gain from longer streaks is negligible. The team consequently does **more experimentation on the 0-7 day user experience** than any other segment. The goal: get users to that 7-day inflection point where the streak becomes self-reinforcing.

### 4. Copy Changes Can Be Massive Wins — Test Everything Cheap

Jackson's first win at Duolingo was an 8-word copy change: "Start a day to extend your streak, but miss a day and it resets." This simple clarification drove over 10,000 additional DAUs — one of the top 3 CURR (Current User Retention Rate) wins ever. Another example: changing a CTA from "Continue" to "Commit To My Goal" was a massive win. Duolingo tests aggressively on copy because "it's so cheap" — just translate a few strings. They've built an entire infrastructure for low-cost copy testing.

### 5. The Power of Intentionality and Opt-Out

Adding an **opt-out button** (instead of just "Continue") was counterintuitively a huge win. Letting users explicitly choose "Yes, I want to hit 30 days" or opt-out made the commitment more intentional. Preselecting a harder goal for users lost significantly — the act of choosing their own goal length (14, 30, 50 days) was where the engagement came from. This principle: friction that forces intentionality can be *good* friction, even when it seems like you're giving users an escape hatch.
