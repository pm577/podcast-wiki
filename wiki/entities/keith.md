# Keith Yandell

Keith Yandell has led an unusually broad range of teams at DoorDash over ~7 years: Legal, HR, Marketing, Customer Support, and currently BD and Corporate Development. Before DoorDash, he led litigation at Uber. He's known for his direct, empathy-first leadership style and for being a key architect of DoorDash's distinctive culture.

## Key Views

**Direct Feedback Is a Culture-Building Superpower**
Yandell's most famous story: after interviewing a VP of Engineering candidate, he told the founder "I think this guy's a jerk." The founder asked him to have dinner with the candidate first. Over dinner, Yandell asked directly: "During the interview you kind of seemed like an asshole. Are you an asshole?" The candidate's gracious response — wanting to know what he'd done wrong and how to improve — convinced Yandell he was wrong. The candidate joined and became his close friend, citing the directness as a key reason. Lesson: giving direct feedback during the interview process is itself a test of culture fit.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]]**

**The Only Thing That Matters Is "Keep Going" — One Yes Changes Everything**
Reflecting on DoorDash's early days (weeks of runway, rejected by everyone), Yandell shares co-founder Tony Xu's philosophy: "It's just the difference between a founder and a non-founder. If you're really a founder, you just have to find a way. You have to keep going. There's no question." The advice he gives to founders facing rejection: "It only takes one yes. But you got to keep going." This relentless persistence is what separates the startups that make it from those that don't.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]]**

**"No Politics, No Assholes" Is a Culture You Have to Enforce**
DoorDash's internal surveys consistently show "no politics, no assholes" as a top cultural attribute. Yandell explains this isn't accidental — it requires active enforcement. Leaders need to be willing to pass on talented candidates who don't meet the culture bar, even when the hire is desperately needed. Ryan Sokol (the VP of Engineering candidate) joined precisely because DoorDash was willing to walk away from a qualified candidate over attitude — it proved the culture was real.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]]**

**Leading Across Multiple Functions Requires First Principles Thinking**
Yandell has led Legal, HR, Marketing, Support, and Corporate Development — an unusually broad portfolio. His secret: strip each function down to first principles. What is the core job this team does? What does success look like? How do we measure it? By ignoring the received wisdom about how each function "should" operate, he's been able to bring fresh approaches and break down silos between teams. The common thread across all of them is understanding human behavior and incentives.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]]**

**Empathy Is a Business Strategy, Not Just a Value**
Yandell's leadership approach centers on empathy — understanding the perspectives of customers, employees, and partners. At DoorDash, this means the legal team doesn't just manage risk; they understand the business context. The support team doesn't just resolve tickets; they feed insights back to product. Empathy isn't soft — it's strategic. It enables better decisions, stronger partnerships, and more resilient teams.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]]**
