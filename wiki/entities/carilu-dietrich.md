# Carilu Dietrich

**Role:** Former CMO who took Atlassian public; advisor to CEOs and CMOs of hypergrowth B2B companies (Segment, Miro, 1Password, Bill.com, Productboard, Sprout Social)  
**Known for:** Deep expertise in hypergrowth marketing, the "organic + inbound + viral word of mouth" growth trifecta, and blunt career advice for aspiring executives.

## Key Views

### 1. Hypergrowth requires organic, inbound, and viral — you can't pay for it

Carilu's foundational insight: to achieve hypergrowth, you must have organic, inbound, and viral word-of-mouth growth channels. You cannot spend your way to hypergrowth — the economics don't work. The biggest driver is an "amazing product that people love to use" (ChatGPT being the ultimate example). Hypergrowth companies compress 5-10 years of normal growth stages into 1-2 years, which means they need to keep hiring 2X-3X leaders who have already seen the next stage of growth.

> "In order to get hypergrowth, you have to have organic, inbound, and viral word of mouth. You can't pay enough to grow at those rates and have a viable company. The biggest thing is an amazing product that people love to use." — Carilu Dietrich

### 2. The executive track requires working harder — there are no shortcuts

Carilu is refreshingly direct about career advancement: getting to the C-suite requires working harder, learning more, pushing yourself, and taking on responsibilities outside your job description. She describes her early-career strategy of working two hours later than everyone else for five years to effectively gain more experience. She also emphasizes taking on "white space" projects — volunteering to fill gaps when a department head leaves or moonlighting in another function. There are "ebbs and flows" (family, health), but there are no shortcuts to knowing a lot.

> "I worked two hours later than everyone else on the team. And I had this thought in my brain that two hours every day for five years would get me how many more years of experience than someone else?" — Carilu Dietrich

### 3. Why CMOs (and CPOs) get fired — and how to avoid it

Carilu shares hard-earned wisdom on executive tenure: CMOs often get fired because they focus on brand and awareness without connecting to revenue. The C-suite needs marketing to drive measurable business outcomes, not just "awareness." Similarly, CPOs get fired when they build features without understanding the business model. Her advice: CMOs must speak the language of the CEO (revenue, retention, unit economics) and build strong relationships with the CFO. Marketing must be accountable for pipeline and revenue, not just vanity metrics.

### 4. Wait as long as possible to hire your first salesperson

Carilu's contrarian advice: delay hiring your first salesperson as long as possible. Product-led growth and founder-led sales should carry the company through the early stages. When you do hire sales, they should be selling into a market where the product already has proven demand and a clear bottom-up adoption path. Premature sales hires create pressure to build features for enterprise deals before product-market fit is established, derailing the product-led growth engine.

### 5. The three stages of hypergrowth: ride the lightning

Carilu outlines the hypergrowth trajectory: companies go through stages of growth that would take normal companies 5-10 years in compressed timeframes — from 10 to 50 people, 50 to 100, 100 to 200. Each stage requires a different operating model, different leadership skills, and different marketing strategies. The leaders who survive are those who recognize when the current playbook has expired and proactively evolve. "Riding the lightning" means accepting that chaos is normal in hypergrowth, and your job is to add just enough structure to keep scaling without killing the energy that got you there.
