# Vijay Iyengar

**Role:** Head of Product at Mixpanel  
**Known for:** Leading Mixpanel's product turnaround — refocusing from multiple products back to a single core analytics product, improving retention from 60% to 90% and NPS from 16 to 50.

## Key Views

### 1. Don't take people away from your core product to fund expansion

Mixpanel's biggest mistake was moving engineers from their core analytics product to build adjacent products (messaging, data infrastructure). The result: the core product fell behind competitors. Vijay's lesson: invest the *profits* from your core into new ventures, not the *people*. If you're the leader in a core product, you must continue to out-invest everyone else in that core.

> "The trap there is that you leave yourself right for disruption in your core because someone else can out-invest you in that core. Invest profits and not people."

### 2. Speed comes from extreme clarity and focus

When Mixpanel refocused on core analytics with 40% revenue churn, they took a radical approach: grouped all churn reasons by category, sorted by ARR impact, took the top 10 items, and made that the entire roadmap. Every engineer got direct access to customers. They shipped ~100 features in the first year and saw immediate improvements in win rate and retention.

> "Speed comes when you have extreme clarity on what you want to do and focus."

### 3. Product architecture is a competitive moat

After the rapid feature shipping phase, Mixpanel hit diminishing returns because they neglected holistic design. Vijay's insight: great products win or lose based on their architecture. The key building blocks — how few can you have? How do they relate? — determine how many features you can hang off them with high reach and discoverability.

> "So many great products win or lose based on their architecture. Notion's pages and blocks architecture is so strong, and you can hang so many features off those core building blocks."

### 4. Engineers should unlearn saying "no" reflexively

Transitioning from engineering to product, Vijay had to unlearn the instinctive "no" that engineers develop from carrying the maintenance burden of half-baked ideas. His alternative: try to make "yes" work first. Sincerely spend 10 minutes considering how an idea could work before shooting it down. Ideas are fragile in their infancy, and a hard no can kill a promising direction.

> "The best way to get to a no, if you ultimately need to get there, is to try to make it work. Start trying to make yes work and document how you've tried to make yes work."

### 5. Use product architecture as the basis for design-driven differentiation

The parallel design-driven initiative at Mixpanel focused on system architecture: identifying the key building blocks, minimizing their number, and defining how users discover and relate to them. This systematic approach to design became a key differentiator and was instrumental in taking NPS from 16 to 50.
