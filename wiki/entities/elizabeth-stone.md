# Elizabeth Stone

Elizabeth Stone is the Chief Technology Officer at Netflix, reportedly the first economist to ever be named CTO at a Fortune 500 company. She previously served as VP of Data and Insights at Netflix, VP of Science at Lyft, COO at Nuna, and worked as a trader at Merrill Lynch and an economist at Analysis Group.

## Key Views

### Economics as a Foundation for Tech Leadership

Elizabeth argues that economics is "a flavor of data science" and a powerful lens for tech leadership. It teaches structured thinking about incentives, unintended consequences, and cause-and-effect — skills that directly translate to product and engineering decisions. She believes tech companies should hire more economists, and that this background gives her a unique perspective on simplifying complex business problems.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/elizabeth-stone.md]

### Talent Density Is the Prerequisite for Culture

Drawing from Netflix's famous culture, Elizabeth emphasizes that high talent density is the foundation upon which everything else is built — candor, learning, freedom and responsibility, and the pursuit of excellence. Without starting with exceptional people, none of the other cultural elements work. This was a core belief of Reed Hastings and remains deeply embedded in how Netflix operates.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/elizabeth-stone.md]

### Context Switching Is the Biggest Challenge of Scaling

Moving from VP of Data to CTO, Elizabeth found that the most dramatic change was the amount of context switching and the feeling of "being behind." With more people to know, more problem spaces to understand, and broader technical expertise to oversee, the CTO role requires rapid learning and comfort with being a generalist across many domains. She advises leaders to embrace that they'll always have a lot to learn.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/elizabeth-stone.md]

### Data and User Research Are Netflix's Secret Weapons

Elizabeth believes Netflix's unique structure for data and user research teams is a key competitive advantage. Unlike companies that silo these functions, Netflix integrates them deeply into product decisions. This allows the company to make bets with confidence based on real user insights and rigorous data analysis — not just intuition. The combination of qualitative and quantitative understanding drives better outcomes.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/elizabeth-stone.md]

### Incentives Thinking Applies Internally and Externally

Elizabeth's economics training shapes how she leads — understanding what motivates teams and anticipating unintended consequences of management decisions. She applies the same lens to external strategy: how competitors will react, what incentives drive customer behavior, and how to design systems that produce the right outcomes without perverse incentives. This structured reasoning about cause and effect has been consistently valuable across all her roles.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/elizabeth-stone.md]
