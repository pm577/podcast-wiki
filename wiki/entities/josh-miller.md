# Josh Miller

CEO and co-founder of The Browser Company, creator of the Arc browser. Previously founded a company acqui-hired by Facebook, where he worked as a product manager. Known for a deeply human-centric approach to product building, company values as "road trip manual," and radical trust-building through building in public.

## Key Views

### 1. Optimize for Feelings, Not Metrics

Silicon Valley's obsession with graphs and metrics (e.g., D5/D7 retention) is effective but leaves a lot on the table. Miller advocates optimizing for the feeling you want the user to experience — joy, speed, organization, focus. The right feeling typically tracks closely with the metric you care about anyway. Great products make people *feel* something, and when they do, they tell others about it organically.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/josh-miller.md]

### 2. Hire for "Heartfelt Intensity"

The most important hiring criterion: people who show up with fire in their belly, something they're out to prove or achieve. Not just craft obsession, but a personal mission. Give them an ambitious prompt and get out of their way. Miller's company has attracted senior leaders from Chrome, Slack, and Tumblr to join as individual contributors because they see Arc as the work that will define their careers.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/josh-miller.md]

### 3. Company Values as a "Road Trip Manual"

Miller hated traditional corporate values until he realized his team naturally embodied certain traits. Instead of a corporate landing page, they wrote an essay called "Notes on Road Trips" — a semi-fictional story about a teenager and their father taking a road trip — to express how they work. Values: "Assume You Don't Know," "Start by Asking What Could Be," "Make Them Feel Something." The process involved interviewing every employee and extracting their actual words.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/josh-miller.md]

### 4. Radical Trust Building Through Transparency

The reason The Browser Company shares board meetings, design reviews, and raw internal conversations publicly is not "radical transparency" but **radical trust building**. Given how much trust in tech has eroded, the bet is that letting people see your imperfections and humanity is the only way to earn their trust. "Why should they trust us if we've had our trust broken again and again?"^[raw/transcripts/lenny/josh-miller.md]

### 5. No PMs — Verbs Over Titles

The Browser Company has no product manager role. Instead, the verbs of product management (setting direction, coordinating, communicating) are distributed based on what a project needs. An infrastructure engineer PMs a performance project; someone from the membership team PMs a customer-facing feature. This requires hiring "mutts" — multidisciplinary people who think of themselves as "I make things" rather than "I am a [role]."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/josh-miller.md]

### 6. Membership and Storytelling as Full-Stack Teams

Instead of splitting customer success, support, research, and marketing into separate orgs, Miller created a **Membership Team** (owns the relationship from first touch to last) and a **Storytelling Team** (tells the company story to people who haven't yet used Arc). This full-stack, humanistic approach avoids the fragmentation of traditional org structures.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/josh-miller.md]
