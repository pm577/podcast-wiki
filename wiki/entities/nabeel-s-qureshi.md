# Nabeel S. Qureshi

Nabeel S. Qureshi appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss palantir's operating model, talent, and building data companies.

## Key Views

**The Forward Deployed Engineer as a founder forge** — Palantir's forward deployed engineers lived with customers Monday–Thursday, building solutions on-site and iterating multiple times per week. This rapid build-measure-learn cycle created an unparalleled training ground for founders. "You would spend maybe Monday to Thursday and you would actually go into the building where the customer worked and you would work alongside them... So you get four of these, five of these cycles every single week, and you're moving incredibly fast." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

**The distinctive 'bad signal' in hiring** — Great companies intentionally repel certain people to attract the right talent — the signal must be polarizing enough to turn some off. "He thinks that a lot of the best recruiters in the world or the companies that attract talent, they put out this distinctive bad signal and it has to turn some people off. That's the key of a good, bad signal." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

**No titles forces you to earn your place** — Palantir avoided job titles to eliminate unproductive internal competition and Goodhart's Law dynamics, creating a culture where influence had to be continuously re-earned. "Thiel writes about this in Zero to One and his take is just that as soon as we have these titles, you have a thing that people are competing for... you always had to earn your place in the company." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

**The data iceberg — 95% is integration, not analysis** — The real pain in large organizations isn't analytics but the invisible 95% of effort spent accessing, cleaning, joining, and normalizing data before any analysis can happen. "It's this iceberg analogy where the actual analysis is actually just the tip of the iceberg, it's kind of the last five or 10%, and the 95% before that is, I am gaining access to the data, I am cleaning the data, I'm joining the data, I'm normalizing it." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

**Principles must be disagreement-forcing, not platitudes** — A useful principle is one that a reasonable person could disagree with — not something universally agreeable like "move fast." "They would write principles such as move fast and I would always be, 'Everyone likes to move fast.' It is not a good principle actually because nobody can really disagree with this reasonably." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

# Nabeel Qureshi

Nabeel Qureshi is an entrepreneur, writer, researcher, and visiting scholar of AI policy at the Mercatus Center alongside Tyler Cowen. He spent nearly eight years at Palantir as a forward deployed engineer working on public health projects with US federal agencies, including COVID-19 response and AI in drug discovery. He was also a founding member of GoCardless. He is one of the best sources for understanding how Palantir builds product leaders.

## Key Views

### Palantir's Founder Factory: 30% of PMs Start Companies

Nabeel reveals that 30% of PMs who leave Palantir start their own company — the highest rate of any company in the world. The secret: Palantir screens for "independent-minded people who weren't afraid to push back" and people with "broader intellectual interests." The culture produces PMs who question every frame, think for themselves, and have strong convictions — exactly the traits needed to found companies.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

### Forward Deployed Engineer to PM: The Only Path

At Palantir, you cannot become a PM without first proving yourself as a forward deployed engineer. These engineers go into the customer's building, work alongside them Monday through Thursday, and sit at a desk in their office. "You basically could not become a PM any other way." This ensures every PM has deep, firsthand understanding of customer problems before they ever manage a product.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

### The World's Best Data Platform

Nabeel describes Palantir's core offering as "the world's best data platform" — achieving outcomes for customers tactically through deep data integration. Two versions: Gotham (optimized for intelligence and defense) and Foundry (optimized for commercial use cases). The platform approach means Palantir works with the largest customers (Fortune 50, governments) and embeds deeply in their operations.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

### Independent Thinking Is the Core Trait

The key trait Palantir screens for above all else: independent-mindedness. Candidates must be willing to push back against authority, question every assumption, and hold strong convictions. "They screened really hard for a few traits in particular. One is very independent-minded people who weren't afraid to push back." Combined with broad intellectual interests, this creates the unique Palantir culture.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

### AI Policy and the Future

As a visiting scholar researching AI policy, Nabeel brings his Palantir-grounded perspective on how AI will impact government, defense, and public health. His work with the NIH on creating the largest medical dataset in the world and his COVID-19 response experience give him a unique vantage point on applied AI in high-stakes environments.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]
