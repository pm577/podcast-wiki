# Nabeel S. Qureshi

Nabeel S. Qureshi appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss palantir's operating model, talent, and building data companies.

## Key Views

**The Forward Deployed Engineer as a founder forge** — Palantir's forward deployed engineers lived with customers Monday–Thursday, building solutions on-site and iterating multiple times per week. This rapid build-measure-learn cycle created an unparalleled training ground for founders. "You would spend maybe Monday to Thursday and you would actually go into the building where the customer worked and you would work alongside them... So you get four of these, five of these cycles every single week, and you're moving incredibly fast." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

**The distinctive 'bad signal' in hiring** — Great companies intentionally repel certain people to attract the right talent — the signal must be polarizing enough to turn some off. "He thinks that a lot of the best recruiters in the world or the companies that attract talent, they put out this distinctive bad signal and it has to turn some people off. That's the key of a good, bad signal." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

**No titles forces you to earn your place** — Palantir avoided job titles to eliminate unproductive internal competition and Goodhart's Law dynamics, creating a culture where influence had to be continuously re-earned. "Thiel writes about this in Zero to One and his take is just that as soon as we have these titles, you have a thing that people are competing for... you always had to earn your place in the company." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

**The data iceberg — 95% is integration, not analysis** — The real pain in large organizations isn't analytics but the invisible 95% of effort spent accessing, cleaning, joining, and normalizing data before any analysis can happen. "It's this iceberg analogy where the actual analysis is actually just the tip of the iceberg, it's kind of the last five or 10%, and the 95% before that is, I am gaining access to the data, I am cleaning the data, I'm joining the data, I'm normalizing it." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]

**Principles must be disagreement-forcing, not platitudes** — A useful principle is one that a reasonable person could disagree with — not something universally agreeable like "move fast." "They would write principles such as move fast and I would always be, 'Everyone likes to move fast.' It is not a good principle actually because nobody can really disagree with this reasonably." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nabeel-s-qureshi.md]
