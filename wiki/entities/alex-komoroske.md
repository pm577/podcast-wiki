# Alex Komoroske

**Role:** 13-year Google veteran (Chrome, Search, AR, Maps); former head of corporate strategy at Stripe; now founder  
**Known for:** Gardener vs. builder mindset, adjacent possible theory, strategy salons, pioneering systems-thinking approaches to product strategy.

## Key Views

### 1. Think like a gardener, not a builder — compound growth beats effort

Alex's central framework: the builder mindset ("I have a plan, then manipulate things to match it") can't create more value than the effort you put in. The gardener mindset finds things that can grow on their own and directs, curates, and nurtures them. When done properly, the results look like magic because they compound beyond your direct effort. This applies to products, teams, communities, and careers.

> "So much of the way we tackle problems is the builder mindset... it can't possibly create more value than the effort you put into it. What I look for instead are things that can be gardened, things that can grow on their own." — Alex Komoroske

### 2. The adjacent possible — where real innovation happens

Drawing from Stuart Kauffman's concept, Alex emphasizes that the most valuable innovations emerge at the boundary of what's currently possible — the "adjacent possible." His approach to strategy involves systematically exploring and expanding the adjacent possible rather than trying to predict the far future. This means creating conditions for serendipitous discoveries rather than forcing predetermined outcomes.

### 3. Organizational kayfabe — the unspoken narratives that block change

Alex introduces the concept of "organizational kayfabe" (borrowed from pro wrestling) — the shared fiction that everyone in an organization agrees to believe, even though they know it's not entirely true. These unspoken narratives are often the biggest blockers to strategic change. Identifying and gently exposing kayfabe is a high-leverage strategic act.

### 4. Strategy salons and the 17,000-note compendium

Alex's personal knowledge management system: a "Compendium" of 17,000+ unpublished working notes that he reviews, connects, and synthesizes daily. He convenes "strategy salons" — small, invite-only conversations with diverse thinkers — to pressure-test ideas at the edge of his thinking. His advice: the compound effect of daily note-taking and cross-pollination of ideas creates strategic insight that can't be achieved through episodic deep work alone.

### 5. LLMs as "magical duct tape" — don't use them as oracles

Alex's pragmatic take on AI: treat LLMs as cheap, universal duct tape for connecting systems and augmenting human capabilities — not as oracles that produce finished answers. The most powerful use cases come from assuming you have this magical connective tissue and asking "what can I build now that this is basically free?" rather than trying to get the model to replace human judgment wholesale.

> "How can you take LLMs for granted? How can you assume you now have this magical duct tape? Don't assume it's going to solve all your problems. Assume it's an inexpensive, universally available thing and then ask what you can do with it." — Alex Komoroske
