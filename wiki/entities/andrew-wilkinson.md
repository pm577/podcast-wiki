# Andrew Wilkinson

**Role:** Co-founder and CEO of Tiny (often called "Berkshire Hathaway of the internet"), owner of 40+ businesses including Dribbble, Letterboxd, AeroPress, and Serato  
**Known for:** Bootstrapped Tiny from zero to ~$300M in revenue, buying and holding profitable businesses long-term, unconventional startup advice, and deep AI automation.

## Key Views

### 1. Fish Where the Fish Are — The Boring Niche Advantage

Charlie Munger's most important business lesson: if every fisherman is elbowing each other at the popular pond, walk into the forest and find a fishing hole with lots of fish and no competition. Andrew's biggest mistakes were entering business models where others had repeatedly failed ("I can do this better"). He lost $10M trying to compete with venture-backed Asana (Flow project management app). The winning formula: boring industries people don't think about — software that fills out government forms ($30M/year), a DJ software company (Serato), a movie social network (Letterboxd). "Nobody wakes up and thinks 'I want to make form-filling software,' but they would if they could make $20M a year."^[episodes/lenny/andrew-wilkinson]

### 2. Lazy Leadership — Be Teflon for Tasks

Andrew's counterintuitive management philosophy: the goal is to get away from the things you hate as quickly as humanly possible. The pressure-washing business he recently started is a vehicle: at scale, the founder never touches a pressure washer, doing only sales and marketing. The distinction between a **business** (scalable) and a **job** (owner must do the work) is critical. "Lazy leadership" means architecting the business so you only do what you love.^[episodes/lenny/andrew-wilkinson]

### 3. Moat-First Investing — Brand, Network Effects, and "Hard to Mess Up"

When Tiny buys a business, they look for something so well-built they can't mess it up. The three moats Andrew values:
- **Brand** — pricing power and customer loyalty (like AeroPress, the coffee maker)
- **Network effects** — users won't leave because everyone they know is there (Letterboxd, Dribbble)
- **Switching costs** — painful to rip out (Salesforce — though Andrew dislikes this model because it's un-consumer-friendly)
The ideal acquisition: existing management stays, nothing changes, and the business compounds over decades.^[episodes/lenny/andrew-wilkinson]

### 4. The First-Time Entrepreneur's Gym Analogy

"You don't walk into the gym on day one and try to deadlift 300 pounds." First-time entrepreneurs who say "I'm going to build the next great AI company" are making the equivalent mistake. Andrew's first business was a simple web design agency (Metalab) — immediate positive feedback, low complexity. That first win built the confidence and narrative to keep going through later failures (pizzeria, designer cat furniture, online DJ school). The advice: start with something easy that gives you a win, then build up. Starting small also means you avoid competing with VC-backed giants — the "Things guy" (a small team running a to-do app for 20 years, living an amazing life) has won more than Dustin Moskovitz (Asana's billionaire co-founder), depending on how you define winning.^[episodes/lenny/andrew-wilkinson]

### 5. "There Are No Problems — Only People Problems"

Andrew's business partner Chris's truism: any business disaster is fine as long as you're surrounded by great people. A single bad actor (narcissist, psycho) makes everything fall apart. His hard-won hiring heuristics:
- **"If I ever think 'should I fire this person' even once, fire them immediately"** — superstars are unfireable in your mind; doubt is the signal
- **Hire fully formed, not for potential** — mentoring people into being good employees rarely works
- **"To a man with a hammer, everything looks like a nail"** — CEOs will inevitably do what their experience tells them, not what you ask; listen for what they *actually* want to do in interviews
- Spend 20% of time on filtering people carefully^[episodes/lenny/andrew-wilkinson]

### 6. AI as Personal Infrastructure — The Fully Automated Inbox

Andrew has automated his entire workflow using Lindy.ai (no personal connection — genuine super user). His email agent: (1) archives ~20% of thread-traffic he doesn't need to see, (2) labels time-sensitive items for 24-hour attention, (3) simple decisions (lunch invitations) are handled via multiple-choice — he picks an option and the agent composes and sends the reply. This replaced a full-time assistant. He views AI as "the world's most reliable employee who costs $200/month and works 24/7." Other automations include calendar management and emoji-enriching every calendar entry. His thesis: "Do all jobs just become a single prompt?"^[episodes/lenny/andrew-wilkinson]
