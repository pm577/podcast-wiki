# Ben Williams

**Role:** VP of Product at Snyk (developer security platform, $8.6B valuation); previously led product and growth at CloudBees; advisor and consultant in developer tooling  
**Known for:** Architecting Snyk's product-led growth engine in developer security, using a community-first, developer-centric approach to disrupt a traditionally top-down enterprise security market.

## Key Views

### 1. Developer-first security — disrupting a top-down market with bottom-up adoption

Ben explains how Snyk disrupted the security industry by inverting the traditional model. Instead of selling to CISOs who then impose tools on developers (creating friction and resistance), Snyk targeted developers directly with a free CLI tool that made security easy and integrated into their existing workflow. The founders started in a narrow niche: Node.js developers who wanted to check open source dependencies for vulnerabilities. They engaged deeply with the Node.js community — conferences, meetups, online content — and built a freemium product that developers actually wanted to use. This developer-led adoption then pulled in the enterprise sale.

> "They believed that the most effective way to improve application security posture was a developer-first approach. They knew that the developers were increasingly caring about the security of their code in the same way that they cared about performance and functional quality." — Ben Williams

### 2. Micro and macro growth loops — the qualitative model that drives strategy

Ben's approach to growth strategy: document every growth loop (micro and macro), how they connect, and build a qualitative model that creates shared understanding of how the company grows. Then augment with quantitative data for quarter-to-quarter focus. This prevents the common problem of a growth team drowning in ideas without knowing where to focus. Snyk's loops included community-driven awareness (conferences, open source contributions), product-driven acquisition (freemium CLI tool), and community-led sales (developers bringing Snyk to their organizations).

> "Being able to identify the various micro and macro loops, how they're all connected, being able to document them in a qualitative model to communicate a shared understanding of how you grow, it's really powerful." — Ben Williams

### 3. Start narrow, then expand — single persona, single context, single use case

Snyk's early focus was extremely narrow: developers building Node.js apps who wanted to check open source dependencies for vulnerabilities. This was a single persona, single context, single use case. The Node.js community was large enough to be meaningful but small enough that the founders could personally influence it. From this beachhead, Snyk expanded to additional languages, additional security domains (containers, IaC, cloud), and additional buyer personas (security teams, CISOs). The key: never lose the developer-first DNA while expanding upmarket.

### 4. Freemium is the go-to-market — not just a marketing tactic

From day one, Snyk was free to use in some capacity. The freemium model wasn't an afterthought or a lead-gen tactic — it was core to the product strategy. The founders believed that the only sustainably effective path for software organizations to become and stay secure was through developer-led adoption. Free usage creates pervasive adoption, which creates organizational pull, which drives the enterprise sale. Snyk had ~5,000 free users before any attempts at monetization. This patient, product-first approach built the foundation for $8.6B in valuation.

### 5. Product-led growth requires patience before monetization

Ben emphasizes that Snyk's PLG success required discipline: the founders focused on creating value, achieving pervasive adoption, and solving a real problem in a uniquely differentiated way long before figuring out monetization. The answer to "how does your product acquire and retain users" was always product-led — the answer to "how does it monetize" evolved over time. This patience is hard for startups under pressure to show revenue, but it's the path to building a durable PLG business.
