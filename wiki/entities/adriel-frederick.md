# Adriel Frederick

Adriel Frederick is VP of Product Management at Reddit X, where he incubates and scales new products. Previously he was Director of Product at Lyft (marketplace and pricing), an early PM on Facebook's growth team, and a consultant at McKinsey. He was Facebook's first Black PM.

## Key Views

**Algorithms Need People for Judgment Calls** — When working on algorithm-heavy products, a PM's core job is deciding what the algorithm should own and what humans should decide. Algorithms don't understand long-term effects, human responses, or product intent. Build systems as tools that amplify human intent, not black boxes that replace judgment. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/adriel-frederick.md]

**Growth Is Grinding on Core Actions, Not Hacks** — Real growth comes from making core actions stupid easy: finding the product, getting in, finding friends. Most wins aren't hacks — they're hard work on fundamentals like phone sign-up, SMS delivery worldwide, and friend recommendations. Use an 80/20 portfolio: 80% energy on big "cannonballs," 20% on small "lead bullets." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/adriel-frederick.md]

**The Marginal User Framework** — Focus on the person just on the cusp of the desired action in the worst conditions (feature phone, slow connection). Go watch them. Data hints at problems but doesn't give answers. The "10 friends in 14 days" metric was brilliant not for its precision but as a galvanizing organizational rallying cry. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/adriel-frederick.md]

**Diverse Teams Build Better Products Faster** — Growing up in Trinidad taught him that one phone number often maps to multiple people. A globally diverse team can resolve product debates with lived experience in minutes instead of weeks of user research. Diversity stops being "PC" and becomes business value when teams retain diverse talent and reward their perspectives. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/adriel-frederick.md]

**Senior Leadership Is Org Design + Empathy** — The two skills that matter most as you rise: (1) designing organizations with clear goals and smooth processes so teams can do their best work, and (2) taking off your own shoes to understand peers' motivations and fears, then finding common ground. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/adriel-frederick.md]
