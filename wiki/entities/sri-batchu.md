# Sri Batchu

**Role:** Head of Growth at Ramp (fastest-growing SaaS + fintech business in history); former Head of Growth at Instacart; VP of Ops at Opendoor  
**Known for:** Scaling growth at Ramp from $0 to $100M ARR in 2 years; pioneering the "cap table as a growth strategy"; building growth engineering teams that support sales; disciplined use of MECE frameworks for problem solving.

## Key Views

### 1. Cap Table as a Growth Strategy

Ramp's co-founders used their reputation and network to get influential startup founders and operators onto Ramp's cap table. Many of Ramp's earliest customers came from those cap-table connections. This "cap table as a growth strategy" created a strong beachhead in the tech community that Ramp leveraged before expanding to mid-market and enterprise.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

> "One of the things that they did is what I call cap table as a growth strategy, where they did a great job of getting a large number of early stage founders and other influential operators and advisors onto the cap table." — Sri Batchu

### 2. The B2B Growth Channel Progression

Sri has a clear framework for how B2B growth channels should evolve: start with founder-led sales, then hire dedicated salespeople, then begin low-cost targeted marketing (content, community, events), then PR, and only after all of that invest in paid marketing and SEO. The logic: channels get more expensive as you go further, and they become more effective as you understand more about your customers.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

> "The reason for the progression is the channels get more expensive as you go farther along and they get more effective as you understand more about your customers." — Sri Batchu

### 3. Growth Engineering as a Sales Multiplier

Rather than just having a product growth team, Ramp has a dedicated growth engineering team that supports sales efficiency. This team builds automation, lead scoring, prospecting tools, and messaging personalization — directly impacting pipeline. Engineers feel ownership of revenue metrics and pipeline quotas, not just product metrics. This "technologizing" of every channel is Ramp's growth differentiator.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

> "Our sales teams are incredibly efficient... because we have a growth engineering team dedicated to supporting that efficiency, including adjusting third party data and using AI to automate much of their workflow." — Sri Batchu

### 4. Velocity as Culture: Days, Not Quarters

Ramp's velocity culture is anchored by `days.ramp.com` — a live counter showing how many days since the company was founded. This counter appears at every board meeting and all-hands. It reinforces that decisions should be made in days, not weeks or quarters. The growth team works in two-week sprints with ruthless cross-prioritization. Cycle time reduction is the core cultural value.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

> "It's just to remind people that we don't work in years, quarters, weeks, we work in days. Each day matters and so never put out something tomorrow that you know can get done today." — Sri Batchu

### 5. MECE Problem Solving & Small Teams of 10X Operators

Sri advocates for MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) frameworks when attacking any growth problem: break revenue down by customers vs. revenue per customer, then by product line, then by acquisition/activation/retention. At each layer, exhaust all possible causes. On hiring, he believes in paying 10X operators 10X comp — small teams of exceptional people beat large teams of average ones. Talent density depends equally on retention and performance management, not just hiring.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

> "I strongly believe small teams of successful people can drive a lot more impact than larger teams of mediocre people. You have to design a system where you're able to reward 10X operators with 10X comp." — Sri Batchu
