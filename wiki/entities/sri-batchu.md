# Sri Batchu

Sri Batchu appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss lessons from scaling ramp — cap table as growth strategy, payback period, and failing conclusively.

## Key Views

**Cap Table as a Growth Strategy** — Ramp co-founders recruited influential founders and operators onto the company's cap table, and many early customers came directly from those connections, which Batchu calls "cap table as a growth strategy." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

**Payback Period Over CAC** — Batchu argues CAC ignores customer value and LTV-to-CAC is too assumption-laden for young companies; payback period based on contribution margin is more grounded in recency and easier to evaluate quickly. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

**Fail Conclusively, Not Just Fast** — In B2B you lack consumer-scale sample sizes, so you should maximize the treatment effect by throwing every lever at a hypothesis. If it still fails you can kill it permanently instead of revisiting it with each new executive. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

**Reward 10x Operators with 10x Pay** — Batchu strongly believes small teams of exceptional people outperform larger mediocre teams, so compensation systems should differentially reward top performers rather than focus on equality across levels. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]

**Growth Team Structure Is a Red Herring** — Batchu believes debating team structure is largely irrelevant; what actually matters is the culture, rituals, cadences, and a simple north star metric that lets people execute quickly and learn from failure. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sri-batchu.md]
