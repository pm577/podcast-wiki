# Jason Droege

**Role:** CEO of Scale AI (post-$14B Meta investment); former founder of Uber Eats (grew to $80B+ run rate); co-founder of Scour with Travis Kalanick  
**Known for:** Pioneering the data labeling and AI evaluation market via Scale AI; building Uber Eats from idea to industry-defining business; deep expertise in marketplace dynamics, entrepreneurship, and AI's operational realities.

## Key Views

### 1. AI Training is Shifting from Generalists to Experts — and Scale Led the Way

Jason's central thesis on the AI data market: the narrative that competitors spread about Scale being behind on the shift to expert labeling is "bogus." The market naturally evolved from basic preference ranking ("is story A better than story B?") to sophisticated expert tasks: PhDs building entire websites, doctors annotating complex medical cases, lawyers reviewing nuanced legal scenarios. Currently **80% of Scale's expert network has a bachelor's degree or greater, 15% have PhDs**. Scale anticipated and drove this shift by working directly with model builders to identify where models were falling short.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-droege.md]

> "These tasks now take hours of time and they require PhDs and professionals... The general trend right now is going from models knowing things to models doing things." — Jason Droege

### 2. Everything Is Negotiable — The Foundational Startup Lesson

From his Scour experience (founding a company at 19, getting sued for $250B, settling for $1M), Jason learned: "In business and in startups, everything's negotiable. There is no way to do things. There is just the way that you can negotiate your way through the world." This shaped his approach at Uber and Scale — if you can imagine it and align incentives, it can happen. The lesson from Scour: established companies don't have a playbook — they make up numbers too. Don't be intimidated.

### 3. Be Close to Customers by Understanding Their Incentives, Not Just What They Say

Jason's customer research approach: "I don't take what a customer says literally. I look at the underlying incentives of the customer — sometimes ego, sometimes career growth, not just financial." His Uber Eats example: he couldn't get truthful restaurant unit economics from restaurant tours, so he ordered food from restaurants, bought a supplier catalog, and **reverse-engineered ingredient costs himself**. By triangulating ground truth (ingredient costs) against what people told him, he found the real clearing price for delivery commissions. Observation and triangulation beat asking.

### 4. Enterprise AI Takes 6-12 Months to "Truly Robust" — The POC Gap

Jason's ground-truth take on the AI enterprise hype cycle: many POCs fail because getting from 60-70% accuracy to production-ready requires months of iteration, legal approvals, change management, and regulatory compliance. "Easy to learn, hard to master." The MIT study showing AI pilots failing isn't surprising — there's a denominator effect (so many easy experiments inflate the failure rate) and a "last mile" problem similar to data center uptime nines. The impact when you get it right is enormous, but the time to get there is longer than the hype cycle suggests.

### 5. The Bottleneck Is "Digitizing Human Judgment" — Why Humans Stay in the Loop

Jason's long-term view: "Digitizing judgment, human judgment, true subject matter deep expertise is becoming a bottleneck." The same words in a document have different meaning in Company A vs. Company B. Enterprise and government customers need models that understand their specific context, culture, and objectives. This requires domain-specific labeling and evaluation that will keep humans in the loop for the foreseeable future. Rather than a "white collar apocalypse," Jason sees human adaptability winning out, with AI becoming a practical tool rather than a replacement.


### 6. Scale Post-Meta: Fully Independent, Same Governance — Jason's detailed breakdown of the Meta deal structure: Meta invested $14B for 49% non-voting stock, took NO new board seat. Alex Wang fills the seat but now works at Meta. Scale remains fully independent with the same board and governance. Only 15 employees transferred. The business has grown every month since the deal closed, and Scale now has two unicorn-sized businesses inside it (each $100M+ revenue). The reporting around the deal was 'not consistently reported' — this is the first time the CEO has clarified the structure. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-10-09-jason-droege.md]

### 7. From Knowing to Doing: The Model Evolution — Jason's framing of the AI frontier: 'The general trend right now is going from models knowing things to models doing things.' The shift from language models answering questions to agents taking autonomous action is the most consequential transition in AI. The question shifts from 'what does the model know?' to 'how does the agent make decisions?' This creates entirely new categories of work — evaluating agent behavior, designing reward functions, and managing autonomous systems that interact with the real world. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-10-09-jason-droege.md]

### 8. The Scour Story: $250B Lawsuit, $1M Settlement, Everything Is Negotiable — Jason's foundational startup lesson: at 19, his peer-to-peer search engine Scour was sued by entertainment industry associations for a quarter of a trillion dollars. They settled for $1M. The experience taught him that 'established companies don't have a playbook to follow — they just make up numbers.' This shaped the Uber ethos: if you can imagine something and align incentives, it can happen. The lesson from Scour that he carried through Uber Eats and Scale: there is no 'way' to do business — there is only what you can negotiate. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-10-09-jason-droege.md]
