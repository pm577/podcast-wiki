# Kayvon Beykpour

Kayvon Beykpour appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss twitter’s ex-head of product on elon, consumer products, culture, and more.

## Key Views

**Sacred cows are your roadmap — the easiest innovation is doing what everyone said you can’t** — Kayvon’s playbook for transforming Twitter’s stagnant product culture: make a list of everything the org believes is impossible or taboo (reverse-chronological feed, 140 characters, hiding replies, controlling reply spaces), then put that list on the roadmap. "The sacred cows are their own roadmap." Each one you address reveals deeper cultural hesitations and unlocks compounding momentum. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]

**Acquihire entrepreneurs to inject urgency, not just talent** — Twitter’s most ambitious bets (Spaces, Communities, Community Notes, Fleets, Super Follows) were all led by founders whose companies Twitter acquired. The pattern: bring in hungry, capable builders who combine startup urgency with the savvy to navigate a large org. "A lot of them were driven by founders who we basically acquired and said, ‘You are going to run this.’" ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]

**Refining the core is anti-inspirational but necessary — the portfolio balance problem** — Kayvon observed that Twitter’s growth recovery came from "refine the core" (ranked timeline, better notifications) — not Big Bets. But this calcified risk-aversion. The real challenge is maintaining a portfolio: reliable core improvements for growth, plus a separate funnel for speculative bets that the org would otherwise suffocate. "We wanted to create a better balance." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]

**Staff projects with believers, not whoever is available** — A major lesson from Twitter: when teams are staffed by whoever’s available rather than who believes in the idea, projects fail. Hiding replies had an engineer tell the PM "don’t work on this, it’s bad for your career" because it was culturally anathema. Kayvon’s rule: speculative projects need a team that is irrationally obsessed with the idea, not just competent. "The only way you’re going to get through that pain is by having that desire." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]

**Being fired during paternity leave taught him to come to peace with losing agency** — Kayvon was fired by then-CEO Parag Agrawal the day after coming home from the hospital with his newborn, and the same night Twitter signed a term sheet with Elon Musk. His reflection: the silver lining was spending his daughter’s first year with family and avoiding the chaos that followed. "It took me a while to come to terms with it and to be at peace with it. There’s a huge silver lining." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]

# Kayvon Beykpour

Kayvon Beykpour was the longest-serving head of product at Twitter and GM of its consumer division until Elon Musk acquired the company. He originally joined Twitter through the acquisition of Periscope (the world's largest live-streaming platform), which inspired Instagram Live, TikTok Live, and Facebook Live.

## Key Views

**Sacred Cows Are Your Roadmap — Attack What Everyone Thinks Can't Change**
Beykpour transformed Twitter's stagnant product culture by identifying "sacred cows" — features and conventions everyone assumed were untouchable. "What are all the things that you think we're not allowed to change? Let's start there." This reframing turned cultural inertia into a product roadmap. By challenging long-held assumptions (280 characters, chronological timeline, lack of features), his team shipped Super Follows, Communities, Spaces, Fleets, and more in rapid succession.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]]**

**Hire Hungry Up-and-Comers to Break Through Institutional Inertia**
Beykpour used acqui-hires and promoted emerging leaders to drive change. Rather than relying on tenured executives who were invested in the status quo, he found talented product leaders who had something to prove. "I might flame out completely, but hell if I don't try." This hunger created a culture where people were willing to take risks and ship aggressively — the antidote to the "lack of ambition, lack of creativity" that had plagued Twitter.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]]**

**The First Time I Met Elon — A FaceTime Swipe Left or Right**
Beykpour shares a surreal glimpse of Musk's acquisition process: "The first time I ever met Elon was over FaceTime. He was just like, 'Do you want to just come hang out? You can swipe left or swipe right.'" This casual approach to one of the largest tech acquisitions in history set the tone for the chaos that followed. Beykpour was eventually fired during paternity leave — a story he tells with remarkable equanimity.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]]**

**Jobs-to-Be-Done Thinking Unlocks New Product Categories**
Beykpour applied jobs-to-be-done thinking to identify new product opportunities at Twitter. Instead of asking "what features should we add?", his teams asked "what job is the user hiring Twitter to do?" This reframing led to products like Spaces (live audio for real-time conversation), Communities (niche interest groups), and Super Follows (creator monetization) — each addressing a distinct user job that the core tweet experience wasn't fulfilling.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]]**

**The Periscope Lesson: Know When to Shut Down and What You Built**
Beykpour reflects on building and eventually shutting down Periscope. The hardest lesson: knowing when a product has reached its ceiling. Periscope was a category creator, but the feature was ultimately absorbed by larger platforms (Instagram, TikTok, Facebook). His advice: "Know what you actually built." Sometimes the lasting impact isn't your company — it's the feature that becomes standard across the industry, which is a different kind of success.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kayvon-beykpour.md]]**
