# Kevin Yien

Leads product for merchant experiences at Stripe. Previously built the restaurants business and ecosystem teams at Square, and was Head of Product and Design at Mutiny. Known for unorthodox PM wisdom, including decision logs, "unsell" emails in hiring, and automating customer research.

## Key Views

### 1. Don't Go Straight Into Product Management

The purpose of product management is to convert a team's potential energy into realized value. The people who should do this early on are the people building. Starting as an engineer, designer, salesperson, or support gives you a foundation of direct experience. "That's the unique perspective you bring and allows you to take on a role of PM in a good, unique, insightful way." The best PMs come from adjacent disciplines.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-yien.md]

### 2. Keep a Decision Log

"Product sense is just a fancy way of saying you can make good decisions with insufficient data." To build that skill, PMs need as many reps as possible making decisions, documenting the rationale, and — crucially — seeing the outcome. A decision log creates a feedback loop that sharpens judgment over time. The format: what was the decision, what was the context, what did you expect to happen, what actually happened?^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-yien.md]

### 3. The "Unsell" Email for Hiring

When a candidate reaches the offer stage, Yien sends them an email listing all the terrible things about the role — the real fears and downsides. "If you can tell them that upfront and they can read that whole email and still be equally excited to join, you've found yourself an A+ hire." This filters for people who join with eyes wide open and reduces early attrition.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-yien.md]

### 4. Automate Your Customer Research

Traditional user research is slow and doesn't scale. Yien advocates building automated systems that continuously surface customer insights — using AI to analyze support tickets, sales calls, and product usage data. The goal is to have a constant stream of structured insights without requiring a researcher to conduct every interview. This keeps the PM closer to the customer without the overhead.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-yien.md]

### 5. If You Can't Sell or Support Your Product, Don't Build It

"The PM job can become a little too internal — influencing stakeholders and getting alignment. But if you can't sell or support your own product, I don't trust you to build the product." PMs should spend time on the front lines: sales calls, support tickets, demos. This keeps them grounded in real user needs rather than internal politics.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-yien.md]

### 6. The PM-to-Engineer Trust Principle

The most important relationship a PM has is with their engineering team. Building trust requires (1) understanding the technical trade-offs deeply enough to credibly discuss them, (2) never committing the team to something without their input, and (3) being the first to take accountability when things go wrong.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/kevin-yien.md]
