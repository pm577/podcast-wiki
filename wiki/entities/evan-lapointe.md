# Evan LaPointe

Evan LaPointe is the founder of CORE Sciences, which teaches companies and individuals how the brain works and how to apply that understanding to improve collaboration, build better products, make faster decisions, and become more influential. He is a four-time founder, including founding Satellite (the fourth largest analytics product on the internet), which was acquired by Adobe, where he later ran product strategy and innovation.

## Key Views

**The Brain as a College Campus — Send Ideas to the Right Department**
Evan teaches that the brain has distinct systems — a science department (open-minded experimentation), an art department (creative thinking), and a history department (reference and recall). Most people over-rely on their history department because it's the lowest-energy path. The key to better strategy, vision, and product decisions is deliberately routing your thinking to the more creative and experimental parts of your brain: "If you instead send things to the more experimental, open-minded science department, the more creative art department, you get dramatically better answers." Understanding this also helps teams recognize why different people approach problems so differently.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/evan-lapointe.md]]*

**Safety, Reward, and Purpose — The Three Core Brain Systems at Work**
LaPointe's foundational framework for human performance is built on three brain systems: safety (stress, fear, anger), reward (pursuit, desire, transaction), and purpose (impact, understanding, connection). In a meeting, if someone feels unsafe, their brain shifts from productive contribution to seeking safety. Similarly, when someone says "that's not my job," it's the reward system speaking — it's not on their reward list. Purpose — understanding the impact of your work on people — can operate at vanishingly small scales, not just saving-the-world levels. Leaders and managers who understand which system is active can design environments and communications that activate the right one.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/evan-lapointe.md]]*

**The Habitat Terrarium — Design the Environment Before Fighting Hand-to-Hand**
A company or team is like a terrarium: if the environment is set up wrong (too hot, too dry, devoid of life), individual struggles are just a function of the habitat. Most established teams don't design their habitats for high-functioning thinking and interaction. Companies like Canva and Figma invest heavily in creating environments that predispose people to do their best work — e.g., coaches instead of managers at Canva. LaPointe's lens: "There's a mismatch between what science knows and what business does," and the gap equals dysfunction. Shrinking that gap requires intentionally designing the habitat, not just fighting interpersonal fires.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/evan-lapointe.md]]*

**Meetings Are Products — Prime Before You Decide**
Most meetings skip the priming step (aligning on context, principles, and desired outcomes) and jump straight to decision-making — a huge source of wasted time. LaPointe's research shows the average team saves 10–20% of meeting time by fixing this, translating to a half to full day per week. Priming can be as simple as writing 3 minutes of context in the invite: the category of conversation (creative problem-solving vs. tactical execution), the principles that apply, and the mode of thinking. Without this, people enter with fundamentally different assumptions (e.g., one person optimizing for speed, another for accuracy), leading to inevitable "cat fights." The meeting design itself is a product that should be treated with intentionality.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/evan-lapointe.md]]*

**Know Your Brain's Thresholds — Believed, Believable, Conceivable, Inconceivable**
Ideas get sorted by the brain into four buckets based on lived experience and personality. The "inconceivable" threshold is especially dangerous for leaders — it's where truly novel ideas land, and most executives reflexively reject them because their brains are wired to find certain types of thinking painful (e.g., low openness on the Big Five makes abstract thinking literally painful). LaPointe's framework for strategic thinking requires knowing where your own thresholds lie and practicing vulnerability: "Vulnerability is the best option," because the alternatives (hiding it or being unapologetic) are ruinous. The path through is recognizing that "that's a personal problem, not an objective problem" when an idea feels inconceivable.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/evan-lapointe.md]]*
