# Tom Conrad

Tom Conrad appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss billion dollar failures, and billion dollar success.

## Key Views

**Not everyone needs to be a founder** — Tom argues that the startup world's obsession with founding is misguided; many talented people would be better off joining an existing team with a winning formula. "There's this belief that everybody needs to be a founder. I think, in some ways, our industry would be much better off if there were fewer founders. There's an entire category of smart, creative, hardworking, talented, borderline visionary people who can raise that $2 million seed and go off and build some stupid company that's never going to go anywhere." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tom-conrad.md]

**Companies are math equations** — Late in his career, Tom realized that beyond product craft and design, every company is fundamentally an equation describing how investment converts into returns — and if that equation is broken, no amount of iteration can fix it. "While companies are definitely the software that they make and the products that they deliver and that there is an art and science to that… there is also, if you step back, there is an equation that describes the business. It talks about how you turn investment into returns." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tom-conrad.md]

**Failures become dominoes** — Rather than seeing Pets.com and Quibi as career scars, Tom views them as doors that opened unexpected future opportunities and taught lessons that success never could. "Probably the biggest lesson… these things make you better. In some instances, actually, I think in both instances they became dominoes that opened doors for me in my own ambition and my own professional life that maybe just wouldn't have opened at all if I hadn't gone to those companies." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tom-conrad.md]

**Authenticity over polish** — At Pandora, Tom made the radical choice to have zero customer support team and route every email to every employee, creating genuine human interactions that fueled word-of-mouth growth without any paid acquisition. "We did all of that because we thought one of our superpowers as a young company was that we could just engage with our audience as real human beings, not as a shiny brand… And we never spent any money on paid user acquisition, literally no dollars in the entire time I was there, because people loved the product and they told their friends about it." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tom-conrad.md]

**Take big swings like Evan** — Working alongside Evan Spiegel at Snap taught Tom that having the financial backing and conviction to make speculative, high-risk bets — even when conventional planning would prune them — is essential for breakthrough success. "When you have the financial support and the foundational relationship with your investors that Evan has, it really allowed him to take these really big swings, acquire a technology that he thought was game changing, build features speculatively that maybe would be pruned in any other product roadmap planning process." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tom-conrad.md]
