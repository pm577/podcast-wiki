# Robby Stein

Robby Stein is VP of Product at Google, where he oversees core Google Search products including AI Overviews, AI Mode, search ranking, and Google Lens. He was previously Head of Product at Instagram, where he led the launch of Stories, Reels, and Close Friends, growing Instagram to 500M+ daily active users. He was also on the founding team of Artifact with Mike Krieger and Kevin Systrom.

## Key Views

### Relentless Improvement Compounds Into Tipping Points
Robby's product philosophy centers on "embodying relentless improvement" — exerting complete effort in the direction of positive productivity, always making things better, and never being content. He believes most product momentum comes from compounding effects, not single changes. "A lot of times people ascribe a lot of momentum to a one-time change or a single person. I find a lot of this is actually this compounding effect when you think about just every month ruthlessly improving the product." This is what he credits for Google's AI turnaround.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-10-10-robby-stein.md]

### Not Every Great Thing Needs to Be Invented by You
On launching Instagram Stories (widely seen as copying Snapchat): "Not every great thing is going to be invented by you. Facebook probably created the modern feed, but there's a feed for every single product. At the end of the day, you're just robbing your user base of the opportunity to have a better product." This is a pragmatic view of product competition — users benefit when great patterns are adapted and improved for new contexts.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-10-10-robby-stein.md]

### AI Is Expansionary for Search, Not Destructive
Contrary to the narrative that AI is killing traditional search, Robby argues AI is making search grow. Core search needs (finding a phone number, getting directions, checking prices) haven't changed. What's new is the expansion — Google Lens is growing 70% year-over-year, enabling entirely new categories of questions like taking a picture of shoes and asking where to buy them. "There's actually just more and more questions being asked and curiosity that can be fulfilled now with AI."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-10-10-robby-stein.md]

### Urgency Is the Product of Focus, Not Panic
Robby describes Google's current culture as having "an incredible sense of focus and urgency to deliver great products quickly." This isn't panic — it's the result of product teams and research groups (DeepMind) working closely together with clear priorities. He credits leadership for creating this environment but emphasizes it's primarily the compounding effect of focused, monthly improvement cycles that created Google's AI tipping point.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-10-10-robby-stein.md]

### The Vastness of Search Use Is Underappreciated
Robby believes outsiders dramatically underestimate the range of things people use search for — from specific phone numbers to tax payment pages to homework help. This vastness is why search isn't going away. AI doesn't replace these diverse, practical needs — it adds new capabilities on top. "What's interesting is people come to search for just ridiculously wide set of things... I think the vastness of that is underappreciated by many people."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lenny-2025-10-10-robby-stein.md]
