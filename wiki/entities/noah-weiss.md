# Noah Weiss

Noah Weiss appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss the 10 traits of great pms, ai, and slack's approach to product.

## Key Views

**The U-Curve of Founder Involvement** — When working with product-minded founder-CEOs, involve them heavily at the start (strategic buy-in, principles, goals) and at the very end (quality check in working software), but give the team space to explore in the middle. "What I think is important there is that hopefully you're refining in code and you're not still at static design mocks, because using the software is so different than looking at what the software will visually appear." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

**Complaint Storms as Product Discovery** — Slack's most powerful discovery tool is running structured sessions where internal teams surface every customer complaint they've heard, then cluster and prioritize them. This led directly to fixing day-one workspace setup as Slack's biggest self-service growth lever. "We did this thing called Complaint Storms ... we just wrote every single complaint we've ever heard from customers." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

**The Mini-CEO Myth is Dangerous** — The most dangerous piece of advice in product management is telling PMs to act like mini-CEOs. The real job is facilitating the pace and quality of decision-making, not making all decisions yourself. "I think that is how you end up having PMs who try to act like dictators instead of leaders and facilitators." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

**Impact Solves All PM Issues** — If a team is consistently building things people love and moving business metrics, everything else is secondary. The path to career growth as a PM isn't visibility or politics — it's relentlessly driving impact. "If a team is consistently building things people love and changing the direction of the business, everything else is just an input." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

**Data Fluency + Product Taste = Power Combo** — Great PMs need both deep command of customer insights (qualitative and quantitative) and cultivated product taste — the intuition for what people will love before testing. "I actually think taste plus fluency in data, that combination, is a pretty powerful combo." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

# Noah Weiss

Noah Weiss is Chief Product Officer at Slack, where he leads all aspects of the product organization including the self-service SMB business and the teams that launched Huddles and Clips. Before Slack, he was Head of Product at Foursquare, a PM at Google (where he worked on Google Maps and Google+), and started his career at Fog Creek Software.

## Key Views

### Take Bigger Boulder Bets — Get to the Next Hill
Noah's core mental model for product strategy: teams often get lost crawling up one hill, not realizing there's a whole mountain range behind it. Slack incubated new teams from scratch for "boulder bets" — big product areas like huddles and clips — before the existing areas matured. In AI, they prototype with small teams, give them space to run and pilot, and then launch something that "blows people away." The lesson: don't stop at incremental improvement; look for the next hill entirely.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

### The 10 Traits of Great Product Managers
Noah has identified 10 key traits that distinguish great PMs: (1) customer empathy, (2) strong product intuition, (3) ability to say no, (4) data-informed decision-making, (5) excellent communication, (6) technical fluency, (7) strategic thinking, (8) execution focus, (9) leadership without authority, and (10) relentless learning. He emphasizes that no one is born with all of them — they're developed through deliberate practice and diverse experiences.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

### The U-Curve Model for Working With Founder-CEOs
Working with strong product-minded founders (like Stewart Butterfield at Slack or Dennis Crowley at Foursquare) follows a U-curve. Get the founder involved early (the left side of the U) for strategic alignment on goals and principles. Then let the team run and explore in the middle. Bring the founder back at the end (the right side) for a collaborative "bug bash" where everyone refines together. If you skip that final step, the founder won't feel ownership and the launch will be painful.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

### Product Principles as the Language of the Company
Noah advocates distilling a founder's product intuition into codified principles that become the common language of the organization. At Slack, these include "be a great host" — relentlessly saving people's steps with foresight, just like putting clean towels on the bed at an Airbnb. Without principles, every review becomes a Goldilocks exercise where the founder says "no, not quite right" without a constructive framework for discussion.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]

### Complaint Storms — A Method for Better Product
Slack uses a technique called "Complaint Storms" where teams gather user complaints about a product area, cluster them into themes, and derive the underlying needs. This surface-level negative feedback is actually rich with product insight. The key is to listen for what users wish was true — not just what they say is broken. This has been one of Slack's most reliable methods for identifying high-impact product opportunities.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noah-weiss.md]
