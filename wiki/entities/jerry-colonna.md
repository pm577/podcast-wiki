# Jerry Colonna

**Role:** Co-founder and CEO of Reboot (executive coaching firm); former venture capitalist (co-founded Flatiron Partners with Fred Wilson); former partner at JPMorgan Chase; author of *Reboot* and *Reunion*  
**Known for:** His signature question — "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"; combining practical leadership development with deep psychological self-inquiry; pioneering executive coaching for startup founders and tech leaders.

## Key Views

### 1. The Core Question: "How Have I Been Complicit?"

Jerry's most famous question: "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?" The word "complicit" is deliberate — it means "accomplice," not "responsible." You're driving the getaway car, not sticking up the bank teller. The purpose is to **evoke agency**, not blame. Example: "I say I don't want to feel busy all the time, but I feel unnerved if my agenda isn't jam-packed." The question cuts through self-delusion and reveals how we unconsciously participate in our own suffering.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jerry-colonna.md]

> "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want? The purpose of this question is actually to evoke your own agency." — Jerry Colonna

### 2. The Equation: Practical Skills + Radical Self-Inquiry + Shared Experiences = Enhanced Leadership + Greater Resilience

Jerry's leadership development framework:
- **Practical skills** — the "how" that people typically want from a coach
- **Radical self-inquiry** — asking the scary questions: "What am I not saying that I need to say? What am I saying that's not being heard? What's being said that I'm not hearing?"
- **Shared experiences** — sitting in a circle of people who have your back, where you can tell the truth without being fixed or judged
- **Greater resilience** — is the *purpose* of the equation: "I get you want to be a great CEO. What I really care about is you not killing yourself in the process."

### 3. You Don't Have to Be an Asshole to Be Successful

Jerry's most validating message: the idea that you must be miserable or cruel to succeed is "the big lie we're socialized with since childhood." A 19-year-old at one of his talks summarized: "So what you're telling me is you don't have to be an asshole to be successful." The corollary: you don't have to feel miserable just because you're trying to create a career. But success does require doing your inner work — the unsorted baggage will surface eventually, and the price is paid in tears.

### 4. The Busyness Trap — Running from Ourselves

Jerry identifies "busyness" as a primary way leaders avoid self-inquiry. The drive to constantly grow, achieve, and stay busy masks deeper questions about safety, self-worth, and purpose. His personal story: his "lemon drops" — an endless supply of candy from his grandfather that he associated with wealth and safety. When he achieved outward success as a VC, he had lemon drops but didn't feel safe. The hack: **consciousness** — asking "What will that achievement do for me? How do I define success? Where does that definition come from?"

### 5. Radical Self-Inquiry Questions to Ask Yourself

Jerry's toolkit of powerful questions:
- How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?
- What am I not saying that I need to say?
- What am I saying that's not being heard?
- What's being said that I'm not hearing?
- Who would you be without the story of who you are?
- What is it that you wish people in your life knew about you, but you're too afraid to tell them?

"You know you're in the radical self-inquiry zone when the questions take your breath away."
