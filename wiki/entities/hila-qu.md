# Hila Qu

**Role:** Executive in Residence at Reforge; growth advisor, angel investor, and published author (her book was named one of the top 10 business books)  
**Known for:** Leading product-led growth (PLG) at GitLab; her two-part series on "How to Add a PLG Motion" is among the most-read guest posts in Lenny's Newsletter history.

## Key Views

### 1. PLG Is Fundamentally Data-Led Growth (DLG)

Hila's core framework: "PLG is fundamentally DLG — data-led growth." When you offer a free product, you get two things in exchange: **(1)** broader reach (lower barrier to entry), and **(2)** usage data on what features correlate with conversion and retention. If you don't have a data foundation to analyze usage patterns, you're "giving away free product for nothing." This is the most common reason PLG initiatives fail — companies launch a free tier without the instrumentation to understand what drives value.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hila-qu.md]

> "I always say is actually fundamentally DLG, data led growth. So when you give away your free product, what you want to get in exchange are two things. One is a broader reach... Two, you want to understand you usage behavior of those free users." — Hila Qu

### 2. PLG and SLG Converge — Every Company Eventually Needs Both

Hila rejects the PLG-vs-SLG binary. Her observation from working with dozens of companies: **everyone ends up with both**. Pure PLG companies (like Zoom and Canva) eventually add sales teams to close enterprise deals. Pure sales-led companies (like traditional enterprise SaaS) increasingly need PLG to compete with more accessible alternatives. The question isn't whether to do both, but when to add the second motion. It's easier to add sales to a PLG foundation than vice versa.

### 3. The PLG Funnel Is Fundamentally Different from the Sales Funnel

The critical distinction: in a sales-led funnel, the signal is *marketing engagement* — did they open emails, attend webinars, download whitepapers? In a PLG funnel, the signal is *product usage* — did they sign up, use features, hit the aha moment? This creates two conversion paths: **(1)** **Self-serve** — low-price products where users buy with a credit card (automated, efficient); **(2)** **PQL/Sales pass** — high-usage accounts from target companies that get routed to sales for white-glove treatment and larger deals.

### 4. The PLG Foundation: Vehicle, Time-to-Value, Self-Serve Checkout, Data

Hila identifies four essential components for any PLG motion:
1. **Vehicle** — A free version, free trial, or realistic interactive demo that lets users experience value without talking to sales
2. **Time-to-value** — Rapid activation; give users mini "aha moments" quickly, not a single big one
3. **Self-serve checkout** — Simple pricing, localized payments, no friction in the buying process
4. **Data foundation** — Instrument everything to understand which behaviors predict retention and conversion

### 5. The Activation "Aha Moment" — How GitLab Found Theirs

Hila walks through GitLab's process for finding their activation metric: brainstorm potential high-value actions, run correlation analysis against 90-day conversion and retention rates, then validate with experimentation. GitLab's aha moment: **2 users using 2+ features within 14 days**. This captures both the team collaboration aspect (inviting a coworker = seeing value) and the platform aspect (using multiple features = platform stickiness). The process reveals that activation isn't a single action for complex B2B products — it's a combination that must be discovered through data, not assumed.
