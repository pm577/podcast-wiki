# Christopher Lochhead

*14-time #1 bestselling author (Play Bigger, Niche Down), podcaster, creator of Category Pirates Substack, and former 3x public company CMO. Known as the "Godfather of Category Design."*

## Key Views

1. **Design your own category instead of competing**: In tech markets, one company captures ~76% of total market cap. Most entrepreneurs unwittingly decide to "compete in an existing market with a better product" — fighting for the remaining 24%. Legendary companies didn't do that; they created new categories. The category makes the brand.

2. **The "Better" Trap is dangerous**: Most business strategy is about how to be better than competitors. Nobody legendary ever got there that way. Picasso wasn't the 5th best cubist — he created cubism. Category design means creating a new distinction in value that didn't exist before — like Gojo creating "liquid soap" because bar soap was disgusting.

3. **Negative reviews are a badge of honor**: Lochhead prominently displays negative reviews on his website because if you're trying to do anything exponential, you will be criticized. Elvis, the Beatles, and Picasso all were. Instead of fearing criticism, embrace it — it means you're breaking new ground. If nobody hates what you're doing, you're probably not being radical enough.

4. **Focus on the problem, not just the solution**: Most innovators fall in love with their product. Category designers stay obsessed with the problem. Gojo didn't just create better bar soap — they reimagined "how do I wash my hands without disgusting soap?" and then "how do I wash my hands without water?" Each problem reframe created a new category (liquid soap, then hand sanitizer).

5. **Create demand vs. capture demand**: Most companies actively invite comparison (Gartner reports, feature comparison tables). Category pirates refuse to compare — they want to be irreplaceable. The goal isn't "better than" — it's creating a market where the switching costs to even think about switching are "switch to what?" There is no alternative because you defined the category.
