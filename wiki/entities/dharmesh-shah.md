# Dharmesh Shah

Dharmesh Shah is the co-founder and CTO of HubSpot (valued at $30B). He is the keeper of HubSpot's culture and one of the most fascinating first-principles thinkers in tech. Known for having zero direct reports in 18+ years at HubSpot, building SoloWare (software for one person), buying chat.com for $15M+, and measuring laughs-per-minute (LPM) in his keynotes, he brings an engineer's decompositional mindset to culture, strategy, and public speaking.

## Key Views

**Lean Into Your Strengths — Don't Become Passably Okay at Management**
Dharmesh has never had a single direct report in HubSpot's entire history. He made this deal with co-founder Brian Halligan at the founding meeting: "I could become passively okay at management with some training... I don't want to spend any years of my life becoming passively okay at something." Instead, he focused on what he was naturally good at and enjoyed — building, creating, code, and culture. This decision allowed him to survive and thrive as HubSpot grew from 70 to 7,000 people. "I get all the upside of scale without the downsides of scale." His framework: talent controls the slope of the learning curve, but most things are acquirable skills — choose which skills to acquire based on what you're already good at and enjoy.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/dharmesh-shah.md]]*

**High Conviction, Low Consensus — Be Right About What Others Think You're Wrong About**
Dharmesh's framework for contrarian bets: "High conviction, low consensus." HubSpot's defining bet was focusing on SMB as their core market — not as a stepping stone to enterprise, but as their permanent identity. For 18 years, investors and board members asked "what's the path to enterprise?" and the answer was always "we are here for SMB." This framing (borrowed from Peter Thiel's "what important truth do few people agree with you on?") requires the discipline to limit how many contrarian bets you make. You need to be right about a few things everyone else is wrong about, for a very long time — not contrarian about everything.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/dharmesh-shah.md]]*

**Simplicity as a First Principle — Binary Decisions Avoid Politics**
Dharmesh believes that binary decisions (transparent vs. not transparent; all information shared vs. none) are dramatically simpler than continuous scales. HubSpot's radical transparency emerged from a simple question: what files should employee #1 have access to? "All of it. Nothing to hide." Binary decisions scale — "if it works for employee #3, why wouldn't it at employee #4, #5, #50?" The seat lottery is another example: instead of office politics over window seats, every quarter everyone shuffles seats via lottery. "Can you imagine the amount of politics we avoided?" The second law of thermodynamics (entropy increases in closed systems) applies to companies — simplicity is the intervention that fights decay.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/dharmesh-shah.md]]**

**Public Speaking as an Engineering Problem — Measuring Laughs Per Minute**
Dharmesh decomposed public speaking into sub-skills like an engineer: standing on stage, holding attention, humor writing, execution. He built custom software to measure LPM (laughs per minute) in his talks, transcribing practice runs and identifying dead zones. "If you're solving literally for that ratio of laughs per minute, there are two ways to improve it: add more laughs, or decrease the number of words between laughs." His tactical tips: put the punchline as the last words of a segment (so the audience has permission to laugh), and within a single setup story, pack multiple punchlines to amortize the investment. This data-driven approach turned a natural weakness into a signature strength.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/dharmesh-shah.md]]*

**SoloWare — Build Software for an Audience of One**
Dharmesh has been building SoloWare for 30 years — software built for exactly one person: himself. His LPM measurement tool, his solo apps — they have no UI testing burden, no user support, and can be abandoned the instant they stop being useful. The principle: "if it stops bringing utility, you can just turn it off." This stands in stark contrast to the VC-funded growth-at-all-costs playbook. Some of his solo projects (like Wordplay, which made $90K/month) accidentally became micro-ware, but the starting point is always solving his own problem. The lesson for founders: start with a problem you deeply understand and care about, not a market-size calculation.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/dharmesh-shah.md]]*
