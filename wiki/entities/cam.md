# Cameron "Cam" Adams

**Role:** Co-founder and Chief Product Officer at Canva ($2.3B ARR, profitable, 60% YoY growth); former designer at Google (Google Wave)  
**Known for:** Canva's unique "coaches not managers" culture, building a design democratization platform bigger than Figma + Miro + Webflow combined, and their freemium-led hypergrowth.

## Key Views

### 1. Coaches not managers — Canva's unique people development system

Cam describes Canva's alternative to traditional management: everyone has a **coach**, not a manager. Coaches work with individuals on skill development, career progression, and "finding joy in building a team." This isn't about performance evaluation — it's about growth. The coach helps you identify when it's time to level up and gives you the tools to do so. This system prevents the common problem where managers are both evaluators and developers, a conflict of interest that limits real growth.

> "We don't really have managers, but everyone at Canva has a coach. They're constantly working with you to look at your skills, but also when it might be time to move on to the next level." — Cameron Adams

### 2. Freemium as a growth engine — not just a lead gen tactic

Canva's freemium model is core to its success. From the beginning, the product was designed to be genuinely useful for free, with premium features as upgrades. This approach created massive organic adoption (95% of Fortune 500 companies use Canva) and a natural upgrade path as users' needs grew. The key insight: the free product must deliver real standalone value, not just be a limited trial. This builds trust, word-of-mouth, and a product that sells itself.

### 3. Product management at Canva — deeply connected to teams

Cam deliberately chose NOT to replicate Google's PM model at Canva. Instead of independent PMs who sit between teams, Canva's PMs are "really connected" to their teams. They own outcomes, not just features. They work in tight triads with design and engineering, and they're expected to understand the market deeply. The PM role is less about process management and more about ensuring the team is building the right thing for the right user, with a clear connection to business outcomes.

> "I didn't want to do product management like they did at Google … I have seen product managers at other companies who are very independent of teams and that seems very weird to me. For us, product managers are really connected." — Cameron Adams

### 4. "Giving away your Legos" — building a culture of shared ownership

One of Canva's cultural pillars is "giving away your Legos" — finding joy in passing on your experience and helping others do great work. Instead of hoarding the most interesting projects, leaders are encouraged to delegate and mentor. This builds a scalable culture where senior people multiply their impact by developing others, and the organization doesn't bottleneck on a few key individuals. It's the opposite of "hero culture" — it's builder culture.

> "I run everyone through the culture of Canva. One of those sections is on giving away your Lego, finding joy in the other things of building a team, passing on your experience, helping other people do great writing or great product building or great engineering." — Cameron Adams

### 5. Embracing AI while staying true to the mission

Canva has aggressively integrated AI features (Magic Studio, text-to-image, AI-powered design suggestions) while maintaining its core mission of democratizing design. Cam's approach: AI should lower the barrier to creating great design even further — not replace the creator, but remove friction. Canva's AI features are designed to make non-designers look like designers, not to make designers obsolete. This mission-aligned approach to AI adoption has helped Canva accelerate growth (60% YoY, and accelerating) while staying true to its founding vision.
