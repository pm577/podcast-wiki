# Chip Conley

**Role:** Founder of Modern Elder Academy (MEA), the world's first midlife wisdom school; former executive at Airbnb (Head of Global Hospitality & Strategy); founder of Joie de Vivre Hotels (second-largest boutique hotel chain in the US); author of 7 books; TED speaker  
**Known for:** Being "the Modern Elder" — bringing wisdom and life experience to the tech world, mentoring Brian Chesky, reframing midlife as a chrysalis (not a crisis), and his philosophy of being "both wise and curious."

## Key Views

### 1. The Modern Elder — being wise and curious, the dumbest person in the room

Chip's signature concept: being a "Modern Elder" means having the wisdom of experience combined with the curiosity of a beginner. When he joined Airbnb at 52 (average age 26), he had to be "both wise and curious, and often the dumbest person in the room." This mindset — knowing what you know while staying open to learning from people half your age — is the key to thriving across generations. The Modern Elder doesn't rest on their resume; they actively seek to be in environments where they're stretched.

> "I was mentoring Brian, but he was also my boss. I was 52, the average age was 26. I had to be both wise and curious, and often the dumbest person in the room." — Chip Conley

### 2. Midlife is not a crisis — it's a chrysalis

Chip reframes midlife (roughly 35-65) as a chrysalis — the transformative stage between caterpillar and butterfly — rather than a crisis. His own experience: he was happier at 64 than at 47 when he was going through his "flatline experience." The midlife chrysalis is a period of shedding old identities and growing into new ones. This insight led him to found the Modern Elder Academy, which helps people navigate this transition with purpose and community rather than fear and denial.

> "If you think about the caterpillar to butterfly journey, midlife is the chrysalis. Midlife is not crisis. I'm happier today at 64 than I was at 47 when I was going through my flatline experience." — Chip Conley

### 3. The product isn't the software — it's the homes and the people

At Airbnb, Chip brought a crucial outsider perspective: when the tech team was obsessed with optimizing buttons and conversion, he reminded them that "the product is the homes and the apartments." He advocated for bringing older hosts into the product development process — people who understood hospitality, not just technology. This expanded Airbnb's view of what "product" means and led to investments in the host experience, not just the guest booking funnel.

> "Everyone's talking about, 'We got to make the product better. We got to optimize this button, and improve conversion.' Isn't the product the homes and the apartments? … Let's get some older people who are hosts in here." — Chip Conley

### 4. Founder mode is great for founders — hard for everyone else

Chip offers an honest take on Brian Chesky's "founder mode" leadership: it works brilliantly for the founder but is challenging for those working for them. Brian assumed everyone would work at the same pace and duration — "Hey, we're having a meeting at 10 PM. Be there." The lesson for founders is that intensity needs to be balanced with sustainability, and for employees, understanding that founder-mode is a feature, not a bug — it comes with the territory of working at a founder-led company.

> "It's great to be in founder mode. It's not as great to be working for someone in founder mode." — Chip Conley

### 5. Wisdom schools for midlife — a new category of education

Chip's creation of the Modern Elder Academy addresses a growing need: there are schools for children, universities for young adults, but nothing for the midlife transition. MEA provides structured programs (part retreat, part school, part community) for people navigating the second half of life. The curriculum draws on psychology, neuroscience, spirituality, and practical life design. Chip believes that midlife wisdom is one of society's most underutilized resources, and that creating spaces for this demographic to connect and grow is both a business opportunity and a social contribution.
