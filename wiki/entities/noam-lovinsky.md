# Noam Lovinsky

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at Grammarly; previously product leadership at Facebook, YouTube, and Thumbtack  
**Known for:** Deep product management wisdom across consumer and enterprise; frameworks for career navigation in product; the "happiness and pain" of being a PM; knowing when to kill a project.

## Key Views

### 1. PM Career Framework: Diversify Your Growth Channels

Just as businesses need diverse acquisition channels, Noam argues that PMs need diverse "career growth channels." Relying on a single channel (e.g., "I'll get promoted at one company") is risky. Smart PMs invest in multiple growth vectors: raw skill development, network building, personal brand, domain expertise, and optionality (keeping a pulse on the job market even when not looking). Product roles have high variance in satisfaction and trajectory — diversification protects against bad luck.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noam-lovinsky.md]

> "Don't have all your career eggs in one basket. Invest in multiple growth channels — skills, network, brand, domain — so you have options when things change." — Noam Lovinsky

### 2. Know When to Kill Your Project

Noam's most practical framework: every PM should regularly evaluate whether their project should be killed. Signs it's time: (1) The original thesis hasn't been validated after 2-3 attempts; (2) The team has lost conviction; (3) A better opportunity has emerged; (4) The problem you're solving has changed. Killing a project isn't failure — it's resource reallocation. The hardest part is overcoming sunk cost bias. He advises PMs to pre-commit to kill criteria before starting a project.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noam-lovinsky.md]

> "One of the most underrated PM skills is knowing when to kill your own project. Most PMs double down too long because they're emotionally invested." — Noam Lovinsky

### 3. Find the Products That Matter Most

At any large company, some products are strategically central and others are peripheral. Noam advises PMs to gravitate toward the products that matter most to the business — the ones the CEO talks about, the ones that drive core metrics, the ones where success or failure has visible consequences. These are the products where PMs can have the most impact, learn the most, and build the strongest reputation. Being on a peripheral product means fighting for resources and attention.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noam-lovinsky.md]

> "Figure out which products are core to the business and which are nice-to-haves. Your career will thank you for choosing the former." — Noam Lovinsky

### 4. The -1 to 1 to 10 to 100 Journey

Noam describes four stages of a product: (-1) pre-product — figuring out what to build; (1) launching — finding first users; (10) scaling — building the growth engine; (100) optimizing — extracting maximum value. Each stage requires different skills, different metrics, and different leadership styles. Many PMs fail because they apply the playbook from one stage to another. He's deliberately worked across all four stages (Facebook at -1, YouTube at -1 to 1, Thumbtack at 1 to 10, Grammarly at 100) to build a complete toolkit.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noam-lovinsky.md]

> "The skills that make you great at one stage of a product's lifecycle will actively hurt you at another. Great PMs learn to recognize which stage they're in and adapt." — Noam Lovinsky

### 5. Embrace the Pain of PM — It Doesn't Go Away

Noam's honest take: the pain of product management (ambiguity, responsibility without authority, constant context-switching, the weight of decisions) never goes away — it just changes form. Junior PMs feel one kind of pain (unclear expectations), senior PMs feel another (strategic isolation, harder trade-offs). The key is not to eliminate the pain but to build resilience and find the parts of the job that energize you. If you're waiting for PM to become "easy," you'll be waiting forever.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/noam-lovinsky.md]

> "The happiness of PM is the highs of seeing something you envisioned come to life. The pain is everything between those highs. Both are permanent features of the job." — Noam Lovinsky
