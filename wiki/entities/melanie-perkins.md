# Melanie Perkins

Melanie Perkins is CEO and co-founder of Canva, currently valued at over $42 billion, generating over $3 billion in annual revenue, with 240M+ monthly active users. She is on track to be the most successful female tech founder in history. She was rejected by 100+ investors while raising her first round.

## Key Views

**Crazy Big Goals Require Feeling Inadequate** — "The thing that I love about a crazy big goal is that you feel completely inadequate before it. You want to work really hard to will it into existence." Melanie keeps a vision wall in her home office showing what she wants the world to look like in 2050. Every project at Canva starts with a Big Vision Deck that takes the project from chaos to clarity. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/melanie-perkins.md]

**100 Rejections Didn't Stop Her** — "It was really clear in my mind that it was the future and I thought the investors were wrong, frankly." Each rejection became feedback: investors said the market wasn't big enough, so she added a slide showing how big she believed it would be. They said she was like other companies, so she added a competitive landscape slide. She turned every "no" into a better pitch. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/melanie-perkins.md]

**Chaos to Clarity** — Every idea at Canva starts in chaos and must be worked through to clarity. The first step at the chaos end is embarrassing — you don't have all the answers or mastery yet. The process of moving from chaos to clarity involves vision decks, rapid prototyping, user testing, and iterative refinement until the path forward becomes clear enough to execute. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/melanie-perkins.md]

**Two-Year Rewrite (That Was Supposed to Take 6 Months)** — Canva's team spent two years rewriting their entire codebase, unable to ship any new features. What they expected to take six months took two years. This period was incredibly painful but ultimately necessary for the platform to scale. The lesson: some rewrites are unavoidable, but always budget 4x your initial estimate. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/melanie-perkins.md]

**From Yearbook Platform to Design Powerhouse** — Canva started as a yearbook publishing platform (Fusion Books) before pivoting to the broader design tool it is today. The pivot was driven by the insight that the same technology that made yearbook design easy could work for any kind of visual communication. AI was in Canva's vision decks before it was widely feasible — they were trying to do AI before AI was a thing. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/melanie-perkins.md]
