# Eeke de Milliano

Eeke de Milliano is Head of Product at Retool and a former product leader at Stripe, where she was one of their first product managers. She helped build foundational Stripe products including Stripe Checkout, Stripe Connect, Stripe Radar, and Stripe Chargeback Protection. She wrote Stripe's internal culture guide and is known for her deep thinking about innovation, process, and organizational design.

## Key Views

### Process Is Variance-Reducing — Use With Care
Eeke's core insight about process: "Process, by definition, is variance reducing. You're introducing it because you worry that the variance in your org is too high. The cost is that while you're reducing the standard and bringing folks up to the average, you're also bringing other folks down to the average — your highest performers, your most creative thinkers." Companies introduce more process as they scale, but should do so deliberately, knowing it may suppress innovation from top performers.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/eeke-de-milliano.md]

### The Trapdoor Decision Framework
Eeke learned at Stripe to distinguish between decisions that are truly irreversible (trapdoors) and those that feel irreversible but aren't. For example, pricing feels like a trapdoor but isn't — you can grandfather existing users and change pricing for new ones. Titles, on the other hand, are a real trapdoor — "once you've given someone a title, you can't really take it away." This framework enables faster decision-making by identifying where the real risk lives.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/eeke-de-milliano.md]

### Writing Culture Creates Clear Thinking
Stripe had a very strong writing culture — business reviews, strategy memos, product reviews. Eeke's belief: "I don't think you can be a good writer, unless you're a clear thinker. If you couldn't write well, it was actually pretty hard to be successful at Stripe." Writing forces rigorous thinking and creates a shared understanding that verbal communication can't replicate.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/eeke-de-milliano.md]

### PMs Are Needed When the Organization Gets Complex
Stripe famously had no PMs for its first ~100 employees. Eeke explains: when you're building for developers (your engineers are your customers), PMs aren't as essential because the builders understand the users. But as the customer base expands and the organization becomes more matrixed (especially in fintech with legal, compliance, and country-specific infrastructure), PMs become critical to "bring that whole story together and make the whole machine move."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/eeke-de-milliano.md]

### Great Companies Make Many Good Decisions Consistently
Reflecting on what made Stripe special, Eeke notes there wasn't one or two pivotal decisions — it was "making a lot of really good decisions all the time, big or small." This came from a culture of "think rigorously" — no one would ever cite a "best practice" without being asked to go deeper. Combining that rigor with the ability to not overthink (using the trapdoor framework) allowed Stripe to move fast while maintaining quality.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/eeke-de-milliano.md]
