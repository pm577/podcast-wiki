# Keith Yandell

Keith Yandell appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss longtime executive at doordash (chief legal officer, led hr, marketing, customer support, and corporate development) and former litigation lead at uber, on leading with empathy, radical transparency, and building high-trust teams.

## Key Views

**Make People Argue the Other Side to Generate Empathy in Tough Decisions** — When teams deadlock over competing priorities (e.g. profitability vs. growth), Keith asks each side to make the strongest case for the opposing position. This forces people to genuinely understand the other perspective and often leads them to persuade themselves toward alignment. It is impossible to stay in a polarized position after you've had to argue the best possible version of the other side. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]

**Publish a 'How to Work With Me' Document That Includes Your Own Weaknesses** — Keith created a document sharing his expectations, the constructive feedback he's received and is working on, and his commitments to the team (e.g. 'I will help you find your next job'). This radical transparency builds trust, attracts top talent, and helps new hires acclimate to the culture from day one. A candidate told him the document was the deciding factor in joining DoorDash. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]

**Tell Your Team You Will Help Them Find Their Next Job, Even Outside the Company** — Keith explicitly commits to helping people land their next role, even if it means losing them. This creates psychological safety, encourages honest conversations about dissatisfaction, and builds a long-term reputation as a leader who genuinely puts people first. It actually increases retention and attracts boomerang employees who return years later. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]

**Demand Constructive Feedback — Don't Just Invite It, Require It** — Borrowing from Uber's T3 B3 system (three positives, three constructive points), Keith insists that direct reports give him critical feedback during one-on-ones. He follows up by thanking them, visibly actioning the feedback, and circling back to show progress. If you create a space where constructive feedback is required, it becomes normal and people stop holding back. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]

**Use Direct, Even Blunt, Honesty About Culture Fit to Find the Right People** — Keith once told a VP of engineering candidate he seemed like 'an asshole' during a debrief, then had dinner to give that feedback directly. The candidate appreciated the candor, joined, and later said it was the deciding factor — because he knew DoorDash wouldn't compromise culture for talent. Being willing to walk away from brilliant people who don't fit the culture is a powerful signal to the entire organization. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/keith-yandell.md]
