# Raaz Herzberg

Raaz Herzberg appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss building wiz: the fastest-growing startup in history.

## Key Views

**"I don’t understand" is the most powerful question in early-stage startups** — Raaz joined Wiz as the first PM and found herself sitting through 10-15 customer calls a day without understanding what they were building. She was afraid to ask, but when she finally confessed, it triggered a 5-hour discussion that led to the pivot from network security to cloud security — the move that made Wiz the fastest-growing software company ever. "It’s hard to get the courage, but by now, ‘I don’t understand’ is my favorite question." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

**The real PMF signal: pricing questions and POV requests, not “that’s interesting”** — Raaz’s key lesson on detecting PMF: polite interest (“that’s interesting, keep me posted”) is not a signal. Real demand looks like customers asking “how much does this cost?”, “when can we start a POV?”, or “let me connect you to my team.” Humans have a bias toward affirmation; you have to actively train yourself to listen for the harder signals of genuine pull. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

**Sell it before you build it — founders closed the first deals themselves** — When Wiz found PMF, things moved so fast that they were closing contracts before they had a sales team. Raaz and the founders personally negotiated and closed deals, which taught them exactly what customers valued. When they hired their first salesperson, they could confidently say "we’ve already sold millions of this." Her lesson: don’t delegate early customer conversations — do them yourself to learn what pull really looks like. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

**Don’t push; look for the pull from the other side** — When Wiz’s first Fortune 10 customer asked for a POV, Raaz was scared they weren’t ready and sent a long list of technical requirements to buy time. The customer filled it out the next day. Her insight: early-stage B2B startups should look for customers who actively pull, not ones who need to be pushed. "I don’t want to push somebody into a POV if they’re not committed. It’s going to be a journey we take together." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

**From PM to CMO — the product-to-marketing pipeline creates unnatural advantages** — Raaz made an unusual pivot from product (VP of Product) to marketing (CMO) at Wiz. Her product background gave her a unique lens: she knows what makes a product defensible and can translate that into marketing that doesn’t rely on industry jargon. Her advice: product leaders moving into marketing should bring their customer empathy and skepticism of generic positioning — it makes for more authentic, differentiated messaging. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

# Raaz Herzberg

Raaz Herzberg is the Chief Marketing Officer and VP of Product Strategy at Wiz, the fastest-growing software company in history ($100M ARR in 18 months, over $500M ARR within 5 years). She was one of the first 10 employees and started as the first PM before transitioning to CMO — an unconventional path from engineer to product to marketing. Previously, she led security products at Microsoft including Azure Sentinel.

## Key Views

### The Pivot That Made Wiz: Ask "What Exactly Are We Building?"

When Raaz joined as Wiz's first PM, the founders were still figuring out the idea. For weeks, they pitched a network security concept to CISOs who said "sounds interesting" but showed no real urgency. Raaz realized: "I still did not exactly understand what we were going to build, which was confusing, because I was a PM, supposed to start building it." Her honest question — "What exactly are we doing here?" — triggered the pivot to cloud security, which had immediate pull. The lesson: pay attention when you can't explain the product concretely, and when customer questions shift from "tell me more" to "how do I buy?"^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

### Signals of Product-Market Fit: The Question Shift

Raaz describes the moment Wiz knew they had PMF: customer questions changed from polite interest to actionable urgency. "Silly the call sounded like... 'How are you pricing this, or when can we start doing a POV?'" She notes that humans have a bias for affirmation — we want to hear "yes" — but the real signal is when prospects drive the conversation toward procurement, not discovery.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

### The Product-to-Marketing Transition

Raaz made an unconventional career move from VP of Product to CMO. Her insight: marketing is the natural next chapter for product leaders who understand the customer deeply. "I had a ton to learn about marketing, but I knew the product, the customer, and the market inside out." She believes many CMOs fail because they don't have this product depth, and many product leaders should consider marketing as a growth path.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

### The "Dummy Explanation" Test

Raaz emphasizes the importance of being able to explain your product simply. If you can't explain it in one sentence that any customer understands, you don't have clarity. This was the core issue in Wiz's early days — the founding team was so technical they couldn't articulate what they were building in plain terms. Simplicity forces clarity of thought.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]

### Pay Attention to Where the Heat Is

Raaz advises leaders to "pay attention to where the heat is within the organization." The areas generating the most customer energy, team energy, or market buzz are where you should invest. Wiz's cloud security pivot was driven by noticing that every customer call came alive when discussing cloud security problems, not network security. Following the heat is a reliable compass.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/raaz-herzberg.md]
