# Adam Fishman

**Role:** Growth and product leader; former CPO at Imperfect Foods, Head of Growth at Patreon, first growth hire at Lyft; Executive in Residence at Reforge  
**Known for:** The Growth Competency Model, PMF (People-Mission-Financials) framework for candidate job selection, and deep expertise in onboarding as a retention lever.

## Key Views

### 1. The Growth Competency Model — four pillars of growth talent

Adam's framework for hiring, evaluating, and developing growth professionals: **(1) Growth Execution** — running experiments, shipping quickly, data fluency; **(2) Customer Knowledge** — deep qualitative and quantitative user understanding; **(3) Growth Strategy** — growth loop modeling, capital allocation, prioritization; **(4) Communication & Influence** — stakeholder management, team leadership, framing work in business terms. Junior hires should excel at execution and customer knowledge; strategy and influence develop over time.

> "Customer knowledge and growth execution — those are the two most important competencies when you're bringing in a younger, earlier stage person because teaching somebody how to execute is not that fun." — Adam Fishman

### 2. Onboarding is the highest-leverage growth investment

Onboarding touches 100% of users — more than any other feature. It's the first chance to deliver on the brand promise and also the moment users are most motivated. Adam has seen companies shift retention curves by 10–20 percentage points through onboarding improvements. At Patreon, connecting creators with a human at the right time improved first-month revenue by 25%, which they then productized into "opinionated defaults."

> "Onboarding is the only part of your product experience that 100% of people are ever going to touch. It's also the first opportunity that you have as a company to deliver on the promise that you made out in the marketplace." — Adam Fishman

### 3. Opinionated defaults — make it hard to do the wrong thing

Rather than micromanaging user choices, Adam advocates for "opinionated defaults": guide users toward proven best practices while preserving their freedom to override. At Patreon, this meant defaulting creators to a 3-tier pricing model (proven optimal) rather than letting them pick an ineffective single tier. The principle applies broadly — from pricing to onboarding flows to product design.

> "The best things to do turned out to be opinionated defaults — making it hard to do the wrong thing when you're setting up your Patreon page and easy to do the right thing but not eliminating choice." — Adam Fishman

### 4. PMF for candidates — People, Mission, Financials

Adam's framework for evaluating a potential employer: **(People)** — will you enjoy working with this team? Can you have productive disagreements? **(Mission)** — does the company make people's lives better? **(Financials)** — does the company have fiscal discipline? He recommends doing back-channel references, observing team dynamics, and asking questions like "Tell me about the last strategic offsite — where did people disagree, and how did you resolve it?"

> "You are actually investing an even more scarce resource — your time. There is no way to get more time. If you make a bad investment decision, you can always get more money. But time is fleeting." — Adam Fishman

### 5. Internal growth hires outperform external ones

Adam strongly recommends hiring internally for growth roles: faster time to results, known track record, and deeper customer knowledge. The exception is specialist roles (SEO, paid acquisition) where deep expertise accumulated over years is hard to replicate. He describes three growth archetypes: the **painter** (generalist, builds foundations), the **architect** (designs systems), and the **surgeon** (precision specialist).

> "I tend to skew more towards internal hire because I believe in creating opportunity inside a company. You have faster time to results and much less likelihood of making the wrong hire." — Adam Fishman
