# Rahul Vohra

Rahul Vohra appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss superhuman's secret to success.

## Key Views

**The Real Secret Behind Virality** — Rahul learned from LinkedIn's head of growth that no product sustains a viral factor >1 for long. The true engine of growth isn't viral mechanics but spontaneous word of mouth. "It is word of mouth. It is the virality you can't measure that isn't a mechanic that isn't in a feature." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]

**The Product-Market Fit Engine** — Rahul's famous PMF framework: ask users "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" Focus relentlessly on the "somewhat disappointed" segment whose main benefit resonates. "Politely disregard those people and their feedback. Because even if you built everything that they asked for, they're still pulling you in a different direction." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]

**Game Design Over Gamification** — Drawing on his professional game design background, Rahul explains that adding points and badges (gamification) undermines intrinsic motivation, while building features that feel like "toys" — indulging playful exploration — is what truly makes products lovable. "The best games are constructed out of toys. Why? Because then they are fun on both levels, the toy and the game itself." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]

**Positioning-First Pricing** — Before setting Superhuman's $30/month price, Rahul first nailed the positioning (best-in-class email for high-performers), then used the Van Westendorp method to find the price where "it starts to feel expensive, but you'd have to give some thought to buying it." "It turns out that the median answer was $30 per month, and that's how we picked our price." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]

**The Single Decisive Reason (SDR)** — From Reid Hoffman: for any important decision, identify one reason sufficient on its own to justify it. "If they can't yet isolate it, that tells me they haven't yet figured out why they want to make the decision. Multiple low quality reasons rarely add up to a high quality reason to do something." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rahul-vohra.md]
