# Ada Chen Rekhi

**Role:** Executive Coach, Former SVP of Marketing at SurveyMonkey, Co-founder of Notejoy (acquired by LinkedIn)  
**Known for:** Curiosity Loops framework, executive coaching for founders, career decision-making, and the Values Exercise for aligning career with personal priorities.

## Key Views

### 1. Curiosity Loops Beat Single-Source Advice

Ada's signature framework: instead of asking one person "what should I do?" or going it alone, run a structured mini-survey of 5–10 carefully chosen people. A good curiosity loop question is **specific, solicits rationale, and is unbiased** — never lead with your own opinion. Ask *subject-matter experts* who understand the topic and *people who know you well* who can assess fit. Keep the ask lightweight (e.g., "pick 2 of 10 options and tell me why") so busy people will answer. Close the loop by sharing what you decided — it feels good to help, and people remember.^[episodes/lenny/ada-chen-rekhi]

### 2. Inner Scorecard vs. Outer Scorecard (Warren Buffett's Framework)

Ada draws on Buffett's distinction: the **outer scorecard** is status, title, logo-name companies, wealth — how the world evaluates you. The **inner scorecard** is whether you lived your values, had adventure, were kind, learned something. The ego monster in the corner of the room screams for the glamorous option, but it's the inner scorecard that predicts whether you'll be happy waking up 20 years from now. A recurring mistake she sees: optimizing a resume for 20 years, retiring, and realizing you never asked what *you* actually wanted.^[episodes/lenny/ada-chen-rekhi]

### 3. Don't Be the Boiled Frog — Watch for Inertia in Your Career

The frog-in-boiling-water metaphor: small discomforts accumulate degree by degree. A bad manager, a misaligned role, a plateau in learning — none alone is catastrophic, but together they trap you. Ada's heuristic: monitor the **temperature of your learning**. If you aren't being stretched and growing, and if you've tried changing the situation internally without success, it's time to leave. Staying because your resume will look better is exactly the "outer scorecard" trap. The opposite — a job where you're learning and challenged every day — can be worth staying in for decades.^[episodes/lenny/ada-chen-rekhi]

### 4. Explore and Exploit — A Career Strategy Framework

Ada's early career was "wild" — from entry-level sales at Microsoft to SVP of marketing at SurveyMonkey in 7 years. Her framework: **Explore** (try different domains, company sizes, roles — you have little to lose when young) vs **Exploit** (once you've found a rich vein, go deep). At LinkedIn, she explicitly told her manager: *"I'm here to learn to be a better founder."* She optimized for specific skill gaps (growth, subscriptions, pricing) rather than title or comp. The winning combination that landed her the SurveyMonkey role? Startup founder experience + big-company growth + subscriptions + product marketing — a unique bundle that no single role would have given her.^[episodes/lenny/ada-chen-rekhi]

### 5. "Most People Don't Need a Coach" — And What to Do Instead

Ada's hot take: for the vast majority of people, a coach is the wrong investment. Before paying for coaching, exhaust these alternatives:
- **Run a curiosity loop** — get 5–10 diverse perspectives instead of one coach's opinion
- **Take a structured course** (e.g., Reforge) for broad landscape knowledge
- **Build a peer community** — the support network that lasts years
Coaches are genuinely valuable in two specific scenarios: (a) rapid scaling situations (founders in chaos who need accelerated learning), and (b) sensitive or interpersonal topics where a safe, rational sounding board is essential. When hiring a coach, talk to 2–3 candidates — the single most important factor is personal chemistry, not credentials.^[episodes/lenny/ada-chen-rekhi]

### 6. Eating Your Vegetables — Deliberate Practice in Discomfort

Ada's "eating your vegetables" principle: it takes **10–12 exposures** to develop genuine appreciation for something new (based on child-palate research). Early in her career she was awkward at networking, so she gave herself a rule: attend one event per week, hand out 10 business cards, and physically touch the back wall before leaving. After several weeks, familiar faces appeared and the skill became natural. Many of those relationships remain foundational to her network today. The lesson: separate *genuine dislike* (you've done it many times and still hate it) from *dislike because you're new/bad at it*. Power through the first 10–12 reps before deciding.^[episodes/lenny/ada-chen-rekhi]
