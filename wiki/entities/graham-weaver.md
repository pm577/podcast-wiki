# Graham Weaver

Graham Weaver teaches a top-rated course at Stanford's Graduate School of Business called "Managing Growing Enterprises," where he primarily helps students figure out what to do with their lives. His full-time job is founder and CEO of Alpine Investors, one of the top-performing private equity funds in the world. He won Stanford GSB's 2024 MBA Distinguished Teaching Award.

## Key Views

### The Genie Framework: What Would You Wish For?

Graham's most impactful exercise: imagine a genie gives you one wish — whatever you throw yourself into will turn out great. What would you choose? This bypasses the fear of failure that prevents people from pursuing what they truly want. Most people spend their lives optimizing for the wrong things because they never ask themselves this question honestly. The exercise reveals what you'd pursue if success were guaranteed.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/graham-weaver.md]

### Breaking Out of Autopilot

Graham's core message: most people live on autopilot — "get up, work out, drive into work, fight traffic, return some emails, fight traffic on the way home, rush through dinner, go to bed." It's not an intentional life. The first step to changing your trajectory is recognizing you're on autopilot. Then, you need to create deliberate moments of reflection — asking whether your daily actions align with what you actually want from your one life.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/graham-weaver.md]

### Everything You Want Is on the Other Side of "Worse First"

Graham's principle: any worthwhile change involves short-term pain. Better body? The first gym sessions aren't fun. Better career? The first difficult conversation is uncomfortable. "If I'm optimizing for tomorrow and I just want to have a great day tomorrow, I'm going to stay exactly where I am." The willingness to endure "worse first" separates those who grow from those who plateau. Most people never move past plateaus because they optimize for the next 24 hours.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/graham-weaver.md]

### Choose Something Worth Suffering For

Graham's philosophy: life involves suffering regardless — you may as well choose something worth suffering for. This reframes the "follow your passion" cliché into something more honest. The question isn't "what will make me happy?" but "what am I willing to struggle for?" The most fulfilling paths are not the easiest — they're the ones where the struggle feels meaningful.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/graham-weaver.md]

### Create Accountability Structures

Graham emphasizes that insight without accountability is useless. His students create specific accountability mechanisms — weekly check-ins, commitment contracts, peer groups — to ensure they act on what they've learned. Knowing what you should do is easy; actually doing it requires external structures that keep you honest when motivation fades. He recommends finding an accountability partner who will ask the hard questions regularly.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/graham-weaver.md]

## Notable Quotes

> "Imagine that you're walking home from work, you see this bright, shiny object, and you realize it's a magic lamp... If that were true and you had that genie, what would you wish for?"

> "Pick anything. You want a better body? Okay, you're going to need to go to the gym. When you go to the gym the first few times, it's going to not be that fun. The first move is negative."

> "Everything that you want is on the other side of worse first."
