# Tanguy Crusson

**Role:** Head of Jira Product Discovery, Atlassian  
**Known for:** Leading zero-to-one product incubation inside Atlassian, building Jira Product Discovery (one of Atlassian's fastest-growing products), and designing the Point A internal incubator program.

## Key Views

### 1. "Don't eat your own bullshit" — past success assumptions don't travel

Tanguy's biggest lesson from the HipChat failure: the assumptions that made Atlassian successful with developers didn't translate to the enterprise communications market. The playbook of bottom-up adoption through tech teams failed against Slack's consumer-grade experience aimed at business users. His rule: **"What took you here won't take you there."** Validate every assumption about your distribution channel, target user, and go-to-market motion — especially when you think your existing playbook will work in a new market.

> "We didn't do enough to validate that assumption early enough. We did a lot of work, a lot of work even on other things, even when faced with signals that this might not work." — Tanguy Crusson

### 2. Competitive myopia is a silent killer

During HipChat's decline, the team fell into the trap of reacting to every Slack feature release. Tanguy's countermeasure: **anchor back to user interviews** whenever the team starts obsessing over competitors. Watch recorded user sessions together to remember who you're building for. He advises checking competition at most every quarter — the market is huge, and obsessing over what competitors shipped a year ago based on their customer research is a losing game.

> "Your competitor, if you think of what they do as an iceberg, the top side, what comes out of the water is what they've shipped in terms of features, but it's based on all this stuff that they've built in terms of research and understanding of their customer base." — Tanguy Crusson

### 3. Create scarcity inside a resource-rich company

Large companies over-invest in new bets. Tanguy's approach: **frame every new initiative as likely to fail** (he calls it "70% chance this doesn't exist in 6 months"). This serves two purposes: (a) it keeps the rest of the org from piling on with process, design guidelines, and platform dependencies, and (b) it preserves team autonomy to "hack shit together" and test with customers fast. For Jira Product Discovery, he kept the team at 7 people and actively refused offers of additional resources from the platform.

> "Let's not drag the rest of the company into it. The appetite that the company has right now is these seven people." — Tanguy Crusson

### 4. The "Why Now?" question is more important than the business case

After spending months pitching an IT operations initiative that everyone agreed was a good idea but nobody acted on, Tanguy realized the missing piece: urgency. A great business case without a compelling **"why now"** will sit on a shelf at a big company with infinite opportunities. The team that later succeeded with Jira Service Management nailed this framing. For internal innovators, the perishability of an opportunity is the key unlock to getting executive commitment.

> "There may not be a trigger for the why now. So we need a very strong trigger for why now to go after it. And I did not do a good enough job at articulating this." — Tanguy Crusson

### 5. The Point A incubator model: Wonder → Explore → Make → Impact

Tanguy helped design Atlassian's internal incubator, Point A, structured in four stages:
- **Wonder:** Validate there's a problem area worth entering and articulate why Atlassian should move there
- **Explore:** Validate solutions — not by building, but by getting customers to play back the solution via prototypes (JPD validated entirely through Figma mockups and Zooms before any code)
- **Make:** Build in stages (alpha → beta → GA), dogfooding extensively
- **Impact:** Monitor business impact once live

Key principles: treat it like the team is being hired, not the business being bought; keep the first milestone small (3 months, not the "3-year plan" that scares acqui-hires); and psychologically protect the team by guaranteeing they can return to their previous role if the bet fails.
