# Arielle Jackson

**Role:** Marketer in Residence at First Round Capital; former growth marketer at Google (Gmail early days) and Square (Square Stand launch); brand strategist for 100+ startups including Patreon, Loom, Front, Eero, Maven  
**Known for:** A structured brand development framework (purpose, positioning, personality), startup naming expertise (named 30+ companies), and the "bar test" for positioning copy.

## Key Views

### 1. Brand development = Purpose + Positioning + Personality

Arielle's three-part brand framework: **Purpose** (why does your company exist beyond making money? — this drives culture and retention), **Positioning** (who is it for, what problem do they have, how do they solve it today, what do you make, and what will users tell others?), and **Personality** (five dimensions of brand personality from Jennifer Aker's research: sincerity, excitement, competence, sophistication, ruggedness). Most tech companies spike in "sincere" and "competent" — but to be interesting, brands need tension: "We are X, but not Y" (e.g., "playful but not silly" for Google).

> "Brands are like people … these days, especially when brands show up in places where people show up, like TikTok and Instagram, your brand certainly needs a personality or else you end up like ADP trying to be fun for the summer, which just feels really off and weird." — Arielle Jackson

### 2. The naming spectrum: descriptive → suggestive → evocative → empty vessel

Arielle categorizes names across a spectrum. **Descriptive** (Internet Explorer — tells you exactly what it does). **Suggestive** (Chrome — the chrome around a browser window). **Evocative** (Maven — Yiddish for "one who understands," perfect for a cohort-learning platform; Seesaw — nostalgic, back-and-forth motion fitting an edtech app). **Empty vessel** (Yahoo, Eero — mean nothing initially but can be filled with meaning through massive marketing spend). Her personal preference: suggestive/evocative names where "when I tell you what the company does, you're like, 'oh, that makes sense'" — the name does some of the marketing work for you.

> "If there is a meaning behind your name, your name is doing a little bit more of your marketing work for you." — Arielle Jackson

### 3. The "Bar Test" — positioning in human language

Arielle's key technique to avoid jargon-ridden positioning: pretend you're at a bar with someone in your target audience. Can you say, "Hey, I just started using [product], it's this really great [category], [benefit]"? And can the other person naturally respond? If you can say it out loud and it sounds like something a real human would say, you've nailed it. This counters the common B2B pitfall of writing copy that "leverages and empowers" — language nobody actually uses in conversation.

> "The document, when I get the first draft back from founders often has things like leverages and empowers and nobody talks like that. … Getting this into 'turns your iPad into a point of sale,' 'blankets your home in fast, reliable wifi' — really basic stuff." — Arielle Jackson

### 4. Positioning must come before messaging — and before coding

Arielle's foundational belief: positioning dictates everything in marketing. The exercise starts with five questions — who is it for, what's their problem, how do they address it today, what do you make, how does it work, what will they tell others? This distills into the classic positioning statement: "For [target audience] who [need/opportunity], our [product] is a [category] that [benefit], unlike [competitor/old way], our product [differentiator]." She had a founder in her class say, "I'll never write a line of code without doing positioning first" — which she calls "music to my ears."

> "Positioning dictates so much of your marketing and should always be the first thing you do." — Arielle Jackson

### 5. PR tactics for early-stage startups: exclusives, local press, and story hooks

Arielle's practical PR advice for seed-stage companies: run launch announcements as **exclusives** (one outlet) because embargo briefings don't work like they used to. Make your story relevant to the *reader*, not just to you — tie it to a trend (e.g., Vitable Health framed around the Great Resignation). **Don't sleep on local press** — local outlets are hungry for stories and often more receptive than NYT/WSJ. And never announce just funding — use the funding as a "news hook" to tell a larger story about product momentum, customers, or partnerships. Get your story straight before you even start pitching — if you can't describe your company in one sentence, no reporter can either.

> "Don't do a straight funding announcement … Use that funding announcement as a news hook to tell a larger story." — Arielle Jackson
