# Tamar Yehoshua

Tamar Yehoshua is President of Product and Technology at Glean. Previously she was CPO at Slack (scaling through IPO and Salesforce acquisition), led product and engineering for Google Search, spent five years at Amazon as Director of Engineering, and has served on boards including ServiceNow and Snyk.

## Key Views

**Do a great job at whatever job you're in right now.** Tamar's #1 career advice is often overlooked: product managers are ambitious and constantly eyeing the next role, but you won't get promoted unless you knock your current job out of the park. Beyond table stakes (technical knowledge, metrics understanding), the distinguishing factor is whether you're driving genuine impact for the business — not just doing what you were asked to do.

**Understanding people and motivations is the highest-leverage leadership skill.** Tamar credits her psychiatrist father for teaching her to "see the whole room" — to read people's reactions, understand their motivations, and ask why people do what they do. This skill applies both to building products (understanding user motivations) and leading teams (understanding what drives team members). She warns against being overly reliant on metrics at the expense of intuition.

**A company doesn't have to be well-run to win.** This provocative insight from Tamar's experience: she can name companies with high executive turnover, unhappy employees, and constant reorgs that are growing like crazy — and well-run companies that flatlined. Product-market fit and distribution trump operational excellence in the early-to-mid stages. However, once you reach a certain scale (5,000-10,000 people), you need professional management.

**Have a beginner's mind.** Tamar channels Marc Benioff's advice to go into every situation assuming you know nothing and truly listen. She cautions against the natural tendency to assume your feature is the most important thing to put front and center in the interface. You haven't earned that right — you need perspective on what users are actually trying to achieve.

**Don't be afraid to eliminate your own role or team if it's the right thing for the company.** Early in her career, Tamar was offered a management role, went on vacation, and came back to recommend the team shouldn't exist. Her manager was stunned but agreed. This willingness to prioritize company impact over personal career progression is what distinguishes true leaders.
