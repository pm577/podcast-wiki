# Ami Vora

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at Faire, former VP of Product at WhatsApp, former Head of Product for Facebook Ads ($55B business), employee #150 at Facebook  
**Known for:** Radical curiosity in disagreements, metaphor-driven leadership (the hill climb, dinosaur brain, putting on the coat), and building authentic, trust-based product cultures.

## Key Views

### 1. "Fascinating, Tell Me More" — Curiosity Over Being Right

Ami's signature move (observed and named by Meta CTO "Boz"): when faced with profound disagreement, instead of reacting defensively, she says "Fascinating — you have to tell me more why you think that" — and she means it. This didn't come naturally; she originally loved being right. But she learned that "it's more important to get to the outcome than to be right." The shift: assume every person in every meeting knows something you don't. Every disagreement is a learning opportunity. The mechanism: **take a pause** before reacting viscerally, then reinterpret the feeling as curiosity rather than threat. "The positive feedback of learning something new and getting a better outcome far outweighs the desire to be right."^[episodes/lenny/ami-vora]

### 2. The Dinosaur Brain — Executives Can Only Hold Three Facts

Ami's framework for product reviews: executives have a "tiny little brontosaurus brain" that can only hold ~3 facts at a time. The best service a PM can do is **bring a recommendation**, not all the data. Key rules for product reviews:
- **Keep it simple** — write everything you need, then cut almost all of it
- **Bring a clear recommendation** — force yourself to be opinionated
- **Calibrate on principles, not decisions** — the goal of a review is to establish principles so the team can make future decisions independently
- **My manager owns context, I own the recommendation** — managers provide pattern-matching and cross-company context; the PM is the deep expert^[episodes/lenny/ami-vora]

### 3. The Hill Climb — Local Optimum vs Global Optimum

Ami's central career and strategy metaphor: you're on a hill you've optimized (local optimum). In the distance, you see a much higher mountain (global optimum). To get there, you must climb down into the valley, cross an unknown chasm, and climb back up — without knowing if you'll make it. Examples: desktop → mobile, old job → new job, current strategy → new strategy. "The thing that gets me through the valley is remembering what the summit feels like." Most of the time the answer is yes, it's worth it, but it always feels like a slog in the middle.^[episodes/lenny/ami-vora]

### 4. Put On the Coat of the Job — Trust-Based Career Decisions

Ami's career decision framework: instead of a spreadsheet of axes and projections, she "puts on the coat of the job" — imagines the daily reality: who she'd have lunch with, what she'd think about on her commute, what problems she'd solve. The emotional response is more telling than the spreadsheet. Her best work comes from **trust** — working with people who have her back, who let her take risks, who feel like friends. She deliberately avoids having a 5-year plan: "Whenever I get in that zone of 'there's only one outcome and I have to get there,' I'm not my best."^[episodes/lenny/ami-vora]

### 5. Lead With Metaphors — "How Should This Product Make You Feel?"

Ami learned from a manager (Eric Antino) that metaphors and analogies are the most powerful leadership tool. Instead of abstract specifications, ask: "What do you want your users to feel when they use this product?" If everyone agrees the feeling should be "sitting in Dolores Park with friends on a sunny Saturday," they'll naturally build something consistent. She uses metaphors constantly: putting on the coat (career decisions), dinosaur brain (how execs process info), hill climb (strategy transitions). The advice: **think about what you want people to feel, and build the analogy from that feeling.**^[episodes/lenny/ami-vora]

### 6. Authenticity Over Perfection — "I'm Working From My Bathroom"

Ami's commitment to authenticity: she works from her bathroom (most closing doors between her and three kids during WFH). She deliberately shows that successful people are messy and imperfect. Her insight: when she was coming up, successful women seemed like "superwomen" who never slept and always wore high heels. She realized that's a harmful myth. "No one's got it fully figured out. Most of us are winging it and learning through trial and error, and it's all fine."^[episodes/lenny/ami-vora]
