# Albert Cheng

**Role:** Growth leader at Chess.com; formerly led growth at Duolingo and Grammarly  
**Known for:** Driving growth at three of the world's most successful consumer subscription products, creating the explore-and-exploit growth framework, and applying AI to accelerate growth experimentation.

## Key Views

### 1. The Explore & Exploit framework — find mountains, then climb them

Albert's core mental model for growth: **explore** to find the right mountain to climb, then **exploit** to focus resources on climbing it effectively. Too much exploration leads to scattershot experiments; too much exploitation leads to local maxima and stagnation. The magic is oscillating between the two. At Chess.com, the growth team discovered (through exploration) that 80% of game reviews happen after a win — counterintuitive since the feature was designed for learning from losses. They flipped the experience to show brilliant moves and encouraging messages after losses, which grew game reviews by 25% and subscriptions by 20%. Then they entered **exploitation mode**: spreading that insight (positive reframing) across other product areas like puzzles and lessons, amplifying the win 10x.

> "When you do find a thing that breaks through the noise, you can take that experiment win and expand it out 10X across your organization. When done right, you oscillate between explore and exploit until you saturate." — Albert Cheng

### 2. Show, don't gate: the reverse trial that doubled Grammarly's upgrades

The biggest monetization win at Grammarly: they were only showing free users spelling and grammar corrections (the "correctness" features), so the product's brand became "a spell checker." The growth team started **sampling paid suggestions** (tone, clarity, full-sentence rewrites) and interspersing them among free users' writing — a capped, real-time reverse trial. Instead of free users seeing Grammarly as limited, they saw it as a powerful tool they were just scratching the surface of. **Upgrade rates nearly doubled.** The insight: in a freemium product, your free experience should reflect the full capabilities of the product, even if you can only give limited access. Let people taste what they're missing.

> "All of a sudden, people were seeing Grammarly as a much more powerful tool than they were before and our upgrade rates nearly doubled just through this change." — Albert Cheng

### 3. Growth = connecting users to value, not metrics hacking

Albert defines growth simply: **connect users to the value of the product.** This reframes growth from a set of manipulative tactics to a user-centric discipline. He observes that consumer subscription companies succeed when they have high existing-user retention (the compounding daily habit) and word-of-mouth distribution. His benchmarks: D1 retention of 30-40% is "solid" for a consumer app. But the most important metric is **current user retention** — the stickiness of the existing user base. As companies mature, resurrecting dormant users (which can number in the hundreds of millions) becomes a higher-ROI lever than acquiring net-new users — requiring a dedicated resurrected-user experience.

> "User retention is gold for consumer subscription companies. If you don't retain your users, then a lot of the onus is on getting them to pay on day one. That's super hard." — Albert Cheng

### 4. Agency over experience — hire for high clock speed, not deep expertise

Albert's counterintuitive hiring lesson from leading growth teams across three companies: **high-performers have high agency and high clock speed** — they don't necessarily need deep category experience. In the AI era, past experience can become a crutch, because learned habits must be intentionally discarded as the ground shifts. At Duolingo, the company hires talented people right out of college and gives them incredible experimentation tooling — the "Green Machine" approach where the product experience changes multiple times per day per user. The structure is rigid, but the ideas are anything but.

> "Sometimes experience could be a crutch, especially in this world where the grounds are shifting so fast with AI. A lot of your learned habits actually need to be intentionally discarded." — Albert Cheng

### 5. Use AI to democratize data — build text-to-SQL Slack bots

At Chess.com, Albert's team built a **Slack bot that answers ad-hoc data questions using text-to-SQL.** Anyone in the company can ask "How many subscribers do we have in South Africa?" or "How long did people play puzzles last month?" and get an instant answer without involving a data analyst. The surprising effect: people ask questions they'd be embarrassed to submit to a human analyst, dramatically increasing data-informed decision-making across the company. Albert sees this as a template for every company — AI as the first-pass data provider, freeing analysts for deeper, higher-value work.

> "Human nature: if you have a question you feel embarrassed to ask, you just don't ask the question. By having these tools, you get a pretty large explosion of questions being asked." — Albert Cheng
