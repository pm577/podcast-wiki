# Hamilton Helmer

Hamilton Helmer is a world-leading expert on business strategy and the author of *7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy*, widely considered the best book on strategy by leaders including Peter Thiel, Reed Hastings, Patrick Collison, and Daniel Ek. His framework on sustainable competitive advantage has shaped how some of the most iconic companies in tech were built and defended.

## Key Views

### Power Requires Both Benefit and Barrier

Hamilton defines competitive power using Warren Buffett's moat metaphor but adds precision: "Power requires a benefit and a barrier." A castle (benefit) alone isn't enough — you need an unbreachable moat (barrier) that prevents competitors from replicating what you do. Many companies claim superiority in team quality or execution speed, but these are "treadmills" — they provide advantage only as long as you keep running, and they can be copied.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hamilton-helmer.md]

### Start Thinking About Strategy Before Product-Market Fit

Hamilton's view has evolved: he now believes founders should think about strategy even before achieving product-market fit. Strategic conversations with early-stage founders revealed unexpected richness. It's not about a formal plan — it's about understanding which business propositions tilt toward "power availability." The questions change by stage, but the strategic lens applies always. "When should you be thinking about this? The answer is always."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hamilton-helmer.md]

### Strategy Is a Long Time-Constant Concept

Hamilton distinguishes strategy from tactics by time horizon. The Pearl Harbor example: a massive tactical success but a catastrophic strategic move because it solved FDR's problem of getting US buy-in for war. "Tactical short, strategy long." In business, strategy is about the fundamental determinants of business value — the NPV of expected cash flow decades out. Any decision with a short time horizon is tactics, not strategy.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hamilton-helmer.md]

### Iconic Businesses Often Have Multiple Acts

Hamilton's research reveals that most iconic businesses have second, third, or fourth acts — AWS at Amazon, CPUs at Intel, iPhone at Apple. These weren't part of the original plan. Understanding your source of power from the original business informs how you can apply it to new opportunities. Strategy isn't just about defending your position — it's about knowing what fundamental advantage you can extend into adjacent markets.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hamilton-helmer.md]

### Concepts Must Be Guided by Usefulness

Hamilton is a disciple of mathematician John von Neumann's view that concepts must be guided by usefulness, not aesthetics. His strategic frameworks were developed through decades of practical consulting, not theoretical exercise. "The domain of things you want to include in the conversation" about strategy must be narrowed to what actually drives business value. Everything else, however intellectually appealing, is noise.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/hamilton-helmer.md]

## Notable Quotes

> "Warren Buffett famously said, in business: 'I look for economic castles protected by unbreachable moats.'"

> "You're on a treadmill and if you stop running on that treadmill, you get creamed, but it's not power."

> "Think of Pearl Harbor — the worst possible strategic move because it completely solved Franklin Delano Roosevelt's problem."
