# Crystal Widjaja

Crystal Widjaja is Chief Product Officer at Kumu, a social super app in the Philippines, and previously built and led the growth team at Gojek (now GoTo) through its early years. Gojek is the largest super app in Southeast Asia, completing more rides per day than Lyft and more food deliveries than GrubHub, Uber Eats, and DoorDash combined. She also founded Generation Girl, a nonprofit helping young women get into STEM.

## Key Views

### Do Things That Don't Scale
Crystal's growth philosophy is built on extreme scrappiness. At Gojek, they rented a stadium to hire 60,000 drivers in a few weeks, pre-loading phones and SIM cards. For testing a subscription feature, they created a WhatsApp group with 100 drivers, had them manually sell subscriptions to customers, and processed payments through intern-run backend operations. "What is the user experience that we want to create? How do we manifest that as quickly as possible? Let's just try that first." Don't build before you validate.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/crystal-w.md]

### Experiment With 30 Users — You'll Get the Same Trend
Crystal is contrarian about early-stage experimentation. "Even if you have a sample size of 30, the data you get back — the underlying information won't be very different at larger scales." While precision improves with more data, the trend direction is the same. "What's better than having 30 data points? Certainly having 100. But what's better than having zero is definitely 30." This empowers startups to test aggressively without waiting for statistical significance at scale.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/crystal-w.md]

### Fix the Step Before Conversion, Not Retention
Crystal's key insight about growth: don't optimize retention directly — fix the step right before conversion. Users make decisions based on preceding events. At Gojek, the problem wasn't getting people to order food; it was trust in new merchants. They hacked trust by showing "your friend ordered from this restaurant" via Facebook Connect, which doubled the likelihood of purchasing from a new restaurant. The lesson: identify the setup moment that blocks conversion and fix that first.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/crystal-w.md]

### Retention Benchmarks: 60% for Free, 20-30% for Paid
Crystal provides concrete retention targets: for free products, weekly retention should be at least 60%. For paid products, 20-30% monthly retention is a good target. And for early friends-and-family stage, retention should be near 80% because "if you can't even convince the people who care about you to use the product, it probably isn't going to solve the job for anyone else." Early retention only goes down as you expand, so start high.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/crystal-w.md]

### Why Super Apps Work in Asia But Not the US
Crystal explains why the super app model succeeds in Asia: phone storage is limited, people may not have computers, and there's cultural trust in conglomerates. "When your phone is full, are you going to delete a photo of your kid or delete this app? You're probably going to delete the app." For any app to survive on a limited-storage device, it needs to handle many jobs. In the US, abundant cloud storage and device capacity make this less critical.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/crystal-w.md]
