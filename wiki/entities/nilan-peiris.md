# Nilan Peiris

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at Wise (one of the fastest-growing and profitable fintech companies globally); long-tenured CPO who helped scale Wise from early stage to public company  
**Known for:** Frameworks for driving organic word-of-mouth growth; building product-led companies that grow through recommendation loops; Wise's "blow their socks off" philosophy.

## Key Views

### 1. Word of Mouth is the Only Sustainable Growth Engine

Nilan's core thesis: most growth channels are rented — paid ads, SEO, affiliate partnerships — but word of mouth is owned. Wise has built its business around organic recommendation, where customers actively tell others about the product. The key insight is that word-of-mouth isn't something you add after building a product — it must be designed into the product experience from day one. If your product doesn't naturally make people want to tell others, no referral program will fix that.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

> "To get to recommendation, you're going to blow your user's socks off. That's the only way they're going to tell someone about you." — Nilan Peiris

### 2. Blow Their Socks Off: The Recommendation Threshold

Nilan's framework for driving word of mouth is simple but hard: you need to create an experience so remarkable that users feel compelled to share it. It's not about optimizing conversion rates incrementally — it's about finding the moment in the product that creates genuine delight and surprise. At Wise, that meant making international money transfers feel instant, transparent, and shockingly cheap compared to banks. The recommendation threshold is crossed when the user thinks "this is too good to keep to myself."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

> "Some people focus on conversion rate — 'I'm going to make this really slick.' That's cool, you get a bit more growth. But to get to recommendation, you're going to blow your user's socks off." — Nilan Peiris

### 3. Measure the Right Things: Active Users, Not Just Signups

Nilan warns against optimizing for vanity metrics like total signups. Wise focuses on active users who complete a transaction — because a signed-up user who never transacts has zero value and zero word-of-mouth potential. The real growth lever is increasing the number of users who experience the core value ("the socks-off moment"). All team goals and success metrics should tie back to this: are we getting more users to that moment?^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

> "A user who signs up but never transacts is not a user. They haven't experienced the product. The only thing that matters is getting people to that first wow moment." — Nilan Peiris

### 4. Squad-Based Product Teams with Clear Ownership

Wise organizes around squads — small, cross-functional teams with clear ownership of specific customer problems or outcomes. Each squad has end-to-end ownership including discovery, delivery, and measurement. Tribes provide overall strategic direction without dictating how squads execute. This structure enables autonomy while maintaining alignment. The key is that squads are accountable for outcomes, not output — they succeed when customer behavior changes, not when features ship.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

> "Squads are accountable for the results of that squad. Tribes provide overall leadership and a light touch strategy. That's how we've evolved." — Nilan Peiris

### 5. Product-Led Growth Means Building a Product That Grows Itself

Nilan distinguishes between "growth hacking" (adding tricks to drive signups) and genuine product-led growth (building a product whose core value prop naturally drives expansion). Wise's growth isn't driven by viral mechanics — it's driven by the fundamental insight that if you save people 90% on international transfers, they will tell everyone they know. The product itself is the growth engine. His advice to founders: don't ask "how do we get more users?" Ask "what would make our current users unable to stop talking about us?"^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

> "The best growth strategy is building a product that's so good, your customers become your marketing department." — Nilan Peiris
