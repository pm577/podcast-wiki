# Nilan Peiris

Nilan Peiris appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss how to drive word of mouth at wise.

## Key Views

**Word of mouth is engineered, not accidental — 70% of Wise’s growth is referral-driven** — Nilan’s core thesis: word of mouth is a systematic growth engine, not a happy accident. Wise engineered it by making NPS the primary KPI, linking it to referral behavior (detractors = low referrals, promoters = 8x more referrals per NPS point), and obsessively mining NPS comments for themes. "If you move the NPS from 30% to 50%, you increase the viral coefficient of your customer base." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

**Blow your users’ socks off — 10x better is the threshold for recommendation** — Most product teams stop when "it works." Nilan argues that’s not enough. For word of mouth, you need to give users an experience they didn’t know was possible — 8-10x cheaper or faster than the alternative. "When you are in that place of doing something that no one has ever done before, that’s when you get it." At Wise, that meant reducing remittance costs from 6% to 0.35% and making transfers instant. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

**Price, speed, ease of use — the three pillars of word-of-mouth product design** — Through thousands of NPS comments and customer conversations, Nilan identified that only three things drive recommendation: price (cheaper), speed (faster), and ease of use (simpler). Everything else (trust, brand, features) is table stakes. "Customers kept telling us the same things: ‘Make it faster, make it cheaper, make it easier to use.’" ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

**Mission-driven growth — an email without a call to action outperformed every marketing campaign** — When Wise rebranded and shared their mission ("make the world’s money move instantly for almost nothing") in an email with no CTA, no signup button, it was forwarded more than any other marketing they had ever done. The emotional connection to the mission (“people on visas, immigrants who struggled with this problem”) drove authentic advocacy that no paid channel could replicate. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

**Work backwards from the theoretical minimum — incrementalism never gets to 10x** — Nilan advocates asking "what is the theoretical minimum cost/maximum speed for this?" and then taking a multi-year engineering investment to get there, rather than iterating incrementally. Wise’s atomic cost allocation (every bill traced to the transaction that caused it) let them identify the 20% of customers driving 80% of costs, raise their prices, and drop prices for everyone else. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

# Nilan Peiris

Nilan Peiris is Chief Product Officer at Wise (formerly TransferWise), one of the fastest-growing and profitable tech companies in the world. He has been at Wise for over 11 years, helping scale it from a small startup to a company moving $12 billion a month with 16 million customers. He is credited with building Wise's extraordinary word-of-mouth growth engine — 70% of new users discover Wise through a friend.

## Key Views

### Word of Mouth Is a Product Problem, Not a Marketing Problem
Nilan's core insight: word of mouth flows from a product so good it "blows your users' socks off." To get recommendation (NPS 9-10), you must deliver an experience the customer didn't know was previously possible. "Some people focus on conversion rate — you get a bit more growth. But to get to recommendation, you're going to blow your user's socks off. You have to give them an experience they didn't know was previously possible." He advocates building a **10x better product** than what exists.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

### NPS Is the Leading Indicator of Viral Growth
Wise discovered that moving NPS from 30% to 50% doubles the viral coefficient — every customer tells more people. The ROI of NPS improvement is enormous because it compounds. Nilan's team mapped the relationship: NPS scores of 1-6 have very low invite rates. Scores of 7-8 double the invites. 8-9 doubles again. 9-10 doubles again. Improving the product experience creates a compounding growth effect that far exceeds any one-time conversion optimization.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

### The Three Pillars of Word of Mouth: Price, Speed, Ease of Use
Through NPS comments, customers consistently told Wise the same things: make it faster, cheaper, and easier to use. These became the company's three product pillars. The key insight: customers would use Wise at 2x cheaper than banks, but they only talked about it when it was **8-10x cheaper**. Incremental improvement doesn't drive word of mouth — you need a dramatic, category-redefining difference.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

### Work Backwards From the Theoretical Minimum
Instead of incremental improvement, Nilan advocates asking: what is the theoretical minimum cost for moving money into a market? What is the theoretical maximum speed? Then work backwards and invest the years needed to achieve it. "How do we take two years and end up there?" Incremental steps will never get you to a 10x better product — you need to fundamentally reimagine the infrastructure.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]

### Trust Is Solved by Friends, Not Landing Pages
Wise's experience showed that trust is nearly impossible to solve through marketing alone. "What people trust is their friends. And this really was way stronger a trust signal than anything I could put on a landing page." Marketing aids recall, but the core trust signal comes from personal recommendation. The shortcut to solving trust problems: deliver a great experience and make it so good people recommend it.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nilan-peiris.md]
