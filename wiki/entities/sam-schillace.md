# Sam Schillace

Deputy CTO and Corporate Vice President at Microsoft. Creator of Google Docs (via his startup Writely, acquired by Google). Founded six startups, was SVP of Engineering at Box through its IPO, worked at Intuit, Macromedia, and was a VC at Google Ventures. One of the most important builders in the history of cloud productivity software.

## Key Views

### 1. Do the Thing You Feel Guilty to Get Paid For

"We tend to undervalue the things we're good at. We tend to think work has to be unpleasant. If something is easy and fun, we don't tend to think it's valuable." Schillace's career advice: find the thing you'd feel guilty getting paid for and do the hell out of it. Work doesn't have to be hard to be valuable. Your natural talents and interests are likely your highest-leverage contribution.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sam-schillace.md]

### 2. Innovation Comes from Playing Around

The genesis of Google Docs wasn't a grand strategic plan — it was three guys in a living room messing around with an interesting idea about collaborative editing. Schillace's philosophy: create space for unstructured exploration. Many of the most impactful products (real-time collaboration, cloud documents) started as experiments, not business plans.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sam-schillace.md]

### 3. The Oldest Google Doc — A Lesson in Long-Term Thinking

The very first Google Doc (October 2005) still exists and works, though its front-end and back-end have both been rewritten multiple times. The Computer History Museum wants to curate it. This illustrates a key insight: building systems that survive multiple technology migrations requires both good engineering and the humility to rewrite when necessary. The document's survival is a testament to thoughtful data architecture.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sam-schillace.md]

### 4. Optimism Is a Superpower for Innovation

Schillace is strikingly optimistic about technology's trajectory. His view: pessimism is easy and common; optimism is rarer and more powerful. Optimistic founders and builders are more likely to attempt hard things, persist through setbacks, and see opportunities others miss. This isn't naive positivity — it's a deliberate choice that enables innovation.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sam-schillace.md]

### 5. Big Companies Can Innovate — They Just Need to Protect the Explorers

Despite his startup roots, Schillace believes large companies like Microsoft can be innovative if they create "protected spaces" where small teams can experiment without the pressure of quarterly business goals. His role as deputy CTO involves creating those conditions — letting small groups pursue ambitious ideas without being absorbed into the existing product machine.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sam-schillace.md]
