# Laura Schaffer

VP of Growth at Amplitude. Previously spent over 7 years at Twilio as Head of Growth and PM lead of the growth platform and experimentation platform, and prior to that VP of Product and Growth at Rapid (formerly Bandwidth). Known for her career growth framework and expertise in experimentation and developer-focused growth.

## Key Views

### 1. Career Growth: Don't Wait for a Path — Carve One

The common approach — work hard in your existing role, impress your manager, wait for promotion — is fragile because it depends on your manager's ability and the company's trajectory. Schaffer's alternative: find valuable insights your executives don't have access to and bring them to life. Executives lose connection to customers as companies grow. If you surface customer insights that align with north star metrics, they will create a role around you. She did this at Bandwidth (turning sales insights into a self-serve e-commerce flow) and at Twilio.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/laura-schaffer.md]

### 2. Run Experiments When You Believe Something Will Hurt — That's Where the Learnings Are

The most valuable experiments are the ones where you expect a negative outcome. In Schaffer's case, adding questions to a signup flow she was sure would hurt conversion actually **improved** it by 5%. The instinct is to protect metrics you care about, but the biggest insights come from testing your assumptions about what will hurt. If you're not running experiments you're nervous about, you're not learning enough.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/laura-schaffer.md]

### 3. Sell to Engineers by Showing, Not Telling

Developers are skeptical of marketing and value technical credibility above all. The approach: build something they can try immediately, provide excellent documentation, and respect their autonomy. At Twilio, growth came from making the product inherently shareable and letting developers discover it through the experience of building, not through sales pitches.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/laura-schaffer.md]

### 4. Gut vs. Data Is a False Dichotomy

The best product decisions use both. "Data tells you what's happening; gut tells you what's worth trying." The key is building enough experience (through reps of making decisions and seeing outcomes) that your gut becomes a reliable pattern-matching engine. Decision logs help accelerate this learning.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/laura-schaffer.md]

### 5. Growth Strategy Must Be Tied to Product Truths

A growth strategy isn't just a list of tactics (email, ads, referrals). It must start with understanding your product's core value proposition and the natural way users discover and adopt it. The growth motion should amplify what's already working, not force an unnatural acquisition channel. Schaffer's approach: identify the product's inherent growth loops and double down on them.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/laura-schaffer.md]
