# Nickey Skarstad

Nickey Skarstad appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss director of product management at duolingo, formerly product lead at airbnb, etsy, and shopify, on translating vision into goals, operationalizing product quality, second-order decision-making, brainstorming, and building influence as a pm.

## Key Views

**Product Quality Should Be the North Star, Balanced Against Growth** — Airbnb doesn't ship product if it's not good. Use quality metrics as balancing indicators alongside growth metrics. At Airbnb Experiences, the top-line goal was review rate and quality rather than just bookings. At Etsy, the team added friction to onboarding to improve first-sale-in-seven-days, which actually accelerated long-term seller success even though it slowed raw volume. Protecting quality is a strategic choice, not a trade-off. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nickey-skarstad.md]

**Bring Your Team Along When Setting Vision and Strategy** — Good product work is not democratic — you need one clear decision-maker — but you must include the team in the journey so they buy in. Talk to leadership for organizational context, and make sure the CEO/founder is bought in because they control resourcing. Use the vision-mission-strategy-OKR pyramid to cascade from 10-year aspirations to quarterly execution. A vision nobody owns is just words on a slide. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nickey-skarstad.md]

**Use One-Way vs Two-Way Door Decisions to Give Teams Autonomy** — One-way door decisions (hard to reverse, like defining what a good Airbnb experience is) deserve deep deliberation and broad alignment. Two-way door decisions (easily reversible) should be delegated so teams can move fast. Build the muscle of second-order thinking by asking "how will this decision cascade through our system?" Most product teams waste energy debating two-way door decisions as if they're one-way. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nickey-skarstad.md]

**Cross-Functional Review Gates at Three Moments** — Product review meetings fail when they happen at the function level in silos. You need cross-functional gates at: (1) a first principles check-in (what are we solving for and why), (2) an approach/architecture review (how will we solve it), and (3) a ship-readiness check. This prevents teams from spending months building before discovering leadership disagrees with the fundamentals. The gates should be about alignment, not approval. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nickey-skarstad.md]

**Audit Your Calendar to Find the Right Role** — Color-code your meetings as red (drains energy), yellow (neutral), or green (energizing). A preponderance of red and yellow means you're in the wrong seat. Also, be a broad consumer — download new products constantly, follow non-tech cultural commentators, and diversify your feed. Understanding the cultural zeitgeist makes you a better product builder and marketer. Product sense is built through diverse consumption, not just shipping features. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nickey-skarstad.md]
