# Alex Hardiman

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at The New York Times; former CPO at The Atlantic; former Facebook News product lead  
**Known for:** Leading product across NYT's news, cooking, games, audio, and advertising products; bridging journalism and technology.

## Key Views

### 1. Mission-first product strategy — impact in service of purpose

Alex's framing: at the New York Times, business goals and impact metrics serve the mission ("seek the truth and help people understand the world"), not the other way around. Growing the subscription business exists to strengthen an informed democracy. For product managers, this means you're driving metrics *and* helping important stories find their real audience to trigger policy change — a dimension of impact she didn't feel at Facebook.

> "Our impact and our business goals are in service of our mission, which is to seek the truth and help people understand the world, not the other way around." — Alex Hardiman

### 2. Product management in a mission-driven organization is fundamentally different

Alex contrasts her experience at Facebook (where product decisions were primarily engagement-driven) with the NYT (where mission and purpose create a different decision-making framework). PMs at NYT don't just optimize for subscribers — they consider whether a deeply reported story triggers an important policy change or law. This dual mandate (commercial + mission) requires a more nuanced approach to prioritization.

### 3. Leading through crisis — from Facebook post-2016 to NYT during COVID

Alex has been "at the center of the storm" multiple times: at Facebook right after the 2016 election, then at NYT as COVID hit. Her experience navigating product decisions during institutional crises provides lessons on maintaining focus, supporting teams under pressure, and balancing speed with responsibility when the world is watching.

### 4. Product teams working with journalists — a unique collaboration model

The NYT's product-engineering-journalism triad is a distinctive operating model. Alex discusses how product teams learn to collaborate with journalists, respecting editorial independence while bringing product thinking to news distribution, audience development, and digital experiences. The key is understanding that journalists are not just "users" of product tools but partners with their own professional norms and values.

### 5. Subscription growth through product quality and habit formation

Under Alex's leadership, NYT has grown its digital subscription business dramatically. Her approach emphasizes product quality as a growth driver — the Cooking and Games products are designed as habit-forming daily rituals, not just content repositories. Each product has its own identity and business model while contributing to the overall subscription bundle.
