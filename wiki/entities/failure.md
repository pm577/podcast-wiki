# Failure

This is a special compilation episode of Lenny's Podcast that curates the most insightful failure stories from past episodes. Featuring stories from Katie Dill (Stripe), Paul Adams (Intercom), Tom Conrad (Zero/Quibi/Pets.com), and Sri Batchu (Ramp), the episode explores failure as an overlooked source of wisdom and growth.

## Key Views

### Come In Listening, Not Swinging — Trust Must Be Earned
Katie Dill's story: she joined Airbnb's design team and "came in swinging" ready to make changes. A month later, half the design team staged an intervention with a written list of everything she was doing wrong. The core issue: she hadn't earned their trust. "Regardless of each individual thing, what was very clear was that the missing piece, the theme across all of that, is that I hadn't earned their trust." She shifted to listening first, and "a couple months later, we had the best engagement scores in the company."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/failure.md]

### Failure Is Not Learning — Fail Conclusively
Sri Batchu's insight: "Failure is not that you didn't drive revenue — failure is not learning." Growth experiments have roughly a 30% success rate, so most things fail. The key is designing experiments that "fail conclusively" — providing definitive answers so you don't keep re-trying the same bad idea when new executives come in. "You can only learn if you've designed the right test and failed conclusively."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/failure.md]

### Build Companies From a Place of Abundance, Not Fear
Paul Adams's lesson from Google's failed social products (Google Buzz, Google+): "A lot of the motivation for those projects came from a place of fear. It didn't come from a place of 'let's make a great product for people.'" Products built from competitive fear — trying to catch up to Facebook — lead to poor outcomes. In contrast, products like Google Maps were built from a genuine desire to solve a user problem. The source of motivation matters enormously.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/failure.md]

### Companies Are Math Problems — Fix the Equation, Not Just the Product
Tom Conrad's lesson from Quibi and Pets.com: product excellence alone can't save a company if the foundational business equation is broken. "If the equation is fundamentally broken or a big swing in and of itself, no amount of iteration and execution can get you out of the failed outputs of the broken equation." Quibi's bet on premium short-form content required $6-10 billion to build a sufficient library — no amount of execution could fix that math. Know when the equation itself is the problem.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/failure.md]

### Ship Fast, Ship Early, Ship Often
Paul Adams on Intercom's culture: "Ship fast, ship early, ship often. In that idea is the idea of failure. It's not going to go right, and it's going to go wrong more often than not. But if you ship early and fast and learn fast, you can change fast, and you can improve fast." This creates a culture where failure is normalized as part of the learning process. The tension between high standards and shipping fast is a constant navigation, but embracing that tension is what enables product teams to innovate.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/failure.md]
