# Sri Batchu

Sri Batchu leads growth at Ramp, the fastest-growing SaaS and FinTech business in history (reaching $100M ARR in two years). Previously he was Head of Growth at Instacart and VP of Ops at Opendoor. He's an expert on B2B growth strategy, team structuring, and operational velocity.

## Key Views

**The B2B growth channel progression follows a predictable order: founder-led sales, first sales hires, low-cost marketing, then paid channels.** Sri outlines a clear path: start with founder-led sales, hire your first salespeople, then begin low-cost targeted marketing (content, community, small events, PR), and only after all of that invest in paid marketing and SEO. The reason is simple — channels get more expensive as you go, and they become more effective once you deeply understand your customers.

**Cap table as a growth strategy.** Ramp's co-founders (Eric and Karim) brought a large number of early-stage founders, influential operators, and advisors onto their cap table. Many of these became initial customers. While Ramp is now majority mid-market and enterprise (not startups), this early strategy seeded the company's growth through the tech founder community's love and advocacy.

**Growth engineering teams can make every channel more efficient.** Rather than finding a unique growth channel, Ramp's secret sauce is using technology and data to make all channels more efficient. They have a dedicated growth engineering team that works on sales automation — using AI and third-party data to make sales teams more efficient — well before AI became a buzzword. This data-driven approach is applied systematically across every growth channel.

**The vast majority of Ramp's growth comes from new customer acquisition, not expansion.** Despite having a product (corporate cards) where expansion through increased spending is natural, Sri notes that new customer acquisition still drives most of their growth. The company is adding so many new customers that the expansion lever, while meaningful, is secondary. This underscores the power of product-market fit in the core product.

**Lean team with high revenue per employee is a deliberate design choice.** Ramp operates with under 500 people at massive scale, resulting in significantly higher revenue per employee than competitors. This efficiency is intentional — the company avoids bloat and focuses on building systems and technology that amplify the impact of each team member rather than hiring more people to solve problems.
