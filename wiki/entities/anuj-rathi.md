# Anuj Rathi

**Role:** Chief Product and Marketing Officer at Jupiter Money, former SVP Revenue and Growth at Swiggy (7 years), first Product Manager at Flipkart  
**Known for:** The 4 BB framework for product strategy, "full-stack PM" philosophy, working backwards with three divergent options, and deep product thinking for the Indian market.

## Key Views

### 1. The 4 BB Framework for Product Strategy

Anuj's framework for strategic resource allocation across four buckets:
1. **Brilliant Basics** — tech debt, platform stability, hygiene. Investment needed but can't be branded negatively as "tech debt."
2. **Bread and Butter** — the regular product backlog: feature enhancements, bug fixes, version two iterations, experiments.
3. **Big Bets** — cross-team initiatives that require working backwards together. High risk, high reward.
4. **Breaking Bad** — existential pivots: redefining what the company is (e.g., Swiggy moving from food delivery to "convenience company").
The key insight: these trade-offs are **product strategy** decisions, not PM-level prioritization calls. Leaders must explicitly decide how to allocate focus across the four buckets and accept the consequences (e.g., "less investment in tech basics means more downtime — that's the trade-off we're accepting for this breaking bad pivot").^[episodes/lenny/anuj-rathi]

### 2. The Full-Stack PM — Own Outcomes, Not Features

Product managers must think across the entire user journey, not just their feature area. Anuj's full-stack PM:
- **Thinks like a marketer** — understands positioning, the single value prop that will bring a lazy/vain/selfish user to the product
- **Thinks like a salesperson** — recognizes that PMs are in the "business of influence," both internally with engineers/leadership and externally with users
- **Thinks like a designer** — maps every pixel, every copy word, every emotional state of the user
- **Uses "show, don't tell"** — creates detailed visual walls of the full user experience, showing the trigger, the journey, and the emotional state at every step
Cites the book *Range* (David Epstein): Roger Federer played multiple racket sports before focusing on tennis. The breadth of experience creates better pattern-matching. The same is true for PMs.^[episodes/lenny/anuj-rathi]

### 3. Users Are Lazy, Vain, and Selfish (Scott Belsky's Framework)

Anuj applies Scott Belsky's user psychology framework relentlessly:
- **Lazy** — "I don't have time for this. Blow my mind away or I'm gone."
- **Vain** — "I have a habit. You expect me to change it for your new app?"
- **Selfish** — "Show me what's in it for me."
When building for new users, especially in India, this framework forces teams to connect the marketing message all the way through onboarding with a consistent value prop. Most teams neglect this. At Swiggy, reframing every touchpoint from the user's lazy/vain/selfish perspective instantly showed results. At Jupiter, it helps cross-sell by respecting that the user may only want one service today.^[episodes/lenny/anuj-rathi]

### 4. Three Divergent Options Before Committing

Anuj requires his teams to always explore three divergent approaches before picking one. For product strategy (the 4 BB), for PR/FAQs (working backwards), for user journeys:
- Write three full PR/FAQs with distinct approaches
- Present all three to leadership with a **recommendation** for one
- The other two demonstrate that you've considered alternatives and incorporated the best elements
This serves as a leadership tool — people see their input reflected (or understand why it was rejected), and it forces the team to think through the full space of possibilities instead of anchoring on the first idea.^[episodes/lenny/anuj-rathi]

### 5. Excellence Over Speed — Most Experiments Should Be Thought Experiments

Anuj's contrarian take: when forced to choose, **choose excellence over speed**. Most experiments, he believes, should stay as thought experiments — smart people doing meta-thinking can predict failure without running the test. "A lot of experiments would just not even be tried out because they're obviously going to fail." This is contrary to the "move fast and break things" mantra. Speed and excellence are two axes; ideally maximize both, but if forced, pick excellence. The framework doesn't mean don't experiment — it means be thoughtful about which experiments are worth the company's time.^[episodes/lenny/anuj-rathi]

### 6. Working Backwards Applied as a Full-Company Alignment Tool

Anuj adapted Amazon's working backwards process into a practical tool:
- Start with a PR/FAQ that specifies the **launch date, value proposition, customer quote, business quote**
- Use it for **negotiation** — the VP Engineering can say "too aggressive"; the pricing team can say "can't deliver that quote"
- Use it for **alignment** — if the date changes, goals change too; all interdependencies surface
- **FAQs are process setters** — at Jupiter, every PR/FAQ must answer "How will this be fully compliant?"; at Swiggy, every marketplace change must address impact on all three sides (consumer, delivery partner, restaurant)
This one-pager becomes the single source of truth that marketing, engineering, design, pricing, and operations all align around.^[episodes/lenny/anuj-rathi]

### 7. The Three Things That Make a Great PM

Anuj's simple filter for whether someone should be a PM:
1. **Raw smarts** — problem identification and problem-solving ability, higher-order thinking
2. **Drive/grit** — curiosity, learnability, never giving up, consumer-backward thinking
3. **Influence** — "You're in the business of influence." If you don't see yourself as someone who can get better at influence, this field won't serve you well.
His contrarian opinion: most people who call themselves product managers shouldn't be — they entered the field without fully understanding what it takes.^[episodes/lenny/anuj-rathi]

# Anuj Rathi

**Role:** Chief Product and Marketing Officer at Jupiter Money; former SVP Revenue & Growth at Swiggy (7 years); first PM at Flipkart  
**Known for:** Pioneering product management in India, the "full-stack PM" concept, scaling Swiggy's growth.

## Key Views

### 1. The leadership diagnostic: can't do, won't do, or not set up to do?

Anuj's framework for diagnosing execution gaps: when things don't happen as a leader wants, there are only three reasons — the person **can't do** (capability issue), **won't do** (motivation/alignment issue), or **was not set up to do** (systems/process issue, which is the leader's fault). Each requires a different intervention: coaching/training for capability, vision/alignment conversations for motivation, and process redesign for setup failures.

> "There are only three reasons why things do not happen: the person can't do, which is capability; they won't do, which is motivation or alignment; or they were not set up to do, which is your problem." — Anuj Rathi

### 2. The full-stack PM — own product, growth, and business outcomes

Anuj advocates for PMs who go beyond feature delivery to own business outcomes end-to-end. At Swiggy and Jupiter, he built PMs who understand growth mechanics, revenue models, marketing, and analytics — not just product specs. This "full-stack" orientation makes PMs more effective partners to leadership and better at making trade-offs.

### 3. Building product in India — unique challenges and opportunities

As a product leader across India's top tech companies (Flipkart, Swiggy, Snapdeal), Anuj shares insights on building for the Indian market: extreme price sensitivity, mobile-first (often mobile-only), language diversity, and the importance of offline-online integration. These constraints drive creative product solutions that wouldn't emerge in Western markets.

### 4. Growth is a mindset, not a team

Anuj argues that growth shouldn't be siloed into a "growth team" — it should be a mindset embedded across product, engineering, and marketing. Every product decision should consider acquisition, activation, retention, and revenue implications. At Swiggy, he embedded growth thinking into every product pod rather than centralizing it.

### 5. Prioritization through first principles, not templates

Anuj pushes back against rigid prioritization frameworks (RICE, ICE, etc.) as crutches. He advocates for first-principles thinking about what creates the most customer and business value, combined with a clear understanding of your strategic context. Frameworks are useful starting points, but they shouldn't replace judgment.
