# Shweta Shrivastava

Shweta Shrivastava appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss senior director of product management at waymo, formerly cpo of nauto, head of product management at aws, and product leader at cisco, on product management in autonomous driving, safety-first product development, and building trust in ai systems.

## Key Views

**Safety-First MVP: The Bar for Minimum Viable Product Is Radically Different in Autonomous Driving** — In autonomous driving, safety cannot be compromised, raising the bar for what constitutes a viable minimum product. The core product management philosophy of 'iterate and deploy' applies at a much higher safety threshold. Every release must be measured against human driving baselines, not just feature completeness, fundamentally changing how product velocity is measured. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shweta-shriva.md]

**Human-Like Behavior, Not Robotic Behavior, Builds Rider Trust** — Waymo trains its deep-learning models on human driving data (discarding bad driving behavior) so the car feels natural, predictable, and familiar rather than robotic. This helps riders move from initial awe to a feeling that the ride is 'uneventful' within the first five minutes. The goal is not just safety but a driving style that passengers instinctively trust. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shweta-shriva.md]

**Driving Is Fundamentally a Social Activity That Autonomous Systems Must Master** — The vehicle must learn social norms, body language, gestures, and city-specific driving conventions (e.g. when pedestrians cross without a walk signal) that vary across geographies. The driving problem is far more interactive and nuanced than a purely technical one — it requires modeling human social behavior at scale. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shweta-shriva.md]

**Product Managers in Autonomous Driving Need Deeper Technical Depth and Higher Tolerance for Ambiguity** — Compared to traditional software, PMs at Waymo must dive into technical details as needed, navigate greater uncertainty and ambiguity, and have the tenacity to play a long-term game driven by the mission of saving the 1.35 million lives lost annually to traffic accidents. The feedback loop from product decision to real-world outcome is measured in years, not weeks. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shweta-shriva.md]

**Commercial Deployment, Not Just Tech Milestones, Sustains Investor Confidence** — The key to maintaining long-term buy-in from backers like Alphabet is showing meaningful progress through real-world commercial deployments and accelerating milestones, rather than chasing short-term optics. A complex hardware-software system like autonomous driving requires patience from both builders and investors, and real-world deployments are the only credible proof of progress. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shweta-shriva.md]
