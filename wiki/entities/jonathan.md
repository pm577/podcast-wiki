# Jonathan Lowenhar

Jonathan Lowenhar is the co-founder of Enjoy The Work, an executive coaching firm that has helped over 165 founders become great CEOs. A former startup founder and private equity CEO himself, he's developed frameworks for the critical transition from founder-mode to CEO-craft that most founders never learn.

## Key Views

**Founder Is an Attitude; CEO Is a Craft — They're Different Jobs**
Lowenhar makes a crucial distinction: "To be a founder is a state of being, it's an attitude. To be a CEO is a craft." The skills that make someone a great founder — grit, tenacity, vision — are completely different from the skills needed to run a company well. Most venture investors can describe their ideal founder in one set of words and their ideal CEO in an entirely different set. The problem is that nobody teaches founders how to learn the CEO craft while doing the job.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jonathan-lowenhar.md]]*

**Phase One: Build Something People Want. Phase Two: Build a Company Around It**
Lowenhar argues that the startup journey has two distinct phases that require different mindsets and skills. Phase one is entirely about product-market fit. Phase two — which most founders fail at — is about building an organization around that product. "Building a company is always the same. I don't care if it's MedTech or fintech or hardware or consumer." The skills for phase two (financial planning, organizational design, hiring systems, go-to-market) are learnable, but almost no one explicitly teaches them.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jonathan-lowenhar.md]]*

**The Magic Box Paradigm: Make Yourself Unavailable for Sale**
Lowenhar's Magic Box framework for exits argues against the traditional "build a list of buyers and pitch them" approach. Instead, the goal is to be so strategically valuable that buyers come to you. "You're never for sale. In fact, you have seduced a buyer. They see the fantasy, they fall in love." By focusing on inflicting "strategic pain" on potential acquirers — making it impossible for them to ignore your competitive advantage — you create the conditions for a premium exit without ever putting yourself on the market.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jonathan-lowenhar.md]]*

**"Founder Mode" Is Permission to Not Learn the Job — Reject It**
Lowenhar has strong feelings about the popular "founder mode" concept: "That article just got me hot. It really felt like an excuse. We were giving founders permission to not learn the job." He argues that the best CEOs know when to calibrate between founder-mode instincts and manager-mode discipline. The greatest leaders move fluidly between both modes depending on what the situation demands, rather than using one label as a shield against developing the skills they lack.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jonathan-lowenhar.md]]*

**Every Well-Run Company Has a Rhythm — And Founders Can Learn It**
Lowenhar's realization after running multiple company types (public, private equity, startup): when companies were run well, they were all run well in the same way. "It's unmistakable, you can't miss it." This universal rhythm — of planning, execution, review, and adjustment — can be taught. His firm spends its time codifying that rhythm and helping founders internalize it, because the alternative (being fired by your board or burning out) is too common and too painful.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jonathan-lowenhar.md]]*
