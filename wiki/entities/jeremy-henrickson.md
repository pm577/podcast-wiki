# Jeremy Henrickson

**Role:** SVP of Product at Rippling (leading product and design across three continents); former CPO at Coinbase (oversaw 10x growth during the 2017 crypto boom)  
**Known for:** Expertise in maintaining velocity at scale; the "design for the most complex use case first" approach (anti-MVP); building new product lines via small founder-like teams within a large company.

## Key Views

### 1. Maintain Velocity at Scale: Small Teams, Clear Missions, Dive Deep

Jeremy's formula for keeping velocity as organizations grow: **(1)** Small teams with clear missions — "If there's 300 people trying to work on one thing, the sheer communication challenges make it impossible to act quickly." **(2)** Invest in a clear platform layer — reduces decision-making complexity for everyone. **(3)** Leaders must **dive deep** — "It's very tempting to float up as a leader and say 'you take that hill.' Where you really learn is by being in the trenches." **(4)** Ensure teams have the right experience mix for their current stage — zero-to-one people aren't always good at scale.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jeremy-henrickson.md]

> "It's very, very tempting to float up here as a leader and say 'Hey, you take that hill over there.' Where you really learn where the challenges are is by just being there with the people in the trenches." — Jeremy Henrickson

### 2. The Rippling Model: Founder-Like Teams for New Products

Rippling's approach to building new products: start with a single entrepreneurial engineer (not a PM), pair them with a designer, and let them recruit 2-4 other zero-to-one engineers. This tiny team spends 6-9 months going from "blank sheet of paper to launched product." The CEO (Parker Conrad) and Jeremy act as DRIs, giving feedback every few weeks. This model has been replicated a dozen times — the time and attendance product was built by a 4-person team in ~9 months. The key: monomaniacal focus on one thing, minimizing horizontal communication.

### 3. Anti-MVP: Design for the Most Complex Use Case First

Jeremy's controversial take: MVPs can do a disservice. "When you design a minimum viable product, you're optimizing for speed. You minimize deeper product thinking about what can fully differentiate our product." The problem: if you only think through simple cases, you make architectural assumptions that are extremely hard to unwind later. Instead, Rippling designs for the hardest use case first (e.g., the "healthcare hospital administration case where it's mission-critical"), then builds simpler cases on top. This takes more time upfront but saves exponential time later. The global payroll product example: they launched 6 very different countries simultaneously to build a platform that was 80% shared infrastructure.

### 4. Navigating Extreme Growth at Coinbase — Focus on Security and Commitment

During Coinbase's 40x growth in 2017, Jeremy's leadership approach was: **(1)** Security always came first — no trade-offs on customer money safety. **(2)** Make clear company-level decisions about uncertain topics (e.g., "Is Ethereum going to be a thing?") — then go full speed until you decide to go a different direction. **(3)** Build teams you can trust in the trenches — "Saturday mornings something would break and we'd need to jump on it." The intensity was a "dream and a nightmare," but the rate of learning was unmatched.

### 5. Lessons From the Crucible — The Silver Lining of Chaos

Jeremy's perspective on leading through extreme periods (40x growth, new daughter born, sleep deprivation): "It is those experiences that have most accelerated my personal growth because it's in the crucible of things being hard." His advice to others going through it: step back, talk with friends, set it in context. "Three, four, five years from now, you'll realize you did something amazing and learned a lot that you took with you."
