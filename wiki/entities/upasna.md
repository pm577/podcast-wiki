# Upasna Gautam

Upasna Gautam is a product manager at CNN Digital, where she leads the team building the content management system that journalists use to write and publish stories. She's also a longtime meditation and mindfulness teacher who brings equanimity practices into high-pressure product management.

## Key Views

**Equanimity is a superpower in chaotic environments.** Working in news product management requires thriving in chaos, not just ambiguity. Upasna credits years of mindfulness practice with developing equanimity — mental calmness and composure even in the highest-stress situations. This ability to pause before reacting rather than being reactive is the root solution to many workplace challenges.

**Build buffers into everything when your customers have unpredictable schedules.** At CNN, journalists can be pulled away at any moment by breaking news. Upasna's team builds buffers of a day, week, or even a month into their onboarding and testing cycles to account for this. Rather than fighting the chaos, they design their processes around it, knowing that their editorial customers' priorities will shift instantly.

**Four-touchpoint stakeholder engagement system.** Upasna implemented a structured system for working with journalists that includes weekly demo days, working sessions, breaking news dress rehearsals, and office hours. Each serves a distinct purpose — demo days evangelize the product, working sessions recreate workflows collaboratively, dress rehearsals stress-test the platform, and office hours provide open access for questions.

**Reframe user testing as collaborative "working sessions."** By changing the name from "user testing" to "working sessions," Upasna made journalists feel like partners in the process rather than subjects being tested. This shift improved collaboration and feedback quality, building trust that makes it easier to say "no" to feature requests when needed.

**Product management in news is fundamentally about serving internal customers.** Unlike B2B or B2C PMs, Upasna's primary customers are her colleagues — journalists and editors. This means the typical power dynamic is reversed: her "customers" have their own urgent jobs (covering breaking news) that will always take priority over product work. Success requires adapting to their schedules, not the other way around.
