# Tristan de Montebello

Tristan de Montebello is the co-creator of Ultraspeaking, widely regarded as one of the most effective methods for rapidly improving public speaking and communication skills. In 2017, he became the fastest competitor to reach the finals of the World Championship of Public Speaking.

## Key Views

**Speaking is a meta skill, not a specialized skill.** Tristan argues that improving your speaking transforms every area of your life — work, relationships, family — just like fitness does. People underestimate how transformative becoming a better speaker can be because it affects everything, not just presentations and meetings.

**If you're not enjoying speaking, you're doing it wrong.** Tristan views enjoyment as a barometer for effective speaking. Since humans are naturally evolved to communicate, discomfort signals that you've adopted counterproductive habits like overthinking or trying to control every word. The goal is to feel like yourself, the way you do when talking to close friends or family.

**Speaking is a subconscious flow-state process, not a conscious one.** The breakthrough insight for Tristan was that the best speaking happens when you're not thinking about speaking at all. Instead of focusing on symptoms (filler words, racing speech), he advises addressing root causes — like discomfort with slowing down, pausing, or allowing emotion under pressure. Fix the root cause and the symptoms resolve naturally.

**Most public speaking advice focuses on the wrong things.** Traditional advice often adds more frameworks and tactics to consciously think about, which makes speaking worse. Tristan's approach is about "upgrading the software" by removing bad habits (bugs) and installing new ones, allowing speakers to stay in flow state without getting pulled out by self-consciousness.

**Practice must be enjoyable to be sustainable.** Just like a fitness journey, public speaking improvement requires consistent practice. If the practice itself isn't enjoyable, people will quit after two weeks. Tristan structures exercises as games that reward participants, creating positive reinforcement loops that keep people engaged in their development.
