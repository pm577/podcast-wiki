# Shaun Clowes

**Role:** Chief Product Officer at Confluent (Apache Kafka company); former CPO at Salesforce's MuleSoft and Metromile; first Head of Growth at Atlassian, where he led product for Jira  
**Known for:** Deep expertise in data infrastructure products and AI; the "great AI products are all about the data" thesis; building product teams at data-platform companies.

## Key Views

### 1. Great AI Products Are All About the Data

Shaun's central thesis: the difference between good and great AI products isn't the model — it's the data strategy. Companies that win with AI have clean, well-organized, accessible data that powers their models. The model is a commodity; the data pipeline is the competitive advantage. He argues that most companies invest 10x more in modeling than in data infrastructure, which is exactly backwards.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shaun-clowes.md]

> "Everyone obsesses over the model, but the real work is in the data. The companies that win with AI are the ones with the best data pipelines, not the best models." — Shaun Clowes

### 2. Data Products Require a Different Product Mindset

Building data products is fundamentally different from building SaaS products. Data products need: (1) extreme reliability — data can't be wrong; (2) different discovery methods — users don't know what data they need until they see it; (3) ecosystem thinking — data is only valuable when connected to other data. Shaun argues that product managers who succeed in data have a deeper technical understanding and a longer time horizon for measuring success.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shaun-clowes.md]

> "Data products are harder to build than traditional SaaS because the value is indirect. You're not solving a discrete problem — you're enabling a thousand solutions you can't predict." — Shaun Clowes

### 3. PLG in Infrastructure: Self-Serve Must Be Real

Shaun pioneered product-led growth at Atlassian, proving that even infrastructure/developer products can grow through self-serve. The key: make the product genuinely usable without a salesperson. This means investing in documentation, onboarding flows, sample data, and community support. Many enterprise companies claim to be PLG but still require sales calls for core functionality. True PLG means someone can get real value from your product in under 30 minutes without talking to anyone.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shaun-clowes.md]

> "Product-led growth in infrastructure isn't about giving away a watered-down version. It's about making the real product genuinely usable by a single developer in 30 minutes." — Shaun Clowes

### 4. The Three Horizons of Product Strategy

Shaun's product strategy framework: Horizon 1 (0-12 months) — optimize and extend the current product; Horizon 2 (1-3 years) — build adjacent capabilities that expand the market; Horizon 3 (3-5+ years) — invest in moonshots that could transform the category. Most companies over-index on Horizon 1. The key is maintaining a balanced portfolio where you're investing in all three horizons simultaneously, even when short-term pressure pushes toward Horizon 1 exclusively.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shaun-clowes.md]

> "If all your investment is in Horizon 1, you'll be stable today and irrelevant tomorrow. You need to be working on all three horizons simultaneously." — Shaun Clowes

### 5. Enterprise AI Adoption Requires Trust, Not Just Features

Shaun's perspective on selling AI into enterprises: enterprises don't buy AI features — they buy trust. They need to trust that the data is secure, that the model is reliable, that the vendor will be around in 5 years, and that the output is auditable. The product features are table stakes; trust is the differentiator. Companies that rush to market with AI features without building trust foundations (security certifications, explainability, data governance) will lose to slower, more deliberate competitors.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/shaun-clowes.md]

> "Enterprises don't buy AI because it's cool. They buy it because they trust it. Trust is the moat in enterprise AI." — Shaun Clowes
