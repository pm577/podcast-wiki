# Bret Taylor

**Role:** Co-founder and CEO of Sierra (AI agents for customer service); former Co-CEO of Salesforce, CTO of Meta, Chairman of OpenAI Board; co-creator of Google Maps and the Facebook Like button; co-founder of FriendFeed and Quip  
**Known for:** An unmatched track record in tech (Google Maps, Like button, FriendFeed, Quip, Salesforce), his conviction that all software will become agentic, and his philosophy of "waking up every morning asking what's the most impactful thing I can do today."

## Key Views

### 1. The entire software market is moving to agents and outcomes-based pricing

Bret's central thesis at Sierra: the whole market is going towards **agents** and **outcomes-based pricing**. Selling productivity software is fundamentally hard because you're asking customers to pay for potential value that they must realize themselves. With agents, you can price based on actual outcomes delivered — e.g., successfully resolved customer service tickets. This aligns incentives perfectly: the vendor only gets paid when value is actually created. This is "so obviously the correct way to build and sell software" that Bret believes it will become the dominant model.

> "The whole market is going to go towards agents. I think the whole market is going to go towards outcomes-based pricing. It's just so obviously the correct way to build and sell software." — Bret Taylor

### 2. The most impactful thing — a daily decision filter

Bret's simple but powerful daily practice: wake up every morning and ask, "What is the most impactful thing I could do today?" This mindset has guided him through CTO of Facebook, co-CEO of Salesforce, Chairman of OpenAI during the Sam Altman crisis, and building Sierra. It's a forcing function for prioritization that works at any scale — whether you're a PM at Google or a CEO. The discipline is in asking the question honestly every day and having the courage to act on the answer even when it's uncomfortable.

> "Waking up every morning, what is the most impactful thing I could do today?" — Bret Taylor

### 3. The PM is a leader, not just a spec writer — lessons from his biggest failure

Bret's most formative failure was his first product review as a Google PM. He led "Google Local" (pre-Google Maps) and had a tough review with Marissa Mayer and Larry Page — failing to communicate the vision effectively. The lesson: as a PM, you are fundamentally a leader who must consolidate ideas, get alignment, and drive execution. He got a second chance on "V2" — which became Google Maps (10M users on day one). This experience taught him that failure is often the precursor to massive success if you learn from it.

> "They gave me another shot to do the V2 of it that resulted in Google Maps. We got about 10 million people using it on the first day." — Bret Taylor

### 4. "Thin wrapper around GPT" is wrong — applications are stickier than people think

Bret strongly pushes back on the "thin wrapper" narrative about AI startups. The same criticism was made in the 1980s about "thin wrappers around an RDBMS" — yet companies like Salesforce, SAP, and Oracle built massive, durable businesses. ChatGPT itself has a real moat despite competition from Google, Meta, and Elon Musk. The application layer in AI involves complex workflows, integrations, data, trust, and UX that create genuine stickiness. Sierra's agents, for example, require deep integration with a company's systems, knowledge base, and policies — not something a model alone can replace.

> "The thing that people got very wrong is this whole thin wrapper around GPT, that's really wrong. In fact, here's how wrong it is. Back in the '80s, that same phrase was used, but it was 'thin wrapper around an RDBMS.'" — Bret Taylor

### 5. Success after failure is empowering — embrace the second chance

Bret's Google Maps story is a powerful testament to resilience: his V1 of Google Local was a failure, but he got another shot and created one of the most important products of the internet era. His message to builders: it's possible to succeed in spite of massive failure. The key is learning from what went wrong, not being paralyzed by it, and showing up for the next opportunity with humility and determination.
