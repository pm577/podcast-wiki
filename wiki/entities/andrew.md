# Andrew Wilkinson

**Role:** Co-founder and CEO of Tiny — a holding company (the "Berkshire Hathaway of the internet") owning 40+ businesses including Dribbble and AeroPress  
**Known for:** Bootstrapping from zero to $1B+ net worth through acquiring and holding profitable internet businesses.

## Key Views

### 1. Fish where the fish are — don't try to build in crowded, failing spaces

Andrew's #1 business lesson from 75+ companies: the biggest mistakes come from entering business models where others have repeatedly failed, thinking "I can do this better." Instead, follow Charlie Munger's advice: fish where the fish are. Go into spaces with proven demand and existing successful players, not spaces where everyone has tried and failed. First-time entrepreneurs trying to build "the next great AI company" is like walking into the gym and trying to deadlift 300 pounds on day one.

> "The biggest mistakes I've made have been going into business models where other people have repeatedly failed and thinking, 'I can do this better.'" — Andrew Wilkinson

### 2. AI as the world's most reliable employee — $200/month, works 24/7

Andrew's perspective on AI: it's like having an employee who costs $200 a month, never sleeps, and works 24/7. This will massively reshape knowledge work. His provocative framing: "Do all jobs just become a single prompt?" This doesn't mean everyone is replaced — it means the nature of work fundamentally changes when the marginal cost of cognitive labor approaches zero.

> "It's like having the world's most reliable employee who costs $200 a month and works 24/7. So many knowledge work jobs are going to change massively." — Andrew Wilkinson

### 3. Boring businesses compound — the Tiny acquisition philosophy

Tiny's approach: buy profitable, unsexy businesses in stable markets, hold them forever, and let compounding work. The holding company model (inspired by Berkshire Hathaway) avoids the pressure of VC-backed growth-at-all-costs. Andrew argues that "boring" businesses with predictable cash flows and durable competitive advantages outperform flashy startups over the long term.

### 4. Bootstrapping builds different muscles than VC funding

Andrew bootstrapped Tiny from zero to hundreds of millions. His view: bootstrapping forces discipline, creativity, and genuine customer focus that VC money can soften. When you can't buy growth, you have to earn it — and that builds a fundamentally stronger business. The Tiny model of acquiring already-profitable businesses is a form of "acqui-bootstrap" that combines the best of both worlds.

### 5. Founder liquidity and the case for secondaries

Andrew is a vocal advocate for founders taking money off the table early. His experience running Tiny showed him that many founders hold on too long, waiting for an exit that may never come, or take VC money at terms they later regret. Selling a portion of your company (or selling to Tiny) provides financial freedom that makes you a better, more patient operator.
