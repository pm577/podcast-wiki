# Christina Wodtke

**Role:** Author of *Radical Focus* (the definitive OKR book); Stanford University professor; speaker and consultant on building high-performing teams  
**Known for:** The most practical and accessible guide to OKRs for startups and tech companies; the "celebrate Fridays" ritual; making OKRs work for real teams.

## Key Views

### 1. OKRs Are a Communication Tool, Not a Performance Management System

Christina's core framing: OKRs are first and foremost a tool for focus and alignment — they help everyone in the organization know what matters most. They are NOT for performance reviews, compensation, or punishment. When OKRs are tied to compensation, people sandbag (set easy goals) and the entire system breaks. OKRs should be aspirational (60-70% completion is good) and focused on outcomes, not outputs.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christina-wodtke.md]

> "OKRs are not a performance review tool. If you tie them to comp, people will game them and you'll lose all the benefits. They're about focus and alignment." — Christina Wodtke

### 2. Three Company OKRs, Three Team OKRs

Christina's rule of thumb: no more than three company-level OKRs at a time. Each team should have no more than three team OKRs that ladder up to the company ones. More than three creates confusion and dilution of focus. Each objective should have 2-3 measurable key results. The entire company should be able to recite the top three objectives from memory — if they can't, you have too many.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christina-wodtke.md]

> "If everyone in your company can't recite the top three objectives, you have too many. Three is the magic number. Any more and you're not focused." — Christina Wodtke

### 3. Key Results Must Be Measurable, Not Binary

Christina's most common fix for broken OKRs: key results should be on a 0-1.0 scale, not binary (shipped/didn't ship). A binary KR is an output, not an outcome. For example, "increase trial-to-paid conversion from 3% to 5%" is a good KR. "Ship new pricing page" is a project, not a KR. The KR should measure the change in customer behavior or business result, not the completion of a task.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christina-wodtke.md]

> "A key result that's either done or not done is not a key result — it's a project. A KR must be measurable on a scale. Did user behavior actually change?" — Christina Wodtke

### 4. Celebrate Wins — Don't Just Chase Goals

Christina emphasizes that teams often grind toward goals without pausing to celebrate progress. She advocates for "Friday celebrations" — a simple weekly ritual where teams share wins, no matter how small. This builds momentum, morale, and a sense of progress. Gratitude and recognition are not soft skills — they're essential for sustaining performance through the inevitable rough patches.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christina-wodtke.md]

> "People do not value celebrations enough. I've had CEOs who said 'well, it was the middle of the quarter, so we didn't start OKRs, but we did start Friday celebrations and oh my God, things are already better.'" — Christina Wodtke

### 5. Cadence and Rhythm Matter More Than the Format

Christina's final insight: the format of your OKRs matters much less than the cadence of check-ins. A weekly check-in (30 min, team level, focused on progress and blockers) is what makes OKRs work. Companies that spend weeks crafting the perfect OKR document but never revisit it are wasting their time. The rhythm creates accountability and momentum. Her advice: start simple, set the weekly check-in rhythm, and iterate the format over time.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christina-wodtke.md]

> "It's not the OKR document that matters — it's the weekly conversation about progress. The rhythm is what makes the system work." — Christina Wodtke
