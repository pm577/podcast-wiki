# Christina Wodtke

*Author of Radical Focus, Stanford lecturer, and the "queen of OKRs." Former product leader at LinkedIn, MySpace, Zynga, and Yahoo. Consults with companies on OKR processes.*

## Key Views

1. **OKRs are a vitamin, not a medicine**: If your company lacks strategy, empowered teams, or psychological safety, OKRs won't fix it — they'll just reveal everything that's wrong. Companies that succeed with OKRs already "have their shit together." OKRs supercharge strong cultures but can't create them.

2. **The atomic unit of OKRs is a weekly question**: The heart of OKRs isn't a template — it's asking "What am I doing this week to get closer to my outcomes?" If you can answer that consistently, you can drop all the formal OKR machinery. The weekly rhythm of reflection is more important than the quarterly planning.

3. **Radical focus means one big rock per quarter**: Companies fail at OKRs by trying to do everything at once (the "peanut butter" problem — spreading 1% across 100 things). One objective per quarter, one big rock to move. Next quarter, move the next rock. This accelerates accomplishment far more than doing many things poorly.

4. **Celebration is undervalued**: The simple act of a Friday celebration — sharing what was awesome in marketing, design, or engineering — changes team dynamics. CEOs who "didn't have time for OKRs" started with Friday celebrations and saw immediate improvement. It makes people feel part of something special.

5. **"How do we know?" is the key question for key results**: Strategy connects to objectives, and key results answer "how do we know we succeeded?" The question forces measurable outcomes — user testing scores, landing page signups, technical feasibility validation. Estimation is a learned skill; practicing it quarterly makes teams better at predicting.
