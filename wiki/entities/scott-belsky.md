# Scott Belsky

Adobe's Chief Strategy Officer and EVP of Design and Emerging Products. Founder of Behance (acquired by Adobe). Author of *The Messy Middle* and *Making Ideas Happen*. Angel investor in Pinterest, Uber, Airtable, and many others. One of the most respected product thinkers in Silicon Valley.

## Key Views

### 1. Conviction Is the Only Reason to Keep Going

When founders ask whether to continue, Belsky asks: "How much conviction do you have in the solution you're building?" If you have *more* conviction now than when you started (despite all the hard lessons), you're in "the messy middle" — stick with it. If you have *less* conviction, quit. "If you've lost conviction, you should not be doing what you're doing in the world of entrepreneurship."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/scott-belsky.md]

### 2. Build Only Half the Features You Want

Product sense is knowing what *not* to build. Belsky advocates building only half the features you think you need — the other half is probably noise. Every feature you cut is a win for focus, speed, and user experience. The best products are distinguished not by what they have but by what they deliberately exclude.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/scott-belsky.md]

### 3. The "First Mile" Experience Is Everything

The initial experience a user has with your product — the first few seconds and minutes — determines whether they'll ever discover the depth of what you've built. Most teams underinvest in onboarding and overinvest in features. Belsky's rule: spend disproportionate time on the first mile because if users don't get past it, nothing else matters.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/scott-belsky.md]

### 4. The Messy Middle Is Where Products Are Made or Broken

Between the exciting start and the successful finish lies the "messy middle" — the long, unglamorous slog where most products fail. The key to surviving: break the journey into manageable milestones, celebrate small wins, and maintain faith in the vision when progress feels slow. The messy middle isn't a bug in the process; it's the process itself.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/scott-belsky.md]

### 5. AI Changes Everything — But Human Taste Still Matters Most

Belsky sees AI as the biggest transformation in creative tools since the shift to digital. But his core insight: AI amplifies human creativity rather than replacing it. The bottleneck becomes taste, judgment, and curation — skills that are harder to automate than execution. The best products will be built by people who combine AI leverage with strong creative instincts.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/scott-belsky.md]
