# Sarah Tavel

Sarah Tavel is a General Partner at Benchmark, one of the most preeminent venture capital firms in the world, where she focuses on consumer and marketplace startups. Prior to Benchmark, she was the first product manager at Pinterest, leading product for the discovery team (home feed, search, recommendations). She sits on the boards of Chainalysis, Hipcamp, Rekki, Cambly, and Medely, and is a founding member of All Raise.

## Key Views

### The Hierarchy of Engagement — Three Levels of Consumer Product Success
Sarah's flagship framework for building enduring consumer products has three levels. **Level 1: Core Action** — identify the single action that signals a user understands and values the product (pinning on Pinterest, sending a snap on Snapchat, writing a note on Evernote). This becomes the north star metric. **Level 2: Retention** — the product must get better the more you use it, and users must have more to lose by leaving. Pinterest's "picked for you" feed made the experience more personalized with every pin. **Level 3: Self-Perpetuation** — every user interaction should create loops that bring in new users or re-engage dormant ones. Network effects, growth loops, and re-engagement notifications are the key levers.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

### Markets Are Currents, Not Bodies of Water
Sarah's mental model for evaluating markets: "I think a lot of people think about markets like bodies of water — a big body of water that we're going after. I actually think the most interesting markets are like currents — there's something happening in the market that creates a current where you can have a plank of wood on the river and it's going to pull you forward." This is why she cares less about market size and more about the dynamics of change. Is there a tailwind that will pull the company forward, or does the founder have to build something huge from scratch?^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

### Vanity Metrics Are the Enemy
Sarah has an "allergic reaction to vanity metrics." When founders show her "up and to the right" graphs of sign-ups, downloads, or MAUs, she looks past them. The real question: are users completing the core action? "The MAU thing doesn't really mean anything. It's really looking at users completing the core action." She advises founders to pick the right action — one that scales to enough users and, if optimized for, leads the team to build the right things.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

### Failed Level 3: When the Flywheel Breaks
Sarah points to Houseparty and Clubhouse as examples of products that mastered Level 1 and Level 2 but couldn't sustain Level 3 (self-perpetuation). Both relied on push notifications to pull people back — but the more people you followed, the more notifications you got, creating notification fatigue. "Even though the flywheel should be spinning faster, it starts breaking down." This nuance is often missed: growth loops can degrade at scale if they create negative experiences for users.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

### The Hierarchy of Marketplaces
Sarah's second framework applies the same layered thinking to marketplaces. Level 1: **Liquidity** — can buyers and sellers find each other? Level 2: **Trust and Safety** — can they transact with confidence? Level 3: **Efficiency** — does the marketplace keep getting better at matching? The most successful marketplaces (like eBay, Airbnb, Uber) solved all three levels, and each level required fundamentally different product and operational strategies. Most marketplace failures come from trying to solve Level 3 before achieving liquidity at Level 1.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]
