# Petra Wille

Petra Wille appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss how to be the best coach to product people.

## Key Views

**The Five Ingredients of Product Coaching** — Petra's core coaching framework from Strong Product People: (1) a solid definition of what a good product person looks like in your context, (2) a clear idea of where each PM is today plus their "next bigger challenge," (3) a shared vision for their future, (4) a development plan they own, and (5) consistent follow-up. "Step number one is really having a solid definition of what a good product person looks like in your context… step number two is having a clear idea where every PM currently is in their current career… and then even more important is what I call the next bigger challenge." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

**Why Many Product Leads Struggle to Coach** — A key coaching gap: many people leading product teams come from marketing, business, data, or engineering backgrounds and have never worked as a product manager themselves, so they lack empathy for the PM role. "They often come from a marketing background, or a business background, or from the data background… or the engineering background, but they often never have worked in a product management role. So they're missing a lot of basic product management practice and a lot of empathy for the struggle of the product people." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

**Start with the Development Plan, Not the Perfect Compass** — Don't get paralyzed trying to create the ideal competency framework first — just start where the PM is, since they usually know what they want to improve. "I usually advise people to start with the development plan because even if you have never done the assessment and even if you don't have your compass, your definition of what makes a good product manager, you usually have an idea… of what they want to learn next or where they want to get better at." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

**Consistency Beats Intensity in People Development** — Small, regular coaching investments (weekly nudges, monthly check-ins) are far more effective than big annual performance reviews for both the PM and the product lead. "All of us know consistency beats intensity. So, really, the smaller time investments on a weekly basis and that applies for the PM's time investment in learning new things and it applies to the product leads investment and helping their people to grow." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

**The PMwheel: A Coaching Conversation Starter** — Petra developed the PMwheel — eight skill buckets (understand problems, find solutions, planning, get it done, listen & learn, team, personal growth, agile) — as a practical tool to initiate coaching conversations with PMs who aren't sure what to work on. "It is often that coaches come totally unprepared to the coaching… And then they're just like, 'Okay, they told me to show up. Petra, what should we do in these sessions?' And then that's why I created the PMwheel to have these first conversations." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

# Petra Wille

Petra Wille is an independent product leadership coach and author of *Strong Product People: A Complete Guide to Developing Great Product Managers*. For over a decade, she has been helping product teams and product leaders expand their skill sets through one-on-one coaching, group coaching, corporate workshops, and speaking. Based in Hamburg, Germany, she has coached over 130 people individually and influenced an estimated 50,000+ product professionals through her work.

## Key Views

### The Five Ingredients of Coaching Product People
Petra's framework for product leaders who want to develop their PMs: (1) **Have a clear definition** of what a competent product person looks like in your context — your compass. (2) **Know where each PM is today** and have a vision for where they could go — including the "next bigger challenge" you'd assign if you could. (3) **Share that vision** with them and align on a direction. (4) **Create a development plan** — specific steps they can take. (5) **Follow up consistently** — small nudges over time beat big annual reviews. "Consistency beats intensity."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

### Start With the Development Plan, Not the Compass
Petra's practical advice for product leaders overwhelmed by the idea of creating a full competency framework: just start with the development plan. "Even if you have never done the assessment and even if you don't have your compass, you usually have an idea of what they want to learn next." A PM might say they want to improve at storytelling or prioritization — use that as the starting point. The follow-up (small, regular check-ins) is what makes the difference. Create a new development plan once a quarter.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

### Storytelling Is a Career Accelerator (or Staller)
Petra observes that storytelling ability is one of the most underestimated career factors for PMs. "Getting promoted is way harder if you're not good at telling stories and rallying the team behind the shared goal." Even if other team members help compensate, not being able to tell compelling stories is "a bit of a career staller." PMs who invest in their storytelling and public speaking skills dramatically increase their ability to influence and lead.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

### Product Management Is a Career, Not a Role
Petra emphasizes that product management requires continuous learning across many dimensions — user research, strategy, data analysis, experimentation, storytelling, stakeholder management. "It's not a role, it's a career being in product. There's so many things to learn and so many things to get good at." The T-shaped profile isn't enough — PMs need to be more like an eight-legged creature, understanding business, users, technology, and process simultaneously.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]

### Build Your Own Compass Before Waiting for the Company's
Many product leaders wait for HR or the company to create a formal career ladder before they start developing their PMs. Petra's advice: don't wait. "Start with your own personal team — grade your own compass and encourage your peers to grade theirs. It's better to start helping your product folks than being totally paralyzed by the fact that it's not used in the whole company." Your team only has you as their manager — give them orientation now.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/petra-wille.md]
