# Tony Fadell

"Father of the iPod" and one of the leading product thinkers of the last 30 years. As SVP of Apple's iPod division, led the creation of the first 18 generations of iPod and contributed to the original iPhone. Later founded Nest Labs (Nest Thermostat), acquired by Google for $3.2BN. Currently Principal at Future Shape, a global investment and advisory firm coaching deep tech founders. Author of the book *Build*.

## Key Views

### 1. The Three Hats of Being a CEO

Fadell defines the three distinct hats a CEO must wear: the Visionary (setting direction), the Operator (executing day-to-day), and the Coach (developing people). He argues that the best leaders know which hat they are wearing at any given moment and are deliberate about switching between them. The most common failure mode is wearing the wrong hat at the wrong time.

^[raw/transcripts/20vc/20vc-2022-07-25-20vc-tony-fadell-on-the-3-hat.md]

### 2. Product Marketing Lessons from Steve Jobs

Drawing on his years working alongside Steve Jobs, Fadell shares specific marketing lessons: the importance of simplifying the message to its essence, leading with the problem not the feature, and the discipline of saying "no" to 90% of ideas so the remaining 10% can shine. He distinguishes between truly great product marketing and the superficial version that most companies practice.

^[raw/transcripts/20vc/20vc-2022-05-11-20-product-iphone-creator-to.md]

### 3. Entrepreneurs Need To Be Coachable

Fadell emphasizes that raw talent is not enough — entrepreneurs must be coachable. He argues that VCs need to be more direct with founders about hard truths, and that founders who cannot receive candid feedback will inevitably hit a ceiling. Self-doubt and vulnerability, when channeled correctly, are strengths not weaknesses.

^[raw/transcripts/20vc/20vc-2021-03-15-20vc-tony-fadell-the-father.md]

### 4. The First Trillionaire Will Innovate Around Climate Change

Fadell predicts that the world's first trillionaire will come from solving climate change, not from software or finance. He argues that the scale of the climate problem demands innovation on a level comparable to the industrial revolution, and that deep tech solutions in energy, materials, and infrastructure will create more value than any software company has to date.

^[raw/transcripts/20vc/20vc-2021-03-15-20vc-tony-fadell-the-father.md]

### 5. Post-Mortems and Product Reviews: Art vs Science

Fadell shares how the best product teams conduct post-mortems and product reviews. He tackles the debate of product management as art versus science — data versus gut instinct — arguing that the best product decisions come from the intersection of quantitative data and qualitative intuition, not from either alone.
