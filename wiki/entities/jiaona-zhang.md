# Jiaona Zhang (JZ)

**Role:** SVP of Product at Webflow; lecturer in Product Management at Stanford; former Senior Director of Product at WeWork; longtime product leader at Airbnb, Dropbox, and Pocket Gems  
**Known for:** Popularizing the "Minimum Lovable Product" concept; deep teaching experience (6+ years at Stanford); insights from the Airbnb Plus story; frameworks for PM career growth and roadmapping.

## Key Views

### 1. Minimum Lovable Product > Minimum Viable Product

JZ's core framework: "In a world where there are so many different options, it's hard to just feel like, 'Hey, use this thing. It barely meets a quality bar.'" A Minimum Lovable Product (MLP) means deeply understanding what a "lovable" quality bar is for your users and the competitive context. If you're replacing a terrible manual workflow (spreadsheets), your bar can be lower — even a rough product is lovable compared to the alternative. If users have great options, the bar is higher. The key is understanding user tolerance and competitive dynamics.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jiaona-zhang.md]

> "In a world where there are so many different options, it's hard to just feel like, 'Hey, use this thing. It barely meets a quality bar.' Minimal lovable products is the new MVP." — Jiaona Zhang

### 2. The Airbnb Plus Mistake — Don't Confuse Vision with Feasibility

JZ's biggest product mistake: Airbnb Plus. The problem was real (people wanted quality guarantees), but the solution (physically inspecting homes) had impossible unit economics. The lesson: separate **vision** (dreaming big) from **execution** (being thoughtful). Great visionaries work backwards from an ideal end state, but you need to break it into learning phases with explicit milestones. "Hey, we're trying X for six months to learn these specific things" — and have clear go/no-go criteria at each phase. If the unit economics don't work at the beginning, they probably won't work at scale.

### 3. The #1 Mistake New PMs Make: Jumping to Solutions

JZ's Stanford teaching experience: the hardest thing to untrain in new PMs is jumping to solutions. "Your job is not to call the shots. Your job is to understand opportunities and edit." The PM role is about influence, not authority — you have no direct reports. The most effective PMs become "known for something" — shepherding complex launches, working on technically complex problems — which earns them a reputation and more responsibility.

### 4. Pushing Back on Founders: Understand the Spirit, Then Offer Better Options

When a founder is committed to a bad idea, JZ's approach: **(1)** Understand the *spirit* of what they're trying to achieve. **(2)** Get alignment on the goal. **(3)** Come back with better options. "A lot of these people are very smart and motivated. They ultimately want to do the right thing for their users." If you have data and a better path, it's rare someone will insist on a failing solution. She literally moved off the Airbnb Plus team to work on the review system — a more scalable approach to the same problem.

### 5. Break Big Visions Into Phased Learning Cycles

JZ's recommendation for balancing big vision with execution: **be explicit about the phase you're in**. "We're in the learning phase and we explicitly are trying to learn these things" vs. "We have this really big vision and we're just going to go at it." The latter is not recommended. Define what success looks like in small intervals (quarters, not years), with clear go/no-go at each point. This prevents multi-year sunk cost fallacies and lets teams pivot rapidly.

# Jiaona Zhang (JZ)

Jiaona "JZ" Zhang is SVP of Product at Webflow and a lecturer at Stanford teaching product management. She was previously Senior Director of Product at WeWork, a longtime product leader at Airbnb, and a PM at Dropbox and Pocket Gems. She's known for the concept of "minimum lovable products" and for teaching thousands of aspiring PMs.

## Key Views

**Build Minimum Lovable Products, Not Minimum Viable Products**
JZ advocates for shifting from MVP (Minimum Viable Product) to MLP (Minimum Lovable Product). The distinction is critical: viable means the product works; lovable means people actually want to use it and recommend it. She argues that teams too often ship something technically functional but emotionally flat, then wonder why adoption stalls. The goal should be to find the smallest set of features that makes a user truly delighted, not just not-frustrated. This reframes the entire build-measure-learn loop around emotional response rather than functional adequacy.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jiaona-zhang.md]]*

**Untrain Yourself from Jumping to Solutions — Start with Problems**
The most common mistake JZ sees in new PMs — and even experienced ones — is getting attached to a solution before understanding the problem. "You get really attached to a solution, a way of implementing something that you can see in your head that you want to build." Her Stanford course's first lesson is to ignore the solution entirely and spend time with users understanding their real problems. This is harder than it sounds because the desire to build is what draws people to product management in the first place.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jiaona-zhang.md]]*

**Great PMs Are Editors, Not Authors — Influence Is Your Only Authority**
Many aspiring PMs think the job is about calling the shots. JZ corrects this: "You're not a CEO. You have very little true authority because you don't actually manage anyone. A lot of it is all through influence." The real job is to understand opportunities and pull together different possibilities — you're editing, not authoring. This reframes the PM role from decision-maker to sensemaker, where success depends on building trust and alignment rather than exercising authority.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jiaona-zhang.md]]*

**Make Yourself Known for One Thing — The Specialist Advantage**
JZ advises PMs to become really good at and known for something specific — whether it's shepherding complex launches, working with go-to-market teams, or tackling technically complex problems. "People tend to flock and give responsibility to the people that are known for being excellent at something." This creates a reputation flywheel: the more you're known for a specific strength, the more high-impact projects come your way, which builds your skills and reputation further.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jiaona-zhang.md]]*

**Airbnb Plus: A Case Study in Solution-First Thinking and Ignoring Unit Economics**
JZ uses Airbnb Plus as her biggest product mistake — a cautionary tale about jumping to solutions. The team tried to solve the trust problem by sending inspectors to vet properties, ignoring that Airbnb's strength was its platform (not operations) and that the unit economics would never work at scale. The better approach: leverage existing platform assets like guest reviews to solve the same problem. "If you understand the unit economics don't work at the beginning, don't assume they'll magically work at scale."
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jiaona-zhang.md]]*
