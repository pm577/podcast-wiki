# Julia Schottenstein

Julia Schottenstein appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss product lead at dbt labs, formerly vc at nea, co-host of the analytics engineering podcast, on m&a strategy, competitive positioning, pricing philosophy, and evaluating early-stage startups.

## Key Views

**Inflict Pain on Potential Acquirers, but Keep Them as Friendly Partners** — Julia advises founders to build a competitive advantage that makes it impossible for potential buyers to ignore them, while staying friendly and open so you don't burn bridges. M&A is about creating plan Bs, and you shouldn't shut the door prematurely by taking too adversarial a stance. The best M&A leverage is having a viable path as an independent company, because then you have the upper hand in every conversation. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]

**Compete with Three Pillars: Hold Your Vision, Grow the Pie, and Lean Into Your Strengths** — dbt Labs' competitive philosophy is: (1) ignore noise and stay true to your own vision, (2) work with ecosystem partners to expand the total opportunity rather than fighting over slices, and (3) know which few areas to defend (the standard itself) while leaving everything else open to partners. This long-term, 'grow the pie' mindset avoids distraction and builds durable moats. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]

**Have the Pricing Conversation Before You Build, Not When Your Sales Team Is Struggling** — You don't decide whether to have a pricing conversation — only when. dbt Labs' core value is 'more concerned with value creation than value capture,' pricing at a small fraction of the 20-35% value they deliver relative to warehouse spend. When they ran their first price change, they learned critical price elasticity data that would have been riskier to discover later at larger scale. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]

**Evaluate Early-Stage Startups on Four Dimensions: People, Market, Product, and Distribution** — As a former VC at NEA, Julia looks for (1) a founder who can paint a compelling vision while sweating day-to-day details, (2) a growing, chaotic market with room for a new entrant, (3) a product that users can't stop talking about and eagerly evangelize, and (4) an unfair distribution advantage (open-source bottoms-up or enterprise top-down). No startup scores 10/10 on all four, so you should ask which weakness you personally can de-risk. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]

**Start Thinking About M&A Strategy When You Don't Need It** — The best time to plant seeds with potential acquirers (via partnerships, corp dev meetings, ecosystem relationships) is long before a Hail Mary situation arises. For companies without an independent path, those early relationships become the lifeline. Building relationships with corporate development teams when you don't need anything is the single highest-leverage M&A preparation you can do. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]
