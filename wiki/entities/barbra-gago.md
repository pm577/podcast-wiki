# Barbra Gago

Barbra Gago is founder and CEO of Pando, democratizing career progression. Previously she was CMO and Global Head of Marketing at Miro, where she helped create the visual collaboration category. Before that she was VP of Marketing at Greenhouse (bringing the product to market) and Head of Marketing at Culture Amp (closing their first 50 customers).

## Key Views

**Category Creation Requires Validation and Education** — Building a new category means three things simultaneously: validating the category exists (through analyst reports, directory sites, buyer intent signals), creating thought leadership explaining why the category matters, and educating buyers that there is a category they should budget for. You can't just declare a category — it must be confirmed by the market. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/barbra-gago.md]

**Miro Didn't Just Build a Product — It Built a Category** — Miro didn't position itself as "whiteboarding software." It created and owned "visual collaboration" as a category. This required consistent messaging across every touchpoint, content that taught people what visual collaboration meant, and analyst relations to get Gartner and Forrester to recognize the space. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/barbra-gago.md]

**Rebranding Is a Signal, Not a Fix** — A rebrand should signal a fundamental shift in strategy, market, or audience — not fix a bad logo or color scheme. When Miro (formerly RealtimeBoard) rebranded, it signaled the transition from a real-time whiteboarding tool for engineers to an enterprise visual collaboration platform for everyone. If nothing strategic has changed, a rebrand is wasted. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/barbra-gago.md]

**Opinionated Software Wins** — Software that takes a strong point of view about how work should get done attracts passionate users and repels the wrong ones. Miro's opinion: collaboration should be visual, async-first, and inclusive of everyone in the organization. This conviction guided product decisions, from infinite canvas to template libraries. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/barbra-gago.md]

**Go-to-Market for a New Category Is Different** — When you're creating a category, you can't just compete on features. Your GTM must teach the market why the old way is broken and what the new approach means. This means heavy investment in content marketing, analyst education, community building, and customer case studies that demonstrate the paradigm shift in action. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/barbra-gago.md]

# Barbra Gago

**Role:** Founder and CEO of Pando (employee progression platform); former CMO and Global Head of Marketing at Miro (led rebrand and category creation); VP Marketing at Greenhouse (go-to-market); Head of Marketing at Culture Amp  
**Known for:** Creating the "visual collaboration" category at Miro, expert on category creation vs. category adoption, and opinionated software as a brand strategy.

## Key Views

### 1. Category creation is about scope, not novelty — and sometimes you should abandon ship

Barbra's key insight from Miro vs. Greenhouse: at Miro, "online whiteboard" was too small a category to contain the product's ambition, so they created "visual collaboration" — a bigger category that could become essential to every company. At Greenhouse, they tried to create a new category for recruiting software but abandoned it because the existing "Applicant Tracking System" (ATS) category was already validated, had analyst coverage, and had buyer budget allocated. The lesson: create a category only when your existing category is too small; otherwise, win in the existing one. Category creation requires analyst validation, thought leadership, buyer education, and budget creation — it's expensive.

> "When you're building a category, you need to make sure that there is a category that's validated by analysts and directory sites … but also, you want to have a lot of traction in thought leadership — why is this the category? What are the unique value propositions?" — Barbra Gago

### 2. The rebrand is a strategic tool — not just a new logo

At Miro, Barbra led a full rebrand from "RealtimeBoard" to "Miro" as part of the category creation strategy. A rebrand signals a fundamental shift — in Miro's case, from a niche whiteboard tool to a platform for visual collaboration used by entire organizations. The rebrand was timed with the category launch, ensuring that the name, visual identity, and category all reinforced each other. Her advice: don't rebrand just because you're tired of the old name — rebrand when you're making a strategic pivot that the market needs to understand.

### 3. Opinionated software wins — take a stand on how work should be done

Barbra advocates for building "opinionated software" — products that have a clear point of view about the best way to do something, rather than trying to be all things to all people. Opinionated software creates stronger brand affinity, clearer positioning, and more passionate users. It also makes it easier to say "no" to features that don't fit. Miro's opinionated stance was that visual collaboration is a fundamentally better way to work than text-based tools — and they designed the entire product experience around that belief.

### 4. The people-tech connection: building brands in the future of work

Barbra's career has been centered on the "people tech" space — Culture Amp (employee engagement), Greenhouse (hiring), Miro (collaboration), and now Pando (career progression). Her thesis: the future of work requires tools that empower people, not just manage them. At Pando, she's building an "employee progression platform" with the audacious goal of "killing performance reviews" — replacing them with continuous coaching, structured progression, and transparency that creates accountability on both sides.

### 5. Brand building during hypergrowth: consistency is the hardest part

Having scaled brands through hypergrowth at multiple companies, Barbra emphasizes that maintaining brand consistency gets harder as you grow. The brand "bible" (purpose, positioning, personality, visual identity, voice, tone) must be embedded in new employee onboarding and revisited at every product milestone. Her framework ensures that as the company enters new markets, launches new products, or hires new teams, the brand doesn't fragment. The CMO's job is to be the guardian of that consistency, especially during periods of rapid scaling.
