# Christian Idiodi

Christian Idiodi is a partner at Silicon Valley Product Group (SVPG) alongside Marty Cagan. After a long product career and founding multiple companies, he now works closely with product leaders at companies big and small, implementing and improving their product management discipline.

## Key Views

### The Essence of PM: Wake Up on Behalf of Someone Else
"You wake up on behalf of someone else to solve a problem for them, and you have to do it well enough that they give you something back in return." Christian calls this a "certificate of appreciation" — it can be revenue, engagement, loyalty, or referrals. If it's not fun, you're probably doing it wrong. If it's not hard, you're also probably doing it wrong. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christian-idiodi.md]

### Why Product Management Is So Often Disliked
PMs earn a bad reputation when they become "project managers" — focused on shipping features rather than solving problems. The antidote: rediscover the essence of the job. Spend time with customers. Develop deep empathy for their struggles. Let the problem, not the roadmap, drive your decisions. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christian-idiodi.md]

### Coaching Is the Most Undervalued PM Skill
Christian emphasizes that coaching reports is the highest-leverage activity for senior PMs. His approach: ask questions instead of giving answers. "What do you think is going on here?" "What options have you considered?" "What would success look like?" This builds the coaching muscle in others and develops independent decision-makers. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christian-idiodi.md]

### The One Go-To Discovery Method
When asked for his single favorite method for figuring out what to build, Christian points to continuous customer discovery — not as an event but as an ongoing practice. The best teams have a permanent channel of customer interaction, not a user research sprint that happens once a quarter. The more you talk to customers, the better your bets become. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christian-idiodi.md]

### Building Trust with Leaders Through Transparency
Christian's tactic for earning trust from executives: proactively share both good and bad news. Leaders hate surprises, especially bad ones. If you surface problems early along with proposed solutions, you build credibility. The leader who always brings good news but hides problems is the first to lose trust when things go wrong. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/christian-idiodi.md]
