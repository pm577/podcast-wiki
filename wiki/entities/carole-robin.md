# Carole Robin

**Role:** Stanford professor (20+ years teaching "Touchy Feely" — Interpersonal Dynamics); co-author of *Connect: Building Exceptional Relationships with Family, Friends, and Colleagues*  
**Known for:** The "Three Realities" and "Stay on Your Side of the Net" frameworks for interpersonal feedback, the art of inquiry, and teaching that feelings belong in the workplace.

## Key Views

### 1. The Three Realities — stay on your side of the net

In any exchange between two people, there are three distinct realities: **(1)** your intent/experience, **(2)** your observable behavior (words and actions), and **(3)** the impact on the other person. You only have access to two of the three — yours and your behavior. The other person's reality (impact) is theirs. Drawing a "net" between reality 1 and reality 2, Carole teaches: "Stay on your side of the net." Never assume you know someone else's intent.

> "When you stay on your side of the net and say: 'When I speak and the only thing I get back is a grunt — I don't feel heard,' the other person can't argue with that. That's your reality. That's the purpose of feedback — to move into a problem-solving conversation." — Carole Robin

### 2. The feedback formula — behavior, feeling, impact

Carole's specific structure for giving constructive feedback: **When you do [specific behavior], I feel [emotion], and I'm telling you because [outcome you want].** The key is to use real feeling words (hurt, frustrated, dismissed) — not attributions like "I feel that you don't care" (which is a thought, not a feeling). This formula shifts the probability of a productive conversation dramatically.

> "I feel that you don't care and I feel you're being insensitive are not feelings. They're attributions and imputed motives. That's where we make our biggest mistakes. Calling someone insensitive is the most insensitive thing you can do — it makes them defensive." — Carole Robin

### 3. Pinches before crunches — address small issues early

Carole teaches the concept of "pinches" (small irritations) vs "crunches" (blow-ups). Most people let pinches go because it "doesn't seem worth it." But unaddressed pinches compound into crunches. Her advice: substitute the word "it" for "I, you, we" — "I'm not worth it, you're not worth it, we're not worth it" — and then decide if it's worth raising.

> "If I'm doing something mildly irritating and you don't tell me, what am I going to do? Keep doing it. And are you going to get less irritated or more irritated? Address it while it's still a pinch before it becomes a crunch." — Carole Robin

### 4. Anger is a secondary emotion — lead with the real feeling

Carole's insight: anger is almost always a shield for more vulnerable emotions like fear, hurt, or loneliness. Anger distances; other feelings connect. She teaches leaders to name the emotion underneath — especially in professional settings, where showing vulnerability builds trust rather than undermining authority.

> "Anger is a distancing emotion. Hurt, fear, sadness, loneliness, happiness, joy — those are connecting emotions. What a disservice to business to not help people understand that." — Carole Robin

### 5. The Art of Inquiry — stay curious, not judgmental

Most people ask questions to confirm a hypothesis — that's not inquiry. True inquiry requires suspending judgment and asking open-ended questions that start with "what," "how," "when," "where" — not "why" (which triggers defensiveness). The two antennae everyone has: one tracking your own internal state, one sensing what's going on with others. Developing both antennae is the foundation of interpersonal competence.

> "You cannot be curious if you've already decided you know what's going on. Stay away from 'why' questions — they make people defensive. Questions that start with 'what,' 'how,' 'where' are much more likely to invite real conversation." — Carole Robin

# Carole Robin

Carole Robin spent over 20 years teaching the Stanford Graduate School of Business course "Interpersonal Dynamics" — affectionately known as "Touchy Feely." She is the co-author of *Connect: Building Exceptional Relationships with Family, Friends, and Colleagues* and founded Leaders in Touch.

## Key Views

### "I Feel That You Don't Care" Is Not a Feeling
Carole's most important distinction: the difference between a feeling and a thought disguised as a feeling. "I feel that you don't care" is actually a judgment, not a feeling. Real feelings are simple: sad, angry, scared, hurt, embarrassed, lonely. When giving feedback, use genuine feeling words — not pseudo-feelings that are actually accusations. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/carole-robin.md]

### The Touchy Feely Model of Interpersonal Dynamics
The Stanford course teaches a structured model for building deeper relationships: (1) Self-awareness — understanding your own emotions and reactions; (2) Other-awareness — genuinely understanding another person's perspective; (3) Skilled communication — expressing yourself in ways that build connection rather than defensiveness. The model applies equally to personal and professional relationships. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/carole-robin.md]

### Giving Feedback That Lands
Effective feedback follows three rules: (1) Describe specific behavior, not personality; (2) Share the impact on you using genuine feelings; (3) Make it a request, not a demand. "When you interrupt me in meetings, I feel dismissed and frustrated. Would you be willing to let me finish my thought before responding?" This structure minimizes defensiveness and maximizes understanding. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/carole-robin.md]

### The "Touchy Feely" Course Saved My Marriage
Carole shares that students often tell her the class "saved my marriage" — even though the course is ostensibly about business relationships. This reveals a fundamental truth: interpersonal skills are transferable across every domain of life. The ability to connect authentically, give and receive feedback, and navigate conflict is the foundation of all successful relationships. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/carole-robin.md]

### Vulnerability Is Essential for Leadership
Carole teaches that leaders who show appropriate vulnerability build more trust, not less. Admitting uncertainty, sharing feelings, and asking for help signals strength — not weakness. The key is intentional vulnerability: sharing in service of the relationship or the goal, not dumping emotional baggage indiscriminately. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/carole-robin.md]
