# Nancy Duarte

CEO of Duarte Inc., which has helped create over 250,000 presentations for the world's most influential leaders, including Apple, TED, Google, the World Bank, and famously Al Gore's *An Inconvenient Truth*. Bestselling author and world authority on presentation design and storytelling.

## Key Views

### 1. The "What Is, What Could Be" Framework for Influence

The single most powerful communication framework: create contrast between the current reality ("what is") and a better future ("what could be"). This creates tension that drives people to want change. "I can get my husband to do chores for me on the weekends with a real quick 'what is, what could be, new bliss.'" The framework works in any format — meetings, phone calls, one-on-ones.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nancy-duarte.md]

### 2. The Best Presenters Defer to Experts

Al Gore's secret: after five years of working with Duarte's team, he would "pause and touch his chin and really think, and really consider that we might actually be experts." More often than not, he adopted their recommendations. The most powerful people in the world are often the most receptive to expert guidance. Humility in listening to specialists is a superpower, not a weakness.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nancy-duarte.md]

### 3. Groundswells Take Years, Not Moments

Al Gore traveled city-to-city for five years before *An Inconvenient Truth* became a movie. "He went city to city to city, planting seeds for a groundswell." The overnight success of the presentation was the culmination of a half-decade of consistent work. Great storytelling at scale requires long-term commitment, not a single big moment.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nancy-duarte.md]

### 4. Data Needs a Story to Land

People think presentations are about data, but data without narrative doesn't stick. The most impactful presentations (like Gore's) combine rigorous data with a compelling human story. Duarte's approach: stand in front of data, make it visceral, and connect it to something the audience cares about. Scale on a 90-foot screen helps, but the principle applies at any size.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nancy-duarte.md]

### 5. Presentation Skills Are Needed Every Day, Not Just on Big Stages

"Most people think the only time you need to present well is when you have a big stage talk." In reality, every moment of influence — a meeting, a phone call, a performance review — benefits from the same storytelling principles. The "what is/what could be" contrast is a daily tool for anyone who needs to persuade.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nancy-duarte.md]
