# Wes Kao

Wes Kao appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss persuasive communication, managing up, and building cohort-based courses.

## Key Views

**MOO (Most Obvious Objection)** — Before sharing an idea, spend two minutes anticipating the most obvious objections. This prepares you to address concerns proactively rather than being blindsided. "A lot of times we're surprised by the questions that we get especially in meetings, we feel blindsided. When really, if you thought for even two minutes about what are obvious objections that I'm likely to get, you often immediately come up with what some of those things are." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

**Sales, Then Logistics** — Never start with the how before you've sold the why. If your audience hasn't bought into the purpose, diving into details will only generate confusion and silence. "A common mistake that I see is overestimating the amount of buy-in that you have from your audience. So that looks like jumping straight into talking about the logistics... when in reality your audience has not yet decided if they even want to do the thing." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

**Concise ≠ Brief — Preparation is the Real Solution** — True concision is about economy of words and knowing your core point — which requires preparation, not just editing. "Being concise is not about absolute word count, it's about economy of words. It's about the density of the insight that you're sharing... you can't cut to the chase unless you know what the chase is." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

**Strategy, Not Self-Expression (Giving Feedback)** — When giving constructive feedback, the goal is behavior change — not venting your frustrations. Trim everything that doesn't serve that goal, typically keeping only 10% of what you initially want to say. "The goal is behavior change. So if that's the goal, trim everything else that you were about to say that does not actually contribute to that goal." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

**Managing Up = Share Your Point of View** — Stop asking your manager "What should we do?" Instead, come with a recommendation and ask for feedback. This reduces your manager's cognitive load and signals strategic thinking. "When you just ask your manager, 'Hey manager, what should we do?' You're putting a lot of cognitive load on your manager... Whereas if you instead said, 'Hey manager, here's what I think we should do. How does that sound?' You give them something to build off of." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

### 1. **If You're Not Getting the Reaction You Want, Look in the Mirror** — Wes's fundamental principle: instead of blaming others for not understanding you, ask 'how might I be contributing to this confusion?' Communication is a skill you can get better at. The best starting point is agency — you can only control your own behavior. If you're getting skepticism, apathy, or avoidable questions, it's likely a communication problem, not an audience problem. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao-20.md]

### 2. **Practice Like It's Game Day — Treat Every Stakeholder Like an Executive** — Most operators save their best communication for executive presentations and coast with everyone else. The problem: you only present to execs once a month — not enough reps to get good. Instead, treat every cross-functional teammate, direct report, and manager with the same care. Every interaction is practice for the high-stakes moments. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao-20.md]

### 3. **Concision Is About Knowing Your Point, Not Shortening Your Words** — 'Don't bury the lead' assumes you know what the lead is. The bottleneck to being concise is not word count — it's clarity of thought. You can't cut to the chase unless you know what the chase is. Preparation is the only real solution: even 30-60 seconds before a meeting to ground yourself on 'why am I here and what do I want to share' dramatically improves concision. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao-20.md]

### 4. **The Yes-Yes-Yes, Next-Next-Next Mindset** — When presenting to executives, they often agree by slide 4 of a 15-slide deck. Take the win and move on. Don't feel compelled to show every slide you prepared — you might talk them out of agreeing. If they've made the decision, stop presenting. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao-20.md]

### 5. **Managing Up = Share Your Point of View, Not Just Your Question** — Instead of asking your manager 'What should we do?', come with a recommendation and ask for feedback. This reduces their cognitive load, signals strategic thinking, and gives them something to build off of. The difference is subtle but powerful: 'Here's what I think we should do — how does that sound?' vs. 'What should we do?' Always lead with your point of view. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao-20.md]
