# Matt Abrahams

Matt Abrahams is a renowned communication expert, professor at Stanford University's Graduate School of Business, host of the *Think Fast, Talk Smart* podcast, and author of *Think Faster and Talk Smarter*. He coaches executives and individuals on public speaking and communication skills.

## Key Views

**Dare to Be Dull — Strive for Connection Over Perfection** — The biggest impediment to good communication is the pressure we put on ourselves to be perfect. Instead of trying to be the most interesting or profound, just "dare to be dull" — answer the question, give the feedback, be engaged. Dialing down self-evaluation frees cognitive bandwidth for actual connection with the audience. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-abrahams.md]

**Reframe Anxiety as Excitement** — The physiological response to anxiety (racing heart, shallow breathing) is identical to excitement. Labeling it as "I'm excited" rather than "I'm nervous" changes how we experience it. Research by Alison Wood Brooks at Harvard shows that people who reframe anxiety as excitement are perceived as communicating better. Before any talk, say: "This is exciting. I get to share my point of view." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-abrahams.md]

**Use Visualization and Mantras** — Visualize yourself in the speaking environment — walking to the stage, delivering the presentation, stepping off — as a mental dress rehearsal that desensitizes you. Use a personal mantra like "I have value to add" or "Last time this went well" to turn down negative self-talk. Write it on a post-it or set a phone reminder to flash before speaking. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-abrahams.md]

**Breath Control — The Exhale Is Magic** — The relaxation response happens during the exhale. Rule of lung: make your exhale twice as long as your inhale (e.g., 3 counts in, 6 counts out). This slows heart rate, steadies the voice, and brings you into the present moment. A double inhalation (breathe in fully, then sneak a little more air in, then long exhale) is particularly effective. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-abrahams.md]

**Prepare to Be Spontaneous** — Most public speaking is on the spot (Q&A, toasts, feedback, apologies). To be good at spontaneous speaking, you must prepare — just like athletes practice and jazz musicians rehearse. Techniques include: turning presentations into conversations (ask yourself questions and answer them), using tongue twisters to get present-oriented and warm up your voice, and distracting the audience (start with a video or question) to reduce early-stage anxiety. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/matt-abrahams.md]
