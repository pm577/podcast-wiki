# Jerry Colonna

Jerry Colonna is one of the world's most respected executive coaches. He co-founded Flatiron Partners with Fred Wilson (one of the most successful early-stage funds), was a partner at JPMorgan Chase, and is the co-founder and CEO of Reboot, an executive coaching firm. He's the author of *Reboot* and *Reunion* and is known for his signature question: "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"

## Key Views

**"How Have I Been Complicit?" — The Question That Evokes Agency**
Colonna's most famous coaching question uses the word "complicit" deliberately, distinguishing it from "responsible." "Complicit does not mean responsible — think of the word accomplice. You are driving the getaway car, you're not sticking up the bank teller." The purpose is to evoke agency, not guilt. If you say you don't want to feel busy but feel unnerved when your calendar isn't packed, you've been complicit in creating the business you claim to hate. The question cuts through self-delusion and reveals where you actually have power to change things.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jerry-colonna.md]]*

**Practical Skills + Radical Self-Inquiry + Shared Experiences = Enhanced Leadership + Greater Resilience**
Colonna's equation came to him mid-talk at Naropa University. Practical skills (the "how") plus radical self-inquiry (understanding your relationship to money, self-worth, and your family history) plus shared experiences (talking about the craziness in your head) equals not just better leadership but enhanced resilience. "The whole point of what we refer to as the equation really boils down to that point: how do we grow up and become the leaders, the adults we were born to be without feeling like crap?"
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jerry-colonna.md]]*

**You Don't Have to Be an Asshole to Be Successful — And You Don't Have to Be Miserable**
A 19-year-old audience member once summarized Colonna's entire philosophy: "So what you're telling me is you don't have to be an asshole to be successful." Colonna couldn't have said it better himself. The corollary: you don't have to feel miserable just because you're trying to create a career. He argues that the "big lie" we're socialized with is that external success (money, title, house) will make us happy. His own story — outward success masking suicidal depression — proves otherwise. Consciousness is the hack: ask yourself what you believe success will actually do for you.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jerry-colonna.md]]*

**The Lemon Drops Metaphor: Chasing Safety, Not Wealth**
Colonna shares a personal story of his grandfather's endless supply of lemon drops — which for him as a child symbolized wealth, safety, and stability. As an adult, he achieved outward success (had his "lemon drops") but still didn't feel safe. This revealed that his career choices were shaped by a childhood need for security, not by conscious adult desire. He encourages leaders to explore the stories from their past that drive their present behavior — because until you understand the "lemon drops" you're chasing, you'll never feel like you have enough.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jerry-colonna.md]]*

**The Fear of Self-Inquiry — "What If I Have to Quit My Job and Move to the Woods?"**
Colonna acknowledges that many people avoid radical self-inquiry because they fear the answers will force a complete life overthrow — quitting their job, giving up luxuries, disappointing their family. His response: consciousness doesn't demand destruction; it demands clarity. Understanding why you make the choices you make actually gives you more freedom to choose intentionally. Most people find that greater awareness leads to better decisions within their existing context, not wholesale abandonment of their lives.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jerry-colonna.md]]*
