# Ivan Zhao

**Role:** Co-founder and CEO of Notion  
**Known for:** Building one of the most beloved productivity platforms in the world; enduring 4 "lost years" before finding product-market fit; keeping the company lean and profitable for most of its life; deeply philosophical approach to computing tools inspired by Douglas Engelbart and Alan Kay.

## Key Views

### 1. The "Lost Years" — 4 Years of Failure Before Finding PMF

Ivan is brutally honest about Notion's early struggle: "First three to four years as the lost years." The first version was a developer tool that "most people just don't care" about. It took 2 years to realize they needed to build a **productivity tool** (not a developer tool), then another year to realize they built on the wrong technical foundation (Google Web Components instead of React) and had to **throw away the entire codebase and restart**. The lesson: build for what people actually use every day, hide your grand vision behind something familiar. "Sugar-coated broccoli" — people don't want the broccoli (creating software), but they like the sugar (productivity tools).^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ivan-zhao.md]

> "What is the building a product or business? You want user. You want revenue. That's the product business. And building for something you want the world to have is building for your value... They are different energy. You need to create a balance." — Ivan Zhao

### 2. Computing Pioneers' Vision — No Separation Between Builders and Users

Ivan's philosophy is deeply influenced by Douglas Engelbart's "Augmenting Human Intellect" paper and Alan Kay's work at Xerox PARC. The original vision of personal computing (Smalltalk/Alto) had no separation between users and app builders — everything was malleable. Steve Jobs saw the GUI but missed the underlying malleability, creating the application model we have today. Notion's mission: restore that original vision — "everybody can create their software" — hidden inside a familiar productivity suite.

### 3. Stay Lean — Delay Hires, Profitability as a Treadmill

Ivan deliberately kept Notion small: no salesperson until $10M+ ARR, first PM hired at 50 people, still profitable with most venture capital still in the bank. His reasoning: "overhead is actually more from internal communication." When you have people who can design, market, sell, and code, you naturally keep the company small. Profitability puts you on "a positive treadmill" — no need to raise money every 18-24 months, just focus on building. Run the company on your own product (Notion runs on Notion) to reduce coordination overhead.

### 4. Don't Be Afraid to Reset — Progress Through Better Abstractions

Ivan's view on resetting (rewriting code, changing strategy, letting go of the team): "You can create progress through better abstractions. And that thing compounds faster." The kernel of a great system can be tiny (like Lisp at ~100 lines) yet produce complex, valuable behavior. Humans think linearly, but progress through better abstractions compounds exponentially — you can catch up to years of work quickly if you find the right model. This applies to code, company structure, and even personal mindset. "You can reset your code base. You can reset your mental model."

### 5. Product-Market Fit Is a Gradual Ramp, Not a Binary Event

Ivan rejects the "PMF moment" narrative. For Notion, it was gradual: "Oh, good. We have people who care about this thing we make now. Oh, good. People reach out to us who are paying us." The signal was subtle — investor cold outreach, dog treats arriving at their non-public office address. An investor (Shana Fisher) had to tell Ivan: "You clearly don't need money. You're just trying to feel good about external validation." The realization: customer feedback, not investor interest, is the real signal. Highs are never too high, lows are never too low — "it never hit us as a binary state."
