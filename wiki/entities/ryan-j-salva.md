# Ryan J. Salva

Ryan J. Salva appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss vp of product at github, where he incubated and launched github copilot, on ai product development, incubating big bets at large companies, and his non-traditional path from philosophy to product.

## Key Views

**Incubate Big Bets in a Separate R&D Team** — GitHub Next was ring-fenced from operational demands to work on horizon-two and horizon-three projects. Copilot started there without expectations of immediate monetization or enterprise-grade security. The first step is simply investing in smart people and letting them be creative. This R&D buffer is essential for moonshots that would never survive a traditional product roadmap process focused on quarterly metrics. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-j-salva.md]

**Transition R&D Projects with People, Not Handoffs** — When Copilot moved from GitHub Next to a product team, the researchers moved with it for a finite period to transfer knowledge and seed the operational squad. The transition was based on having a replacement ready who had absorbed all necessary context, not on a calendar date. The product team getting ownership of their own roadmap was critical — you cannot delegate roadmap to an R&D team. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-j-salva.md]

**Inline Autocomplete Keeps Developers in Flow** — The UX breakthrough for Copilot was inline autocomplete (gray italicized text in the editor) rather than a side panel or chat interface. This kept developers in the flow state rather than context-switching. The key insight was that AI should augment rather than replace — framing Copilot as an "AI pair programmer" that keeps a thinking human at the keyboard. Performance was critical: the sweet spot for suggestion latency is ~200ms. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-j-salva.md]

**A Non-Traditional Background Was an Asset, Not a Liability** — Ryan studied philosophy of aesthetics and 20th century critical theory — not computer science. His focus on how humans communicate and express creativity directly informed how he thought about Copilot. The most impactful product leaders don't need to be the best engineers; they need to understand how people create and where they get stuck. A liberal arts training taught him pattern recognition and systems thinking that pure CS training might not have. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-j-salva.md]

**Invest 5-10% of Capacity in Bold R&D, 60% in Incremental Improvements** — Ryan's portfolio approach: ~5-10% of team capacity for audacious research projects (Copilot-level bets), ~60% for incremental improvements to in-market products, and ~25-30% for operations. At startups you're all-in on one big bet, but at scale this portfolio approach ensures you're investing in future growth while maintaining current products. The hardest part is protecting that 5-10% from being cannibalized by urgent operational needs. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-j-salva.md]
