# Jason Lemkin

**Role:** Founder of SaaStr (world's largest community for SaaS founders); investor deploying $200M+ into B2B startups; former CEO/Co-founder of EchoSign (sold to Adobe, grew to $100M+ ARR)  
**Known for:** Deep, tactical advice on building B2B sales teams; running the largest SaaS conference in the world (15K+ attendees); being the most prolific sharer of SaaS wisdom on Twitter/LinkedIn.

## Key Views

### 1. Hire Two Sales Reps Before a VP of Sales — And Hire People You'd Buy From

Jason's single most repeated piece of advice: hire two individual sales reps who hit quota *before* hiring a VP of Sales. The VP of Sales' job is to scale from rep 3 to 300 — not to be the first rep. A VP hired too early is a "Hail Mary that never works." For those first two reps, ignore logos on resumes. The one cheat code: "They have to be people you would **buy your own product from**." A quirky founder who loves your terrible early-stage product beats a polished Twilio sales rep every time. Leads are too precious to waste on someone who can't sell them.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-m-lemkin.md]

> "Leads are so precious in the early days. So wait, and I know it's painful... You need that quirky pyromaniac that loves your product and can sell it." — Jason Lemkin

### 2. Founders Are "A+ Middlers" — Use That Superpower

Most founders think they're bad at sales. Jason reframes: founders are "A+ middlers" — brilliant at the middle of the conversation (understanding the product, solving customer problems) even if they struggle with opening or closing. Customers *love* talking to the CEO. "If I want to buy CRM, I don't get to talk to Marc Benioff, but I get to talk to you. It's magical for early adopters." The only skill founders need to add: learning to ask "What would it take to get going?" — the close. Everything else comes naturally.

### 3. Sales Is Not Optional — Be Honest About Your Go-to-Market Motion

Jason's tough love: if your first 10 customers needed a conversation to close, you don't have a self-serve product — no matter how much you wish you did. Founders who hate sales and fire their sales team pay a devastating price. Example: a company fired its sales team at $5M ARR and collapsed to $1M. The lesson: sales isn't "icky" — it's helping customers solve problems. Even PLG darlings (Slack, Canva, Notion) eventually built enterprise sales teams. It's a question of *when*, not *if*.

### 4. Interview 30 Sales Reps — Then Hire the One Who Loves Your Product

Jason's hiring process: interview 30 candidates. 20 will be unusable (no prep, didn't visit your website). 8 will be okay but not great. 1-2 will be "magicians" — they ask the right questions and describe your product's value better than you can. That's your hire. Criteria: **(1)** Would I buy my product from this person? **(2)** Do they genuinely love this product? **(3)** Can they be a product guru for your category? Ignore LinkedIn logos, ignore whether they were "let go" from their last job.

### 5. The Biggest SaaS Mistake: Hiring a VP of Sales Before Having a Repeatable Process

Founders must close the first 10-15 customers themselves. The signal to hire: you're spending >20% of your time on sales and need leverage. But before hiring a VP, you need: (a) two reps hitting quota, and (b) a repeatable sales process. Otherwise you're asking someone to do four impossible jobs simultaneously — find PMF, be the first rep, be the second rep, and build the sales machine. The VP of Sales must also "carry a bag" (be in deals 20-30 hours/week) in the early days, not just manage.
