# Bob Moesta

Bob Moesta is the co-creator of the Jobs to Be Done framework alongside Clay Christensen. He has started eight companies and is currently CEO of the Rewired Group, helping companies unlock hidden insights. He recently published "Job Moves," applying JTBD to career transitions based on interviews with over 1,000 people.

## Key Views

**Context Makes the Irrational Rational** — The biggest misconception about JTBD is focusing on pain/gain rather than context/outcome. When someone's behavior seems irrational, it's because you're missing their context. "Nine times out of 10, it's because you don't have the rest of the story." Understanding the full context — not just the functional problem — reveals why people do what they do. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta.md]

**Progress, Not Solutions** — Customers don't "hire" products for features. They hire them to make progress in a specific circumstance. The progress includes functional, emotional, and social dimensions. A milkshake isn't purchased for nutrition — it's hired by a commuter to make the drive to work more interesting and to feel virtuous about a "healthy" breakfast. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta.md]

**The Struggle Moment** — JTBD interviews should focus on the moment of struggle — the specific instance when a person decided they needed to change. Not hypotheticals, not generalities. Ask: "Tell me about the last time you realized you needed a different solution." The details of that moment reveal the real job. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta.md]

**When JTBD Doesn't Apply** — The framework is powerful for existing behaviors people want to change (switching jobs, buying a car). It's less useful for: (1) truly novel behaviors people have never experienced (the first web browser), (2) habit-adjacent improvements (make my existing tool slightly better), and (3) purely emotional purchases without functional progress. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta.md]

**The Four Forces of Progress** — Every decision to change is driven by four forces: Push (dissatisfaction with current situation), Pull (attraction of the new solution), Anxiety (fear of change), and Habit (inertia of current behavior). To drive adoption, increase push and pull while decreasing anxiety and habit — most product teams only focus on the pull side. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta.md]


### 1. **Employees Hire Companies More Than Companies Hire Employees** — The moment you stop making progress in your career is the moment you start looking for another job. Luck is when opportunity meets preparedness — and preparedness is about understanding yourself. Most people who switch jobs end up in a worse position because they don't know what they actually need. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta-20.md]

### 2. **Job Features Are Not Job Experiences** — Salary, title, and perks are features. What keeps you at a job — or drives you away — are experiences: whether you feel respected, whether you're learning, whether the work gives you energy. Money is often a surrogate for respect or a way to signal 'I'm keeping up,' not the actual need. Unpacking the 'why' behind wanting more money reveals the real job experience you're seeking. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta-20.md]

### 3. **The Four Quests of Career Transitions** — Across 1,000+ interviews, all job moves fall into four buckets: (1) Get Out — energy is drained, need breathing room; (2) Take the Next Step — plateaued, need skills/capabilities; (3) Regain Control — like what you do but the volume/pressure is too much; (4) Realign — stretched into areas you're not good at, need to return to what you love. Most people don't know which quest they're on, so they solve the wrong problem. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta-20.md]

### 4. **Energy Drivers and Energy Drains: The 95/5 Trap** — Most people spend 95% of their time doing work that drains their energy to get 5% of joy. The target is 40-50% energy-giving work — at that ratio, work no longer feels like work. Bob's technique: go back through your career, capture moments where you gained energy, dissect what caused it, and use those as design requirements for your next role. Also capture energy drains — they often correlate with your bottom-five StrengthsFinder traits. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta-20.md]

### 5. **Hire Your Complement, Not Your Clone** — Most people hire others like themselves, creating large blind spots. Bob and his business partner of 25 years are exact opposites — what Bob loves, his partner hates and vice versa. They trust each other enough to take off the work that drains the other. Team diversity isn't about demographics; it's about finding people who love doing the things that drain your energy. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bob-moesta-20.md]
