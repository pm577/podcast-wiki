# Lauryn Isford

Lauryn Isford is a product growth leader who most recently led Growth at Airtable. Previously, she was a product growth lead at Meta (Facebook) working on user growth, internet.org, and Facebook growth in India. She also led growth for Blue Bottle's e-commerce business. She is known for her contrarian take that teams experiment too often and should use more judgment.

## Key Views

### Don't Experiment Everything — A Contrarian Growth Take

Lauryn's spicy take: growth teams are too dependent on A/B testing. She identifies two valid reasons to experiment: understanding precise metric impact and risk mitigation for dramatic changes. "Sometimes you don't need to experiment. If activation rates go up 6% versus 7%, that precision actually doesn't help all that much." Experiments are expensive — they burn engineering, analyst, and PM time that could be spent shipping. Her advice: experiment when you need to, especially as risk mitigation, and let product development rigor do more work.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

### Onboarding Is the Undervalued Growth Lever

Onboarding is "that first really important choke point from which so many important metrics and results flow." Whether you have a self-serve product, sandbox demo, or freemium tier, getting onboarding right unlocks conversion, retention, and expansion. At Airtable, Lauryn's team rebuilt onboarding with a "Guided Onboarding" wizard that reduced cognitive load, added personalization by use case, and built ongoing education (the "Mole" — an in-product tip system that pops up from the bottom).^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

### Activation Rate: Lower Can Be Better

Counterintuitive insight: "An activation rate that falls in a lower percentage range — maybe five to 15% — is better than one that falls in a higher percentage range because it means there's likely much higher correlation with long-term retention." If you make activation harder, the people who make it through are much more likely to stick. Don't optimize for high activation rate; optimize for activation that predicts retention.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

### Build a Culture of Results, Not Experiment Credits

To escape the trap of experiment-everything, Lauryn says you need a culture that rewards impact measured through other means: qualitative customer feedback, closed deals, retention signals. "A culture around the impact you had on customers as measured by qualitative feedback is important. If there's a culture around having to point to numbers, then the team will bias experiments all the time." This must come from the top.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]

### Ship When You Know It's Right

At Airtable, Lauryn's team shipped a new feature (form submission copy) without an A/B test. They knew from rigorous customer analysis and data work that customers wanted it. "We just turned it on and wanted to see what would happen because we knew from rigor that this is something that people wanted." The move was validated when the top-line signup metrics moved. Not everything needs a counterfactual.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lauryn-isford.md]
