# Bill Carr

**Role:** Co-author of *Working Backwards*; 15-year Amazon veteran (VP of Digital Media, launched Amazon Music, Prime Video, Amazon Studios); former COO at OfferUp  
**Known for:** Codifying Amazon's unique ways of working — the Working Backwards PR/FAQ process, single-threaded leadership, disagree and commit, input vs output metrics, and the Bar Raiser hiring program.

## Key Views

### 1. Single-threaded leadership — ownership, speed, and agility

When Amazon grew too complex for the CEO to be in every decision, they created small, autonomous teams each with a single leader owning a dedicated domain. These teams had all the cross-functional resources they needed (engineering, design, product) either reporting directly or dedicated to them. This replaced "project orientation" (swarm and move on) with "program orientation" (a team that always owns that area). The trade-off is potential loss of functional excellence — counterbalanced by central standards, promotion panels, and cross-org code reviews.

> "We moved from a project orientation to a program orientation. Instead of thinking about things on a project-by-project basis, the team thinks holistically about what they need to do to improve their area." — Bill Carr

### 2. Disagree and Commit — the most misunderstood leadership principle

Bill's nuanced explanation: you must "have backbone" and voice disagreement when you have new information or a different point of view. But once the decision-maker acknowledges they've heard and understood your perspective, you must genuinely commit. True commitment means understanding why the direction was chosen — not just going along resentfully. If you still disagree after the decision, focus on the "kernel" of what the leader believes and try to make that viable.

> "Where people get confused is they don't understand when they're supposed to stop disagreeing. Once you've provided your information and they've processed it and decided to go this way with that knowledge, that is the point to commit." — Bill Carr

### 3. Working Backwards — start with the customer, not constraints

Amazon's innovation process starts from the customer's needs and works backward, deliberately ignoring financial, legal, and engineering constraints at first. The core artifact is the PR/FAQ — a press release announcing the finished product plus a FAQ. Jeff Bezos took it "as an article of faith" that serving customers well would eventually drive revenue and free cash flow. This counteracts the natural tendency to start from internal constraints and work forward.

> "If we served customers well, if we prioritized customers and delivered for them, things like sales, revenue, active customers, share price, and free cash flow would follow. We took it as an article of faith." — Bill Carr

### 4. Input metrics vs output metrics — manage what teams can control

Amazon divided metrics into two categories: **output metrics** (revenue, active customers — the results you want) and **input metrics** (things the team can directly influence, like click-through rates or page load times). Bill warns against "fitness functions" or compound metrics — mixing several metrics into one index obscures what's actually driving results. Each important metric should be managed individually.

> "The fitness function was not a good idea. By putting metrics all together, you basically obfuscate what's happening. We need to break each one out individually and manage them each in its own way." — Bill Carr

### 5. The Bar Raiser program — institutionalizing hiring standards

Amazon created a Bar Raiser role: a trained interviewer who sits on every hiring panel but does not report to the hiring manager. Their sole job is to uphold the company's hiring bar and prevent shortcuts. They have veto power over any hire and are evaluated on the quality of hires over time, not on speed. This counteracts the natural pressure to fill open roles quickly and ensures standards don't slip as the company scales.

> "The Bar Raiser's only job is to maintain the quality of the bar. They have no incentive to fill the role quickly; their incentive is to ensure every hire meets or exceeds the standard." — Bill Carr
