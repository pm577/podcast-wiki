# Sarah Tavel

**Role:** General Partner at Benchmark (one of the top VC firms); former partner at Greylock; former product leader at Pinterest during its hypergrowth phase  
**Known for:** The "Hierarchy of Engagement" framework for consumer products; deep expertise in consumer product growth and engagement loops; investing in early-stage consumer and marketplace companies.

## Key Views

### 1. The Hierarchy of Engagement

Sarah's most famous framework for consumer products: a four-level pyramid. Level 1 (Base): retain users — can you get them to come back? Level 2 (Core): drive habitual use — do they come back without being prompted? Level 3 (Growth): create organic loops — do users bring other users? Level 4 (Revenue): monetize — can you capture value without breaking engagement? Each level builds on the one below. Most consumer startups fail because they try to solve Level 3 or 4 before they've solved Level 1.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

> "You can't build a growth loop until you have retention. You can't monetize until you have a growth loop. The hierarchy is sequential — you must master each level before moving up." — Sarah Tavel

### 2. Retention is the Only Metric That Matters Early

Sarah's strong opinion: for early-stage consumer products, retention is the single most important metric. Not growth rate, not revenue, not engagement — retention. If you can retain users over time (especially week-over-week for the first 12 weeks), you have a viable product. If you can't, no amount of growth or monetization will save you. She advises founders to obsess over retention curves and cohort analysis before anything else.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

> "In the early days, there is only one metric that matters: retention. If your retention curve flattens, you have something. If it slopes to zero, nothing else matters." — Sarah Tavel

### 3. Find the "Habit-Forming" Magic Moment

Sarah's framework for building habit-forming products: identify the specific moment when a first-time user experiences the core value and thinks "I need to come back." For Pinterest, it was finding an image that perfectly matched their taste. For any product, it's the intersection of the user's intent and the product's core value. The magic moment must occur within the first session — if it takes multiple sessions to find value, most users will never get there.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

> "There is a magic moment in every great product — the instant when the user gets it. Your job is to get every new user to that moment as fast as possible." — Sarah Tavel

### 4. Consumer Products Need a "Hooked" Loop, Not Just Utility

Sarah argues that consumer products need more than utility — they need a "hooked" loop that creates internal triggers. Utility products (calendars, notes, calculators) have high value but low engagement because there's no internal trigger to return. Habit-forming products create a craving — a feeling of "I need to check this." Her investment framework: does the product solve a recurring need with variable reward? If yes, it has potential for habit formation. If it's purely utilitarian, growth will always require paid acquisition.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

> "Pure utility products are valuable but rarely become habits. The best consumer products create a craving — an internal trigger that brings users back without being prompted." — Sarah Tavel

### 5. Marketplace Flywheels: Start with the Hard Side

From her experience investing in marketplaces, Sarah advises founders to start with the harder side of the marketplace (usually supply). For Airbnb, that was listings. For Uber, that was drivers. The harder side requires more investment, more patience, and more creative problem-solving. But once you have sufficient supply, demand-side growth becomes much easier. Founders who start with the easy side often find themselves with plenty of demand but not enough supply to satisfy it.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/sarah-tavel.md]

> "Marketplaces always fail on the hard side. If you don't have enough supply, all the demand in the world just creates a bad experience. Start with the hard problem." — Sarah Tavel
