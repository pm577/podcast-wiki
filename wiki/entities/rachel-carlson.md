# Rachel Lockett

Rachel Lockett is a sought-after executive coach and former HR leader at Stripe and Pinterest. She works with CEOs, founders, and tech leaders on emotional intelligence, resilience, courage, and leadership skills. Her coaching approach combines practical frameworks with deep empathy, helping leaders navigate the human challenges of building companies.

## Key Views

### Stop Having All the Answers

"Most leaders, especially technical leaders, assume they have to have all the answers. People have climbed the ladder because they've been dependable, reliable, the smartest person in the room. But great leaders know that when you try to advise and have the answer all the time, you're not actually equipping your team to solve hard problems. You're training your team to come to you with all the hard problems." The shift: coach, don't advise.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rachel-lockett.md]

### The Goal of Conflict Is Mutual Understanding, Not Winning

Rachel reframes how leaders should approach difficult conversations. "There's a misguided view that the goal is to convince the other person that what they're doing is wrong. Actually, the goal of any conflict is to create mutual understanding." This shift from adversarial to collaborative transforms how teams resolve tension. Her GROW technique (Goal, Reality, Options, Will) helps people figure out solutions for themselves.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rachel-lockett.md]

### It's Your Job to Navigate Your Career, Not Your Manager's

"What I notice in big companies is people are often annoyed or frustrated with their management for not making their job more interesting. No, your manager's job is to help you perform in the job you are hired to do. It's your job to navigate your career." Rachel emphasizes personal agency: "When people are in their gifts and their strengths, they have more energy." Don't wait for someone to hand you the right role.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rachel-lockett.md]

### "Would I Enthusiastically Rehire This Person?"

Rachel's framework for making tough talent decisions: ask yourself honestly, "Would I enthusiastically rehire this person?" If the answer is no, the decision is already made — you just need to act on it. This cuts through the avoidance and ambiguity that leads to underperformers lingering on teams, draining energy from everyone.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rachel-lockett.md]

### Start Small with People Skills

The human side of business can feel overwhelming. Rachel's advice: start small. Pick one skill — active listening, asking powerful questions, or reframing a difficult conversation as a growth opportunity — and practice it intentionally. "It is achievable, and it's actually most natural. Leaders want to care about the people they work with. They want to empower those around them." Small deliberate practice compounds into transformed relationships.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/rachel-lockett.md]
