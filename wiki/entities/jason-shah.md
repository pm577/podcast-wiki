# Jason Shah

**Role:** Product leader at Alchemy (blockchain infrastructure); former product leader at Airbnb, Amazon, Microsoft/Yammer; founder of do.com  
**Known for:** Deep expertise in product management across Web3, Web2, and enterprise; writing "The Product Manager's Guide to Web3" (one of Lenny's most popular guest posts); blending product and business thinking from Amazon's working backwards methodology.

## Key Views

### 1. "Pushing Back" Is the Wrong Frame — Shift Direction Instead

Jason reframes the concept of "pushing back" on stakeholders: "I couldn't imagine a word more viscerally negative. It gears people into a mindset of 'I need to disagree, I need to say no.'" Instead, he advocates: **shift direction** — understand the other person's goal, find alignment, and redirect energy toward a better outcome. This positive framing transforms adversarial interactions into collaborative ones. The key: start by understanding what the goal is, then align your alternative with that goal.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-shah.md]

> "Pushback... starts from a place of I need to disagree, I need to say no. It's a very negative mindset... alternatively could be about how do I shift the direction on something, or how do I help the business actually succeed when I disagree." — Jason Shah

### 2. The Amazon PRFAQ — Working Backwards with Concrete Language

Jason's most formative process from Amazon: the PRFAQ (Press Release + Frequently Asked Questions). Structure: **(1)** Introduction announcing the product, **(2)** Problem (one paragraph, concrete), **(3)** Solution, **(4)** Customer quote (must be specific — no word "great" allowed), **(5)** Leadership quote, **(6)** Call to action. The discipline forces clarity: "If you say something is 'great because we deliver in two hours' vs. 'great because selection is wide,' the strategic implications are completely different." Internal FAQs force you to address elephants in the room. Every word matters — "kill your darlings" to achieve strict concision.

### 3. Progress Is the Only True Morale-Builder

Through Web3's boom-bust cycle, Jason learned that maintaining team morale comes from one thing: **making progress**. "No speech, no sort of extrinsic motivators really works. People get really excited when they see progress." During Airbnb's 85-90% revenue collapse, the same principle applied — the teams that focused on shipping and customer progress pulled through strongest. At Alchemy, shipping Solana support during crypto winter proved to developers that the platform was real, keeping the community engaged.

### 4. Web3 Products Can Scale Surprisingly Far Without Traditional PM

Jason's observation: Web3 products (like Uniswap, which does more daily volume than Coinbase with ~100 people vs. 5,000+) have scaled remarkably without traditional product management playbooks. This is changing — teams are increasingly hiring PMs as products mature, competition increases, and complexity grows. The role of a PM shifts from "not needed" in early Web3 (where community management and token mechanics dominated) to "highly valued" as products reach scale and need strategic differentiation.

### 5. Amazon's Writing Discipline — Numbers Over Adjectives

The Amazon writing culture that stuck with Jason: never use subjective words like "great" - instead use concrete, measurable language: "will save customers 20 minutes each day." The 5-card writing tips every Amazon employee gets includes: be concise, be specific, use numbers, kill your darlings. Fewer words means every word carries 10x weight, which forces intentionality about decisions and trade-offs. This applies to emails, documents, and especially PRFAQs.
