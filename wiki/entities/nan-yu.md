# Nan Yu

Nan Yu appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss nan yu (linear head of product) on building beloved b2b products and the 'feel bad' discovery framework.

## Key Views

**Speed and Quality Are Not a Trade-Off** — The belief that speed and quality are in tension is "devious misinformation spread by the slow." Real speed comes from deep competence — experts produce higher quality work faster. The key practice is getting a working prototype out in the first 10% of your time budget to validate or invalidate key hypotheses before too much investment is sunk. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]

**Feel Bad Discovery — Emotional Hooks as Product Opportunities** — The goal on customer calls is to "feel bad in the same way that customers feel bad." Dig past surface-level feature requests by asking "how do you feel?" until you uncover the specific emotional pain moments in their workflow. Build solutions that eliminate those bad feelings, not just the surface-level ask. This is how Linear finds non-obvious product opportunities in competitive markets. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]

**Prioritize ICs Over Middle Managers to Avoid Bloat** — The #1 category of feature requests to reject is customization and reporting features requested by middle managers that come at the cost of making individual contributors' workflows worse. This is the core promise of Linear — they will not make this trade-off. When incentives are misaligned between user segments, always choose the IC experience. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]

**Systemized Creativity via Extreme Prototyping** — To find non-obvious solutions, ask "how extreme can you take it?" along a single dimension (e.g., fastest possible vs. safest possible), build those extreme versions quickly, ship them to real users, and find the balanced solution that emerges between the two poles. This expands the search space beyond your defaults and prevents you from choosing among mediocre options. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]

**The Double Triangle — PM as Bridge Between Build and Sell** — Product management should be partially a go-to-market discipline. The PM sits in the middle of two triangles: the build side (engineering, design, PM) and the sell side (sales, marketing, PM). PMs should originate messaging and language for their audience, collaborate deeply with marketing on release notes and copy, and use deep customer knowledge to help the entire organization speak natively to customers. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/nan-yu.md]
