# Ben Horowitz

**Role:** Co-founder of Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), the world's largest VC firm ($46B+ committed capital); author of *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* and *What You Do Is Who You Are*; creator of the legendary "Good Product Manager, Bad Product Manager" essay  
**Known for:** Hard-truth leadership philosophy, investing in tech giants (OpenAI, Cursor, Databricks, Figma), and his distinctive "PM as mini-CEO" perspective.

## Key Views

### 1. Leaders must make decisions nobody likes — that's where value is added

Ben's core leadership tenet: if everybody agrees with a decision, you haven't added any value — they'd have done it without you. The only value a leader adds is making choices that most people don't like. This requires building the "psychological muscle" to make calls in the abyss, between two terrible options. His own example: taking Netscape public with $2M trailing revenue because the alternative (bankruptcy) was worse. The leader's job is to pick the better of two horrible options and move forward without hesitation.

> "The psychological muscle you have to build to be a great leader is to be able to click in the abyss and go, 'Okay, that way's slightly better. We're going to go that way.' If everybody agrees with the decision, then you didn't add any value because they would've done that without you." — Ben Horowitz

### 2. The PM is the mini-CEO — and that's not an insult

Ben stands by his famous framing from "Good Product Manager, Bad Product Manager." The PM doesn't need to generate every idea — the job is to consolidate good ideas, prioritize them, and get everyone aligned with high-fidelity understanding. This is fundamentally a leadership role (not an authority role), because nobody reports to the PM. The PM must work backwards from "the product works" — not from a spec, an interview, or any intermediate output. The CEO parallel is appropriate because the function is the same: consolidate, prioritize, align, deliver.

> "What matters is that the product works. And you have to get all the way to there and work backwards from that and you can't do that without leadership because it is about … you're just the keeper of the vision." — Ben Horowitz

### 3. Judge people by their strengths, coach them on their strengths

Ben's philosophy on talent (learned partly from Shaka Senghor and Al Davis): invest in strength, not lack of weakness. Judge people on what they can do, coach them on what they can do — don't obsess over their flaws. Everybody's uneven. This is especially relevant to venture capital, where you're betting on people more than ideas (initial ideas almost always change). His defense of investing in Adam Neumann (WeWork founder) after the WeWork collapse exemplifies this: "You don't judge a person by the worst thing that ever happened to them."

> "Venture capital is really about investing in people. … One of the things we emphasize inside the firm is, look, we're investing in strength, not lack of weakness. I want to know how good, are they world-class?" — Ben Horowitz

### 4. AI is not a bubble — the businesses are real

Ben pushes back hard on AI bubble narratives. He distinguishes the dot-com bubble (1996-2000), where unit economics didn't work and businesses had no revenue, from the current AI boom, where companies are genuinely growing fast with real revenue. His rule of thumb: when everybody thinks it's a bubble, it's probably not — you need capitulation (everyone believing it's NOT a bubble) for prices to truly go out of control. The infrastructure layer (cheapest/lowest-latency model inference) and the application layer are where the biggest value will accrue, but foundation models require $2B+ to compete.

> "These businesses are all working, and they're being priced appropriately for how they're growing. … The products … are working so much better than any technology product that we've ever built has worked. It's just mind-blowing how good this stuff is." — Ben Horowitz

### 5. The best writing is the stuff you've already synthesized — write it in five minutes

Ben's observation on creative output: the things you write in five minutes often end up being much better than things you write in five weeks. "Good Product Manager, Bad Product Manager" was written quickly from a place of frustration — and it's become one of the most-read pieces in tech because it was already fully synthesized in his head. He notes that musicians, writers, and creatives all report the same phenomenon: the stuff you've already internalized so deeply that you just have to write it out is usually the best stuff.

> "The idea is that you have the things that you write in five minutes end up being much better than things you write in five weeks. … The thing that you've already synthesized so much that you just have to write it out, that's the best stuff." — Ben Horowitz
