# David Singleton

*Chief Technology Officer at Stripe, overseeing engineering and design. Previously VP of Engineering at Google (10+ years). Key architect of Stripe's product-minded engineering culture.*

## Key Views

1. **Co-create products with early users via shared Slack channels**: Stripe built Stripe Billing by co-creating with Figma and Slack — sharing early builds, getting real-time feedback, and not releasing broadly until the alpha group was "super, super happy." Every engineer building product at Stripe exercises PM-like skills: deep user understanding, curiosity, and iterative refinement.

2. **The singularity of purpose attracts a certain kind of person**: Stripe's mission — "increasing the GDP of the internet" — isn't just marketing. It's the lens through which every product decision is made. This attracts long-term thinkers who want to dig into difficult, unglamorous problems (like payment infrastructure) that require tenacity and collaboration. Mission as a talent magnet is real.

3. **Patient, personal hiring beats a recruiting machine**: For critical roles, Stripe will meet tens or hundreds of people and wait years for the right candidate. Hiring managers build deep personal relationships with candidates before they join. The process is structured (consistent loops, real-work exercises) but deeply personal. This combination ensures both fairness and genuine connection.

4. **Structured hiring loops with real-work exercises**: Engineers pair-program with interviewers using actual Stripe-like problems — Google and Stack Overflow allowed. PMs do a written exercise followed by discussion. No trick questions, no whiteboard hazing. The goal is to simulate the actual work and see how candidates collaborate, learn, and dig into details.

5. **Product-minded engineers can delay the need for PMs**: Stripe famously waited until ~200 employees to hire their first PM. Engineers at Stripe are expected to be deeply curious about users, dig into adjacent problems, and propose solutions. This doesn't replace PMs long-term, but it ensures that when PMs do arrive, engineering already has a strong product orientation and the collaboration is additive rather than compensatory.
