# Julia Schottenstein

Julia Schottenstein is a product lead at dbt Labs (dbt Cloud product) and an active angel investor in data and infrastructure startups. A former VC at NEA, she brings a rare investor-turned-PM perspective. She also co-hosts the Analytics Engineering Podcast. Her insights span M&A strategy, open-source business models, and product-led growth in the data space.

## Key Views

**M&A Is About Creating Plan Bs — Inflict Strategic Pain to Get Noticed**
Schottenstein advises founders to think about M&A as creating optionality, not as an exit strategy. For any company, there are only 2-3 buyers who will find what you're building extremely strategic. To get noticed: "I would figure out the area that you bring a competitive advantage. And I would inflict pain on that potential buyer. Make it impossible for them to not notice you." But do this in a way that keeps the relationship friendly — don't prematurely shut down conversations by being too combative.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]]*

**Evaluate Companies Like an Investor: People, Market, Product, Distribution**
Schottenstein uses the same framework she used as a VC to evaluate early-stage companies to join. When she discovered dbt, she assessed: CEO Tristan Handy had "range" — the rare ability to paint a compelling industry vision while being deeply detailed in day-to-day execution. The market (cloud data warehouses) was exploding. The product had fanatical users. The distribution was through open-source community. This four-part filter (people, market, product, distribution) is her recommendation for anyone evaluating whether to join an early-stage company.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]]**

**Fanatically Loved Products Create a Different Kind of Career Move**
Schottenstein was so convicted about dbt that when she lost the investment deal to Sequoia, she asked to invest 20% of her personal net worth — which the board vetoed. She then asked to join the company instead. What made dbt special: users described their experience not as using a tool but as an identity. "The way they described their experience on dbt was unlike anything I had heard before." This level of user fanaticism is the signal she looks for — in investing and in career moves.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]]**

**Pricing Is a Product Decision — It Defines Your Market Position**
Schottenstein treats pricing as an integral part of product strategy, not an afterthought for the finance team. For open-source companies especially, the pricing model signals what you value and who you serve. dbt's approach of having a generous free tier (dbt Core) and a paid cloud product (dbt Cloud) creates a natural upgrade path while building the broadest possible user base. The pricing conversation should start early and be revisited constantly as the product and market evolve.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]]**

**Competition Is a Signal You're Onto Something — Use It as a Compass**
Rather than fearing competitors, Schottenstein views them as validation that you're in a good space. The key is understanding what your specific advantage is and doubling down on it. For dbt, it was the community-led, open-source approach — not trying to out-enterprise the incumbents. She advises PMs to study competitors not to copy them but to clarify their own differentiation. If you can't articulate why someone will choose you over alternatives, you haven't done the strategic work yet.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/julia-schottenstein.md]]**
