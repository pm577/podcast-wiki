# Tamar Yehoshua

Tamar Yehoshua appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss president of product & technology at glean, formerly chief product officer at slack and product leader at google and amazon, on product leadership, ai strategy, cross-functional relationships, and why companies don't need to be well-run to win.

## Key Views

**Companies Don't Need to Be Well-Run to Win** — You can have high executive turnover, reorgs, and unhappy employees but still produce amazing numbers if you have strong product-market fit. Conversely, a well-oiled machine can flatline if nobody wants what you're building. Product-market fit solves most problems — if people are clamoring for your product, the chaos of hyper-growth is survivable. Once you reach scale (5,000+ people), then you need professional management. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tamar-yehoshua.md]

**Don't Build a Career Plan — Follow Great People** — Tamar never had a career plan. Instead, she followed people who were the best at what they did. Go where the great people are congregating because even if the company fails, you keep the skills and the network. Skills can't be taken away; a company can fail but you'll always have what you learned. The most valuable career asset is not a title or a company name — it's the deep expertise you develop working alongside top talent. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tamar-yehoshua.md]

**The Most Important Thing a Product Leader Does Is Build Cross-Functional Relationships** — You must have a strong engineering partner and be aligned on roles and responsibilities. No playing one leader against another. Spend time together, do all reviews jointly (OKR reviews, weekly exec reviews), build trust and respect, and be direct when you disagree. The product-engineering partnership is the single most leveraged relationship in any technology company. When it breaks, nothing works. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tamar-yehoshua.md]

**Design for Tomorrow's Users, Not Just Today's Power Users** — Don't over-index on making vocal users unhappy when making product changes. If you have to redo the UI and "Who Moved My Cheese" complaints arise, do it — but be respectful, transparent, and authentic about why. Listen to people and explain your reasoning. If people feel heard, it makes a huge difference in adoption and retention even when the change disrupts their workflow. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tamar-yehoshua.md]

**AI Will Blur the Lines Between PMs, Engineers, and Designers** — PMs who are purely execution-focused will see their jobs at risk, but strategic, creative PMs who can synthesize disparate pieces will thrive. The key is to actually use AI tools — feed transcripts to LLMs for sentiment analysis, automate status reporting, summarize articles. Don't dismiss AI because it's not perfect yet; iterate on prompts and you'll find massive productivity leverage. The product leaders who embrace AI personally will be the ones who build the best products. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/tamar-yehoshua.md]
