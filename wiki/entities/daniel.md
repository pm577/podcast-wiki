# Daniel Lereya

Daniel Lereya is Chief Product Officer at monday.com, where he has been for 8+ years, helping scale the company to 250,000+ customers. He is known for his philosophy of radical transparency and relentless focus on impact.

## Key Views

### Radical Transparency with Employees
Before going public, monday.com shared every bit of information with employees — including financials, strategy, and challenges. Daniel found this didn't demoralize people; instead, it "gives them a sense of deep partnership." Radical transparency turns employees into owners who think like founders, not just executors. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/daniel-lereya.md]

### Relentless Until Impact Is Validated
Daniel describes the ideal product person as "someone that is relentless until he gets this impact, until he validates that this impact is in place." The relentless pursuit of measurable impact — not feature delivery — is the defining characteristic of great product people. "In some cases, doing the biggest impact is not developing another feature, it's about making the current value more accessible." ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/daniel-lereya.md]

### Speed as a Competitive Response
monday.com realized their competitors were shipping much faster. This forced a fundamental shift in how they thought about product development. The response: reduce scope, increase autonomy, and ship smaller increments faster. Speed became a competitive weapon, not just a development metric. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/daniel-lereya.md]

### Everyone's Brain in the Challenge
"We really want everyone's brains in the challenge, and not just one centralized brain and a lot of working hands." This philosophy drives monday.com's culture of distributed decision-making. The PM isn't the sole strategist — every team member is expected to contribute ideas and challenge assumptions. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/daniel-lereya.md]

### Accessibility of Value over Feature Count
Daniel's counterintuitive insight: sometimes the biggest impact comes from making existing value more accessible rather than building new features. If users can't easily discover or use the features you've already built, adding more features only compounds the problem. This is the trap of the "feature factory" mindset. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/daniel-lereya.md]
