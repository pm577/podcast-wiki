# Ryan Singer

**Role:** Former Head of Strategy at 37signals (Basecamp), where he spent nearly two decades; creator of the Shape Up methodology for product development  
**Known for:** The Shape Up framework — a radical alternative to Scrum/agile; fixed-time, variable-scope product development; betting tables and six-week cycles.

## Key Views

### 1. Shape Up: Appetites Over Estimates

Shape Up replaces traditional estimation with "appetites" — fixed time budgets that constrain scope. Instead of asking "how long will this take?" (estimates), you ask "how much time are we willing to spend?" (appetite). The team then has to design a solution that fits within that appetite. This flips the power dynamic: scope is the variable, time is fixed. Most teams fail at estimation anyway — Shape Up eliminates the pretense.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

> "An estimate is like: how long do we think it's going to take? And however long it takes is however long it takes. Most human beings are terrible estimators." — Ryan Singer

### 2. Six-Week Cycles, Two-Week Cool-Downs

Shape Up's rhythm: six weeks of dedicated building followed by two weeks of cool-down (bug fixes, debt, internal projects, exploration). Six weeks is long enough to build something meaningful but short enough that the end is always in sight. If a project can't be done in six weeks, it shouldn't be extended — it should be cut and re-shaped into something smaller. This discipline prevents the death march of projects that stretch for months beyond their original scope.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

> "If we say we're going to give it six weeks and we give it seven or eight or nine, then we're not really giving it six weeks — then we don't really have a system." — Ryan Singer

### 3. Shaping: Designing the Solution Before Coding

Before a cycle begins, work goes through a "shaping" phase where the solution is designed at the right level of abstraction. Shaping answers: what are we building? what are the key technical decisions? what's the scope? what's NOT included? Shaped work is not a detailed spec — it's a well-defined problem with clear boundaries that gives the building team room for creativity and technical judgment. The shaper is usually a senior person who understands both the user need and the technical constraints.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

> "Shaping is designing the solution at the right level of abstraction. It's not a spec — it's a boundary. The team fills in the details." — Ryan Singer

### 4. The Betting Table: Choose Projects Like a Portfolio

Instead of a backlog (which implies everything will eventually get built), Shape Up uses a "betting table" — a regular meeting where leaders decide which projects to fund in the next cycle. Like a venture portfolio, you bet on the projects you think will deliver the best return. Unfunded projects aren't deferred — they're unpicked. This eliminates the illusion of the backlog and forces real prioritization. If you're not willing to bet on it, it shouldn't be on the list.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

> "The betting table changes the conversation from 'when will we get to this?' to 'is this worth betting on?' Those are very different questions." — Ryan Singer

### 5. Small Teams, Deep Autonomy

Shape Up teams are two people (one designer, one programmer) — intentionally small. This forces clear communication, eliminates coordination overhead, and gives the team autonomy to make decisions within the shaped boundaries. The team owns the outcome and has full authority over how to achieve it within the six-week appetite. No daily standups, no sprint planning, no project managers — just two people building something and reporting back at the end of six weeks.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/ryan-singer.md]

> "Two people is the right size for a Shape Up team. Any bigger and you spend more time coordinating than building. Any smaller and you lack the skills to ship." — Ryan Singer
