# Jeffrey Pfeffer

**Role:** Professor of Organizational Behavior at Stanford Graduate School of Business; author of 16 books including *The Seven Rules of Power*; teaches "Paths to Power" — one of the two most popular courses in the Stanford MBA program  
**Known for:** The study of power, influence, and organizational behavior; his course "Paths to Power" (described by students as "the cod liver oil of business school"); research on the Knowing-Doing Gap.

## Key Views

### 1. Power Is a Tool — How You Use It Is Up to You

Jeffrey's central reframe: power is like a knife — a surgeon uses it to cure cancer, a mugger uses it to rob people. "If you want power to be used for good, more good people need to have power." The people who most need to understand power are those from backgrounds that put them at a disadvantage — people of color, women, first-generation professionals. The Seven Rules are **skills that can be mastered**, not personality traits. "This is not about personality. These are skills."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jeffrey-pfeffer.md]

> "If you want power to be used for good, more good people need to have power." — Jeffrey Pfeffer

### 2. The Seven Rules of Power

1. **Get out of your own way** — Stop apologizing, stop imposter syndrome. "If you got the job, you're not only qualified, you're probably overqualified."
2. **Break the rules** — The path to power rarely follows conventional expectations.
3. **Appear powerful** — How you show up matters as much as what you know.
4. **Build a powerful brand** — "No one is going to promote Lenny if they don't know who the hell you are." Visibility without substance is useless; substance without visibility is invisible.
5. **Network relentlessly** — Relationships are the infrastructure of power.
6. **Use your power** — Acquiring power without using it is pointless.
7. **Success excuses almost everything** — What you did to get power will be forgiven, forgotten, or both.

### 3. The Knowing-Doing Gap — Doing Power Assignments

Jeffrey requires students to complete a "Doing Power" project — applying the principles in real life during the quarter. The rationale: "If you go to a French class and never speak French, you'll forget everything. If you don't use what you learn, the learning will disappear." His most extreme example: student Derek Kan's project was getting appointed head of economic policy for Mitt Romney's presidential campaign; he went on to run the $6 trillion US budget as deputy director of OMB.

### 4. Don't Be Judgmental About People on Your Critical Path

One of Jeffrey's most surprising rules: if someone is on your critical path at work, suspend judgment about them. "The only judgment you should make is 'they're on my critical path.'" If you leak disrespect or dislike, your ability to get things done with them goes to zero. "One of the skills senior executives have mastered is you cannot tell what they think of you." This is not about being fake — it's about not letting personal feelings interfere with professional effectiveness.

### 5. Building a Brand: Practical Tactics Beyond LinkedIn

Jeffrey's examples of effective brand-building: Laura Chau (Canaan Partners) used writing, a podcast, networking dinners, and even physical presence (dressing distinctively as a tall Asian woman) to generate deal flow from a non-marquee VC firm. Tristan Walker got hired at Foursquare by *already signing up partnerships* (Starbucks) without waiting for the job offer. The lesson: **do the work before you get the role** — demonstrate value rather than asking for it.
