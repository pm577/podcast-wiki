# Jason Feifer

**Role:** Editor-in-Chief of Entrepreneur Magazine; former editor at Fast Company, Men's Health; author, podcast host, keynote speaker, startup advisor  
**Known for:** Demystifying how startups can (and should) get press; deep understanding of how the media actually works from the inside; years of experience both as an editor and as a founder navigating press.

## Key Views

### 1. Journalists Don't Care About You — They Care About Their Audience

Jason's most important principle for anyone seeking press: "The editor, the writer — they don't care about you. They care about their reader, their listener, or their viewer." You must approach journalists with an understanding of what they're trying to do for their audience and how you fit into that. Don't treat them like a service provider. If you can help them serve their audience, you can get what you want. If not, they are not interested.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/jason-feifer.md]

> "They don't care about you. They care about their reader or their listener or their viewer. That's who they care about." — Jason Feifer

### 2. Know the Publication's Mission — It's Not Just "Write About Entrepreneurs"

Each publication has a distinct editorial mission that determines what stories fit. Entrepreneur Magazine is not "a magazine about entrepreneurs" — it's "a magazine about **thinking**" — looking for counterintuitive decisions and creative problem-solving, not just success stories. Fast Company is about "where business is going." Cosmo has a completely different angle. The critical step: study a publication's content until you understand what all published stories have in common. Then pitch within that frame.

### 3. Think About What Press Is For — Before You Chase It

Don't chase press without a clear goal. "You do it when you know what the money is for." Goals could include: awareness of a new product, investor interest, positioning in a market, establishing founder authority. The wrong reason: "I deserve this because I worked hard." The hot dog stand analogy: a DC hot dog vendor shouldn't pitch Entrepreneur (national audience who can't buy) — they should pitch the local food section (people who can actually visit).

### 4. Most PR People Are Bad — Look for Relationship-Based PR

Jason's warning: "Most PR people are bad at their jobs." Red flags include: (1) recommending press releases as the primary tactic (they have near-zero impact today — even if they end up on Yahoo Finance, nobody reads them there). (2) Guaranteeing press (writers and editors make completely subjective decisions). (3) Spraying mass blasts instead of crafting targeted pitches. What good PR provides: **relationships** with specific journalists and the ability to shape your story for each publication's unique mission. Good PR people also push back on clients.

### 5. Freelance Writers Are Your Best Entry Point

Staff writers at major publications are overwhelmed. Freelance writers are always looking for their next story and are more accessible. Find writers who cover your beat, read their work, understand what they write about, then pitch them. The best way to find them: look at bylines in your target publication. A PR person can be valuable here because they know which freelance writers are currently active and looking for assignments.
