# Albert Cheng

Albert Cheng led growth at three of the world's most successful consumer subscription companies: Duolingo, Grammarly, and Chess.com. A former Google PM and serious pianist, he is known for his "Explore & Exploit" growth framework and applying AI to accelerate growth experimentation.

## Key Views

### The Explore & Exploit Framework — Find Mountains, Then Climb Them
Albert's core mental model: **explore** to find the right mountain to climb, then **exploit** to focus resources on climbing it effectively. At Chess.com, the growth team discovered (through exploration) that 80% of game reviews happen after a win — counterintuitive since the feature was designed for learning from losses. They flipped the experience to show brilliant moves after losses, growing reviews by 25% and subscriptions by 20%. Then they entered exploitation mode: spreading this positive-reframing insight across puzzles and lessons, amplifying the win 10x. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/albert-cheng.md]

### Show, Don't Gate: The Reverse Trial That Doubled Grammarly's Upgrades
Grammarly's biggest monetization win: free users only saw spelling/grammar corrections, making the brand feel like "a spell checker." The team started sampling paid suggestions (tone, clarity, rewrites) among free users — a capped, real-time reverse trial. Upgrade rates nearly doubled. Insight: your free experience should reflect the full capabilities of your product, even with limited access. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/albert-cheng.md]

### Growth = Connecting Users to Value, Not Metrics Hacking
Albert defines growth simply: "connect users to the value of the product." For consumer subscription companies, **current user retention** (stickiness of the existing base) is the most important metric. D1 retention of 30-40% is "solid." As companies mature, resurrecting dormant users (hundreds of millions) becomes a higher-ROI lever than acquiring net-new users. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/albert-cheng.md]

### Agency Over Experience — Hire for Clock Speed, Not Deep Expertise
Counterintuitive hiring lesson from leading growth teams across three companies: high-performers have high agency and high clock speed — they don't necessarily need deep category experience. In the AI era, past experience can become a crutch because learned habits must be intentionally discarded as the ground shifts. Duolingo's "Green Machine" hires talented people right out of college with incredible experimentation tooling. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/albert-cheng.md]

### Use AI to Democratize Data — Build Text-to-SQL Slack Bots
At Chess.com, Albert's team built a Slack bot answering ad-hoc data questions using text-to-SQL. Anyone can ask "How many subscribers in South Africa?" and get an instant answer. The surprising effect: people ask questions they'd be embarrassed to submit to a human analyst, dramatically increasing data-informed decision-making. AI as the first-pass data provider frees analysts for deeper work. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/albert-cheng.md]
