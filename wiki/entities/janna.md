# Janna Bastow

Janna Bastow is the CEO and co-founder of ProdPad, a roadmapping tool for product teams, and co-founder of Mind the Product, the world's largest community of product managers (300,000+ members). She invented the Now-Next-Later roadmapping framework and is a sought-after speaker on product management, roadmaps, and community building.

## Key Views

**Your Roadmap Is a Prototype for Your Strategy, Not a Plan**
Bastow's core insight is that a roadmap should be treated like a prototype — it's a way of checking assumptions, not a fixed plan. "The value isn't in your roadmap, the value is in the roadmapping process." By sharing early assumptions with teams and customers, you validate whether you're solving the right problems before investing heavily in building. Her Now-Next-Later framework replaces traditional timeline-based roadmaps with a more flexible, outcome-oriented approach that communicates priorities without false certainty about dates.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/janna-bastow.md]]*

**Write Your Narrative Before Your Deck — Story First, Slides Second**
Bastow learned from her speaker coach that the most effective presentations start with a narrative, not a PowerPoint deck. She used to build slides first and then try to fit a story around them. Now she starts with Post-it Notes on a wall to map out her story arc, then builds slides to support the narrative. This reversal ensures the talk has emotional resonance and logical flow, with visuals serving the story rather than dictating it.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/janna-bastow.md]]*

**Build Community by Being Consistently Useful, Not by Having a Grand Plan**
Mind the Product wasn't a calculated strategy — it started from Bastow and co-founders admitting they didn't know what they were doing and wanting to learn together. "It was just the sense of sharing and collaborating and learning from each other and just keeping it as grassroots as possible as it grew." The key was consistency: holding monthly meetups without fail, being present whenever there was an opportunity to contribute, and surrounding herself with people who could extend the community's reach beyond her own network.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/janna-bastow.md]]*

**Conferences Are Anti-Lean — Prepare for Thousands of Dollars of Failure Modes**
Bastow is brutally honest about running conferences: they're the least lean thing you can do. "If something goes wrong in a conference, it doesn't happen in the hundreds of dollars of cost, it happens in the thousands of dollars of cost." Venues go bust, speakers cancel after you've paid for their business-class flights, food orders under-deliver. The only way to survive is to have built enough goodwill with your community that they forgive the inevitable screw-ups. This candor grounds the "just run an event" advice in real operational reality.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/janna-bastow.md]]*

**Find Your Fans in the Audience and Speak Only to Them**
Bastow's most tactical speaking advice: find the few people in the audience nodding along and smiling, and direct your talk to them. "No one's going to notice that you're doing your talk just to them." This transforms the experience from performing for a faceless crowd to having a conversation with engaged humans. She also recommends power poses (hands on hips) before going on stage — even if it's a placebo, it works. And walk the stage during tech check the day before to familiarize yourself with the space.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/janna-bastow.md]]*
