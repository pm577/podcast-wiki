# April Dunford

**Role:** Author of *Obviously Awesome* (product positioning) and *Sales Pitch*; 25-year VP of Marketing veteran; advisor to Google, Epic Games, and many startups  
**Known for:** Being the world's foremost authority on product positioning, the positioning framework that has helped thousands of companies find their market fit.

## Key Views

### 1. 40-60% of B2B purchases end in "no decision" — and that's a positioning problem

April's starting insight: the majority of B2B purchase processes don't end in "we chose competitor X" — they end with the buyer saying "not now." This happens because the buyer can't confidently make a choice. The root cause is poor positioning: if you haven't made it obvious why someone should buy *and* how to decide confidently, they'll defer. Your job is to reduce decision risk, not just pitch features.

> "40 to 60% of B2B purchase processes end in no decision. The majority couldn't figure out how to make a choice confidently. They delayed because that's the safe, risk-free thing." — April Dunford

### 2. Positioning is not messaging — it's the foundation

April's crucial distinction: positioning comes *before* messaging. Positioning is the act of defining the context in which your product is the best choice. This includes: who you compete against, the value you uniquely deliver, the customer segment where you shine, and the market category you belong in. Messaging is how you communicate that positioning. Most companies jump to messaging without doing the hard positioning work first.

### 3. The 10-step positioning process

April's framework from *Obviously Awesome*: (1) understand the customers who love you, (2) identify your competitive alternatives, (3) analyze your unique capabilities, (4) find the market context where those capabilities matter, (5) define the market category that fits, (6) capture trends that support your position, (7) articulate your core value, (8) clarify your target customer, (9) develop your positioning statement, (10) test and refine relentlessly.

### 4. The "market category trap" — don't invent a category, find your best context

April cautions against the Silicon Valley obsession with category creation. Instead of trying to invent a new category (which is expensive and confusing), find the *existing* context where your strengths are most valued and your weaknesses are irrelevant. She advocates for "competitive positioning" — picking the right competitor to be compared against — over "category creation."

### 5. The sales pitch is a structured decision-making tool

In *Sales Pitch*, April reframes the pitch as a tool to help buyers confidently make a decision — not a persuasion exercise. Her framework: (1) establish the context of change, (2) define the evaluation criteria, (3) present your solution in that context, (4) prove you can deliver, (5) make it easy to decide. Every element is designed to reduce decision anxiety.
