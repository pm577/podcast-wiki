# Uri Levine

Uri Levine appeared on Lenny's Podcast to discuss uri levine, waze co-founder and serial entrepreneur, on founder crisis management and startup resilience.

## Key Views

**Fall in love with the problem, not the solution** — Levine's central thesis is that the problem itself should serve as the North Star for the entire startup journey. He argues that when you start with the problem, you create a compelling story that makes customers care — they want you to succeed and will help you become successful. The validation heuristic is simple: speak with 20 people you don't know about the problem. If they correct your framing and describe their own version of the problem, it is real. If they say 'I know someone who has this problem,' it is not. Building a solution before validating the problem risks creating something nobody cares about. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine.md]

**The 30-day hiring rule and the cost of inaction** — Levine offers a stark tactical rule: every time you hire someone, mark your calendar for 30 days and ask yourself, 'Knowing what I know today, would I hire this person?' If yes, give them more equity. If no, fire them immediately. The insight is grounded in data: every failed founder he interviewed knew the team wasn't right within the first month but did nothing. The real problem wasn't the wrong hire — it was the CEO's inability to make a hard decision. When a CEO fails to act, top performers leave because they don't want to be in an organization that tolerates underperformance. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine.md]

**Fundraising is a dance of 100 nos — lead with your strongest point** — Levine emphasizes that investors decide whether they like you 'before you sit down,' so your opening must be your strongest point. Most founders waste their most-displayed slide: the title slide. He advises putting the most compelling fact (market size, problem scale, traction) on the first slide since it is displayed longest, and repeating it on the last slide (the 'Thank you' slide), which is the second-longest displayed. He also warns that early-stage fundraising is a 1% game — expect 100 nos for every yes — and that the CEO should go alone to meetings to keep the spotlight on their own story. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine.md]

**The startup journey has three dimensions: roller coaster, failures, and length** — Levine describes three unavoidable dimensions of building a startup. First, it is a roller coaster with far more extreme and frequent ups and downs than a normal business. Second, it is a journey of failures — you will try things that don't work repeatedly until you find what does. The conclusion: if you're afraid to fail, you've already failed, and you must fail fast to have more attempts and increase your likelihood of success. Third, it is a very long journey, with product-market fit typically taking years (four for Waze, five for Microsoft, ten for Netflix). The biggest enemy of good enough is perfect — you just need to iterate until you are good enough to win the market. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine.md]

**Product-market fit has one metric: retention, and winning requires understanding the early majority** — Levine distills product-market fit to a single metric: retention. If users return, you've created value; if they don't, you haven't. He warns that product builders (typically innovators or early adopters) cannot intuitively understand the early majority — one-third of the population who are afraid of change and ask 'What's wrong with what I already have?' The only way to win them is through simplicity (removing unused features) and by directly watching users who fail or churn, not just successful ones. Waze's global expansion failed repeatedly for over a year until iterative conversations with churned drivers revealed what wasn't good enough. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine.md]

### 1. **Two Types of Crisis: Cash and Product-Market Fit** — Every startup faces two abstract crisis types. Cash crisis: your funding, revenue, or runway is jeopardized (lost customer, disappearing investor). PMF crisis: your product is no longer relevant (regulation change, disruptive competition, market shift). Cash crisis means re-planning runway and expenses. PMF crisis means going back to square one — everything you knew is irrelevant. The faster you recognize which type you're in, the faster you can adapt. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine-20.md]

### 2. **Never Give Up and Make Decisions with Conviction** — The two most important behaviors of successful startup CEOs are: (1) never give up — always keep looking for ways to make it work, and (2) make decisions with conviction. If you don't decide with conviction, your team won't follow. If your team won't follow, you won't succeed. You cannot rely on someone else — assume responsibility because you control your own destiny. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine-20.md]

### 3. **Waze vs. Google Maps: When Everyone Tells You You're Doomed, Find the Different Use Case** — When Google announced free turn-by-turn navigation in 2010, investors told Waze to sell for $20-30M because they were 'doomed.' Waze survived because its use case was different: daily commuters (twice a day) vs. occasional navigation ('when I need it'). Simpler, more abstractive maps optimized for frequency over comprehensiveness. Eventually Google acquired Waze. The lesson: your investor's crisis may be your opportunity if you understand your actual use case. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine-20.md]

### 4. **Crisis Response Framework: What's Impacted, How Long, How Much Runway?** — Three questions to ask in any crisis: (1) What is actually being impacted? Runway? Product relevance? Revenue stream? (2) How long will it last? Temporary or permanent new future? (3) How much run rate do you have? Example: $5M/yr revenue, $400K/mo, net burn $200K/mo — if you lose half your revenue with $2.5M in the bank, you have six months. Re-plan immediately. Don't conflate industry-level problems with your own — your problem is your problem. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine-20.md]

### 5. **Product-Market Fit Has One Metric: Retention** — If users come back, you've created value. If they don't, you haven't. When PMF disappears, you need to go back to square one — but founders often fail because they're in love with their solution, not the problem. The classic trap: believing your solution can be marketed better than what the market actually wants. When regulation killed Fibo (tax filing simplified to <3 minutes), Uri decided to avoid regulator-dependent business models entirely. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/uri-levine-20.md]
