# Jeanne Grosser

**Role:** COO at Vercel; former CPO at Stripe, Google GTM leader  
**Known for:** Building world-class go-to-market organizations at Stripe and Vercel, pioneering the GTM Engineer role, and treating go-to-market as a product experience.

## Key Views

### 1. Go-to-market is a product — design the buying journey

Jeanne's core thesis: as technical differentiation narrows, the experience of being sold to becomes the primary differentiator. She advocates mapping every touchpoint from first awareness through five-year retention and designing each interaction like a product feature. At Stripe, she replaced the standard discovery call with a **whiteboarding session** where prospects drew their payment architecture — the customer walked away with a valuable asset, and Stripe learned more about the stack and competitive landscape than any standard qualification call would reveal.

> "We buy a lot of things because of how we feel about them. The experience that you have of being sold to will increasingly differentiate a company and drive buying decisions if products are only different at the margin." — Jeanne Grosser

### 2. The GTM Engineer is the most important new role in go-to-market

Jeanne's team at Vercel was early to hire GTM Engineers — former sales engineers (themselves ex-frontend developers) who build AI agents to automate sales workflows. Their **lead agent** handles inbound qualification: it took one GTM Engineer 6 weeks with ~30% of his time to build, costs ~$1,000/year to run, and replaced 9 out of 10 SDRs while holding lead-to-opportunity conversion flat. The freed SDRs moved to outbound. Key principles: model the agent after the highest-performing human, keep humans in the loop for review, and treat the agent as a force multiplier that shifts people up the value chain.

> "Our processes all always have human in the loop. We take a GTM engineer and have them shadow the highest performing individual in that function." — Jeanne Grosser

### 3. 80% of customers buy to avoid pain, not gain upside

A critical framing Jeanne shares: about 80% of enterprise buying decisions are driven by risk avoidance (not making revenue targets, being outdone by competitors, brand damage) rather than aspirational upside. This means founders who pitch "the art of the possible" are speaking the wrong language for most buyers. The more effective approach is to help customers discover where they're suboptimal through great discovery questions, then position your product as the risk-reducing solution.

> "We all love to talk about the art of the possible. But that's really a sale that's going to resonate with another founder. For everybody else, particularly enterprises, you're avoiding the risk of not making your revenue target next quarter." — Jeanne Grosser

### 4. Build your own GTM agents — don't wait for vendors

Jeanne's team built a **deal-bot** that runs against Gong transcripts to analyze lost opportunities in real time. In one case, the AE said a deal was lost on price, but the agent revealed they'd never reached an economic buyer and failed to demonstrate value. The lost-bot took 2 days to build. Her advice: the build-vs-buy calculus has shifted — internal agents are often cheap ($1K/yr), fast (days/weeks), and more effective because they incorporate your specific context, content, and workflow. She predicts CIOs will shift from procurers of software to builders of internal AI platforms.

> "It's not that hard to build these agents and they aren't that expensive either. That lead agent which runs full stack on Vercel will cost us about a thousand dollars to run for the entire year." — Jeanne Grosser

### 5. A sales org that doesn't feel like a sales org to engineers

Jeanne's litmus test: her account executives should sit in a room with 10 engineers for 10 minutes before anyone realizes they aren't product managers. This requires **deep product depth** in the sales team — the ability to discern signal from noise in customer feedback and feed real product insights back to the engineering roadmap. She builds a diversified sales portfolio mixing traditional AEs with consultant/banker types who bring quantitative skills and P&L thinking, creating a learning environment where each group teaches the other.

> "The best go-to-market orgs on the planet are equal parts revenue driving and R&D. If I have a 20-person sales team, think of the number of customers we talk to in a week." — Jeanne Grosser
