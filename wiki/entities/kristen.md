# Kristen Berman

Kristen Berman is CEO and co-founder of Irrational Labs, where she and her team use behavioral science to help companies like Google, Airbnb, PayPal, Microsoft, LinkedIn, and TikTok build better products. She bridges academic behavioral economics with real-world product design to create measurable business impact.

## Key Views

**Behavioral Economics Bridges Psychology and Economics — Humans Are Predictably Irrational**
Berman explains the foundation of her work: traditional economics assumes people are rational decision-makers. Behavioral economics combines psychology and economics to recognize that people make decisions with emotion, are present-biased, and follow social norms — but in predictable ways. "Once you understand how and why people behave, you can start to change it." This predictability is what makes behavioral science a practical tool for product design, not just an academic curiosity.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kristen-berman.md]]**

**Present Bias Is the Most Common Product Killer**
Berman identifies present bias — the tendency to over-value immediate rewards and under-value future benefits — as the most frequent psychological barrier products face. Users know they should save money, exercise more, or plan for the future, but they consistently choose the easier, more rewarding present option. Products that succeed are those that either make the desired behavior rewarding now (instant gratification) or make the barrier to starting as low as possible (reducing friction).
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kristen-berman.md]]**

**Social Norms Drive Behavior More Than Any Incentive**
Berman's work reveals that social influence is one of the most powerful levers for changing behavior. People look to what others are doing to decide what's appropriate, desirable, or safe. Products that surface social proof — "people like you are doing X" — consistently outperform those that rely on rational arguments or financial incentives. The key is making the social signal specific and relatable, not abstract (e.g., "5,000 people in your city saved $200 this month" vs. "millions of users").
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kristen-berman.md]]**

**Small Changes in Choice Architecture Create Massive Impact**
Berman shares case studies where simple changes in how choices are presented — changing the default option, reordering a list, reframing a trade-off — produced outsized results. At one company, changing a single line of copy from "sign up for automatic payments" to "continue with automatic payments" dramatically increased enrollment. The lesson: the way you structure decisions (choice architecture) often matters more than the options themselves. Defaults are the most powerful tool in the choice architect's toolkit.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kristen-berman.md]]**

**The Friction Audit: Find Every Place Users Have to Stop and Think**
Berman's practical framework for applying behavioral science: conduct a friction audit of your product. Identify every point where a user has to stop, think, make a decision, or overcome confusion. Each friction point is a drop-off risk. The solution isn't always to remove friction — sometimes adding friction in the right place (e.g., a confirmation step before an irreversible action) improves outcomes. The goal is intentional friction design, not friction minimization.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/kristen-berman.md]]**
