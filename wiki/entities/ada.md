# Ada Chen Rekhi

**Role:** Executive coach and co-founder of Notejoy; former SVP of Marketing at SurveyMonkey; former LinkedIn growth marketer; startup founder (acquired by LinkedIn)  
**Known for:** Helping founders scale themselves, career navigation frameworks, and the Curiosity Loop method for structured decision-making.

## Key Views

### 1. The Curiosity Loop — structured advice gathering

Ada's core framework for making better decisions: instead of asking vague questions, send a structured ask to ~10 trusted people. Give them a specific prompt (e.g., "pick your top 2 from these 9 options and tell me why") with rationale requested. A good question is specific, solicits rationale, and isn't biased. She distinguishes heavyweight loops (formal doc-based) from lightweight loops (ask the same question in everyday conversations and look for surprises). This fights bad advice — which is usually well-intentioned but decontextualized.

> "A good example of a question is specific, if it solicits rationale, and it's not biased. You don't want to start a question with 'here's what I think' because people have this tendency to want to please you." — Ada Chen Rekhi

### 2. The values exercise — ground decisions in what matters

Ada uses a 10-15 minute values exercise with her coaching clients: pick from a list of potential value words, filter into groups, stack-rank them. The output helps counter the "external scorecard" — the pressure to optimize for logos, titles, and what others think. She emphasizes that values can shift over time (e.g., autonomy and relationships become more important after starting a family).

> "These lists of values can help ground us from those obvious, externally good-seeming choices to what actually drives us." — Ada Chen Rekhi

### 3. Don't be the frog — escaping the slow boil

Ada warns against the "boiling frog" trap: small dissatisfactions that incrementally increase until you're trapped. The antidote is regular check-ins with yourself — if you'd be excited to have your current job offered to you today, you're in the right place. If not, it's time to make a change. She advocates optimizing for a mix of career success, meaningfulness, and values alignment.

> "It's a terrible outcome to wake up one day and be late career and feel trapped because you have a certain lifestyle... That's a trap we should all try to avoid." — Ada Chen Rekhi

### 4. Explore before you exploit — early career variety

Ada recommends early-career professionals optimize for variety and learning (exploration) rather than depth or prestige (exploitation). Her own career path — startup founder, big company marketing, growth roles, subscriptions experience — created a "winning combination" that made her a sought-after hire. Don't get stuck optimizing your resume; instead build a portfolio of diverse experiences.

> "I was focused on learnings and experiences... it was actually the winning combination that caused SurveyMonkey to send me an InMail." — Ada Chen Rekhi

### 5. The worst career trap is optimizing your resume instead of your life

Ada challenges the external scorecard mindset (Ivy League schools, famous logos, impressive titles). She argues that optimizing for resume-builders indefinitely means you retire and realize you lived someone else's life. Her framework: ask who you're trying to please. The values exercise surfaces the gap between what looks good on paper and what actually fulfills you.
