# Annie Duke

**Role:** Decision-making expert, former professional poker player ($4M+ tournament earnings), author of *Thinking in Bets* and *Quit*, Special Partner at First Round Capital  
**Known for:** Applying poker strategy to business decision-making, combating cognitive biases, the "Quit" framework, and structured decision processes.

## Key Views

### 1. Discover Independently, Discuss in the Room, Decide Privately

Annie's most impactful and easiest-to-implement framework: meetings should only be for **discussion**. Discovery (gathering opinions) and deciding should happen independently. Her method:
- **Before the meeting**: send out a Google Form or survey asking everyone for their independent estimates, forecasts, or forced-rank priorities. People input without seeing anyone else's answers.
- **In the meeting**: share the anonymized spread of opinions and facilitate discussion focused *only* on where people disagree. Avoid crosstalk and interruptions. Use reflective listening: "What I heard you say is... is that right?"
- **After the meeting**: one person (or a private vote) makes the final call.
This avoids the loudest/most confident person dominating, surface-area of disagreement expands (you see the true spread), and people feel heard and endowed to the decision even if it doesn't go their way.^[episodes/lenny/annie-duke]

### 2. "Nevertheless" — The Leadership Word

Annie's go-to move for parenting, management, and leadership: hear people out fully, reflect back what they said, then say "Nevertheless, this is the decision." Key distinction between a **coercive** model (convincing, interrupting, "you're wrong") and a **curious** model (conveying information, clarifying, reflecting). The goal isn't alignment/agreement — it's understanding. "Alignment is dumb — it doesn't exist. You have 10 people in a room and they're never going to agree. The goal shouldn't be coercing agreement; it should be hearing the full spread of opinions and then making a call."^[episodes/lenny/annie-duke]

### 3. Pre-Mortems Are Only Useful With Kill Criteria

The pre-mortem (imagining a project has failed and working backward to understand why) is only valuable if you set **kill criteria** upfront — concrete, measurable signals that tell you to stop. Without pre-committed actions, a pre-mortem is just a thought exercise. "Commit to actions that you're going to take if you see those signals."^[episodes/lenny/annie-duke]

### 4. Mental Time Travel — "How Will I Feel in 10 Years?"

Annie's core decision tool, taught to her children and applied to all decisions: project yourself into the future. Most things that feel catastrophic now will be funny Thanksgiving stories. "Nothing is as important as it seems when you're thinking about it" (Kahneman's focusing effect). For parenting, for business decisions, for career choices — mentally time-travel to gain perspective and defuse the emotional intensity of the present moment.^[episodes/lenny/annie-duke]

### 5. Resulting and the 50% → 65% Hiring Edge

Annie's key distinction: **never judge decision quality by outcome** (that's "resulting"). In poker and business, good decisions can produce bad outcomes and vice versa. Instead, evaluate the process. Danny Kahneman's work showed that moving from unstructured intuition ("I know a good PM when I see one") to a **structured rubric** with explicit criteria improved hiring hit rates from ~50% to ~65% — a massive jump. The catch: almost nobody does it. Most people who know about decision science don't apply it. That gap is where the edge lives.^[episodes/lenny/annie-duke]

### 6. Danny Kahneman's Humility — The Adversarial Collaboration Model

Annie's personal reflection on Daniel Kahneman: his defining trait was not his genius but his **humility and curiosity**. He invented "adversarial collaboration" — find someone who disagrees with you and jointly design a study to resolve the disagreement. When the "money buys happiness beyond $75K" result was challenged by Matthew Killingsworth, Kahneman didn't defend — he proposed doing the study together. He was always asking "why am I wrong?" He was the first to say "I wish priming wasn't in the book — it doesn't replicate." Annie: "He as a human being was just so spectacular, and I think in the end that dwarfs his body of work."^[episodes/lenny/annie-duke]
