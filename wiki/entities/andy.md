# Andy Raskin

**Role:** Strategic narrative consultant helping CEOs align teams; clients include Gong, Dropbox, Uber, Salesforce, Square  
**Known for:** The "strategic narrative" framework — replacing the "arrogant doctor" pitch with a story about a shift in the world.

## Key Views

### 1. The "arrogant doctor" pitch is broken

Andy's observation: most people pitch like arrogant doctors — "You have a problem/pain, I have a solution/treatment, and here's why mine is better." This structure is formulaic, forgettable, and doesn't inspire action. The alternative that actually works comes from studying great movies: every great pitch starts with a *shift in the world* — the old game is over, there's a new game with new rules, and your company is the key to winning in this new game.

> "The way I learned how to pitch in business school is what I call the arrogant doctor. The structure I found in great movies was different: every movie starts with some kind of shift in the world." — Andy Raskin

### 2. Salesforce as the archetypal strategic narrative

Andy uses Marc Benioff's Salesforce pitch as the perfect example: "Software is over. There's this new world called the cloud — a new game, new rules. That's the new way to win, and we're going to help you play there." This isn't about solving a problem — it's about defining a movement. People want to join a movement, not buy a solution.

### 3. A strategic narrative aligns sales, marketing, product, fundraising, and hiring

A single coherent narrative shouldn't live just in the pitch deck. Andy argues it should be the organizing principle for every function: sales uses it to close deals, marketing uses it to attract leads, product uses it to prioritize features, fundraising uses it to inspire investors, and recruiting uses it to attract talent. If your narrative isn't working across all five, it's not strategic enough.

### 4. Categories are limiting — create a movement instead

Andy pushes back on the obsession with "category creation." Instead of trying to invent and own a category (which requires massive category-creation marketing budgets), frame your offering as the obvious choice in a *new game* that's already emerging. This is more authentic, cheaper to communicate, and harder for competitors to co-opt.

### 5. Signs your narrative needs work

Andy's diagnostic for when your narrative is broken: sales cycles are too long, prospects don't remember what you do, your team gives inconsistent explanations, competitors define the market conversation, and your best customers struggle to explain why they bought. If any of these ring true, the fix isn't better messaging — it's a fundamentally better story about why the world has changed.
