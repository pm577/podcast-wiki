# Bangaly Kaba

**Role:** Director of Product Management at YouTube; former Head of Growth at Instagram, VP Product at Instacart, early Growth PM at Facebook  
**Known for:** The "Understand, Identify, Execute" framework, the "adjacent user" growth theory, and his career framework for choosing where to work and what to work on.

## Key Views

### 1. Understand Work — the anti-pattern to "Identify, Justify, Execute"

Bangaly's core methodology: most teams fall into the trap of **Identify → Justify → Execute** — someone has an idea, pulls data to justify it, and then spends months building something that doesn't work. The antidote is **Understand → Identify → Execute**: deliberately allocating team bandwidth to first understand the problem from first principles before committing to solutions. This means making "understand work" an intentional line item on the roadmap, not something that happens in the background. At Instagram, Bangaly's teams ran this way and achieved 60-70% positive experiment win rates across 15 teams.

> "Understand work is an intentional affordance in your execution to do the work that helps you to de-risk a project and to learn what's going on." — Bangaly Kaba

### 2. The adjacent user theory — the fastest path to growth

Bangaly's adjacent user framework: the easiest users to acquire aren't your core audience or a totally unrelated audience — they're people *adjacent* to your current users who share some but not all characteristics. At Instagram, instead of trying to convert Facebook users (too far) or looking for other photo apps (too close), they targeted users of specific adjacent apps and communities. The framework helps growth teams find the "Goldilocks zone" of acquisition — close enough to convert but far enough to expand the total addressable market.

### 3. Career framework: environment + skills = impact

Bangaly's decision framework for career moves has two dimensions: the **environment** (team quality, growth trajectory, scope for impact) and your **skills** (what you're good at, what you want to build). Impact is achievable only when the right environment meets the right skills. He advises against chasing titles or comp — instead, optimize for the intersection of a high-leverage environment and your unique strengths. The goal is to find situations where your skills are differentially valuable.

> "There's impact that you're really trying to drop and the impact is only achievable by looking at set of variables related to the environment, the set of variables related to your skills." — Bangaly Kaba

### 4. The mentor triad — better than cold outreach

Bangaly's advice for finding mentors: instead of cold-emailing "Will you be my mentor?" use the **triad approach**. Tell people you trust about a specific challenge you're working on, and ask if they know someone who's really good at solving that particular problem. The recommender creates a seed of common purpose, giving the connection a much higher likelihood of success. This targeted, challenge-based approach beats generic mentor-seeking.

> "Instead of just going to someone saying 'Hey, can you be a mentor?' … Come to me and say 'Hey, I'm trying to figure out how to change the way this team operates. Do you know someone who's really good at changing the way teams work?'" — Bangaly Kaba

### 5. The change framework: vision, skills, incentives, resources, action plan

Bangaly carries a five-part change management framework across every organization he's joined: **Vision** (clear direction), **Skills** (can people execute?), **Incentives** (are they motivated?), **Resources** (do they have the tools?), and **Action Plan** (is there a clear path?). For any transformation, all five must be present. He diagnoses which element is missing by listening carefully to what people are complaining about — which reveals the bottleneck to change. This framework travels with him from Facebook to Instagram to Instacart to YouTube.

> "I found this framework travels with me. It's got these five components to it — vision, skills, incentives, resources, action plan — and you need all of those to have change." — Bangaly Kaba

# Bangaly Kaba

Bangaly Kaba was an early growth PM at Facebook, Head of Growth at Instagram, VP of Product at Instacart, and Director of Product Management at YouTube. He is known for his structured frameworks on career decisions, growth strategy, and the "adjacent user" theory.

## Key Views

### Impact = Environment × Skills
Bangaly's career framework: impact is the product of environment variables (manager, resources, scope, team, compensation, culture) and your skills. He scores each environment variable annually (0-2 scale) to objectively assess what's limiting his impact. This framework prevents emotional decision-making about career moves. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bangaly-kaba.md]

### Understand Work — The Anti-Pattern to "Identify, Justify, Execute"
Most teams fall into the "identify, justify, execute" trap — someone proposes a feature, data justifies it, and the team builds it. Bangaly's alternative: "understand, identify, execute." First, deeply understand what's actually happening from first principles. Skipping the "understand" step leads to solving the wrong problems. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bangaly-kaba.md]

### The Adjacent User Theory for Growth
Look at users adjacent to your current core — people who have a similar need but aren't yet using your product. Instead of trying to reach everyone, systematically identify the next ring of users who share attributes with existing power users. This creates a stair-step expansion path. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bangaly-kaba.md]

### The Five Components of Change
When driving organizational change, all five must be present: vision, skills, incentives, resources, and an action plan. Diagnose which is missing and focus there. Most change initiatives fail because leaders assume people lack skills when they actually lack incentives — or vice versa. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bangaly-kaba.md]

### Creating Clarity Is the Highest Form of Senior PM Impact
The more senior you get, the more your job becomes answering: "Are we even investing in the right place?" Creating conviction that an opportunity is strategically important IS impact — even before any code is written. Then validate through quick wins, then medium wins, building a track record. ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/bangaly-kaba.md]
