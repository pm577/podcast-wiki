# Lane Shackleton

Lane Shackleton is the Chief Product Officer at Coda, where he has led product and design for over eight years. He previously was a Group Product Manager at YouTube and a Product Specialist at Google. Uniquely, he started his career as an Alaskan mountain guide — an experience that shaped his approach to preparation, calm under pressure, and team dynamics. He writes extensively about the principles and rituals of great product teams at lane.substack.com.

## Key Views

### Systems, Not Goals

Lane argues that great product teams obsess over systems, not goals. His example: Jerry Seinfeld threw away all his material and built it back up not by setting a goal, but by creating a system (write for an hour every morning, perform every night). "Goals with good intentions don't work." At Coda, they had a "default on" system of customer conversations every Friday — a recurring calendar slot where a customer would visit. "The way that we developed good customer instincts was not the goal, it was really just the system behind it."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lane-shackleton.md]

### Cathedrals, Not Bricks

Inspired by a question from Shishir Mehrotra at YouTube: "Do they know their cathedral?" The idea is that every team member needs to understand the grand vision (the cathedral) they're building toward, not just their individual brick. When Lane was frustrated about his team's performance, Shishir's question revealed the real problem: they didn't have a compelling vision to rally around. Great product leaders invest disproportionate energy in making the cathedral visible.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lane-shackleton.md]

### The Core Job: Turn Ambiguity Into Clarity

"The core job of a product person is to turn ambiguity into clarity." Everything is ambiguous — role, problem, customer, solution. The best PMs get good at spotting ambiguity and systematically resolving it. Lane's five role stages (apprentice through principal) at Coda are designed around increasing ability to handle ambiguity at scale, from "learns about rope" to "invented nylon."^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lane-shackleton.md]

### "Oh Shit" Moments as Career Progress

Lane's unconventional career metric: "How many 'oh shit' moments have you had in the last six months?" Moments where you feel underqualified or stretched are the ones that build new foundations. "If the answer is 'it's been a really long time since I've been stretched in some meaningful way,' then it may be worth digging into." Growth comes from seeking discomfort, not avoiding it.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lane-shackleton.md]

### Leveling Without the Rat Race

Coda uses only five role stages (vs. typical 10-15 levels), not visible across the company, with compensation decided by a central committee, not managers. "As soon as you introduce levels, the incentives flip from being company focused to being individual focused." By keeping levels minimal and invisible, they keep teams oriented toward impact rather than climbing the ladder.^[raw/transcripts/lenny/lane-shackleton.md]
