# Jackie Bavaro

Jackie Bavaro is the author of *Cracking the PM Interview* and *Cracking the PM Career* — two of the most influential books in product management. She was the first PM at Asana and later became Head of Product Management there. Known for demystifying product strategy and career growth for PMs, Bavaro is one of the most respected voices in the product community.

## Key Views

**Strategy Is Not a Mystical Skill — It's a Set of Learnable Practices**
Bavaro demystifies strategy after being told she needed to be "more strategic" at Google with no explanation of what that meant. She defines strategy as making clear choices about what you will and won't do, based on understanding the customer problem, the competitive landscape, and your unique advantages. She emphasizes that great strategy emerges from deep customer understanding and explicit trade-offs — not from frameworks alone. Her book dedicates a whole section to breaking down what "being strategic" actually looks like in day-to-day PM work.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jackie-bavaro.md]]*

**Say Yes Before You Say No — The Collaboration Mindset Shift**
Early at Asana, Bavaro had a defensive mindset of saying no to everyone. Her coach challenged her to "say yes to everything for two weeks." This completely reframed her approach: instead of shutting people down, she learned to interpret their suggestions as signals about real problems. "Even if their solution isn't what I think is best, the problem they're talking about is probably real." This unlocked genuine collaboration and made her a more effective PM. The lesson: saying no too quickly can prevent you from discovering better solutions through others' input.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jackie-bavaro.md]]*

**You Don't Need to Be a Manager to Have Impact or Make Money**
Bavaro challenges the assumption that career growth requires moving into people management. Senior PM at major tech companies makes as much as doctors or lawyers. She argues that many people become managers by default because they think it's the only path to growth. Instead, she recommends considering alternative ways to grow: switching companies, working on higher-impact products, or going deep on platform or infrastructure work where IC PMs can have director-level impact with smaller teams. Management is a two-way door — try it, and if you don't like it, go back.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jackie-bavaro.md]]*

**The Art of Leveling Up: Insert Yourself Into Higher-Level Conversations**
Bavaro shares how she got herself into Asana's planning committee (the CEO, co-founder, head of engineering, and head of business). Instead of demanding a seat, she framed it as helping her boss: "Here are the three questions I want you to get answered at that committee. If they say this, ask this." Eventually she offered to just attend to save him effort. This pattern — adding value first, then expanding scope — is how she learned business strategy, financial planning, and the full picture of running a company. The lesson: find ways to be useful in rooms you want to be in.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jackie-bavaro.md]]*

**The Hidden Burden of Management — Why It's Lonelier and Heavier Than Expected**
Bavaro candidly shares the downsides of being a product leader: you can't be transparent about departures, promotions, or compensation discussions, creating a constant tension between what's best for the company and what's best for your reports. "There are times when what the person wants is a promotion to make more money, and they haven't necessarily earned it yet." This weight caused her burnout after years in management. Her advice: understand that management is fundamentally different from IC work — it's more about holding secrets, making hard trade-offs, and accepting that you won't be universally liked.
*Source: [[raw/transcripts/lenny/jackie-bavaro.md]]*
