# Lauryn Isford — Insights

*Extracted from podcast appearances.*

## Insight 1: An activation rate that falls in a lower percentage range, maybe for most compan...
**Claim:** An activation rate that falls in a lower percentage range, maybe for most companies five to 15%, is better than one that falls in a higher percentage range because it means that there's likely much higher correlation with long-term retention and you're really working hard to get most of your users to reach a state that they're not reaching today.
**Audience:** operator | **Actionability:** 7/10 | **Confidence:** 0.9
**Source:** lauryn-isford
**Context:** Discussion about activation metrics and their correlation with retention.

## Insight 2: Experiment when you need to and primarily see it as a risk mitigation tactic whe...
**Claim:** Experiment when you need to and primarily see it as a risk mitigation tactic when you're making dramatic changes. Let the product development process do more work: spend more time with customers, be more rigorous in understanding precisely what problem you're solving, get mocks in front of people and see how they react.
**Audience:** operator | **Actionability:** 9/10 | **Confidence:** 0.95
**Source:** lauryn-isford
**Context:** Lauryn's contrarian take on experimentation, advocating for less reliance on A/B testing.

## Insight 3: If the business, let's say activation, activation rates go up 6% versus 7%, that...
**Claim:** If the business, let's say activation, activation rates go up 6% versus 7%, that precision actually doesn't help all that much beyond being able to say in your performance review, 'Hey, I increased activation by 7%.' So, it also is expensive to have folks on the ground spending time understanding the results of an experiment that could otherwise be spent on road mapping, on foundational analysis, on shipping things.
**Audience:** operator | **Actionability:** 8/10 | **Confidence:** 0.9
**Source:** lauryn-isford
**Context:** Explaining why not every change needs an experiment, especially when precision doesn't add value.

## Insight 4: To escape the trap of experimenting everything, you have to have a very intentio...
**Claim:** To escape the trap of experimenting everything, you have to have a very intentional culture about doing right by customers and doing the right thing for the business. There have to be other ways to motivate, reward, retain, and develop really good talent, like a culture around the impact you had on customers as measured by qualitative feedback or deals closed.
**Audience:** founder | **Actionability:** 6/10 | **Confidence:** 0.85
**Source:** lauryn-isford
**Context:** Discussing how to build a culture that doesn't rely solely on experiment results for performance evaluation.
