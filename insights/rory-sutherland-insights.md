# Rory Sutherland — Insights

*Extracted from podcast appearances.*

## Insight 1: When a product succeeds, we often forget the crucial role marketing played in it...
**Claim:** When a product succeeds, we often forget the crucial role marketing played in its adoption. Rory even claimed that 'Steve Jobs was not a technologist; he was a pitchman.' Many innovative products, from electricity to the internet, faced significant initial skepticism until marketing won consumers over.
**Audience:** founder | **Actionability:** 7/10 | **Confidence:** 0.9
**Source:** rory-sutherland-vice-chairman-of-ogilvy-uk-author-1
**Context:** Discussion on the success and failure of products and marketing's role in adoption.

## Insight 2: Create space for unorthodox ideas. Remember: the opposite of a good idea can be ...
**Claim:** Create space for unorthodox ideas. Remember: the opposite of a good idea can be another good idea. For example, both high-touch and no-touch hotel check-ins can be excellent experiences. We often act on the assumption that the world is linear, but it is often more complex than that. One helpful habit is to share logical ideas first and then create space for silly and crazy ones.
**Audience:** operator | **Actionability:** 8/10 | **Confidence:** 0.9
**Source:** rory-sutherland-vice-chairman-of-ogilvy-uk-author-1
**Context:** Discussion on balancing rational ideas with irrational ideas and creating space for unorthodox thinking.

## Insight 3: In certain contexts, behaving irrationally can be advantageous because it makes ...
**Claim:** In certain contexts, behaving irrationally can be advantageous because it makes your actions less predictable and thus harder for others to exploit. This unpredictability can be a form of protection or strategic advantage.
**Framework:** Crazy Ivan
**Audience:** general | **Actionability:** 6/10 | **Confidence:** 0.85
**Source:** rory-sutherland-vice-chairman-of-ogilvy-uk-author-1
**Context:** Discussion on how thinking irrationally can be advantageous, referencing the 'Crazy Ivan' concept.

## Insight 4: When innovating, aim for 'most advanced, yet acceptable' (MAYA). People generall...
**Claim:** When innovating, aim for 'most advanced, yet acceptable' (MAYA). People generally prefer evolution over complete reinvention. However, maintaining some idiosyncrasies, or 'the right amount of weird,' can create distinctiveness and interest. For example, Jaguar has a unique and memorable light switch and Veuve Clicquot has a defining yellow label.
**Framework:** MAYA (Most Advanced, Yet Acceptable)
**Audience:** engineer | **Actionability:** 8/10 | **Confidence:** 0.9
**Source:** rory-sutherland-vice-chairman-of-ogilvy-uk-author-1
**Context:** Discussion on the MAYA principle and the importance of distinctiveness in product design.
