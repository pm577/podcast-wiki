# 5 Key Insights from Wes Kao (Maven, altMBA, Section4)

Extracted from: Lenny's Podcast — "Persuasive communication and managing up"

- **MOO (Most Obvious Objection):** Before sharing an idea, spend two minutes anticipating the most obvious objections. This prepares you to address concerns proactively rather than being blindsided. *"A lot of times we're surprised by the questions that we get especially in meetings, we feel blindsided. When really, if you thought for even two minutes about what are obvious objections that I'm likely to get, you often immediately come up with what some of those things are."* ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

- **Sales, Then Logistics:** Never start with the how before you've sold the why. If your audience hasn't bought into the purpose, diving into details will only generate confusion and silence. *"A common mistake that I see is overestimating the amount of buy-in that you have from your audience. So that looks like jumping straight into talking about the logistics, the details of the how to do something, of the process. When in reality your audience has not yet decided if they even want to do the thing."* ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

- **Concise ≠ Brief — Preparation is the Real Solution:** People confuse being concise with being brief, but true concision is about economy of words and knowing your core point — which requires preparation, not just editing. *"Being concise is not about absolute word count, it's about economy of words. It's about the density of the insight that you're sharing... we've all heard 'Don't bury the lead, cut to the chase'... all of these pithy aphorisms assume that you actually know what your core point is. So you can't cut to the chase unless you know what the chase is."* ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

- **Strategy, Not Self-Expression (Giving Feedback):** When giving constructive feedback, the goal is behavior change — not venting your frustrations. Trim everything that doesn't serve that goal, typically keeping only 10% of what you initially want to say. *"The goal is behavior change. So if that's the goal, trim everything else that you were about to say that does not actually contribute to that goal and only keep the part that will make the person want to change, help them understand the benefit to them as well as to the people around them."* ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]

- **Managing Up = Share Your Point of View:** Stop asking your manager, "What should we do?" Instead, come with a recommendation and ask for feedback. This reduces your manager's cognitive load and signals strategic thinking. *"When you just ask your manager, 'Hey manager, what should we do?' You're putting a lot of cognitive load on your manager... Whereas if you instead said, 'Hey manager, here's what I think we should do. How does that sound? Where do you see gaps? Am I thinking in the right direction?' You give them something to build off of and that reduces the amount of mental lift that they have to put forth."* ^[raw/transcripts/lenny/wes-kao.md]
